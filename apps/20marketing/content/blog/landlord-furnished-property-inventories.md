Furnished properties carry a higher inventory burden than unfurnished ones. In addition to documenting the condition of the building — walls, floors, fixtures, fittings — you must also record every item of furniture, every piece of kitchen equipment, every cushion, lamp, and picture frame.

The stakes are correspondingly higher. Missing items, damaged furniture, and stained upholstery are among the most common reasons for deposit deductions in furnished lets. Without a comprehensive, itemised inventory, proving what was in the property at the start of the tenancy is extremely difficult.

This guide covers how to approach furnished property inventories, what to include, and how to handle the specific challenges they present.

## Why furnished properties need more detail

An unfurnished property inventory focuses on the building: the condition of walls, floors, ceilings, windows, doors, and fixed installations like the kitchen and bathroom. There are plenty of items to document, but the scope is essentially the shell and its permanent fixtures.

A furnished property adds hundreds of additional items to that scope. A typical two-bedroom furnished flat might include:

- Living room: sofa, armchairs, coffee table, TV unit, bookshelf, lamps, cushions, throws, rug, curtains, pictures, ornaments
- Kitchen: full set of crockery, cutlery, glassware, pots, pans, cooking utensils, chopping boards, dish rack, kettle, toaster, kitchen towels
- Bedrooms: beds, mattresses, bedding, wardrobes, drawers, bedside tables, lamps, mirrors
- Bathroom: bath mat, shower curtain, towels (sometimes)

Every one of these items needs to be listed individually, described accurately, and photographed. When the tenant checks out, every one needs to be accounted for.

## How to structure a furnished inventory

### Separate condition from contents

Your inventory should address two distinct aspects:

1. **Building condition** — the standard room-by-room assessment of walls, floors, fixtures, and fittings that applies to all properties
2. **Contents schedule** — an itemised list of every removable or replaceable item, with its description, quantity, and condition

Some inventory software combines these into a single workflow, documenting the room condition and its contents together. Others separate them into distinct sections. Either approach works, as long as both are comprehensive.

### Itemise everything

The contents schedule should list every individual item. "Kitchen equipment" as a single line is not sufficient. You need:

- 6x white dinner plates — good condition, no chips
- 6x side plates — good condition, one minor chip on rim of one plate
- 6x bowls — good condition
- 6x mugs — good condition, slight tea staining on two mugs
- Cutlery set: 6x knives, 6x forks, 6x dessert spoons, 6x teaspoons — complete, good condition
- 1x non-stick frying pan, 28cm — good condition, light scratching on interior surface
- 1x medium saucepan with lid — good condition
- 1x large saucepan with lid — good condition, slight discolouration on base

This level of detail may seem excessive, but at check-out it is the only way to confirm whether items are missing, additional, or damaged.

### Describe materials and colours

For furniture, note the material, colour, and any identifying features:

- "Grey fabric two-seater sofa" not just "sofa"
- "Oak-effect laminate coffee table with single drawer" not just "coffee table"
- "White ceramic table lamp with cream shade" not just "lamp"

These descriptions help identify the exact item and its replacement cost if it is damaged or missing.

### Note serial numbers and brands where visible

For larger items and appliances, note the brand and model if visible:

- "Samsung washing machine, model WW70T4020EE, white"
- "Ikea KALLAX shelf unit, 4x4, white"

This information is useful for replacement cost assessment and insurance purposes.

## Photographing furnished properties

Photography is even more important for furnished properties than unfurnished ones. You need to capture:

### Room overview with furniture

A wide shot of each room showing the furniture layout and general condition. This establishes what was in the room and where it was positioned.

### Individual furniture items

Each significant piece of furniture should have its own photograph showing its overall condition. For sofas and upholstered items, photograph the front, the cushions, and any visible wear or marks.

### Close-ups of any existing damage

If a table has a scratch, a sofa has a stain, or a chair has a wobbly leg, photograph it in detail. These pre-existing issues need to be recorded so they are not attributed to the tenant at check-out.

### Kitchen contents

For kitchens, a photograph of the complete set of crockery, cutlery, pans, and utensils — laid out clearly — provides a visual record that complements the written list. This is particularly useful for confirming quantities.

### Bedroom items

Photograph beds (including mattresses before bedding is placed), wardrobes (open to show interior condition), and any provided bedding or soft furnishings.

## Common issues with furnished inventories

### Missing items at check-out

Items go missing. A tenant might accidentally pack a landlord's kitchen knife into their own moving box, break a glass and throw it away without mentioning it, or simply misplace a cushion. Without an itemised inventory, you have no way to identify what is missing.

When items are missing, the deduction should reflect the item's current value, not its replacement cost as new — unless the item was new at check-in. A set of plates purchased five years ago has depreciated; the tenant should pay a proportional cost, not the full new price.

### Damage to upholstery

Sofas, chairs, and mattresses are vulnerable to stains, tears, and general wear. Document their condition meticulously at check-in, including close-ups of any existing marks. At check-out, the same areas should be inspected and photographed from the same angles.

### Kitchen equipment wear

Pots, pans, and cooking utensils wear down with use. Non-stick coatings degrade, knives dull, and chopping boards get cut marks. Some of this is fair wear and tear. The check-in condition is critical — if a frying pan was noted as "new" at check-in and is "heavily scratched, non-stick coating peeling" at check-out after a one-year tenancy, that may go beyond reasonable use.

### Items added by tenants

Tenants sometimes bring their own furniture, appliances, or equipment. At check-out, you need to distinguish between landlord-owned items and tenant-owned items. A clear check-in inventory prevents any confusion about which items belong to whom.

### Storage of landlord's items

Some tenancy agreements allow tenants to store landlord's furniture in a garage, loft, or spare room. If this happens, the stored items should still be documented at check-out to confirm they are present and undamaged.

## Furnished vs part-furnished

Some properties are "part-furnished," meaning the landlord provides the major items (beds, sofa, white goods) but not the smaller items (kitchen equipment, bedding, soft furnishings). The inventory approach is the same — everything provided by the landlord must be listed and documented. The scope is just narrower.

Be clear in the inventory about what is landlord-owned and what is not. This prevents disputes about items the tenant brought and later left behind.

## Setting expectations with tenants

At check-in, walk the tenant through the contents schedule. Make sure they understand that every item listed is expected to be present and in similar condition at check-out, allowing for fair wear and tear. Give them a copy of the inventory and a reasonable period to raise any discrepancies.

This transparency reduces disputes. When tenants know exactly what they are responsible for, they are more likely to take care of the contents and report issues promptly.

## The bottom line

Furnished property inventories require more time, more detail, and more discipline than unfurnished ones. But the protection they provide is proportionally greater. The more items in a property, the more that can go wrong — and the more you stand to lose without proper documentation.

Itemise everything. Photograph everything. Describe everything. It is the only reliable way to protect the investment that a furnished property represents.
