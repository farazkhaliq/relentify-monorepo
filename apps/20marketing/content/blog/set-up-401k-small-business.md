Offering a 401(k) plan is one of the most effective ways to attract and retain employees. It is also one of the most impactful benefits you can provide — helping your team build financial security while giving your business valuable tax advantages.

Many small business owners assume 401(k) plans are only for large companies with dedicated HR departments. That is no longer the case. Modern plan providers have made 401(k)s accessible and affordable for businesses of all sizes, including sole proprietors and single-employee companies.

This guide walks you through the process of setting up a 401(k), from choosing the right plan type to managing ongoing compliance.

## Why offer a 401(k)?

### For your employees

A 401(k) allows employees to save for retirement on a tax-advantaged basis. Contributions reduce their taxable income in the current year, and investments grow tax-deferred until withdrawal in retirement. For many workers, a 401(k) is their primary retirement savings vehicle.

### For your business

**Tax deductions.** Employer contributions to the plan are tax-deductible as a business expense. This reduces your taxable income.

**Tax credits.** Small businesses may be eligible for a tax credit of up to $5,000 per year for the first three years of a new plan, helping offset setup and administration costs. The SECURE Act 2.0 expanded these credits significantly for small employers.

**Recruitment and retention.** A retirement benefit is consistently ranked among the most valued employee benefits. Offering one gives you a competitive advantage in hiring.

**Owner benefits.** If you are a business owner or partner, you can contribute to the plan yourself, building your own retirement savings with tax advantages.

## Types of 401(k) plans for small businesses

### Traditional 401(k)

The standard 401(k) allows employees to make pre-tax contributions, reducing their current taxable income. Employers may choose to make matching contributions, profit-sharing contributions, or both.

**Best for:** Businesses that want maximum flexibility in plan design and are prepared to handle annual compliance testing.

### Safe harbor 401(k)

A safe harbor plan requires the employer to make a minimum contribution — either a matching contribution or a non-elective contribution — in exchange for exemption from most annual compliance tests. This simplifies administration and ensures the plan benefits all employees, not just highly compensated ones.

**Common safe harbor formulas:**
- Basic match: 100% of the first 3% of compensation, plus 50% of the next 2%
- Enhanced match: 100% of the first 4% of compensation
- Non-elective contribution: 3% of compensation for all eligible employees, regardless of whether they contribute

**Best for:** Businesses that want simplified administration and are willing to commit to employer contributions.

### SIMPLE 401(k)

Designed specifically for businesses with 100 or fewer employees, the SIMPLE 401(k) offers lower administrative requirements than traditional plans. Employers must make either a matching contribution (up to 3% of compensation) or a 2% non-elective contribution.

**Best for:** Very small businesses that want an easy-to-administer plan with lower setup costs.

### Solo 401(k)

Also called a one-participant 401(k), this plan is available to self-employed individuals and business owners with no employees (other than a spouse). It allows very high contribution limits because the owner acts as both employer and employee.

**Best for:** Sole proprietors, freelancers, and single-member LLCs with no employees.

## Step-by-step setup process

### Step 1: Choose your plan type

Review the plan types above and select the one that fits your business size, budget, and administrative capacity. If you are unsure, consult a financial advisor or retirement plan specialist.

Key decisions at this stage:
- Will you offer employer matching? If so, what formula?
- Will you allow Roth (after-tax) contributions?
- What will the vesting schedule be for employer contributions?
- Who will be eligible to participate?

### Step 2: Select a plan provider

Your plan provider handles most of the administration, including recordkeeping, investment options, participant communication, and compliance testing.

**What to evaluate:**
- **Fees** — Setup fees, annual administration fees, per-participant fees, and investment expense ratios. Fee structures vary widely, so compare total costs across providers.
- **Investment options** — A range of low-cost index funds and target-date funds is sufficient for most plans. Avoid providers that push expensive proprietary funds.
- **Technology** — Online enrollment, mobile access, and self-service tools for participants.
- **Support** — Dedicated account management and responsive customer service.
- **Compliance support** — Help with annual testing, Form 5500 filing, and regulatory changes.

### Step 3: Adopt a written plan document

Every 401(k) plan requires a formal written plan document that specifies the plan's terms — eligibility requirements, contribution formulas, vesting schedules, distribution rules, and administrative procedures.

Most plan providers supply a pre-approved plan document that has already received IRS approval. Customizing an individually designed plan document is possible but more expensive and typically unnecessary for small businesses.

### Step 4: Set up a trust

Plan assets must be held in a trust for the exclusive benefit of participants. Your plan provider typically establishes this trust as part of the setup process. As the employer, you (or a designated plan administrator) serve as the trustee responsible for managing plan assets prudently.

### Step 5: Develop a record-keeping system

You need to track:
- Employee eligibility and enrollment
- Contribution amounts (employee and employer)
- Vesting schedules
- Loan and hardship withdrawal processing
- Required notices and disclosures

Your payroll system handles the contribution calculations and deductions. An integrated platform like Relentify that combines payroll and accounting ensures that contributions are calculated correctly, deducted from paychecks, and recorded in your financial statements automatically.

### Step 6: Communicate the plan to employees

Provide employees with:
- A Summary Plan Description (SPD) explaining the plan's terms in plain language
- Enrollment materials and instructions
- Information about investment options
- Contact details for questions

Many providers handle participant communication, including enrollment meetings, online education, and annual statements.

## Contribution limits

Contribution limits are set by the IRS and adjusted annually for inflation. As of recent guidelines:

**Employee contributions:** Employees can defer a set annual maximum from their salary (with a catch-up contribution available for employees aged 50 and over).

**Employer contributions:** Combined employee and employer contributions are subject to a separate annual maximum per participant.

**Compensation limit:** Only compensation up to a specified annual limit can be considered when calculating contributions.

Check the IRS website each year for current limits, as they change annually.

## Ongoing compliance requirements

### Annual testing (traditional 401(k) only)

Traditional 401(k) plans must pass annual nondiscrimination tests to ensure the plan does not disproportionately benefit highly compensated employees. The two main tests are:

- **ADP test** — Compares the deferral percentages of highly compensated and non-highly compensated employees
- **ACP test** — Compares employer matching contributions between the same groups

If the plan fails these tests, corrective actions are required — typically refunding excess contributions to highly compensated employees. Safe harbor plans are exempt from most of these tests.

### Form 5500

Plans with more than one participant must file Form 5500 annually with the Department of Labor. This form reports plan financial information, participant counts, and compliance data. Your plan provider typically prepares this filing.

### Participant notices

Several required notices must be provided to participants at specified intervals:
- Safe harbor notice (annually, before the start of the plan year)
- Automatic enrollment notice (if applicable)
- Fee disclosure notice (annually)
- Summary Annual Report

### Fiduciary responsibilities

As the plan sponsor, you have a fiduciary duty to act in the best interest of plan participants. This includes:
- Selecting and monitoring investment options
- Ensuring fees are reasonable
- Following the plan document
- Processing contributions and distributions timely

Fiduciary liability is a serious responsibility. Many small business owners address it by selecting a plan provider that assumes fiduciary responsibility for investment selection and monitoring.

## Common mistakes to avoid

**Delaying contribution deposits.** Employee contributions must be deposited into the plan trust as soon as they can reasonably be segregated from general business assets — typically within a few business days of payroll. Late deposits are a common compliance violation and can result in penalties.

**Ignoring the plan after setup.** A 401(k) requires ongoing attention — annual testing, filings, fee reviews, and investment monitoring. Do not assume your provider handles everything without oversight.

**Choosing high-fee investments.** Over time, high investment fees significantly reduce participant returns. Prioritize low-cost index funds and ensure total plan fees are competitive.

**Not reviewing the plan annually.** Tax law changes, business growth, and participant demographics all affect your plan. Review the plan structure annually to ensure it still meets your needs.

## Getting started

Setting up a 401(k) is a meaningful commitment, but the process has become significantly more accessible for small businesses. Start by requesting proposals from two or three plan providers, compare their fees and services, and choose the one that best fits your business. The tax advantages, employee retention benefits, and long-term retirement savings make it one of the most valuable benefits a small business can offer.
