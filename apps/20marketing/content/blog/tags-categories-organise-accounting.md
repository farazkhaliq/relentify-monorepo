Your chart of accounts categorises transactions by type — revenue, cost of goods sold, rent, salaries, utilities. This structure is essential for producing standard financial reports. But it only gives you one way to look at your data. What if you want to see profitability by location? Revenue by product line? Expenses by department? Marketing spend by campaign?

This is where tags and categories come in. They add additional dimensions to your accounting data, letting you filter, group, and analyse transactions in ways that your chart of accounts alone cannot support.

## The difference between accounts, categories, and tags

### Chart of accounts

Your chart of accounts is the backbone of your accounting system. Every transaction is posted to at least one account. This determines where the transaction appears in your financial statements — revenue on the profit and loss, assets on the balance sheet, and so on.

The chart of accounts answers: **What type of transaction is this?**

### Categories (or classes, departments, cost centres)

Categories add a second dimension. They typically represent structural divisions within your business — departments, locations, divisions, or business units. A single chart of accounts entry can be further classified by category.

Categories answer: **Which part of the business does this relate to?**

### Tags (or labels, tracking categories)

Tags add flexible, user-defined dimensions. Unlike categories, which usually follow a fixed hierarchy, tags can be applied freely and a transaction can have multiple tags.

Tags answer: **What else do I want to know about this transaction?**

## How categories work

### Common category structures

**By department:**
- Sales
- Marketing
- Operations
- Administration
- Research and development

**By location:**
- London office
- Manchester office
- Remote team

**By business line:**
- Consulting
- Products
- Training

**By project:**
- Project Alpha
- Project Beta
- Client XYZ

### Using categories for reporting

When categories are applied consistently, you can generate financial reports filtered or grouped by category. This gives you:

- **Departmental P&L** — Revenue and expenses for each department, showing which are profitable
- **Location-based reporting** — Financial performance by office or geography
- **Business line analysis** — Profitability of each service or product line

### Setting up categories

Define your categories based on the management information you need. Ask yourself: what organisational dimensions do I need to see financial data for?

Keep the structure simple enough to be practical. If assigning a category to every transaction becomes burdensome, compliance will drop and the data will become unreliable.

## How tags work

### Flexible classification

Tags are more flexible than categories. They are typically free-form labels that you can create and apply as needed. Common uses include:

**By project or client:**
- Tag: "Client-ABC"
- Tag: "Project-Launch"

**By campaign:**
- Tag: "Q1-Campaign"
- Tag: "Google-Ads"
- Tag: "Trade-Show-2027"

**By funding source:**
- Tag: "Grant-Funded"
- Tag: "Self-Funded"

**By priority:**
- Tag: "Essential"
- Tag: "Discretionary"

### Multiple tags per transaction

Unlike categories, which usually allow only one per transaction, tags can often be stacked. A single expense might be tagged as "Marketing," "Q1-Campaign," and "Google-Ads." This creates multiple ways to filter and analyse the same data.

### Ad hoc analysis

Tags excel at supporting ad hoc analysis — questions you did not anticipate when setting up your chart of accounts. If you decide mid-year that you want to track spending on a particular initiative, you can create a tag and start applying it immediately without restructuring your accounts.

## Practical examples

### Example 1: Marketing spend analysis

You want to understand how much you spend on marketing and which channels deliver the best return.

**Chart of accounts:** All marketing costs go to a "Marketing" expense account.

**Tags:** Apply tags for each channel:
- Google-Ads
- Social-Media
- Email-Marketing
- Events
- Content-Production

**Result:** You can see total marketing spend in your P&L, and use tags to break it down by channel. Compare spending by channel against the revenue each channel generates.

### Example 2: Multi-location business

You run two offices and want to see profitability by location.

**Categories:** London, Manchester

**Result:** Every transaction is assigned to a location. You can run a P&L for each location separately, identifying which is more profitable and where costs might be managed differently.

### Example 3: Grant-funded activities

You receive a grant that must be spent on specific activities and reported on separately.

**Tags:** "Grant-2027"

**Result:** Tag all income and expenses related to the grant. At reporting time, filter by the tag to produce the required grant expenditure report.

### Example 4: Tracking a specific initiative

You launch a new product line and want to track all associated costs from development through to launch.

**Tags:** "New-Product-X"

**Result:** Tag all expenses across different account categories — development costs, marketing, legal, packaging — that relate to the new product. Get a total investment figure at any time.

## Best practices

### Be consistent

Tags and categories only work if they are applied consistently. If half your transactions are untagged, your reports will be incomplete and misleading.

Establish naming conventions and stick to them. "Google Ads," "GoogleAds," and "google-ads" are three different tags to your software but represent the same thing.

### Keep it manageable

More is not better. Too many categories or tags create confusion and make consistent application difficult. Start with the dimensions you genuinely need for decision-making and add more only when there is a clear need.

### Define ownership

Someone should be responsible for maintaining your category and tag structure — creating new tags when needed, merging duplicates, and ensuring consistency. Without ownership, the system degrades over time.

### Review regularly

Periodically review your tags and categories:

- Are they all still relevant?
- Are there duplicates or near-duplicates that should be merged?
- Are there gaps — useful dimensions you are not tracking?
- Is the team applying them consistently?

### Use your software's features

Modern [accounting software](/accounting) makes tagging and categorisation straightforward. Look for features like:

- **Required fields** — Force a category or tag to be selected before a transaction can be saved
- **Default rules** — Automatically apply tags based on criteria (supplier, amount, account)
- **Filtered reports** — Generate P&L, balance sheet, and other reports filtered by tag or category
- **Bulk editing** — Apply or change tags on multiple transactions at once

### Document your system

Create a brief reference guide that explains your category structure and tag conventions. Share it with anyone who records transactions. This prevents confusion and ensures new team members can apply the system correctly from day one.

## The reporting payoff

The effort of tagging and categorising transactions pays off when you need answers:

- "How much did we spend on the trade show?" — Filter by tag
- "Is the Manchester office profitable?" — Filter by category
- "What is our total investment in the new product line?" — Filter by tag
- "How do Google Ads costs compare to social media this quarter?" — Filter by tags

Without tags and categories, answering these questions requires manual analysis — digging through individual transactions and building spreadsheets. With them, the answer is a few clicks away.

## Start simple

If you are not currently using tags or categories, start with one dimension that would be most useful for your business. Apply it to new transactions going forward. Once the habit is established, consider adding additional dimensions. The key is to start — even a single well-maintained category structure provides more insight than no structure at all.
