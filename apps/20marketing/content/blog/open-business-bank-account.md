A dedicated business bank account is one of the first things you need when starting a business. For limited companies, it is a legal requirement — the company is a separate legal entity and must have its own bank account. For sole traders, it is not strictly mandatory, but it is strongly recommended. Mixing personal and business finances creates confusion, complicates your tax returns, and makes it nearly impossible to get a clear picture of your business performance.

This guide covers everything you need to know about choosing, applying for, and making the most of your business bank account.

## Why you need a separate business account

The reasons go beyond simple organisation:

**Tax compliance.** When you file your tax return, you need to report business income and expenses accurately. If personal and business transactions are mixed together, separating them becomes a time-consuming and error-prone exercise. A dedicated account makes everything straightforward.

**Professional image.** When clients pay you, they see the name on the receiving account. A payment to "Jane Smith" looks less professional than a payment to "Smith Consulting Ltd." It is a small detail, but first impressions matter.

**Accounting efficiency.** Modern accounting software connects to your bank account and imports transactions automatically. When your business account contains only business transactions, bank reconciliation becomes a quick daily task rather than a weekly ordeal of sorting through personal purchases.

**Financial clarity.** With a separate account, you can see your business cash position at a glance. No mental arithmetic to subtract personal spending from the balance. No uncertainty about whether you can afford that new piece of equipment.

## Types of business bank account

### Current accounts

The standard account for day-to-day business banking. You receive payments, make payments, and manage your cash flow. Most business current accounts come with a debit card, online banking, and the ability to set up direct debits and standing orders.

### Savings accounts

A separate account for setting aside money — typically for tax reserves, emergency funds, or planned investments. Interest rates on business savings accounts are generally modest, but the discipline of separating reserves from operating cash is valuable.

### Multi-currency accounts

If you work with international clients or suppliers, a multi-currency account lets you hold, send, and receive money in different currencies without automatic conversion. This can save significant money on exchange rate margins.

### Payment processing accounts

Some businesses need specialised accounts that integrate with payment processors for card payments, direct debits, or recurring billing. These are usually provided alongside a standard current account rather than as a replacement.

## What to look for in a business bank account

### Fees and charges

Business bank accounts vary significantly in their fee structures:

- **Monthly fees:** Some accounts charge a flat monthly fee regardless of usage. Others are free up to a certain number of transactions.
- **Transaction fees:** Charges per payment received, per payment sent, or per cash deposit. These can add up quickly for high-volume businesses.
- **International fees:** Charges for sending or receiving international payments, plus exchange rate margins.
- **ATM and cash fees:** Charges for cash withdrawals or deposits.

Many digital banks offer accounts with no monthly fees and free electronic transactions, making them attractive for businesses that operate primarily online. Traditional banks often charge more but may offer in-branch services and relationship managers.

### Features

Beyond basic banking, consider:

- **Mobile app quality.** You will check your account on your phone more often than on a desktop. A good app saves time.
- **Integration with accounting software.** Automatic bank feeds are essential. Check that your chosen bank integrates with your accounting platform — or that your accounting platform supports your bank. Relentify, for instance, supports open banking connections with all major banks, so your transactions flow in automatically regardless of who you bank with.
- **Card controls.** The ability to freeze cards, set spending limits, and create virtual cards for online purchases.
- **Sub-accounts or pots.** Separate areas within your account for different purposes — tax reserves, savings, specific projects.
- **Speed of setup.** Some digital banks approve applications in minutes. Traditional banks can take weeks.

### Customer support

When something goes wrong with your banking — a payment that does not arrive, a card that stops working, a direct debit that fails — you need to be able to speak to someone quickly. Check what support channels are available and what the typical response times are.

## What you need to apply

The documentation required varies by bank, but typically includes:

**For sole traders:**
- Proof of identity (passport or driving licence)
- Proof of address (utility bill or bank statement)
- Details of your business activity

**For limited companies:**
- Certificate of incorporation
- Details of all directors and shareholders
- Proof of identity and address for each director
- Details of your business activity and expected turnover

Some banks also ask for a business plan or cash flow forecast, particularly for new businesses. This is more common with traditional banks than digital ones.

## The application process

### Step 1: Research and shortlist

Compare at least three or four accounts before making a decision. Consider your transaction volumes, your need for international payments, and whether you value branch access or are happy banking entirely online.

### Step 2: Prepare your documents

Gather everything you need before starting the application. Missing documents are the most common cause of delays.

### Step 3: Apply

Most applications are now online. Digital banks typically complete the process within a day. Traditional banks may take one to three weeks, with some requiring a visit to a branch.

### Step 4: Fund your account

Once approved, transfer your initial capital or first payment into the account. For limited companies, this includes the nominal value of your issued shares.

### Step 5: Connect to your accounting software

Set up your bank feed as soon as the account is active. The sooner you start importing transactions automatically, the less manual data entry you will have to do.

## Digital banks vs traditional banks

The choice between a digital bank and a traditional high-street bank often comes down to what you value most.

**Digital banks** (Starling, Tide, Monzo Business, Revolut Business, and similar) typically offer:
- Fast setup (often same day)
- No or low monthly fees
- Excellent mobile apps
- Automatic categorisation of transactions
- Easy integration with accounting software
- Limited or no branch access

**Traditional banks** (the established high-street names) typically offer:
- In-branch services including cash handling
- Relationship managers for larger accounts
- Established lending products (overdrafts, loans)
- Slightly more complex application process
- Higher fees

For most small businesses and freelancers, a digital bank is the more practical choice. The fees are lower, the setup is faster, and the integrations with modern accounting software are generally better.

If you handle significant volumes of cash or need regular access to in-branch services, a traditional bank may be the better option.

## Tips for managing your business bank account

**Check your balance daily.** It takes 30 seconds and prevents surprises. Many accounting platforms show your bank balance on the dashboard, so you do not even need to log in to your banking app separately.

**Set up a tax reserve.** Transfer a percentage of every payment you receive into a separate savings account or pot. When your tax bill arrives, the money is already there.

**Reconcile weekly.** Match your bank transactions against your accounting records at least once a week. The longer you leave it, the harder it becomes to remember what each transaction was for.

**Review your fees annually.** Your banking needs change as your business grows. An account that was ideal in year one may not be the best option in year three.

**Keep your details updated.** If your business changes address, adds directors, or changes its name, update your bank promptly. Outdated information can cause compliance issues.

## Common mistakes

**Waiting too long to open an account.** Open your business account before you start trading. Receiving your first payment into a personal account creates complications.

**Choosing based on fees alone.** The cheapest account is not always the best. Consider the total value — including features, integrations, and support.

**Not connecting to accounting software.** Manual data entry is slow, error-prone, and unnecessary. Connect your bank feed from day one.

**Ignoring international capabilities.** If there is any chance you will work with international clients or suppliers, choose an account that handles foreign currency efficiently. Retrofitting this later means changing banks.

Opening a business bank account is a straightforward process, but it is worth taking the time to choose well. The right account saves you money, integrates smoothly with your other business tools, and gives you the financial visibility you need to manage your business confidently.
