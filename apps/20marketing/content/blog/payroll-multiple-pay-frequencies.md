Many businesses pay different groups of employees on different schedules. Salaried staff might be paid monthly, while hourly workers are paid weekly or fortnightly. Managing multiple pay frequencies in a single business adds complexity, but with the right approach it is entirely manageable.

This guide explains how to set up and run payroll when you have more than one pay schedule.

## Why businesses use multiple pay frequencies

The reasons are usually practical:

- **Industry norms**: Hospitality, construction, and retail workers often expect weekly pay. Office staff typically expect monthly.
- **Cash flow management**: Hourly workers may need more frequent pay to manage their finances. Monthly pay reduces processing effort for salaried staff.
- **Contractual obligations**: Existing employment contracts may specify different frequencies for different roles.
- **Workforce composition**: A mix of full-time, part-time, and casual workers may suit different schedules.

There is no legal requirement to pay all employees on the same frequency. However, each frequency creates a separate payroll run with its own deadlines, which increases the administrative workload.

## Setting up multiple pay frequencies

### Define your pay groups

Start by grouping employees according to their pay frequency:

- **Monthly**: Typically salaried employees, paid once per month on a fixed date
- **Weekly**: Typically hourly employees, paid every week
- **Fortnightly**: Paid every two weeks
- **Four-weekly**: Paid every four weeks (13 pay periods per year, not 12)

Each group runs as a separate payroll. Your payroll software should support multiple pay groups within a single employer account.

### Set pay dates and deadlines

For each pay group, establish:

- **Pay period dates**: The start and end of each pay period
- **Processing deadline**: The date by which all variable data (timesheets, expenses, etc.) must be submitted
- **Pay date**: The date employees receive their pay
- **Filing deadline**: When the RTI or tax submission must be filed

Create a payroll calendar at the start of each year showing all dates for all pay groups. Share it with managers and anyone involved in submitting data.

### Configure your payroll software

Most payroll systems allow you to create multiple pay schedules within a single company. Each schedule has its own:

- Pay frequency setting
- Pay period dates
- Processing and payment dates
- Employee assignments

When setting this up in your [payroll software](/payroll), make sure each employee is assigned to the correct pay group. An employee should only be in one pay group — do not pay someone through two different schedules simultaneously.

## Tax and deduction calculations

Tax calculations depend on the pay frequency. The annual tax-free allowance and tax bands are divided by the number of pay periods to determine the per-period thresholds.

- **Monthly**: Annual allowance ÷ 12
- **Weekly**: Annual allowance ÷ 52
- **Fortnightly**: Annual allowance ÷ 26
- **Four-weekly**: Annual allowance ÷ 13

Social contribution thresholds work the same way. Your payroll software handles these conversions automatically, but it is important to understand the principle because it affects take-home pay.

An employee paid weekly will have smaller deductions per period than one paid monthly, but they will have more pay periods. Over the full year, the total tax and contributions should be approximately the same for the same annual salary.

### Cumulative calculations

Most tax systems use cumulative calculations, meaning each pay period accounts for all earnings and deductions since the start of the tax year. This ensures the correct total is deducted by year-end regardless of how the pay is distributed across periods.

For weekly and fortnightly payrolls, cumulative calculations can produce fluctuations in take-home pay — especially if overtime or bonuses push earnings above a threshold in one period but not others. This is normal and self-corrects over the tax year.

## Pension contributions

If you use a workplace pension, contributions must be calculated and submitted for each pay period. This means:

- Weekly employees: pension contributions are calculated and submitted weekly
- Monthly employees: pension contributions are calculated and submitted monthly

Your pension provider needs to receive contributions for each pay period. Some providers accept multiple submission frequencies; others prefer everything to be submitted monthly. Check with your provider and align your submission schedule.

If you are using salary sacrifice for pension contributions, the salary reduction applies per period. A weekly employee with a £50 per week salary sacrifice will have their gross pay reduced by £50 each week.

## Reporting and filing

Tax authority filings must be submitted on or before each payday for each pay group. If you run weekly and monthly payrolls, you have filing obligations every week (for the weekly group) and every month (for the monthly group).

This is one of the main administrative burdens of multiple pay frequencies. Each filing must be accurate and on time. Late submissions attract penalties regardless of which pay group they relate to.

Automate your filings wherever possible. Modern [payroll](/payroll) platforms submit RTI or equivalent filings automatically after each pay run, reducing the risk of missed deadlines.

## Reconciliation

Having multiple pay frequencies makes reconciliation more complex. You need to track:

- **Total payroll costs** across all pay groups for each period
- **Tax and contribution liabilities** that combine multiple weekly runs with a monthly run
- **Pension submissions** at potentially different frequencies
- **Payment totals** going out on different dates

A monthly reconciliation should consolidate all payroll activity across all frequencies. Compare:

- Total gross pay across all groups
- Total deductions by type (tax, NI, pension, student loans, etc.)
- Total net payments to employees
- Total liabilities to tax authority and pension providers

When your payroll system feeds directly into your accounting software, much of this reconciliation happens automatically. Platforms like Relentify link payroll and accounting, so journal entries are created for each pay run regardless of frequency.

## Employees who change pay frequency

Occasionally, an employee may move from one pay group to another — for example, a weekly-paid casual worker who becomes a monthly-paid permanent employee.

When this happens:

1. Process their final pay in the old pay group up to the transfer date
2. Add them to the new pay group starting from the transfer date
3. Ensure cumulative year-to-date figures transfer correctly so that tax calculations remain accurate
4. Do not create a gap or overlap in pay periods

Your payroll software should handle the transfer of year-to-date data, but verify this manually. An error in cumulative figures will cause incorrect tax calculations for the remainder of the year.

## Common mistakes

### Missing weekly filing deadlines
Weekly payroll means weekly filing obligations. If you are used to monthly payroll, it is easy to miss the weekly rhythm. Set up automated submissions to avoid this.

### Incorrect period calculations
A four-weekly payroll has 13 periods, not 12. Using 12 periods will result in incorrect tax calculations and underpayment or overpayment throughout the year.

### Inconsistent data collection
Weekly payroll requires weekly timesheet data. If your managers are used to submitting data monthly, they need to adjust their schedule to meet the weekly processing deadline.

### Not reconciling across frequencies
It is tempting to treat each pay group independently, but your tax liability is combined. Make sure you reconcile all groups together each month.

## Is it worth the complexity?

Multiple pay frequencies create more work, but they also reflect the reality of a mixed workforce. The key is to:

- Automate as much as possible
- Set clear deadlines for data submission
- Use payroll software that handles multiple frequencies natively
- Reconcile regularly

With the right tools and processes, running multiple pay frequencies is routine rather than burdensome. The alternative — forcing all employees onto a single frequency — may not suit your workforce or your industry. Choose the approach that works for your business and invest in the systems to manage it efficiently.
