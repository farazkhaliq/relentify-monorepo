Winning new landlord instructions is the growth engine of any letting agency. Without new properties coming onto the books, the portfolio stagnates — and in a market where some landlords inevitably sell, downsize, or switch agents, stagnation quickly becomes decline.

Most agencies approach instruction-winning through a combination of marketing, reputation, and personal relationships. All of these matter. But there is an underused asset that can sharpen your pitch, differentiate your agency, and increase your conversion rate: the data already sitting in your CRM.

## The data advantage

When a landlord is choosing between agents, they are making a decision based largely on trust. Can this agent manage my property effectively? Will they minimise void periods? Will they handle compliance? Will they communicate proactively?

Every agency claims to do all of these things. The difference is proving it. And that is where data comes in.

A CRM that has been well-maintained contains a wealth of evidence about your agency's performance. Average void periods, tenant retention rates, compliance record, time to let, rent achieved versus asking price — all of this data exists in your system, waiting to be used.

When you sit down with a prospective landlord and can say "our average void period across the portfolio is nine days" or "ninety-two percent of our tenancies are renewed," you are not making promises. You are presenting evidence. That is a fundamentally different conversation.

## What data to present

Not all CRM data is relevant to an instruction pitch. The key is to present metrics that directly address the landlord's concerns.

### Void period data

The question every landlord cares about most is: how quickly will you find a tenant? If your CRM tracks the time between one tenancy ending and the next beginning, you can present your average void period — overall, and for properties similar to the one being offered.

If your void period data is strong, lead with it. If it is not as strong as you would like, you can still use it to show a trend — "we have reduced our average void period by thirty percent over the past year" is a credible and compelling story.

### Time to let

Closely related to void periods, time to let measures how quickly a property moves from being listed to having a signed tenancy agreement. This metric demonstrates the efficiency of your marketing, viewings, and referencing process.

### Tenant retention

A high tenant retention rate signals that your properties are well-managed and your tenants are happy. Landlords care about this because tenant turnover is expensive — not just in lost rent, but in re-letting fees, property preparation costs, and the risk of void periods.

### Rent achieved

If your CRM tracks the rent achieved relative to the initial asking price, this data demonstrates your ability to price properties accurately and negotiate effectively. A track record of achieving or exceeding asking prices is a strong selling point.

### Compliance record

A perfect compliance record — no lapsed certificates, no missed deadlines — demonstrates operational rigour. For landlords who are worried about the legal responsibilities of letting a property, this is reassuring.

## Preparing for the valuation visit

The valuation visit — when you visit a landlord's property to assess it and present your services — is the critical moment in the instruction-winning process. Preparation is everything.

Before the visit, use your CRM to research the area. How many similar properties have you let recently? What rents did they achieve? How long did they take to let? What feedback did applicants give about properties in that location?

Prepare a summary document that includes your key metrics, comparable properties you have managed, and any local market insights. This does not need to be a glossy brochure. A well-organised one-page summary, backed by real data, is more impressive than a generic marketing pack.

Also check your CRM for any existing relationship with the landlord. Have they enquired before? Have they been referred by someone in your network? Knowing this context allows you to personalise the conversation rather than delivering a generic pitch.

## Using data in the follow-up

The instruction pitch does not end at the valuation visit. Many landlords take time to decide, especially if they are meeting with multiple agents. The follow-up period is your opportunity to reinforce the impression you made.

Use your CRM to schedule a follow-up within 48 hours of the visit. Reference specific points from the conversation. If the landlord mentioned a concern about maintenance, include a summary of how your maintenance process works. If they were focused on yield, send a comparison showing what similar properties are achieving.

The CRM can also help you track where each prospective instruction is in your pipeline. If you have five valuations pending, you can see which ones need follow-up, which are waiting on the landlord's decision, and which have gone cold and need a different approach.

## Building a referral engine with data

Your existing landlords are your best source of new instructions. A satisfied landlord who refers a colleague is the highest-quality lead you can generate — the conversion rate is dramatically higher than any other source.

Your CRM can help you identify landlords who are most likely to refer. Look for landlords with multiple properties under management, landlords who have been with you for a long time, and landlords who have expressed satisfaction in recent interactions.

You can also track which landlords have already made referrals and thank them appropriately. A simple acknowledgement — "thanks for recommending us to your colleague, they are now on our books" — reinforces the behaviour and encourages further referrals.

## Competitive intelligence

When a landlord is comparing you with other agents, they will often mention what the competition has offered. If Agent X has proposed a lower management fee or a guaranteed rent scheme, you need to respond with something more persuasive than "we are worth it."

Your CRM data allows you to quantify the value you deliver. A lower management fee means nothing if the agent takes two months to find a tenant. A guaranteed rent scheme sounds attractive until you compare it with the actual rent you achieve, which may be significantly higher.

Being able to say "our fee is slightly higher, but our average void period is nine days compared to the industry average of twenty-one, which means your landlord saves more on lost rent than they would on a fee reduction" is a powerful argument — and it is one that only an agency with good data can make.

## Long-term pipeline management

Not every valuation leads to an immediate instruction. Some landlords are planning ahead — considering purchasing a property, thinking about switching agents in six months, or waiting for a current tenancy to end before making a change.

Your CRM should capture these future opportunities and nurture them over time. A landlord who is not ready today may be ready in three months. A scheduled follow-up, a market update email, or an invitation to a landlord event can keep your agency top of mind until they are ready to commit.

This long-term pipeline management is where most agencies fall short. They are good at chasing hot leads but poor at nurturing warm ones. The CRM bridges this gap by tracking every opportunity and reminding you when it is time to re-engage.

## The data flywheel

The more properties you manage, the more data you generate. The more data you have, the more compelling your instruction pitches become. The more instructions you win, the more properties you manage. This is the data flywheel — and it accelerates over time.

Agencies that invest in maintaining clean, accurate CRM data today are building a competitive advantage that will pay dividends for years. Agencies that do not are leaving one of their most powerful assets unused.
