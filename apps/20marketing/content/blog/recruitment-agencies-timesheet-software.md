Recruitment agencies have a uniquely complex relationship with timesheets. Unlike most businesses, where timesheets serve internal purposes — payroll, compliance, project costing — agencies sit at the intersection of three parties: the worker, the client, and the agency itself. Every hour recorded on a timesheet simultaneously determines what the worker is paid, what the client is charged, and what margin the agency earns.

This triangular dependency makes accurate, efficient timesheet management a strategic priority rather than an administrative afterthought. An agency that gets timesheets wrong loses money, damages client relationships, and frustrates its workforce. An agency that gets timesheets right operates with confidence, speed, and defensible data.

This guide covers why recruitment agencies need purpose-fit timesheet software and what features matter most.

## The agency timesheet challenge

### Volume

A mid-sized recruitment agency might have hundreds of temporary workers on assignment at any given time, spread across dozens of client sites. Each worker submits a timesheet every week. That is hundreds of timesheets to collect, verify, approve, and process — every single week.

### The pay-bill cycle

The timesheet is the trigger for two financial transactions: the worker's pay and the client's bill. These need to happen quickly (workers expect to be paid on time) and accurately (errors in either direction cause problems). Any delay or error in the timesheet process directly impacts cash flow and worker satisfaction.

### Multiple rate structures

Different placements may have different rates — standard hourly, overtime, weekend, bank holiday, night shifts. Each client contract specifies its own rate card, and each worker's pay rate may differ. The timesheet system needs to apply the correct rates automatically based on the worker, the client, and the time of work.

### Client approval

Most agency clients require that timesheets are approved by the client before the agency can bill. This introduces a dependency: if the client is slow to approve, the agency cannot bill, but the worker still expects to be paid. Managing this approval loop efficiently is critical.

### Compliance

Recruitment agencies operate under specific regulations in most jurisdictions — temporary worker rights, equal treatment provisions, tax reporting requirements, and working time limits. Accurate timesheet records are the foundation of compliance for all of these.

## What to look for in agency timesheet software

### Worker self-service

Workers should be able to clock in and out or submit their hours through a simple mobile interface. The fewer steps required, the higher the compliance rate. For workers at client sites, GPS-verified clock-in confirms attendance without requiring the client to supervise the process.

### Client approval portal

Clients need a straightforward way to review and approve timesheets for workers at their sites. This might be a web portal, an email-based approval link, or access to a shared dashboard. The approval process should be quick — ideally a single click for routine timesheets, with detailed review available for anomalies.

### Automatic rate calculation

The system should apply the correct pay rate and bill rate based on the worker, the client, the day of the week, and the time of day. This eliminates manual rate lookups and reduces errors. When a worker logs eight hours on a Saturday at a client that pays weekend premium, the system should calculate the correct pay and bill amounts without human intervention.

### Margin visibility

For every timesheet, the agency needs to see the margin — the difference between what the client pays and what the worker costs (including employment costs, not just pay). Real-time margin visibility across all placements helps the agency manage profitability and identify loss-making assignments before they accumulate.

### Payroll and billing integration

Approved timesheets should feed directly into payroll and billing systems, minimising manual data transfer. This integration is where the biggest time savings occur — and where the biggest risks of error exist if the process is manual.

### Compliance tracking

The system should monitor working time limits across all assignments (a worker placed at multiple clients may exceed weekly limits without anyone noticing) and flag compliance issues before they become problems.

## The financial impact of poor timesheet management

### Under-billing

If timesheets understate hours — due to late submissions, missing entries, or conservative rounding — the agency invoices less than it should. Even a small percentage of lost hours, multiplied across hundreds of workers and fifty-two weeks, represents a substantial revenue loss.

### Over-payment

Conversely, if timesheets overstate hours (buddy punching, inflated entries, unverified claims), the agency pays more than the work warrants. This directly erodes margins.

### Delayed billing

When client approvals are slow, billing is delayed. The agency has already incurred the payroll cost but cannot recover it from the client until the timesheet is approved. Managing the approval cycle proactively — with reminders, escalation, and clear SLAs — protects cash flow.

### Payroll errors

Manual timesheet processing is error-prone. Rate miscalculations, misallocated hours, and data entry mistakes lead to incorrect pay — which leads to worker complaints, rectification effort, and in severe cases, loss of workers to competing agencies.

### Compliance penalties

Failure to maintain adequate time records can result in regulatory fines, tribunal awards, and reputational damage. For agencies that supply workers into regulated sectors (care, security, healthcare), the consequences can include loss of licences or accreditation.

## Streamlining the weekly cycle

A well-designed timesheet process for a recruitment agency follows a predictable weekly rhythm:

**Monday to Friday**: Workers clock in and out at client sites using the mobile app. Hours are recorded in real time with GPS verification.

**Friday evening / Saturday**: Workers review and submit their timesheets for the week. Automatic reminders prompt any who have not yet submitted.

**Monday**: Client approvers receive notification of pending timesheets. They review and approve through the portal. The agency follows up on any outstanding approvals.

**Tuesday**: Approved timesheets are processed for payroll. Bill rates are applied and client invoices are generated.

**Wednesday or Thursday**: Workers are paid. Client invoices are issued.

This cycle — from hours worked to pay and bill — runs every week, fifty-two weeks a year. Any friction in the process cascades into late pay, late billing, or both.

## Managing at scale

As an agency grows, manual timesheet processes break. What works for twenty workers does not work for two hundred. The scaling challenges include:

- **Volume**: More timesheets to collect, review, and process
- **Diversity**: More clients with different rate structures and approval requirements
- **Compliance complexity**: More workers to monitor for working time limits and regulatory requirements
- **Geography**: Workers at sites across multiple regions with different rules

The solution is automation: automatic reminders, automatic rate calculations, automatic compliance flags, and direct integration with payroll and billing. The agency's operational team should spend their time on exceptions and relationships, not on data processing.

## Building competitive advantage

In a competitive recruitment market, the speed and accuracy of your timesheet process is a differentiator. Workers choose agencies that pay on time, every time. Clients choose agencies that bill accurately and make the approval process painless.

A timesheet system that delivers both — fast, accurate pay for workers and simple, transparent billing for clients — is not just an operational tool. It is a competitive advantage that improves worker retention, strengthens client relationships, and protects the agency's margins.

Recruitment agencies that treat timesheets as a strategic capability rather than an administrative burden are the ones that scale successfully. The investment in the right system pays returns in every dimension of the business.
