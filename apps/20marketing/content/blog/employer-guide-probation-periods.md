Probation periods give both employer and employee a structured window to assess whether the role is the right fit. Used well, they provide a framework for feedback, development, and early intervention if things are not working out. Used poorly — or not used at all — they become a missed opportunity.

This guide explains how to set up and manage probation periods effectively.

## What is a probation period?

A probation period is a defined period at the start of employment — typically three to six months — during which the employer assesses whether the new employee meets the required standard. During this time, shorter notice periods usually apply, and the process for ending employment is simpler than it would be after the probation ends.

Probation periods are not required by law in most jurisdictions, but they are widely used because they provide a structured approach to evaluating new hires.

## Why use probation periods?

### Early assessment
A probation period creates a formal framework for evaluating a new employee's performance, behaviour, and cultural fit. Without one, performance concerns may drift for months before being addressed.

### Shorter notice periods
Most probation clauses include shorter notice requirements — typically one week — compared to the standard notice period after probation. This provides flexibility if the arrangement is not working.

### Clear expectations
A well-managed probation period communicates clearly what the employee needs to demonstrate to pass. This reduces ambiguity and gives the employee a fair chance to succeed.

### Easier exit
While probation does not remove the need to act fairly and reasonably, it does provide a recognised framework for ending employment early if the employee is not meeting the required standard.

## Setting up the probation period

### Duration
Three months is common, but six months is also widely used, particularly for more senior or complex roles. Choose a duration that gives you enough time to properly assess performance without being unnecessarily long.

State the probation period in the employment contract, including the start date, duration, and what happens at the end (confirmation of employment, extension, or termination).

### Objectives
At the start of the probation, set clear, measurable objectives that the employee needs to achieve. These should be:

- **Specific**: "Complete the product training programme" rather than "learn the role"
- **Measurable**: "Process 20 customer orders per day with less than 2% error rate" rather than "be good at the job"
- **Achievable**: Set targets that a competent new hire could realistically meet within the probation period
- **Time-bound**: Align milestones with the probation timeline

Share these objectives in writing and keep a copy. They form the basis for your mid-probation and end-of-probation reviews.

### Support
Probation is not a test designed to trip people up. It is a period where the employee should receive enhanced support:

- Regular check-ins with their manager (at least every two weeks)
- Access to training and resources
- A buddy or mentor for day-to-day questions
- Clear and timely feedback on performance

If an employee fails probation despite receiving no feedback or support, you have a process problem, not a performance problem.

## During the probation period

### Regular check-ins
Schedule regular one-to-one meetings throughout the probation. These are your opportunity to:

- Review progress against objectives
- Provide specific, constructive feedback
- Identify any concerns early
- Ask the employee how they are finding the role
- Adjust expectations or provide additional support if needed

Document these conversations briefly. A few bullet points summarising what was discussed and any actions agreed is sufficient. This creates a record that is invaluable if you later need to justify a decision about the probation.

### Mid-probation review
Conduct a formal review at the midpoint of the probation period. This is a structured conversation that covers:

- Progress against objectives
- Strengths demonstrated so far
- Areas for improvement
- Any concerns from either party
- Actions for the remainder of the probation

The mid-probation review is particularly important if there are performance concerns. It gives the employee clear notice that they need to improve and specific guidance on what "improvement" looks like. Without this, ending the probation later for poor performance can feel unfair and may be harder to justify.

### Addressing concerns early

If you notice performance or conduct issues, address them immediately. Do not wait until the end of the probation and hope things improve on their own. Early intervention gives the employee the best chance to succeed and demonstrates that you acted fairly.

Document any concerns and the support or guidance you provided. This paper trail is your evidence that the employee was given a fair opportunity.

## End of probation

At the end of the probation period, you have three options:

### 1. Confirm employment
The employee has met the required standard. Confirm their employment in writing, noting that the probation has been successfully completed. Any changes to notice periods or terms that take effect after probation should be confirmed.

### 2. Extend the probation
If the employee is making progress but has not yet fully met the standard, you can extend the probation by a defined period (typically one to three months). This gives them additional time to demonstrate competence.

An extension should be:
- Communicated in writing with clear reasons
- Accompanied by specific objectives for the extension period
- Supported by continued feedback and check-ins

The employment contract should include a clause allowing extension. Without this, extending unilaterally could be challenged.

### 3. End employment
If the employee has not met the required standard despite receiving feedback and support, you may decide to end the employment. This should be done in accordance with the notice period specified in the contract for the probation period.

Even during probation, you should:
- Have documented evidence of the performance concerns
- Have given the employee feedback and an opportunity to improve
- Conduct a final meeting to explain the decision
- Provide the contractual notice or payment in lieu

In many jurisdictions, employees in their early months of employment have fewer rights to claim unfair dismissal, but they still have rights against discriminatory dismissal and other protected grounds. Always act fairly and reasonably.

## Common mistakes

### No clear objectives
Starting probation without specific objectives means you have no objective basis for the end-of-probation decision. Set expectations from day one.

### No feedback during probation
If the first time an employee hears about performance concerns is at the end-of-probation meeting, you have failed the process. Feedback should be ongoing throughout.

### Not documenting anything
Without written records of objectives, check-ins, and feedback, your end-of-probation decision is based on undocumented impressions. This is weak ground for any decision, especially a negative one.

### Allowing probation to expire without a review
If you forget to conduct the end-of-probation review and the employee continues working, probation may be deemed to have been completed successfully by default. Set a calendar reminder and conduct the review on time.

### Being too harsh or too lenient
Some managers use probation as a heavy-handed tool, creating an anxiety-inducing experience. Others are too lenient, passing everyone through regardless of performance. Neither approach serves the business or the employee.

## The bottom line

Probation periods work best when they are structured, supportive, and well-documented. Set clear expectations at the start, provide regular feedback throughout, and make a considered decision at the end. Done right, probation is a tool that helps good employees succeed and gives both parties confidence in the working relationship.
