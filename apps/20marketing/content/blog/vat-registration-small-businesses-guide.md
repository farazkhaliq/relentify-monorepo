VAT registration is one of those milestones in a growing business that generates a lot of questions. When do you have to register? Should you register voluntarily? What changes once you are registered? How do you actually do it?

This guide covers everything a UK small business needs to know about VAT registration, from the legal requirements to the practical implications.

## What is VAT?

Value Added Tax (VAT) is a consumption tax charged on most goods and services sold in the UK. As a VAT-registered business, you charge VAT on your sales (output tax) and can reclaim VAT on your business purchases (input tax). The difference is what you pay to or reclaim from HMRC.

The standard VAT rate is 20%. There are also reduced rates (5%) for certain goods like home energy, and zero rates (0%) for items like most food, children's clothing, and books.

## When must you register for VAT?

### The compulsory registration threshold

You must register for VAT if your VAT taxable turnover exceeds £85,000 in any rolling 12-month period. This is the current threshold — check the latest HMRC guidance as it can change in the Budget.

Important details:

- **Rolling 12 months** — This is not your financial year. HMRC looks at any consecutive 12-month period. You need to monitor your turnover continuously, not just at year end.
- **VAT taxable turnover** — This includes all sales that are not VAT-exempt. It does not include sales of capital assets or exempt supplies.
- **Looking forward** — You must also register if you expect your turnover to exceed £85,000 in the next 30 days alone.

### What counts towards the threshold?

Your VAT taxable turnover includes:

- All standard-rated sales (20%)
- All reduced-rated sales (5%)
- All zero-rated sales (0%)
- Sales of goods or services from the UK

It does not include:

- VAT-exempt sales (such as insurance, finance, education)
- Sales of capital assets
- Non-business income (such as hobby income)

### When to register

If your turnover exceeds £85,000 in any rolling 12-month period, you must notify HMRC within 30 days. Your VAT registration will be effective from the first day of the second month after you crossed the threshold.

If you expect to exceed the threshold in the next 30 days, you must register immediately, and registration takes effect from the beginning of that 30-day period.

## Voluntary registration

You can register for VAT voluntarily even if your turnover is below the threshold. There are good reasons to consider this:

### Advantages of voluntary registration

- **Reclaim VAT on purchases** — If your business spends a lot on VAT-able supplies (equipment, software, professional services), registration lets you reclaim that VAT
- **Professional credibility** — Being VAT-registered can make your business appear more established to potential clients
- **Avoid the threshold cliff** — If you are close to the threshold, registering early avoids the administrative rush when you cross it
- **Better pricing for B2B clients** — Business clients can reclaim the VAT you charge, so it does not increase their real cost

### Disadvantages of voluntary registration

- **Administrative burden** — You must submit VAT returns (quarterly or monthly) and keep detailed records
- **Price increase for non-VAT-registered customers** — If your customers are consumers or VAT-exempt businesses, adding 20% to your prices is a genuine cost increase for them
- **Cash flow impact** — You collect VAT from customers and hold it until your return is due, but you also need to manage the timing of payments to HMRC

For most B2B businesses, voluntary registration makes sense once turnover reaches around £50,000-£70,000, because the VAT you can reclaim on expenses often offsets the administrative cost. For B2C businesses selling to consumers, the calculation is different because your customers cannot reclaim the VAT.

## How to register for VAT

### Online registration (recommended)

1. Go to the HMRC website and navigate to VAT registration
2. You will need:
   - Your business details (name, address, type of business)
   - Your Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR) or National Insurance number
   - Your bank account details
   - Details of your taxable turnover
   - The date you want registration to start
3. Submit the application
4. HMRC will issue your VAT registration certificate within 30 working days, which includes your VAT number

### Choosing your VAT scheme

When you register, you can choose from several VAT schemes. The right choice depends on your business:

**Standard VAT accounting** — You account for VAT based on invoice dates. You charge VAT when you invoice and reclaim VAT when you receive a purchase invoice, regardless of when money actually changes hands.

**Cash accounting** — You only account for VAT when money is actually received or paid. This helps cash flow because you do not pay VAT to HMRC until your customer has paid you. Available to businesses with turnover below £1.35 million.

**Flat rate scheme** — Instead of tracking VAT on every transaction, you pay a fixed percentage of your gross turnover to HMRC. The percentage depends on your industry. This is simpler but may result in paying more or less VAT than under standard accounting. Available to businesses with turnover below £150,000.

**Annual accounting** — You submit one VAT return per year instead of quarterly, with interim payments throughout the year. Available to businesses with turnover below £1.35 million.

## What changes when you register

### You must charge VAT on your sales

From your registration date, you must add VAT to all your taxable sales. Your invoices must show:

- Your VAT registration number
- The VAT rate applied
- The VAT amount
- The total including VAT

### You must keep VAT records

You need to maintain records of all your sales and purchases, showing the VAT charged and paid. Your accounting software should handle this automatically — platforms like [Relentify](/accounting) track VAT on every transaction and generate the figures you need for your return.

### You must submit VAT returns

Under standard accounting, VAT returns are submitted quarterly. Each return covers a three-month period and must be filed within one month and seven days of the period end.

Under Making Tax Digital (MTD), VAT returns must be submitted using compatible software. You cannot simply log into the HMRC portal and enter figures manually.

### You can reclaim VAT on purchases

This is the upside. You can reclaim the VAT you have been charged on legitimate business purchases. This includes:

- Equipment and supplies
- Professional services (accountant, lawyer, etc.)
- Software subscriptions
- Office rent (if VAT is charged)
- Vehicle costs (with some restrictions)

You can also make a one-off claim for VAT on goods purchased before registration (within the last four years) and services (within the last six months), provided they were for business use.

## Common VAT mistakes

### Not monitoring the threshold

Many businesses cross the £85,000 threshold without realising it because they only check their turnover at year end. Monitor monthly and watch the rolling 12-month total.

### Charging VAT before registration

You cannot charge VAT until HMRC has confirmed your registration and issued your VAT number. If you start charging VAT prematurely, you may need to refund it.

### Not reclaiming pre-registration VAT

You can claim back VAT on goods bought up to four years before registration and services bought up to six months before, as long as they relate to your VAT-taxable business. Many businesses miss this.

### Applying the wrong rate

Most goods and services are standard-rated (20%), but some are reduced (5%), zero-rated (0%), or exempt. Applying the wrong rate is a common error that can lead to under or overpayment.

### Missing the filing deadline

Late VAT returns incur penalties under HMRC's points-based system. Each late submission earns a point, and accumulating points leads to financial penalties.

## Deregistration

You can deregister for VAT if your taxable turnover falls below £83,000 (the deregistration threshold, which is lower than the registration threshold). You may also need to deregister if you stop making taxable supplies.

When you deregister, you may need to account for VAT on any stock or assets you hold at the time of deregistration.

## Getting started

VAT registration is a significant administrative step, but it is manageable with the right preparation. Get your records in order, choose the right VAT scheme for your business, invest in MTD-compatible software, and set up a process for filing returns on time.

The businesses that handle VAT well are the ones that build it into their routine rather than treating it as an annual scramble. Regular bookkeeping, automated VAT tracking, and timely filing make the whole process far less stressful than it needs to be.
