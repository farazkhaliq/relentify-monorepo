Every business that tracks time has the same recurring problem: people forget to submit their timesheets. It is not malicious. Workers are busy, distracted, or simply do not prioritise a task that feels administrative. But the consequence is real — delayed payroll, incomplete records, and managers spending time chasing submissions instead of managing the business.

Automatic reminders solve this problem elegantly. Instead of a manager sending awkward messages each week, the system handles it. This guide explains how to set up reminders that work — prompting compliance without becoming annoying.

## Why reminders matter

### Payroll cannot wait

Payroll runs on a schedule. If timesheets are not submitted and approved by the deadline, one of two things happens: the worker's pay is delayed (unacceptable) or the payroll team estimates the hours (inaccurate and creates reconciliation work later). Neither outcome is acceptable as a routine practice.

### Manager time is valuable

A manager who spends thirty minutes each week chasing missing timesheets is wasting over twenty-five hours per year on a problem that automation solves for free. Those hours could be spent on actual management.

### Data completeness

Timesheet data informs more than just payroll. It supports client billing, project costing, compliance records, and workforce reporting. Missing entries create gaps in all of these.

### Consistency

Reminders apply the same prompt to everyone. There is no awkward dynamic of chasing some workers and not others, no perception of favouritism, and no selective enforcement.

## Types of reminders

### Clock-in reminders

Sent at or near the scheduled start time to workers who have not yet clocked in. These serve two purposes: reminding the worker to record their time and alerting the manager that a scheduled worker may not have arrived.

**When to send:** 5-10 minutes after the scheduled start time
**Who receives it:** The worker (primary), optionally the manager (if the worker still has not clocked in after a further period)

### Clock-out reminders

Sent at or near the scheduled end time to workers who are still clocked in. This prompts workers to clock out when they finish, preventing forgotten clock-outs that require manual correction.

**When to send:** At the scheduled end time or 15 minutes after
**Who receives it:** The worker

### Submission reminders

Sent at the end of the pay period to workers who have not yet submitted their timesheet for approval. This is the most important reminder for payroll purposes.

**When to send:** At the submission deadline (e.g., Friday 5pm), with a follow-up if not yet submitted (e.g., Saturday morning)
**Who receives it:** The worker

### Approval reminders

Sent to managers who have pending timesheets awaiting approval. This keeps the approval cycle on track and prevents the bottleneck from shifting from workers to managers.

**When to send:** When timesheets are submitted, with a follow-up if not approved by the deadline
**Who receives it:** The approving manager

### Escalation reminders

If a timesheet remains unsubmitted or unapproved beyond the final deadline, the notification escalates — to a senior manager, the payroll administrator, or whoever is responsible for ensuring the process completes on time.

**When to send:** After the standard reminder has gone unanswered for a defined period
**Who receives it:** The escalation contact

## Setting up reminders effectively

### Step 1: Define the timeline

Map out your pay period and work backward from the payroll processing date:

- **Payroll processing:** Wednesday
- **Approval deadline:** Tuesday noon
- **Submission deadline:** Monday noon
- **First submission reminder:** Friday 5pm
- **Follow-up submission reminder:** Sunday evening or Monday morning
- **Approval reminder:** Monday afternoon (when submissions arrive)
- **Approval follow-up:** Tuesday morning
- **Escalation:** Tuesday noon (if still unapproved)

This timeline gives everyone clear deadlines and multiple prompts before the hard cutoff.

### Step 2: Choose the delivery channel

Reminders should reach workers where they will actually see them:

- **Push notification** from the timesheet app — most effective for mobile workers
- **SMS** — useful as a backup for workers who may not have app notifications enabled
- **Email** — appropriate for office-based workers who monitor email regularly

Most [timesheet platforms](/timesheets) support push notifications as the default reminder channel. Use SMS as a secondary channel for critical reminders (the final deadline or escalation).

### Step 3: Keep the message simple

A reminder should be brief and actionable. Workers do not need a paragraph — they need a nudge:

- "Reminder: Please submit your timesheet for this week by Monday noon."
- "You have not clocked in for your 8am shift. Please clock in now or contact your manager."
- "You have 3 timesheets pending approval. Please review by Tuesday noon."

Include a direct link to the relevant screen in the app, so the worker can act immediately without navigating through menus.

### Step 4: Avoid reminder fatigue

Too many reminders become noise. If workers receive five notifications per day about timesheets, they will start ignoring all of them. Follow these principles:

- **Maximum two reminders per event** (initial reminder plus one follow-up)
- **Space reminders appropriately** (at least several hours apart)
- **Stop reminding once the action is taken** (if the worker submits, cancel the follow-up)
- **Do not remind for things that are not yet due** (no "your timesheet is due in three days" — that is not a reminder, it is nagging)

### Step 5: Track reminder effectiveness

Monitor the response rate to reminders. If most workers submit within an hour of the first reminder, the system is working. If many workers do not submit until after the escalation, the initial reminder timing or channel may need adjustment.

## Best practices

### Make it the system's job, not the manager's

The entire point of automatic reminders is to remove the human follow-up. If managers are still chasing timesheets after implementing reminders, something is wrong — the reminders are not reaching people, the deadlines are unrealistic, or the system is not configured correctly.

### Include both workers and approvers

Missing timesheets are only half the problem. Unapproved timesheets are the other half. Make sure your reminder system covers both sides of the process.

### Align with the pay cycle

Reminders should be timed to the business's actual pay cycle, not generic defaults. If your payroll runs fortnightly, bi-weekly reminders are appropriate. If it runs weekly, weekly reminders are needed.

### Be transparent

Tell your team that automatic reminders are being set up, when they will receive them, and why. This sets expectations and removes any surprise when the first notification arrives.

### Review and refine

After a month of automatic reminders, review the data. What percentage of timesheets are submitted on time? Has it improved? Are there specific workers or teams who consistently miss deadlines despite reminders? Address the outliers individually — the system handles the majority, and the manager handles the exceptions.

## The impact

Businesses that implement automatic timesheet reminders typically see submission compliance improve from the 60-70% range to 90% or above within the first month. The remaining holdouts are usually addressable through individual conversations.

The downstream effect is significant: payroll runs on time with complete data, managers reclaim hours previously spent chasing, and the business has a complete picture of workforce hours for every period. It is one of the simplest process improvements a business can make, and one of the most immediately impactful.
