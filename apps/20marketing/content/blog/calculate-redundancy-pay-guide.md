When you make an employee redundant, calculating their redundancy pay correctly is both a legal obligation and a matter of treating people fairly during a difficult time. Getting it wrong — underpaying or applying the wrong tax treatment — creates additional problems on top of an already stressful situation.

This guide explains how to calculate both statutory and enhanced redundancy pay.

## Who is entitled to statutory redundancy pay?

Employees are entitled to statutory redundancy pay if they meet all of the following conditions:

- They have been continuously employed for at least a qualifying period (typically two years)
- They are being made redundant (their role is being eliminated, not their performance being managed)
- They are an employee (not a contractor or self-employed)

Employees who are dismissed for misconduct during their notice period, or who unreasonably refuse a suitable alternative role, may lose their entitlement.

## The statutory redundancy pay formula

Statutory redundancy pay is calculated using three factors:

1. **Length of service**: The number of complete years of continuous employment, usually capped at a maximum (commonly 20 years)
2. **Age**: Different multipliers apply depending on the employee's age during each year of service
3. **Weekly pay**: The employee's gross weekly pay, subject to a statutory cap

The formula applies a multiplier for each complete year of service:

- **Under a specified age threshold** (often 22): Half a week's pay per year
- **Between the lower and upper thresholds** (often 22-41): One week's pay per year
- **Above the upper threshold** (often 41 and over): One and a half weeks' pay per year

### Worked example

An employee aged 45 with 12 years of continuous service and a weekly pay of £600 (which is below the statutory cap):

- Years of service aged 22-41: 8 years × 1 week's pay = 8 weeks
- Years of service aged 41+: 4 years × 1.5 weeks' pay = 6 weeks
- **Total: 14 weeks' pay**
- **Payment: 14 × £600 = £8,400**

If the employee's weekly pay exceeds the statutory cap, you use the capped amount for the calculation. The cap is set by the government and updated annually.

### Weekly pay calculation

For employees with fixed hours and pay, weekly pay is simply their gross weekly salary.

For employees with variable pay, calculate the average weekly pay over the 12-week period (or other reference period specified in your jurisdiction) ending on the day the redundancy notice is given. Include overtime, commission, and bonuses that form part of the employee's normal remuneration.

## Enhanced redundancy pay

Many employers offer enhanced redundancy terms that exceed the statutory minimum. This might be:

- A higher weekly pay figure (removing the statutory cap)
- A higher multiplier (e.g., two weeks per year instead of one)
- A flat additional payment on top of statutory
- A combination of the above

Enhanced redundancy terms may be set out in:
- The employment contract
- A company policy or handbook
- A collective agreement with a trade union
- A one-off decision at the time of the redundancy

If you offer enhanced terms, be consistent. Offering different enhanced packages to different employees in the same redundancy exercise creates fairness and discrimination risks.

## Tax treatment

### Statutory redundancy pay
Statutory redundancy pay is tax-free in most jurisdictions. No income tax or social contributions are deducted.

### Enhanced redundancy pay
The tax treatment of enhanced redundancy pay depends on the total amount. In many countries, there is a tax-free threshold that applies to the combined total of statutory and enhanced redundancy payments. Amounts above this threshold are taxed as employment income.

For example, in the UK, the first £30,000 of any redundancy payment (statutory plus enhanced) is tax-free. Anything above £30,000 is subject to income tax (but not employee National Insurance).

Note that contractual payments in lieu of notice (PILON) are always taxable as earnings. If the employee's contract includes a PILON clause, the notice pay is taxed normally and does not form part of the tax-free redundancy threshold.

### Processing through payroll
Redundancy payments should be processed through your [payroll](/payroll) system to ensure the correct tax treatment:

- Statutory redundancy pay: coded as tax-free
- Enhanced redundancy pay within the tax-free threshold: coded as tax-free
- Amounts above the threshold: taxed at the employee's marginal rate
- Notice pay: taxed as normal earnings

Your payroll software should handle these distinctions if you categorise the payments correctly. Platforms like Relentify separate redundancy pay elements automatically, applying the correct tax treatment to each component.

## Other payments on redundancy

In addition to redundancy pay, the departing employee may be entitled to:

### Pay in lieu of notice
If the employee is not working their notice period, they receive pay for the notice period they would have worked. This is taxed as normal earnings.

### Accrued holiday pay
Payment for any holiday that has been accrued but not taken. This is taxed as normal earnings.

### Outstanding salary
Any unpaid salary or wages up to the termination date. Taxed as normal.

### Bonus or commission
If the employee is entitled to a bonus or commission for work already performed, check the scheme rules. Many bonus schemes include clauses about eligibility for employees who leave.

Keep redundancy pay clearly separated from these other payments in the final pay calculation. Each has different tax treatment, and combining them into a single figure creates confusion and potential tax errors.

## Common mistakes

### Using the wrong weekly pay figure
The weekly pay for redundancy purposes is gross pay before tax and deductions, subject to the statutory cap. Do not use net pay, and do not forget the cap.

### Not accounting for the age multiplier correctly
The multiplier is applied year by year, based on the employee's age during each year of service. An employee who turned 41 during their employment has some years at the lower multiplier and some at the higher. Calculate each year individually.

### Ignoring the statutory cap
The statutory cap on weekly pay applies even if the employee earns significantly more. Enhanced terms can use the actual salary, but statutory terms use the capped figure.

### Incorrect tax treatment
Mixing up taxable and tax-free elements is a common error. Pay particular attention to the distinction between redundancy pay (potentially tax-free) and notice pay (always taxable).

### Failing to include variable pay
For employees with variable earnings (overtime, commission, shift premiums), calculate the average weekly pay over the reference period. Do not use just the base salary if the employee regularly earns more.

### Not documenting the calculation
Keep a record of how the redundancy pay was calculated, including the service dates, age multiplier applied to each year, weekly pay figure, and any enhanced terms. This protects you if the calculation is queried later.

## Communicating redundancy pay

When you inform an employee of their redundancy, provide a clear breakdown:

- Statutory redundancy pay amount and how it was calculated
- Enhanced redundancy pay (if applicable) and the basis for it
- Notice pay and how it will be paid (worked notice, garden leave, or PILON)
- Holiday pay for accrued but untaken leave
- Any other payments
- The tax treatment of each element
- The total gross and net payment

Transparency at this stage reduces queries, disputes, and the emotional stress of an already difficult situation.

## The bottom line

Redundancy pay calculations involve specific rules that must be followed precisely. Use the correct formula, apply the right tax treatment, and document everything. Process the payments through your payroll system to ensure accuracy and compliance. Treat the departing employee fairly, and make the financial side as clear and painless as possible.
