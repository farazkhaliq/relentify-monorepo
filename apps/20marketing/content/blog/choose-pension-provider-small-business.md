Choosing a pension provider is one of those decisions that feels overwhelming until you break it down. There are dozens of providers, each with different fee structures, investment options, and administrative requirements. But for most small businesses, the decision comes down to a handful of practical factors.

This guide helps you evaluate what actually matters when picking a workplace pension provider.

## Why the choice matters

The pension provider you choose affects three groups of people:

- **Your employees**: They care about fees (which eat into their retirement savings), investment performance, and ease of access.
- **You as the employer**: You care about cost, administrative burden, and compatibility with your payroll system.
- **Your business**: You need a provider that scales as you grow and meets your legal obligations.

A poor choice means higher costs, more administrative work, or both. But the good news is that for most small businesses, several providers do the job well. You do not need the most sophisticated option — you need one that works reliably.

## Types of pension providers

### Multi-employer master trusts

These are large-scale pension schemes that accept employers of any size. They are designed for auto-enrolment and typically offer simple administration. Examples include NEST, The People's Pension, NOW: Pensions, and Smart Pension.

**Pros**: Easy to set up, designed for small employers, generally low cost, accept all employers regardless of size.

**Cons**: Limited investment options, less customisation, may not suit employers who want a premium offering for their staff.

### Insurance company group personal pensions

Providers like Aviva, Scottish Widows, Royal London, and Legal & General offer group personal pension plans. These tend to offer more investment choice and sometimes lower ongoing charges for larger employers.

**Pros**: Wider investment options, often better online platforms for members, may offer employer branding.

**Cons**: May require minimum employer sizes, setup can be more complex, some charge employers directly.

### Self-invested personal pensions (SIPPs)

SIPP providers offer the widest investment choice, including individual shares, funds, and alternative investments. These are less common for auto-enrolment but may suit businesses with financially sophisticated employees.

**Pros**: Maximum investment flexibility, appeals to engaged savers.

**Cons**: Higher charges, more complex to administer, may not be practical for most small business workforces.

## Key factors to compare

### 1. Charges

Pension charges directly reduce your employees' retirement savings, so they matter enormously over a working lifetime. There are two main types:

- **Annual management charge (AMC)**: A percentage of the fund value charged each year. For auto-enrolment schemes, the default fund must have an AMC of no more than 0.75%. Many providers charge less.
- **Contribution charges**: Some providers charge a percentage of each contribution. This is becoming less common but still exists.

Compare the total cost, not just the headline AMC. A provider with a 0.50% AMC and no contribution charge may be cheaper than one with a 0.30% AMC and a 1% contribution charge, depending on contribution levels.

### 2. Payroll integration

This is where many small employers encounter friction. Your pension provider needs to accept contribution data from your payroll system, and the format needs to match.

Check whether your payroll software has a direct integration with the pension provider. If it does, contributions can be submitted automatically after each pay run. If it does not, you may need to export a file, reformat it, and upload it manually — which is time-consuming and error-prone.

Modern [payroll](/payroll) platforms like Relentify are designed to integrate with major pension providers, automating the submission process and reducing the risk of late or incorrect payments.

### 3. Employer costs

Some pension providers charge employers directly — either a setup fee, an ongoing administration fee, or both. Others fund their operations entirely through member charges. For a small business on a tight budget, an employer charge can make a meaningful difference.

Ask each provider:
- Is there a setup fee?
- Is there an ongoing employer charge per member or per year?
- Are there charges for additional services like communications or reporting?

### 4. Investment options

Most employees will remain in the default fund — the investment option they are placed into if they do not make an active choice. The quality of the default fund matters more than the number of available alternatives.

Look for:
- A well-diversified default fund with a sensible asset allocation
- A "lifestyle" or "target date" strategy that automatically reduces risk as the member approaches retirement
- A reasonable AMC on the default fund

If your employees are likely to engage with their pension and make active investment choices, a provider with a wider range of funds may be appealing. But for most small business workforces, the default fund is what counts.

### 5. Member experience

Your employees will interact with the pension provider directly — logging in to check their balance, updating personal details, nominating beneficiaries, and eventually accessing their savings in retirement.

A good member experience includes:
- A clear, modern online portal and mobile app
- Easy-to-understand statements
- Helpful customer service
- Tools for retirement planning

If the provider's member experience is poor, you will end up fielding employee questions and complaints that are really the provider's responsibility.

### 6. Compliance support

A good provider helps you meet your auto-enrolment duties. This might include:
- Automatic assessment of employee eligibility
- Opt-out processing
- Re-enrolment reminders
- Communication templates for employee letters
- Support for your Declaration of Compliance

For small businesses without a dedicated HR or payroll team, this support can be invaluable.

## Comparing the main providers

### NEST

NEST is the government-backed scheme that must accept any employer. It is designed specifically for auto-enrolment and charges no employer fees. The AMC on the default fund is competitive, and the online platform has improved significantly over the years.

NEST is a solid default choice for any small employer who wants a simple, low-cost option. Its main limitations are relatively basic investment options and a contribution charge that applies alongside the AMC.

### The People's Pension

One of the largest auto-enrolment providers, with low charges and no employer fees. The default fund has performed well historically, and the administration is straightforward. Good payroll integration with most major software providers.

### NOW: Pensions

Known for very low member charges and simple administration. The default fund uses a diversified growth strategy. No employer setup fee, though there is a small annual employer charge.

### Smart Pension

A technology-focused provider with a strong online platform and good payroll integrations. Charges are competitive, and the member experience is generally well-regarded.

### Insurance company providers

Providers like Aviva, Scottish Widows, and Royal London offer more comprehensive pension products with wider investment options. They are often a better fit for medium-sized employers or businesses that want to offer a more premium pension benefit.

These providers may charge employer fees and have minimum size requirements, so they may not be practical for very small businesses.

## Making your decision

For most small businesses, the decision process is:

1. **Check your payroll software's integration list**: Start with providers that integrate directly. This alone may narrow the field to two or three options.
2. **Compare charges**: Look at the total cost to your employees (AMC plus any contribution charges) and any direct costs to you as the employer.
3. **Evaluate the default fund**: Check the asset allocation and historical performance. Does it follow a sensible strategy?
4. **Test the member experience**: Log into the member portal as a test user if possible. Is it easy to use? Would your employees be comfortable with it?
5. **Check compliance support**: Does the provider help with communications, opt-out processing, and re-enrolment?

If you are still unsure, start with one of the master trust options (NEST, The People's Pension, NOW: Pensions). These are designed for small employers, charge low fees, and are straightforward to administer. You can always switch providers later if your needs change.

## Switching providers

If you are already with a provider and considering a switch, be aware that:

- You can change providers at any time, but you need to set up the new scheme before closing the old one
- Employees' existing pension pots remain with the old provider unless they choose to transfer them
- You will need to update your payroll to submit contributions to the new provider
- You must continue to meet your auto-enrolment duties throughout the transition

A switch is manageable but takes planning. Allow at least two to three months to set up the new scheme, test the payroll integration, and communicate the change to employees.

## The bottom line

Choosing a pension provider does not need to be agonising. Focus on charges, payroll integration, and the quality of the default fund. For most small businesses, any of the major auto-enrolment providers will meet your legal obligations and serve your employees well.

The most important thing is to have a scheme in place, make contributions on time, and give your employees confidence that their retirement savings are in good hands.
