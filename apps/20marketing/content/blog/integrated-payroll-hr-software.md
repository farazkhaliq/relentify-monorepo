If your business runs payroll in one system and manages HR records in another, you are almost certainly entering the same information twice. Every new starter gets added to both systems. Every salary change gets updated in both. Every leaver gets processed in both. And every time you enter data twice, you create an opportunity for error.

This is not a minor inconvenience. Double entry is a systemic problem that wastes time, causes errors, and creates discrepancies between systems that can take hours to reconcile.

## The double entry problem

Consider what happens when you hire a new employee using separate systems:

### In the HR system:
- Enter personal details (name, address, date of birth)
- Record the job title, department, and start date
- Set the salary and working hours
- Upload the contract
- Configure holiday entitlement
- Set up the probation period

### In the payroll system:
- Enter personal details (again)
- Record the tax code and NI number
- Set the salary (again) and pay frequency
- Configure pension enrolment
- Set up bank details for payment
- Enter any other deductions

That is two sets of data entry for the same person. Now multiply it by every change throughout their employment:

- Salary increases: update both systems
- Address changes: update both systems
- Promotions: update both systems
- Absence: record in HR, adjust in payroll
- Leavers: process in both systems

Each of these is a point where data can diverge. A salary change recorded in HR but not in payroll means the employee is paid the wrong amount. An address change in payroll but not HR means correspondence goes to the wrong place.

## The real cost

### Time
Data entry takes time, and entering the same data twice takes twice as long. For a business with 20 employees processing a monthly payroll, the duplicated effort can easily amount to several hours per month — time that could be spent on work that actually moves the business forward.

### Errors
Manual data entry has an error rate. Industry research suggests that manual data entry produces errors in approximately 1% of entries. When you enter every data point twice, you double the opportunities for error. Some errors are caught quickly (a wrong salary is noticed at payday). Others — like an incorrect pension contribution rate — may go unnoticed for months.

### Reconciliation
When two systems contain overlapping data, someone has to periodically check that they match. This reconciliation work is pure overhead — it would not exist if the data lived in one place.

### Audit risk
Discrepancies between systems create problems during audits. If your HR system shows a different salary figure from your payroll system, which one is correct? Auditors will ask, and the time spent investigating and correcting is far greater than the time a single system would have taken.

## What integration looks like

An integrated payroll and HR system stores employee data once and uses it everywhere it is needed. When you add a new employee, you enter their information once. When their salary changes, you update it once. When they leave, you process it once.

### Single source of truth
Every piece of employee data has one authoritative location. There is no question about which system has the "real" information because there is only one system.

### Automatic data flow
Changes to employee records automatically update all dependent processes:

- A salary change flows to payroll calculations, pension contributions, and financial reporting
- A new starter triggers payroll setup, pension enrolment, and onboarding workflows
- An absence record updates the holiday balance, triggers sick pay calculation, and feeds into absence reporting
- A leaver triggers final pay calculation, P45 generation, and pension cessation

### Consistent reporting
When HR and payroll data live together, reports draw from the same dataset. Headcount reports, payroll costs, absence rates, and workforce analytics are all consistent because they come from the same source.

## Where integration matters most

### New starters
The onboarding process is where integration saves the most time. In an integrated system:

1. Enter the employee's details once
2. The system generates the payroll record automatically
3. Pension enrolment is triggered based on eligibility criteria
4. Holiday entitlement is calculated based on start date and working pattern
5. Tax code and NI category are applied based on the employee's declaration
6. The onboarding checklist tracks all steps in one place

### Salary changes
A pay rise processed in an integrated system updates:
- The employee's record
- Payroll calculations (from the effective date)
- Pension contributions (if based on a percentage of salary)
- Financial forecasts and budgets
- Payslip information

In separate systems, each of these updates must be made independently, with the risk of timing mismatches.

### Absence and sick pay
When an employee reports sick, an integrated system:
- Records the absence in the employee's record
- Calculates statutory or occupational sick pay
- Adjusts the payroll for the affected period
- Updates absence reports and trigger-point tracking
- Generates the appropriate documentation

In separate systems, the absence is recorded in HR and then communicated to the payroll team, who manually adjust the pay run. Delays, miscommunications, and errors are common.

### Leavers
Processing a leaver in an integrated system:
- Calculates outstanding holiday pay
- Generates final pay including any deductions
- Produces the tax form (P45 or equivalent)
- Updates pension provider records
- Archives the employee record
- Removes them from future payroll runs

All from a single "process leaver" workflow.

## What to look for in an integrated platform

### True integration versus "connected" systems
Some vendors sell separate HR and payroll products that connect via an integration layer. This is better than completely separate systems, but it is not the same as a truly integrated platform where both functions share a single database.

With a connected approach, data still needs to sync between two systems, and sync failures can create the same discrepancies you were trying to avoid. A single-platform approach eliminates this risk.

### Payroll accuracy
The payroll component must be accurate and compliant. It needs to handle:
- Tax calculations for your jurisdiction
- Social contribution calculations
- Pension auto-enrolment and contributions
- Statutory payments (sick pay, maternity pay, etc.)
- Year-end processing and reporting

### HR capability
The HR component should cover at least:
- Employee records and document storage
- Holiday and absence management
- Onboarding workflows
- Basic reporting

### Self-service
Employees should be able to:
- View their payslips
- Book holiday
- Update personal details
- Access company documents

Self-service reduces the administrative burden on managers and gives employees direct access to their own information.

### Scalability
The platform should grow with your business. Features you do not need today — performance management, recruitment, advanced reporting — should be available when you do need them, without migrating to a different system.

## Making the switch

If you are currently running separate systems and considering integration:

1. **Audit your current data**: Compare employee records across your systems. Identify discrepancies and clean them up before migrating.
2. **Choose the right time**: The start of a new tax year or financial year is often the cleanest point to switch.
3. **Migrate carefully**: Import your cleaned data into the new system and verify it thoroughly before running the first payroll.
4. **Run in parallel**: Consider running both old and new systems for one or two pay periods to verify the new system produces correct results.
5. **Train your team**: Make sure everyone who uses the system understands how it works.

The migration takes effort, but the long-term benefit — eliminating double entry, reducing errors, and saving hours of administrative time every month — makes it a worthwhile investment.

## The bottom line

Double entry is not a minor inefficiency — it is a structural problem that grows with every employee you add. An integrated payroll and HR platform solves it at the root by storing data once and using it everywhere. The result is less administrative work, fewer errors, and more time to focus on the things that actually grow your business.
