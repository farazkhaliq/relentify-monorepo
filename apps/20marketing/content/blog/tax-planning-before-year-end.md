The weeks leading up to your financial year end represent one of the most valuable planning windows in your business calendar. Decisions made during this period can have a significant impact on your tax bill, your cash flow, and your financial position going into the new year.

Tax planning is not about evasion or aggressive schemes. It is about understanding the rules, using the allowances and reliefs available to you, and timing your financial decisions sensibly. Every business should review their position before year end and consider whether there are legitimate steps they can take to optimise their tax outcome.

## Review Your Profit Position

Start by understanding where your business stands financially. Pull together your revenue and expenses for the year to date, and project what the remaining weeks will look like. This gives you a realistic estimate of your taxable profit.

If your profit is higher than expected, there may be opportunities to accelerate planned expenditure into the current year or defer income into the next. If your profit is lower than expected, the opposite might be appropriate.

Having accurate, up-to-date financial data is essential for this exercise. If you are using [accounting software](/accounting) that tracks income and expenses in real time, generating this projection is straightforward. If you are working from spreadsheets or waiting for your accountant to compile the numbers, start that process early.

## Time Your Expenses Wisely

One of the simplest tax planning strategies is timing your expenses to fall into the most beneficial year. If you have planned purchases — equipment, software, professional development, marketing campaigns — consider whether bringing them forward into the current year would reduce your tax bill.

This works because most business expenses reduce your taxable profit in the year they are incurred. A major purchase made just before year end reduces this year's tax bill, whereas the same purchase made a week later reduces next year's.

There are limits to this approach. Do not buy things you do not need just to reduce your tax bill. The goal is to align the timing of genuine business expenditure with your tax planning objectives.

## Maximise Capital Allowances

If your business has invested in equipment, vehicles, or machinery during the year, ensure you are claiming the full capital allowances available. Many jurisdictions offer enhanced allowances for certain types of investment, such as energy-efficient equipment, electric vehicles, or technology hardware.

Review any assets you purchased during the year and check whether they qualify for immediate write-off, enhanced allowances, or standard depreciation. Missing an available allowance means paying more tax than necessary.

If you are considering a capital purchase that could happen in either this year or next, calculate the tax benefit of each timing option before deciding.

## Review Your Pension Contributions

Pension contributions are one of the most tax-efficient ways to extract profits from your business. Employer pension contributions are typically a deductible business expense, reducing your corporation tax bill, while also building your retirement savings.

Review your pension contributions for the year and consider whether there is scope to make additional contributions before year end. Be mindful of annual contribution limits and ensure any contributions are commercially justifiable.

For director-shareholders, the balance between salary, dividends, and pension contributions is a key tax planning decision. If you have not reviewed this balance recently, the period before year end is the right time.

## Check Your Bad Debts

If you have outstanding invoices that are unlikely to be paid, writing them off as bad debts before year end creates a deduction against your taxable profit. This is money you have already included in your revenue figures, so the write-off simply corrects your accounts to reflect reality.

Review your aged debtors report and identify any invoices that are significantly overdue with no realistic prospect of collection. Document your reasoning for the write-off, as tax authorities may query bad debt claims that seem aggressive.

## Consider Stock Valuation

If your business holds physical stock, the value at which you record it at year end affects your taxable profit. Stock should be valued at the lower of cost or net realisable value.

If you have slow-moving, obsolete, or damaged stock, writing it down to its realistic selling value reduces your taxable profit. Conduct a stock count before year end and honestly assess the value of everything you hold.

## Review Director Loan Accounts

If you are a company director with a loan account, check the balance before year end. Outstanding loans from the company to directors can trigger additional tax charges in many jurisdictions.

If you owe the company money, consider repaying the balance before year end to avoid these charges. If the company owes you money, consider the timing of withdrawals in the context of your personal tax planning.

## Plan Your Charitable Giving

Charitable donations made by your business can provide tax relief while supporting causes you care about. If you have planned donations, consider making them before year end to benefit from the deduction in the current year.

Ensure your donations are made to qualifying organisations and that you have appropriate documentation. The rules around charitable giving and tax relief vary by jurisdiction, so check the specific requirements that apply to you.

## Use Your Annual Allowances

Various annual allowances and reliefs are available to businesses and their owners, but many expire at year end if not used. Review the allowances available to you and check whether any are underutilised.

Common examples include annual investment allowances for capital expenditure, research and development tax credits, employment allowances that reduce employer payroll costs, and personal tax allowances that can be optimised through salary and dividend planning.

## Prepare Your Records

Tax planning is only effective if your records support your claims. Before year end, ensure your bookkeeping is up to date, receipts are filed, bank accounts are reconciled, and any journal entries are properly documented.

Incomplete or disorganised records create problems when you file your tax return. They also make it harder for your accountant to identify planning opportunities. The time you invest in tidying your records before year end pays dividends throughout the tax return preparation process.

## Talk to Your Accountant

If you work with an accountant, schedule a year-end planning conversation rather than waiting until after your year end when opportunities have already passed. A proactive discussion about your financial position, planned investments, and personal circumstances allows your accountant to identify strategies that might not be obvious from the numbers alone.

Come prepared with your year-to-date financial summary, any planned major expenditure, and questions about your tax position. The more your accountant knows about your plans, the better advice they can provide.

## Plan Ahead for Next Year

Year-end tax planning should not be a once-a-year scramble. Use the insights from this year's review to set up better systems for next year.

If your [accounting software](/accounting) provides real-time profit tracking, you can monitor your tax position throughout the year and make adjustments incrementally rather than rushing to act in the final weeks.

Set calendar reminders at quarterly intervals to review your financial position and consider whether any planning actions are appropriate. Spreading the effort across the year produces better outcomes than leaving everything to the last moment.

## The Bottom Line

Year-end tax planning is not about finding loopholes. It is about making informed financial decisions within the rules. By reviewing your position, timing your expenses, maximising your allowances, and consulting with your accountant, you can legitimately reduce your tax bill and start the new year in a stronger financial position.

The key is to act before your year end, not after it. Once the year closes, most planning opportunities close with it. Start your review early, gather your data, and make deliberate decisions that serve your business and personal financial goals.
