Property viewings are the critical moment in the lettings process. Everything before — marketing, enquiry handling, lead qualification — leads up to the viewing. Everything after — offers, referencing, tenancy setup — follows from it. If the viewing goes well, the property gets let. If it goes poorly, the process starts again.

Yet in many agencies, viewings are managed haphazardly. Schedules are juggled across personal calendars. Feedback is captured in handwritten notes that never make it into the system. Follow-ups happen when someone remembers, which is not always soon enough.

A structured approach to viewings management — from scheduling to follow-up — improves conversion rates, reduces void periods, and delivers a more professional experience for both applicants and landlords.

## Scheduling viewings efficiently

The logistics of viewings are deceptively complex. You need to coordinate the availability of the property, the applicant, and the agent. For occupied properties, you also need the current tenant's cooperation. For multiple viewings on the same property, you want to group them to minimise travel time.

### Block scheduling

Rather than scheduling viewings individually as enquiries come in, group viewings into blocks. Allocate specific time slots for viewings on specific days, and offer applicants slots from those blocks. This is more efficient than reactive scheduling and ensures that agents are not spending their entire day driving between properties.

### Open viewings

For properties generating high levels of interest, open viewings — a set time window during which multiple applicants can view the property — are an efficient alternative to individual appointments. They reduce the time commitment for the agent, create a sense of demand among applicants, and allow you to collect multiple expressions of interest on the same day.

### Online booking

Allow applicants to book viewings directly through your website or portal listings. An online booking system connected to your CRM eliminates the back-and-forth of phone and email scheduling. The applicant selects a slot, the booking is confirmed automatically, and the viewing appears in the agent's calendar.

### Confirmation and reminders

No-shows waste everyone's time. An automated confirmation email or message at the time of booking, followed by a reminder 24 hours before the viewing, significantly reduces no-show rates. Include the property address, directions, parking information, and a contact number in case the applicant needs to reschedule.

## During the viewing

The viewing itself is a sales opportunity, and it should be treated as one. Preparation matters.

### Property preparation

Ensure the property is in viewing-ready condition. For vacant properties, this means clean, well-lit, and adequately heated or ventilated depending on the season. For occupied properties, coordinate with the tenant to ensure the property is presentable.

First impressions are formed in the first few seconds. The entrance, the hallway, and the kitchen are the spaces that matter most. If these are clean and welcoming, the rest of the viewing benefits.

### Applicant engagement

A viewing is not a silent walk-through. It is a conversation. Ask the applicant about their requirements, their timeline, and their priorities. Listen to their responses and tailor your presentation accordingly.

If the applicant is a young professional, highlight the proximity to transport links and nightlife. If they are a family, emphasise the garden, the local schools, and the storage space. If they are downsizing, focus on the low-maintenance aspects.

### Capturing feedback

At the end of the viewing, ask for immediate feedback. What did they think? Are they interested? Do they have concerns? This feedback is valuable regardless of the outcome — if the applicant is interested, you can move quickly; if they are not, their reasons help you improve the marketing or presentation of the property.

Record this feedback in your CRM immediately. If you wait until you are back in the office, the details fade. A quick note on your phone, synced to the CRM, captures the information while it is fresh.

## Post-viewing follow-up

The follow-up is where many agencies lose conversions. An applicant leaves a viewing interested but undecided. The agent intends to call the next day but gets busy. By the time the call happens three days later, the applicant has already applied for another property.

### The 24-hour rule

Follow up with every applicant within 24 hours of their viewing. No exceptions. This is the single most impactful change most agencies can make to their viewing conversion rate.

The follow-up does not need to be a lengthy conversation. A brief call or email — "Thank you for viewing the property. Do you have any questions? Would you like to proceed?" — is sufficient. The purpose is to maintain momentum and demonstrate professionalism.

### Automated follow-up tasks

Your CRM should automatically create a follow-up task whenever a viewing is logged, due 24 hours after the viewing date. This ensures that the follow-up happens regardless of whether the agent remembers.

### Landlord updates

After viewings, landlords want to know what happened. How many people viewed the property? What was the feedback? Are there any offers pending? An automated viewing summary — sent to the landlord after each viewing day — keeps them informed without requiring the agent to write individual updates.

## Tracking viewing metrics

Your CRM should track viewing data that helps you optimise your process over time.

**Viewings per property.** How many viewings does it take, on average, to let a property? A high number might indicate overpricing, poor presentation, or an unappealing location. A very low number might suggest underpricing.

**Viewing to offer ratio.** What percentage of viewings result in an offer? This is a direct measure of how well your viewings convert.

**No-show rate.** What percentage of booked viewings result in no-shows? If this is high, review your confirmation and reminder process.

**Time from viewing to offer.** How quickly do applicants make decisions after viewing? If this is slow, your follow-up process may need attention.

**Agent performance.** Which agents have the highest viewing-to-offer conversion rates? What are they doing differently that could be shared with the team?

## Managing multiple applicants

When a property generates interest from multiple applicants, managing the situation fairly and efficiently becomes important. Your CRM provides the framework.

Track every applicant who has expressed interest, their viewing date, their feedback, and their current status in the process. If multiple offers come in, present them to the landlord with full context — not just the rent amount, but the applicant's referencing likelihood, proposed move-in date, and tenancy length.

Communicating with unsuccessful applicants is equally important. A prompt, courteous notification that the property has been offered to another applicant maintains goodwill and keeps the door open for future properties. Your CRM can automate this notification, ensuring that no applicant is left wondering.

## The viewing as a relationship opportunity

Every viewing is an opportunity to build a relationship, even if the applicant does not take that particular property. An applicant who has a positive viewing experience — a professional, knowledgeable agent, a well-presented property, a prompt follow-up — will remember your agency when they resume their search.

Your CRM should capture these applicants as active contacts with their requirements noted. When a matching property becomes available, you can contact them directly — often before the property hits the portals. This proactive matching is one of the most effective ways to reduce void periods and provide a superior service.
