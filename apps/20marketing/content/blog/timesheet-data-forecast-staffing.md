Most small businesses make staffing decisions reactively. They hire when the team is overwhelmed and let attrition handle periods of lower demand. This approach works until it does not — a sudden surge in demand leaves the business scrambling for workers, or a slow period results in expensive idle time.

Forecasting staffing needs is the alternative, and the best forecasting tool most businesses already have is their timesheet data. Historical records of who worked, when, for how long, and on what provide a factual basis for predicting future demand. This guide explains how to use that data to make better staffing decisions.

## What timesheet data tells you about staffing

### Demand patterns

Timesheet data reveals when your business needs the most labour. For many businesses, demand follows predictable patterns:

- **Weekly:** Higher demand on specific days (weekends in hospitality, Mondays in cleaning)
- **Monthly:** Peaks around month-end (accounting, reporting deadlines)
- **Seasonal:** Predictable surges (holiday season in retail, summer in construction, tax season in accounting)
- **Event-driven:** Spikes tied to external events (conferences, product launches, seasonal contracts)

By analysing six to twelve months of timesheet data, you can identify these patterns with precision — not just "we are busy in December" but "we need 40% more labour hours in December compared to February."

### Overtime as a demand signal

Persistent overtime at a site, department, or across the business is a strong signal that the current headcount is insufficient. If a team regularly works ten percent overtime, the business is effectively understaffed by that margin and paying a premium (overtime rates) for the shortfall.

Timesheet data quantifies this: how many overtime hours per week, at what cost, and where the overtime concentrates. This data makes the business case for hiring straightforward: the cost of a new worker versus the cost of ongoing overtime.

### Utilisation rates

If your team's utilisation rate (billable or productive hours as a percentage of available hours) is consistently above 85%, there is little capacity for additional work. If it drops below 65%, there may be too many workers for the current demand. Tracking utilisation over time reveals the trend and helps you anticipate when headcount needs to change.

### Absence patterns

Timesheet data, combined with absence records, shows how much time is lost to holidays, sickness, and other absences. If your business loses an average of 15% of available hours to absence, your staffing plan needs to account for that — you need more workers than the raw demand calculation suggests.

## Building a staffing forecast

### Step 1: Gather historical data

Pull timesheet data for the past twelve months (or longer, if available). Organise it by:

- Total hours per week or month
- Hours by site, department, or project
- Overtime hours
- Absence hours

If your [timesheet system](/timesheets) provides reporting by these dimensions, this step is straightforward. If not, export the raw data and aggregate it in a spreadsheet.

### Step 2: Identify the baseline

Calculate the average demand (in labour hours) for each period. This is your baseline — the normal level of staffing required, assuming no growth or decline.

For example, if your cleaning business averaged 800 hours per week across all sites over the past year, 800 hours is your baseline weekly demand.

### Step 3: Identify seasonal variation

Overlay seasonal patterns on the baseline. If December demand is 40% above average and February is 20% below, your forecast should reflect this:

- February: 640 hours/week (baseline minus 20%)
- Average months: 800 hours/week
- December: 1,120 hours/week (baseline plus 40%)

### Step 4: Factor in growth or decline

If the business is growing — adding clients, opening new sites, expanding services — adjust the baseline upward. Use recent trends (the last three to six months compared to the same period in the prior year) to estimate the growth rate.

If the business is contracting, adjust downward. Be realistic: it is better to forecast conservatively and adjust than to overstaff and then need to reduce.

### Step 5: Convert hours to headcount

Divide the forecasted hours by the available hours per worker. Available hours account for the standard working week minus expected absences:

**Available hours per worker per week = Standard hours x (1 - absence rate)**

For example, if standard hours are 40 and the absence rate is 15%:

40 x 0.85 = 34 available hours per worker per week

If December demand is 1,120 hours/week:

1,120 / 34 = 33 workers needed

If your current headcount is 28, you need 5 additional workers for the December peak.

### Step 6: Decide how to fill the gap

The gap between current headcount and forecasted need can be filled through:

- **Overtime:** Suitable for small, temporary gaps. More expensive per hour but avoids recruitment costs.
- **Temporary staff:** Agency workers or fixed-term contracts for seasonal peaks. Flexible but requires onboarding.
- **Permanent hires:** Appropriate when the increase is sustained, not seasonal. Lower per-hour cost but a fixed commitment.
- **Reallocation:** Moving workers from lower-demand areas to higher-demand areas. No additional cost but requires cross-training.

The right approach depends on the nature and duration of the demand increase.

## Common forecasting mistakes

### Ignoring seasonality

A business that hires based on peak demand will be overstaffed during quiet periods. A business that staffs for average demand will be understaffed during peaks. The forecast must account for the pattern, not just the average.

### Using scheduled hours instead of actual hours

Scheduled hours represent what was planned. Actual hours (from timesheets) represent what happened. If workers consistently exceed scheduled hours, the schedule underestimates demand. Use actual data for forecasting.

### Not accounting for absence

A forecast that assumes 100% attendance will understaff the business by the absence rate. Always factor in expected holidays, sickness, and other absences.

### Treating overtime as free capacity

Overtime is a short-term solution, not a sustainable staffing strategy. If overtime is a permanent fixture, the forecast should convert it into additional headcount. Ongoing overtime costs more than equivalent standard-rate hours, and it comes with compliance and wellbeing risks.

### Forecasting too far ahead

For most small businesses, a three-to-six-month forecast is practical. Beyond that, too many variables change — new clients, lost contracts, economic shifts. Update the forecast regularly (monthly or quarterly) rather than creating an annual plan and forgetting about it.

## Making it practical

You do not need sophisticated software to forecast staffing needs. A spreadsheet, fed with timesheet data, is sufficient for most small businesses. The critical ingredient is the data itself — accurate, consistent, and detailed enough to reveal patterns.

Here is a simple quarterly review process:

1. Export the last twelve months of timesheet data
2. Calculate average weekly hours by department or site
3. Note seasonal peaks and troughs
4. Compare to current headcount and capacity
5. Identify the gap (positive or negative) for the next quarter
6. Decide how to fill it (hire, use temps, adjust schedules, accept overtime)
7. Set a reminder to review again in three months

This takes an hour per quarter and provides a data-driven basis for what is often the business's most expensive resource decision.

## Summary

Staffing forecasting does not require advanced analytics or expensive tools. It requires accurate timesheet data and a simple process for turning that data into decisions. The businesses that forecast well — hiring ahead of demand, managing overtime proactively, and planning for seasonal variation — operate with less stress, lower costs, and better service delivery than those that react after the fact.

Your timesheet data is already telling you what your future staffing needs look like. The question is whether you are listening.
