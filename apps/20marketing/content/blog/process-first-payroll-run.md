You have registered as an employer, hired your first employee, and now payday is approaching. Processing your first payroll run can feel daunting, but it is really just a series of logical steps. Once you have been through it once, each subsequent run becomes faster and more routine.

This guide takes you through a first payroll run from start to finish, explaining each step in plain language.

## Before you begin: what you should already have

Before processing your first payroll, make sure you have:

- **Employer registration** with your tax authority, including your employer reference number
- **Employee details** including full name, address, tax identification number, tax code, and bank account details
- **A payroll calendar** showing your chosen pay frequency (weekly, fortnightly, monthly) and the dates of each pay period
- **Salary or rate information** for each employee, as stated in their contract
- **Pension scheme details** if you are required to auto-enrol employees into a workplace pension

If any of these are missing, sort them out before attempting your first run. Incomplete information leads to errors, corrections, and frustrated employees.

## Step 1: Confirm the pay period

Your first decision is establishing what period this payroll covers. If you pay monthly, your first pay period might be 1 April to 30 April, with payment on the last working day of the month. If you pay weekly, it might be Monday to Sunday with payment the following Friday.

Whatever schedule you choose, document it clearly. Every employee should know:

- What dates the pay period covers
- When they will be paid
- What the cut-off date is for submitting hours, expenses, or other variable pay

Consistency matters. Once you set a schedule, stick to it.

## Step 2: Gather variable pay data

For salaried employees, gross pay is straightforward — it is the same amount each period. But if you have hourly workers, or anyone earning overtime, commission, or bonuses, you need to collect that data before you can calculate pay.

This might include:

- **Timesheets**: Hours worked by each hourly employee. Make sure these are approved by a manager before processing. Using [digital timesheets](/timesheets) rather than paper records significantly reduces errors at this stage.
- **Overtime records**: Hours worked beyond the standard threshold, and the applicable overtime rate.
- **Commission or bonus amounts**: These may come from sales reports, manager approvals, or company-wide schemes.
- **Expense reimbursements**: Depending on how you handle expenses, some may be paid through payroll.

Collect all variable data before the processing deadline you have set. Chasing missing timesheets on the day you need to run payroll is a problem you want to avoid.

## Step 3: Calculate gross pay

For each employee, calculate their gross pay for the period:

- **Salaried employees**: Annual salary divided by the number of pay periods. For monthly pay, divide by 12. For weekly, divide by 52.
- **Hourly employees**: Total hours worked multiplied by the hourly rate, plus any overtime at the applicable rate.
- **Variable additions**: Add any bonuses, commissions, or other taxable payments.

Gross pay is the starting point for all deductions. Double-check these figures — an error here cascades through every subsequent calculation.

## Step 4: Apply tax deductions

Using each employee's tax code, calculate the income tax due on their earnings for this period. The exact method depends on your jurisdiction, but the general process is:

1. Determine the tax-free amount for the period (from the tax code)
2. Subtract the tax-free amount from gross pay to get taxable pay
3. Apply the relevant tax rate(s) to the taxable amount

If you are using cumulative tax codes, you also need to factor in year-to-date earnings and deductions to ensure the correct amount is being collected across the full tax year.

This is where payroll software earns its keep. Tax calculations involve tax tables, rate bands, and cumulative tracking that are tedious and error-prone to do manually. If you are processing payroll by hand, consider using the tax authority's published calculator tools.

## Step 5: Apply other mandatory deductions

Beyond income tax, there are usually other mandatory deductions:

- **Social contributions**: National insurance, social security, or similar payroll taxes. These have their own thresholds and rates, and both the employee and employer typically contribute.
- **Student loan repayments**: If applicable, these are deducted at a percentage of earnings above a threshold.
- **Court orders**: Any legally mandated deductions such as child support or debt repayment orders.

Each of these has specific calculation rules. Make sure you are using the current year's rates and thresholds — these change annually.

## Step 6: Apply voluntary deductions

After mandatory deductions, apply any agreed voluntary deductions:

- **Pension contributions**: The employee's share of their workplace pension contribution.
- **Salary sacrifice amounts**: If the employee participates in any salary sacrifice schemes.
- **Union dues or professional subscriptions**: If deducted at source by agreement.

Make sure you have written authorisation for all voluntary deductions. Deducting money from an employee's pay without proper authority is a legal issue.

## Step 7: Calculate employer contributions

Some deductions are not taken from the employee's pay — they are additional costs that you, the employer, bear. These typically include:

- **Employer social contributions**: Your share of national insurance, social security, or equivalent.
- **Employer pension contributions**: Your contribution to the employee's workplace pension.
- **Apprenticeship levy or training levies**: If applicable in your jurisdiction.

These do not affect the employee's net pay, but they are part of your payroll costs and need to be calculated, recorded, and paid to the relevant bodies.

## Step 8: Calculate net pay

Net pay is what lands in the employee's bank account. The formula is simple:

**Net pay = Gross pay − Income tax − Employee social contributions − Other employee deductions**

Review each employee's net pay before proceeding. Does it look reasonable? Is it roughly what you would expect based on their salary? A sanity check at this stage catches most errors before they become real problems.

## Step 9: Generate payslips

Every employee is entitled to a payslip showing a breakdown of their pay. The payslip should include:

- Employee name and the pay period dates
- Gross pay (and a breakdown if there are multiple components)
- Each deduction listed separately with amounts
- Net pay
- Year-to-date totals for earnings and deductions
- Employer pension contributions (in many jurisdictions)

Payslips can be issued on paper or electronically. Digital payslips are increasingly the norm and are easier to store and search.

## Step 10: Make payments

With net pay calculated and payslips generated, it is time to pay your employees. Most businesses use bank transfers (direct deposit), which can be processed individually or as a batch payment through your bank.

Key things to check:

- **Bank details are correct**: A wrong account number means the payment goes to the wrong person. Verify details when employees first provide them.
- **Payment timing**: Ensure the payment will clear by the agreed payday. Bank processing times vary, so submit payments with enough lead time.
- **Payment reference**: Include a clear reference on each payment so employees can identify it in their bank statements.

## Step 11: File with your tax authority

After processing payroll, you must report the details to your tax authority. This filing typically includes:

- Each employee's gross pay, tax deducted, and social contributions for the period
- Details of any new starters or leavers
- Employer contributions

Many jurisdictions require this information to be submitted on or before payday. Late filing attracts penalties, so treat this as an essential step — not something to do when you get around to it.

Payroll software that files electronically on your behalf is one of the best investments you can make. It removes the risk of missed deadlines and ensures data accuracy.

## Step 12: Reconcile and record

After the pay run is complete:

- **Reconcile your payroll account**: The total of all net payments plus all deductions paid to third parties should equal the total gross pay plus employer contributions.
- **Record the payroll journal in your accounts**: Payroll is one of the largest expenses for most businesses. Making sure it is correctly recorded in your accounting system is essential for accurate financial reporting.
- **Store records securely**: Keep copies of all payslips, filings, and calculations for the required retention period.

## After your first run

Congratulations — you have processed payroll. The first time takes the longest because everything is new. By your second or third run, the process will feel routine.

A few things to establish going forward:

- **Set calendar reminders** for every key payroll date
- **Create a checklist** for each pay run so nothing gets missed
- **Review your process** after the first few runs and look for improvements

Payroll is one of those areas where small investments in getting the process right pay for themselves many times over. Whether that is better [timesheet software](/timesheets), a properly integrated [payroll system](/payroll), or simply a well-documented procedure that anyone in your team can follow.

The goal is a process that runs smoothly every period, without surprises.
