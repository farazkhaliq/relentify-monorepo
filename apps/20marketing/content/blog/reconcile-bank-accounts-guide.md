Bank reconciliation is one of those tasks that many business owners put off because it sounds tedious. And honestly, it can be tedious — but it is also one of the most important things you can do to keep your financial records accurate.

At its core, bank reconciliation is the process of comparing your accounting records with your bank statement to make sure they match. When they do not match (and they often will not, at least initially), you need to find out why and fix it.

## Why bank reconciliation matters

### Catching errors

Mistakes happen. You might enter a transaction twice, record the wrong amount, or forget to record a transaction entirely. Reconciliation catches these errors before they compound into bigger problems.

### Detecting fraud

Unauthorised transactions — whether from stolen card details, employee fraud, or bank errors — show up during reconciliation. The sooner you spot them, the sooner you can act.

### Ensuring accurate reports

Your profit and loss statement, balance sheet, and cash flow reports are only as accurate as the data they are built on. If your bank balance in your accounting system does not match your actual bank balance, every report that uses that figure is wrong.

### Tax compliance

Accurate records are essential for tax filing. Discrepancies between your books and your bank statements can trigger questions during an audit and may result in penalties.

### Peace of mind

There is real value in knowing that your numbers are correct. When you reconcile regularly, you can trust your reports and make decisions with confidence.

## How bank reconciliation works

The basic process is:

1. Take your bank statement balance at a specific date
2. Take your accounting system balance at the same date
3. Compare them
4. Identify and resolve any differences

The differences (called reconciling items) fall into a few categories:

### Outstanding payments (unpresented cheques)

You have recorded a payment in your books, but it has not yet cleared the bank. For example, you wrote a cheque on the 28th, but the recipient did not cash it until the next month.

### Deposits in transit

You have recorded income in your books, but it has not yet appeared on your bank statement. This is common with card payments that take a day or two to settle.

### Bank charges and interest

Your bank may charge fees or pay interest that you have not yet recorded in your books. These appear on the bank statement but not in your accounting records until you enter them.

### Errors

Sometimes you or the bank have made a mistake. A transaction might be recorded at the wrong amount, in the wrong account, or not at all.

### Unrecorded transactions

Direct debits, standing orders, or automatic payments that you have not yet entered in your books. These appear on the bank statement but not in your accounting system.

## Step-by-step: manual bank reconciliation

If you are doing this manually (or want to understand the process before using software), here is how:

### Step 1: Gather your documents

You need:
- Your bank statement for the period
- Your cash book or accounting records for the same period
- The previous reconciliation (if any) as a starting point

### Step 2: Compare the opening balances

Your accounting system's opening balance for the period should match the bank statement's opening balance (after accounting for any outstanding items from the previous reconciliation). If they do not match, investigate before proceeding.

### Step 3: Check off matching transactions

Go through each transaction on the bank statement and find the corresponding entry in your accounting records. When they match (same date, same amount, same party), tick both off.

### Step 4: Identify unmatched items

After working through all transactions, you will have items that do not match:

- **On the bank statement but not in your books** — These need to be recorded. Common examples: bank fees, interest, direct debits you forgot to enter.
- **In your books but not on the bank statement** — These are timing differences. Cheques not yet cashed, deposits not yet cleared, or transfers in progress.
- **Amount differences** — A transaction appears in both places but the amounts differ. Find out which is correct and fix the error.

### Step 5: Adjust your records

Enter any transactions that are on the bank statement but missing from your books. Correct any errors in your records.

### Step 6: Prepare the reconciliation statement

Your reconciliation statement should show:

```
Bank statement balance:              X,XXX.XX
Add: Deposits in transit            +  XXX.XX
Less: Outstanding payments          -  XXX.XX
Adjusted bank balance:               X,XXX.XX

Accounting system balance:           X,XXX.XX
```

The adjusted bank balance should equal your accounting system balance. If it does, your reconciliation is complete.

## Using software for bank reconciliation

Modern accounting software makes reconciliation significantly faster:

### Bank feeds

Most accounting platforms can connect directly to your bank account and import transactions automatically. This eliminates the need to manually compare paper statements with your records.

With bank feeds enabled in platforms like [Relentify](/accounting), transactions appear in your accounting system as they clear the bank. You then match them to the corresponding entries in your books — or create new entries for transactions you have not yet recorded.

### Automatic matching

Good software will automatically match transactions that are identical in date, amount, and description. This handles the bulk of the reconciliation work, leaving you with only the exceptions to review manually.

### Suggested matches

When automatic matching fails (because the date or amount is slightly different), software can suggest probable matches for you to review and confirm.

### Reconciliation reports

Software generates reconciliation reports automatically, showing the bank balance, your book balance, and any outstanding items. This replaces the manual reconciliation statement.

## How often should you reconcile?

### Monthly (minimum)

At an absolute minimum, reconcile your bank accounts monthly. This keeps discrepancies manageable — finding one month's errors is much easier than finding a year's worth.

### Weekly (recommended for active businesses)

If you process a high volume of transactions, weekly reconciliation prevents a backlog and catches errors sooner.

### Daily (for high-volume businesses)

Businesses processing dozens or hundreds of transactions daily benefit from daily reconciliation. With bank feeds, this can be done in minutes.

### After bank statement close

Always reconcile after your bank statement closes for the period. This gives you a clean cut-off point.

## Common reconciliation problems and solutions

### The balance is off by a round number

If you are out by exactly 100, 500, or 1,000, you have likely missed a transaction entirely. Check for unrecorded deposits, payments, or bank charges.

### The balance is off by a small amount

Small differences (a few pence or cents) often indicate a rounding error, a transposition error (typing 54 instead of 45), or a bank fee you have not recorded.

### The difference is exactly double a transaction amount

This usually means you have recorded a transaction twice, or you have recorded a deposit as a payment (or vice versa).

### Transactions on different dates

A payment recorded on the 30th in your books might appear on the 1st on your bank statement, or vice versa. This is a timing difference and is normal — it resolves itself in the next reconciliation.

### Foreign currency transactions

If you deal in multiple currencies, exchange rate differences can cause small discrepancies. Record exchange rate gains or losses as separate transactions.

## Tips for making reconciliation easier

1. **Reconcile regularly** — The longer you wait, the harder it gets
2. **Use bank feeds** — Automatic transaction imports save enormous time
3. **Record transactions promptly** — Enter expenses and income as they happen, not in bulk at month end
4. **Keep a consistent process** — Same time, same method, same person
5. **Separate business and personal** — Mixing accounts makes reconciliation much harder
6. **File your reconciliation reports** — Keep them for your records and for any audit requirements
7. **Investigate every difference** — Do not just adjust figures to make them balance without understanding why they differed

Bank reconciliation is not exciting work, but it is the foundation of financial accuracy. Do it regularly, do it thoroughly, and your financial reports will be something you can actually trust.
