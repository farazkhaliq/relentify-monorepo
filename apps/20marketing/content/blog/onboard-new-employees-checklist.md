The first week at a new job shapes how an employee feels about your company for months to come. A well-organised onboarding experience signals that you are professional, prepared, and invested in their success. A chaotic one — where the laptop is not ready, nobody knows they are coming, and they spend three days reading outdated manuals — tells them the opposite.

This guide provides a practical first-week onboarding checklist that any small business can implement.

## Why onboarding matters

Research consistently shows that employees who have a positive onboarding experience are significantly more likely to stay with the company long-term. Poor onboarding, on the other hand, is one of the top reasons people leave within their first six months.

Good onboarding is not just about paperwork. It is about making the new person feel welcome, giving them the tools and information they need, and setting clear expectations from day one.

## Before they arrive

The best onboarding starts before the employee's first day. In the week before they start:

### Administrative preparation
- [ ] Send the employment contract for signing (if not already done)
- [ ] Collect personal details needed for payroll: tax information, bank details, pension preferences, emergency contacts
- [ ] Set up their record in your [payroll](/payroll) system
- [ ] Conduct the right to work check
- [ ] Enrol them in workplace pension (if applicable)
- [ ] Add them to your employee directory or HR system

### Equipment and access
- [ ] Order and set up their laptop or computer
- [ ] Create email account and any necessary software accounts
- [ ] Set up access to shared drives, project management tools, and communication platforms
- [ ] Prepare their workspace (desk, chair, monitor, stationery)
- [ ] Order any specialist equipment they need
- [ ] Prepare building access (keys, security pass, door codes)

### Communication
- [ ] Send a welcome email with first-day logistics: start time, where to go, who to ask for, dress code, parking or transport information
- [ ] Inform the team that a new person is joining: their name, role, and start date
- [ ] Brief the line manager on the onboarding plan
- [ ] Assign a buddy or point of contact for the first week

Having everything ready before they arrive shows respect for their time and creates a strong first impression.

## Day one

The first day sets the tone. Keep it structured but not overwhelming.

### Morning

**Welcome and introductions**
- [ ] Meet the new employee at reception or the entrance
- [ ] Give a tour of the workplace: desk, kitchen, bathrooms, meeting rooms, fire exits
- [ ] Introduce them to their immediate team
- [ ] Introduce them to key people in other departments they will interact with

**Equipment and access**
- [ ] Hand over laptop, phone, keys, and any other equipment
- [ ] Walk through logging in, email setup, and essential software
- [ ] Confirm they can access everything they need
- [ ] Provide Wi-Fi credentials and printer access

**Essentials briefing**
- [ ] Review health and safety basics: fire exits, first aid, emergency procedures
- [ ] Walk through security protocols: building access, alarm codes, visitor procedures
- [ ] Explain the day-to-day basics: working hours, breaks, kitchen etiquette, parking

### Afternoon

**Paperwork and policies**
- [ ] Complete any remaining employment paperwork
- [ ] Provide the employee handbook (or show them where to find it digitally)
- [ ] Walk through key policies: holiday booking, sickness notification, expenses, IT security
- [ ] Have them acknowledge receipt of the handbook

**First conversation with their manager**
- [ ] Discuss the role and immediate priorities
- [ ] Explain what the first week will look like
- [ ] Set expectations for the probation period
- [ ] Schedule regular catch-ups for the first few weeks

**End of day check-in**
- [ ] Ask how the day went
- [ ] Address any questions or concerns
- [ ] Confirm tomorrow's schedule

## Days two to five

The rest of the first week is about building knowledge, relationships, and early momentum.

### Role and responsibilities
- [ ] Provide a detailed overview of the team's work and how their role fits in
- [ ] Walk through current projects and priorities
- [ ] Introduce the tools and systems they will use daily
- [ ] Share relevant documentation, process guides, and templates
- [ ] Explain reporting lines and decision-making structures

### Meet the wider team
- [ ] Schedule brief introductory meetings with key colleagues outside their immediate team
- [ ] If relevant, introduce them to clients or external contacts they will work with
- [ ] Include them in team meetings and social events

### Training
- [ ] Complete any mandatory training: health and safety, data protection, compliance
- [ ] Begin role-specific training based on their skill level and experience
- [ ] Provide access to learning resources and development materials
- [ ] Schedule hands-on training sessions with experienced team members

### Early goals
- [ ] Set two to three achievable goals for the first month
- [ ] Assign a small, meaningful task they can complete in the first week
- [ ] Provide the context and support they need to succeed

Giving a new employee a real task early — not busy work, but something that contributes — helps them feel valued and gives them a sense of accomplishment.

### Daily check-ins
- [ ] The manager or buddy should check in briefly each day during the first week
- [ ] Ask what is going well and what is confusing
- [ ] Adjust the pace if they are feeling overwhelmed or under-stimulated
- [ ] Provide feedback on any work they have completed

## End of first week

At the end of the first week, have a slightly longer conversation:

- [ ] Review how the week went from their perspective
- [ ] Confirm they have access to everything they need
- [ ] Reiterate the first-month goals
- [ ] Schedule the next check-in (weekly during the first month is ideal)
- [ ] Ask for feedback on the onboarding process itself

## Beyond the first week

Onboarding does not end on Friday. The first month, and ideally the first three months, should include structured support:

### First month
- Weekly check-ins with the line manager
- Completion of initial training modules
- First meaningful project or contribution
- Introduction to any stakeholders not yet met

### First three months (probation period)
- Regular performance conversations
- Mid-probation review to discuss progress and any concerns
- Increasing responsibility and independence
- End-of-probation review and confirmation of ongoing employment

### Six-month mark
- Follow-up conversation about how the role is developing
- Discussion about longer-term development goals
- Any adjustments to the role based on experience so far

## Making it work in a small business

You do not need an elaborate onboarding programme. Small businesses can deliver excellent onboarding with a few simple practices:

- **Use a checklist**: The list above can be adapted to your business and reused for every new hire. Print it out or keep it in your HR system.
- **Assign a buddy**: Even in a team of three, having someone specifically responsible for helping the new person navigate the first week makes a difference.
- **Prepare in advance**: Most onboarding problems come from poor preparation. If the laptop is ready, the email works, and someone is expecting them, you are ahead of most employers.
- **Be human**: Welcome them genuinely. Learn their name. Take them for coffee. Small gestures have an outsized impact on how someone feels about joining your company.

Modern [payroll](/payroll) and HR platforms like Relentify can help automate the administrative side of onboarding — collecting employee details, setting up payroll records, managing documents, and tracking that all checklist items have been completed. This frees you to focus on the human side of welcoming a new team member.

## The bottom line

A good first week does not require a big budget or a dedicated HR team. It requires preparation, structure, and genuine attention to the new person's experience. Invest in those first five days, and you dramatically increase the chances that your new hire becomes a long-term, engaged member of your team.
