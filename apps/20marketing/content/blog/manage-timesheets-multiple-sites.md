Running a business across multiple sites introduces a layer of complexity that single-location businesses never encounter. Schedules vary by location. Workers move between sites. Managers may oversee teams they rarely see in person. And the timesheet data that should tie it all together often ends up fragmented, inconsistent, or incomplete.

For businesses in cleaning, construction, security, care, hospitality, or any other multi-site sector, getting timesheet management right is not a back-office concern. It directly affects payroll accuracy, client billing, labour compliance, and operational efficiency.

This guide covers the challenges specific to multi-site timesheet management and the practical steps to solve them.

## The multi-site challenge

In a single-location business, timesheet management is relatively straightforward. Everyone clocks in at the same place, the same manager reviews the data, and the same rules apply. Multi-site operations break all of these assumptions.

### Different sites, different rules

Each site may have different shift patterns, break requirements, overtime rules, or client expectations. A cleaning contract at a hospital might require night shifts with specific break schedules, while a contract at an office building runs standard daytime hours. Your timesheet system needs to accommodate these differences without requiring a separate configuration for each one.

### Workers who move between sites

Many multi-site businesses have workers who split their time across locations — a care worker visiting three clients in a day, a construction labourer moving between two projects, or a cleaner covering for a colleague at a different site. The timesheet system needs to capture which site the worker was at and for how long, not just their total hours for the day.

### Distributed management

When managers are responsible for multiple sites, they cannot physically observe attendance. They rely entirely on the timesheet system to tell them who showed up, when, and where. If the system is unreliable or difficult to use, managers either spend hours chasing data or simply approve everything without checking.

### Inconsistent practices

Without a centralised system, each site tends to develop its own habits. One site uses paper timesheets, another uses a spreadsheet, and a third has an informal "just tell me your hours" arrangement. This inconsistency makes it impossible to generate consolidated reports, compare performance across sites, or ensure that every location meets the same compliance standards.

## Building a multi-site timesheet system

The solution is a single, centralised system that accommodates the variations between sites while maintaining consistent data standards. Here is how to set one up.

### Step 1: Create a site register

List every location where your workers operate. For each site, record:

- Site name and address
- Client name (if the site belongs to a client)
- Operating hours and shift patterns
- Break rules and overtime thresholds
- The manager or supervisor responsible for approving timesheets

This register becomes the foundation of your timesheet configuration. Every clock-in will be associated with a site, and every report can be filtered by site.

### Step 2: Define geofences

If your workers clock in from mobile devices, set up a geofence for each site. A geofence is a virtual boundary defined by GPS coordinates and a radius. When a worker clocks in within the boundary, the system automatically associates their timesheet with that site.

This eliminates the need for workers to manually select their location — a step that is frequently forgotten or entered incorrectly. It also prevents workers from clocking in before they arrive at the site.

Platforms like [Relentify](/timesheets) allow you to set up geofences for each site from a central dashboard, making it straightforward to manage dozens or even hundreds of locations.

### Step 3: Assign workers to sites

Each worker should be assigned to one or more sites. This serves two purposes:

1. **Scheduling**: You can see at a glance which workers are available at each site
2. **Validation**: The system can flag or reject clock-ins at sites the worker is not assigned to, catching errors before they reach payroll

For workers who move between sites, the system should allow them to clock out of one site and clock into another during the same day. The resulting timesheet should show separate entries for each location, making it easy to allocate costs and bill clients accurately.

### Step 4: Set up site-level approval

Approval workflows should match your management structure. If each site has a dedicated supervisor, that person should approve timesheets for their site. If a regional manager oversees multiple sites, they should have a consolidated view with the ability to drill down into individual locations.

The key principle is that the person approving timesheets should have enough knowledge of the site's operations to spot anomalies — a clock-in at 3am on a site that operates 9-to-5, or a twelve-hour shift at a location where the standard is eight hours.

### Step 5: Standardise the process

Even though sites may have different shift patterns and rules, the timesheet process should be the same everywhere:

- Workers clock in and out using the same method (app, kiosk, or both)
- Breaks are recorded in the same way
- Timesheets are submitted and approved on the same cycle
- The same data flows into payroll, regardless of which site it originated from

Standardisation is what makes multi-site data usable. Without it, you are comparing apples to oranges — or worse, trying to aggregate data from incompatible sources.

## Reporting across sites

One of the biggest benefits of a centralised timesheet system is the ability to generate reports that span multiple sites. Useful reports include:

### Hours by site

How many labour hours were worked at each site during a given period? This is the foundation of client billing for service businesses and project costing for construction and maintenance companies.

### Cost by site

If you know the hourly rate for each worker, you can calculate the total labour cost per site. This is essential for understanding profitability — a site that generates revenue but consumes disproportionate labour costs may not be as profitable as it appears.

### Overtime by site

Which sites are generating the most overtime? If one location consistently requires more hours than scheduled, it may be understaffed, inefficiently managed, or subject to scope creep from the client.

### Attendance and punctuality

Are workers arriving on time? Are there patterns of lateness at specific sites? This data helps managers address attendance issues before they affect service quality.

### Break compliance

Are workers at all sites taking their mandated breaks? If a particular site has low break compliance, that warrants investigation — it may indicate understaffing or a culture that discourages rest.

## Common pitfalls

### Allowing each site to do its own thing

The temptation is to let each site manager handle timesheets their own way. This works in the short term but creates chaos at scale. Payroll receives inconsistent data, compliance gaps emerge, and the business loses the ability to compare performance across sites.

### Not updating site configurations

Sites change. New locations are added, old ones are closed, shift patterns evolve, and workers transfer between sites. If the timesheet system does not reflect these changes promptly, the data becomes unreliable. Assign someone to maintain the site register and update configurations as the business evolves.

### Ignoring travel time

For workers who move between sites during the day, travel time may be billable or compensable. If the timesheet system only records time at each site, the gaps between clock-out and clock-in represent lost data. Consider how travel time should be captured — either as a separate entry type or as part of the overall shift.

### Over-complicating the setup

It is easy to over-engineer a multi-site timesheet system with elaborate rules, approval hierarchies, and reporting requirements. Start with the basics: sites, workers, clock-in, breaks, approval, export. Add complexity only when you have a specific, demonstrated need.

## Scaling up

The beauty of a well-designed multi-site timesheet system is that it scales. Adding a new site means creating a new location in the system, defining a geofence, and assigning workers. The existing processes, approval workflows, and reporting structures accommodate the new site automatically.

This scalability matters because multi-site businesses rarely stay static. Contracts are won and lost, new locations open, and the workforce grows. A timesheet system that cannot scale with the business becomes a constraint rather than a tool.

## The payoff

Multi-site timesheet management is one of those operational capabilities that separates well-run businesses from chaotic ones. When it works, payroll is accurate, clients are billed correctly, compliance is documented, and managers have the data they need to make good decisions. When it does not work, every downstream process suffers.

The investment in setting it up properly — centralised system, consistent processes, site-level configuration — pays for itself quickly and continues to deliver value as the business grows.
