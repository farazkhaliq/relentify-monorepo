If you run payroll in the UK, you will encounter three forms repeatedly: the P45, the P60, and the P11D. Each serves a different purpose, and each comes with specific rules about when it must be issued and what it must contain.

Despite being fundamental to UK payroll, these forms are a source of confusion for many employers. This guide explains each one clearly.

## The P45: when an employee leaves

A P45 is issued when an employee leaves your employment. It contains a summary of their pay and tax for the current tax year up to the date they left.

### What is on a P45?

The P45 contains:
- The employee's name and National Insurance number
- Their tax code at the date of leaving
- The date they left your employment
- Their total pay in the tax year up to the leaving date
- The total income tax deducted in the tax year up to the leaving date
- Your employer PAYE reference

The form has four parts. Part 1 is sent to HMRC (this happens automatically through your RTI submission). Parts 1A, 2, and 3 are given to the employee. The employee gives Parts 2 and 3 to their new employer, who uses the information to set up their payroll correctly. Part 1A is for the employee's own records.

### When to issue a P45

You must issue a P45 on or before the employee's last working day. In practice, most employers generate it as part of the final pay run. Do not wait until the employee asks for it — it is your legal obligation to provide it.

If an employee dies while in your employment, you should still complete a P45 and send it to HMRC. Parts 2 and 3 should be sent to the employee's personal representative.

### Why the P45 matters

The P45 ensures continuity of the employee's tax record. Without it, their new employer will not know how much they have earned and how much tax has been deducted so far in the tax year. This can result in the new employer applying an emergency tax code, which usually means the employee overpays tax until HMRC corrects the situation.

For you as the leaving employer, the P45 also confirms that your obligation to deduct tax for that employee has ended.

## The P60: end-of-year summary

A P60 is a summary of an employee's total pay and deductions for the entire tax year (6 April to 5 April). It is issued to every employee who is on your payroll on the last day of the tax year.

### What is on a P60?

The P60 contains:
- The employee's name and National Insurance number
- Their tax code
- Total pay in the tax year
- Total income tax deducted
- National Insurance contributions (employee and employer)
- Any statutory payments (SSP, SMP, SPP, ShPP)
- Student loan deductions, if applicable

### When to issue a P60

You must issue P60s to all employees who were employed on 5 April (the last day of the tax year) by 31 May following the end of the tax year. This gives you just under two months to process the final payroll of the tax year and generate the forms.

P60s can be issued on paper or electronically. If you issue them electronically, the employee must be able to print or save a copy.

Employees who left during the tax year do not receive a P60 — they received a P45 when they left, which serves a similar purpose for the portion of the year they were employed.

### Why the P60 matters

Employees need their P60 for several reasons:
- Filing a self-assessment tax return
- Applying for a mortgage or loan (as proof of income)
- Claiming tax refunds
- Proving their income for benefit applications

As an employer, issuing P60s correctly is a legal requirement. Failure to provide them on time can result in penalties from HMRC.

## The P11D: benefits and expenses

A P11D is used to report benefits in kind and expenses provided to employees that are not processed through payroll. These are non-cash benefits that have a taxable value, such as company cars, private medical insurance, or interest-free loans above a certain threshold.

### What is reported on a P11D?

Common items reported on P11Ds include:

- **Company cars**: The make, model, CO2 emissions, list price, and the dates the car was available
- **Private medical insurance**: The cost of the premium or the cash equivalent value
- **Interest-free or low-interest loans**: If the total outstanding balance exceeds a threshold at any point in the tax year
- **Living accommodation**: If you provide housing for an employee
- **Assets provided for private use**: Such as company equipment used at home
- **Subscriptions or memberships**: Professional memberships, gym memberships, etc., paid by the employer

You do not need to report items that are covered by a dispensation or that are exempt (such as mobile phones provided primarily for business use, or workplace parking).

### When to file P11Ds

P11Ds must be submitted to HMRC by 6 July following the end of the tax year. You must also give a copy to each affected employee by the same date.

Alongside the P11Ds, you must submit a P11D(b) form, which shows the total Class 1A National Insurance due on all benefits reported. The Class 1A NI payment is due by 22 July (or 19 July if paying by post).

### Payrolling benefits in kind

As an alternative to P11Ds, you can "payroll" benefits in kind. This means adding the cash equivalent value of the benefit to the employee's taxable pay each month, so that tax is collected through PAYE in real time.

If you payroll a benefit, you do not need to report it on a P11D. However, you must register with HMRC to payroll benefits before the start of the tax year. You still need to report Class 1A NI on the benefits, but the tax is handled through regular payroll.

Payrolling benefits is increasingly popular because it simplifies administration and avoids the annual P11D filing burden. If you offer company cars, medical insurance, or other significant benefits, it is worth considering.

### Common P11D mistakes

- **Failing to report all benefits**: If you provide any non-cash benefit to an employee, check whether it needs to be reported. The list of exemptions is specific — do not assume something is exempt without checking.
- **Incorrect valuations**: The cash equivalent value of a benefit is not always obvious. Company cars use a specific formula based on list price and CO2 emissions. Medical insurance uses the cost of the premium. Get the valuation right.
- **Missing the deadline**: Late P11D filing attracts penalties. The deadline is 6 July — mark it in your calendar.
- **Forgetting Class 1A NI**: Reporting the benefit on the P11D is only half the job. You also owe employer Class 1A National Insurance on the value of the benefit.

## How these forms fit into your payroll process

The three forms serve different purposes at different points in the employment lifecycle:

| Form | When | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| P45 | Employee leaves | Summarises pay and tax to date |
| P60 | End of tax year | Summarises total pay and tax for the year |
| P11D | After tax year end | Reports non-cash benefits for tax purposes |

All three are generated from your payroll data, which is why maintaining accurate payroll records throughout the year is so important. If your payroll data is correct, generating these forms is largely automatic.

Modern [payroll software](/payroll) handles P45 generation as part of the leaver process, produces P60s at year-end, and can either generate P11Ds or payroll benefits in kind. ## Key takeaways

- **P45**: Issue on or before the employee's last day. Contains pay and tax to date.
- **P60**: Issue by 31 May for all employees on your payroll at 5 April. Contains full-year summary.
- **P11D**: File by 6 July for employees with non-cash benefits. Pay Class 1A NI by 22 July.

Getting these forms right is a basic compliance requirement. Miss a deadline or issue incorrect information, and you face penalties from HMRC and frustrated employees. Build them into your payroll calendar and let your software do the heavy lifting.
