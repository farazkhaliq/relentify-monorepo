# Managing Your Accounts When Selling a Business

Selling a business is one of the most significant financial events in any owner's life. Yet many sellers undermine their own valuation by neglecting their accounts in the months and years before a sale. Buyers scrutinise financial records intensively during due diligence, and messy, incomplete, or questionable accounts can reduce offers, delay completion, or kill deals entirely.

Whether you are planning to sell in six months or five years, the time to get your accounts in order is now. This guide explains what buyers look for and how to present your financial position in the best possible light — honestly and effectively.

## Why Accounts Matter in a Sale

When a buyer evaluates your business, they are fundamentally trying to answer one question: what will this business earn me in the future? Your historical accounts are the primary evidence they use to make that assessment.

Clean, well-organised accounts signal a well-managed business. They suggest that the owner understands their numbers, that the business operates professionally, and that the financial history is reliable. Conversely, disorganised or incomplete accounts raise red flags. Buyers assume that if the financial records are messy, other aspects of the business might be too.

Beyond perception, accounts directly affect valuation. Most business valuations are based on a multiple of earnings (EBITDA or net profit). If your accounts understate true profitability because of sloppy record-keeping or excessive owner benefits run through the business, you will receive a lower price than the business is actually worth.

## Start With a Financial Health Check

Begin by reviewing at least three years of financial statements — profit and loss accounts, balance sheets, and cash flow statements. Look at them through a buyer's eyes.

**Consistency:** Are your revenue and profit trends consistent and explainable? Sudden spikes or drops without clear explanations make buyers nervous. If there is a legitimate reason for a dip (losing a major client, a one-off expense, a pandemic), document it clearly.

**Accuracy:** Do your accounts reconcile? Are bank balances correct? Are all transactions recorded? Any discrepancies discovered during due diligence will erode buyer confidence.

**Completeness:** Are all assets and liabilities recorded? Unrecorded liabilities (pending legal claims, tax disputes, warranty obligations) that surface during due diligence can derail a deal.

If you find issues, address them now rather than hoping buyers will not notice. They will.

## Clean Up Your Books

Preparing accounts for sale often requires what accountants call "normalisation" — adjusting the reported figures to reflect the true economic performance of the business.

**Owner's salary and benefits:** Many owner-managed businesses run personal expenses through the company — cars, insurance, travel, entertainment. These are legitimate tax planning strategies, but they depress reported profit. When selling, you need to identify these add-backs so the buyer can see what the business would earn under normal management.

**One-off expenses:** Legal fees from a dispute, costs of relocating the office, or an extraordinary bad debt should be separately identified. These are not recurring costs and should not be included in normalised earnings.

**Related party transactions:** If you rent the business premises from yourself, employ family members at above-market rates, or buy materials from a company you own, these transactions must be disclosed and adjusted to market rates. Buyers will re-price them anyway, so it is better to do it yourself with clear documentation.

**Depreciation and amortisation:** Review your depreciation policies. Are they reasonable and consistent? Aggressive depreciation might have reduced your tax bill but also reduced your reported profits. Consider whether your policies fairly reflect the useful life of your assets.

## Strengthen Your Balance Sheet

Buyers examine the balance sheet as carefully as the profit and loss account. A strong balance sheet supports a higher valuation.

**Collect outstanding debts:** Chase aged receivables aggressively. Outstanding debts that are months old look like potential write-offs to buyers and reduce the perceived value of your current assets.

**Clear old stock:** Obsolete or slow-moving inventory should be written off or sold at a discount. Buyers will discount the value of questionable stock anyway — better to clean it up and present a realistic figure.

**Settle disputes:** Unresolved legal claims, tax disputes, or supplier disagreements create uncertainty that buyers price in heavily. Resolve whatever you can before going to market.

**Formalise agreements:** Any informal arrangements — verbal leases, handshake deals with suppliers, undocumented employee benefits — should be put in writing. Buyers need certainty, and informal arrangements provide none.

## Prepare for Due Diligence

Due diligence is the process where the buyer (and their advisors) thoroughly examine your business before completing the purchase. Financial due diligence is the most intensive part.

Be prepared to provide:

- Three to five years of management accounts (monthly)
- Three years of audited or reviewed annual accounts
- Tax returns and correspondence with tax authorities
- Bank statements for all accounts
- Aged receivables and payables reports
- Asset registers with depreciation schedules
- Employee records including contracts and benefit details
- Details of all loans, leases, and financial commitments
- Revenue breakdown by customer, product, and geography
- Details of any legal claims or disputes

Organise these documents in a virtual data room — a secure online repository where potential buyers and their advisors can access everything they need. Having everything organised and accessible demonstrates professionalism and accelerates the sale process.

## Revenue Quality Matters

Not all revenue is valued equally. Buyers pay premium multiples for predictable, recurring revenue and discount volatile or concentrated income.

**Recurring revenue** from subscriptions, retainers, or long-term contracts is the most valuable. It provides predictable future cash flow and reduces risk.

**Repeat revenue** from loyal customers who buy regularly but without contractual commitment is the next best. Document customer retention rates and average customer lifetime value.

**One-off revenue** from project-based work or one-time sales is the least valuable because there is no guarantee it will continue after the sale.

**Customer concentration** is a major risk factor. If one customer accounts for more than 20-25% of revenue, buyers will worry about what happens if that customer leaves. Ideally, diversify your customer base well before going to market.

Present your revenue analysis clearly, showing the split between recurring, repeat, and one-off income, and demonstrating that no single customer dominates your sales.

## Cash Flow Is King

Ultimately, what a buyer is purchasing is future cash flow. Your historical cash flow statements demonstrate the business's ability to generate cash consistently.

Ensure your cash flow statements are accurate and clearly presented. Highlight free cash flow — the cash left after operating expenses and capital expenditure. This is the money available to service debt, pay dividends, or reinvest in the business. A strong free cash flow track record significantly increases your valuation multiple.

Address any periods of negative cash flow honestly, with clear explanations. Was it a seasonal dip? An investment phase? A one-off event? Buyers understand that cash flow fluctuates, but they need to understand why.

## Tax Planning Considerations

Selling a business has significant tax implications. The structure of the sale — asset sale versus share sale — affects how much tax you pay and how. Tax treatment varies significantly by jurisdiction, so professional advice is essential.

However, some general principles apply. Keeping your tax affairs in order is critical. Any outstanding tax liabilities, ongoing disputes with tax authorities, or aggressive tax positions that might be challenged will reduce buyer confidence and potentially the sale price.

If you have been running excessive personal expenses through the business, consider cleaning this up well before the sale. Tax advisors can help you restructure things in a way that is both compliant and presentable to buyers.

## Timing the Sale

The financial state of your accounts can influence the optimal timing of your sale.

Ideally, sell when your accounts show an upward trend. Three consecutive years of growing revenue and profit tell a compelling story. If you are in a dip, consider waiting until the trend improves — unless there are strategic reasons to sell now.

Also consider the timing within your financial year. Selling mid-year means preparing interim accounts, which adds complexity. Selling shortly after year-end, with freshly prepared annual accounts, can be cleaner and simpler.

## Working With Advisors

Selling a business is a specialist activity. Assemble a team that includes:

- An accountant experienced in business sales to prepare and normalise your accounts
- A corporate finance advisor or business broker to manage the sale process
- A solicitor experienced in business transactions to handle the legal side
- A tax advisor to structure the sale efficiently

These advisors cost money, but they typically pay for themselves many times over through higher sale prices, smoother processes, and better tax outcomes.

## Start Now, Not Later

The most important advice for any business owner considering a sale is to start preparing early. Cleaning up accounts, diversifying revenue, strengthening the balance sheet, and documenting everything properly takes time — often 12 to 24 months.

The businesses that achieve the highest sale prices are the ones that have been managed with an eventual sale in mind. Their accounts are immaculate, their revenue is diversified, their documentation is comprehensive, and their financial story is clear and compelling.

Even if you are not planning to sell for years, running your business as if you might sell tomorrow is simply good practice. It creates a more valuable, more resilient, and better-managed organisation — whether you ultimately sell it or not.
