Every January, millions of US employers face the same deadline: get W-2 forms into the hands of their employees and filed with the Social Security Administration. It happens every year, and yet it remains one of the most error-prone tasks in payroll administration.

A single mistake on a W-2 — a wrong Social Security number, an incorrect wage figure, a missed filing deadline — can trigger penalties, employee complaints, and follow-up correspondence with the IRS that drags on for months. Understanding the form, the process, and the deadlines is essential for any business with employees.

## What is a W-2 form?

Form W-2, officially titled "Wage and Tax Statement," is the document you use to report annual wages paid to each employee and the taxes withheld from their pay. Every employee who received compensation during the tax year must receive a W-2, regardless of the amount earned.

The W-2 serves two purposes. It gives employees the information they need to file their personal income tax returns. And it provides the Social Security Administration (and through them, the IRS) with the data they need to verify that taxes have been properly reported and paid.

## Who must receive a W-2?

You must issue a W-2 to every individual who was your employee at any point during the tax year and who received wages, tips, or other compensation. This includes:

- Full-time and part-time employees
- Seasonal and temporary workers
- Employees who left during the year (including those who were terminated)
- Deceased employees (issued to the estate or surviving spouse)

Independent contractors do not receive W-2 forms. They receive Form 1099-NEC if you paid them $600 or more during the year. Getting this classification wrong is a common and costly mistake — if the IRS determines that someone you treated as a contractor was actually an employee, you could owe back taxes, penalties, and interest.

## Key boxes on the W-2

The W-2 has multiple boxes, each reporting a specific type of income or withholding. The most important ones are:

- **Box 1 — Wages, tips, other compensation:** Total taxable wages for federal income tax purposes. This excludes pre-tax deductions such as 401(k) contributions and certain health insurance premiums.
- **Box 2 — Federal income tax withheld:** The total federal income tax you deducted from the employee's pay during the year.
- **Box 3 — Social Security wages:** Wages subject to Social Security tax, up to the annual wage base ($168,600 for 2024). This may differ from Box 1 because pre-tax retirement contributions are still subject to Social Security tax.
- **Box 4 — Social Security tax withheld:** The employee's share of Social Security tax (6.2% of Box 3).
- **Box 5 — Medicare wages and tips:** Wages subject to Medicare tax. There is no wage base cap for Medicare.
- **Box 6 — Medicare tax withheld:** The employee's share of Medicare tax (1.45% of Box 5, plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on wages above $200,000).
- **Box 12 — Various codes:** Reports items such as 401(k) contributions (code D), employer-sponsored health coverage cost (code DD), and HSA contributions (code W).
- **Box 13 — Checkboxes:** Indicates whether the employee is a statutory employee, participated in a retirement plan, or received third-party sick pay.

State and local tax information is reported in Boxes 15 through 20.

## Filing deadlines

The deadlines are firm and have been moved up in recent years to help combat tax fraud:

- **January 31:** You must furnish Copy B and Copy C of the W-2 to each employee. You must also file Copy A with the Social Security Administration. These are the same deadline — there is no longer an extended period for SSA filing.

If January 31 falls on a weekend, the deadline moves to the next business day.

Electronic filing is mandatory if you are filing 10 or more W-2s. The SSA's Business Services Online (BSO) portal is the standard method, though many [payroll software](/payroll) providers can file electronically on your behalf.

## How to file

### Paper filing (fewer than 10 forms)

If you file fewer than 10 W-2s, you may submit paper forms. You must use the official red-ink Copy A forms, which can be ordered from the IRS. Photocopies are not accepted by the SSA's scanning equipment.

Mail Copy A of all W-2s along with Form W-3 (the transmittal form that summarizes all W-2s) to the Social Security Administration.

### Electronic filing (10 or more forms)

Register for the SSA's Business Services Online portal at ssa.gov/bso. You can upload W-2 data in the SSA's specified format, or use payroll software that generates the file automatically.

Electronic filing is faster, more accurate, and provides immediate confirmation that your submission was received. Even if you are below the 10-form threshold, electronic filing is recommended.

## Common W-2 mistakes

**Incorrect Social Security numbers.** Verify each employee's SSN against their Social Security card when they are hired. The SSA offers a verification service (SSNVS) that allows you to check names and SSNs before filing.

**Mismatched names.** The name on the W-2 must match the name registered with the SSA. If an employee has changed their name (due to marriage, for example), they should update their records with the SSA before you file.

**Wrong wage amounts.** Reconcile your W-2 totals against your quarterly Form 941 filings before submitting. The IRS cross-checks these amounts, and discrepancies trigger correspondence.

**Forgetting to include fringe benefits.** Taxable fringe benefits — such as personal use of a company vehicle, group term life insurance over $50,000, or certain gift cards — must be included in Box 1. Failing to report them understates the employee's taxable income.

**Filing late.** Late filing penalties range from $60 to $310 per form, depending on how late the filing is, up to a maximum of $3,783,000 per year for large businesses. If the failure is due to intentional disregard, the penalty is $630 per form with no maximum.

## Correcting a W-2

If you discover an error after filing, you must submit Form W-2c (Corrected Wage and Tax Statement) along with Form W-3c (Transmittal of Corrected Wage and Tax Statements). The correction should be filed as soon as the error is discovered.

Common situations requiring a W-2c:

- Incorrect employee name or SSN
- Wrong wage or tax amounts
- Missing or incorrect Box 12 codes
- State or local tax reporting errors

Provide the corrected form to the employee as well as the SSA. The employee may need to file an amended personal tax return (Form 1040-X) if the correction changes their taxable income.

## State W-2 filing requirements

In addition to the federal filing with the SSA, most states require you to file W-2 data with the state tax authority. The deadlines, formats, and submission methods vary by state. Some states participate in the Combined Federal/State Filing Program, which allows you to submit state data through the SSA along with your federal filing.

Check the specific requirements for every state where you have employees. Multi-state employers often find this the most time-consuming part of the W-2 process.

## Keeping records

The IRS recommends keeping copies of W-2s and related payroll records for at least four years after the due date of the return or the date the tax was paid, whichever is later. Some states require longer retention periods. A safe practice is to keep payroll records for at least seven years.

Store records securely, whether in paper or electronic format. W-2s contain sensitive personal information including Social Security numbers, and a data breach could expose you to identity theft claims and regulatory action.

## Simplifying the process

The W-2 filing process is straightforward in principle but easy to get wrong in practice. The best way to avoid errors, penalties, and employee complaints is to maintain accurate payroll records throughout the year — not just at filing time.

Modern [payroll software](/payroll) generates W-2s automatically from your payroll data, handles electronic filing, and flags potential errors before submission. For businesses with more than a handful of employees, it is one of the most worthwhile investments you can make.
