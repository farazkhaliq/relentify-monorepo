Compliance is one of the non-negotiable responsibilities of any letting agency. Every property under management has a set of legal requirements — certificates that must be current, inspections that must be conducted on schedule, documents that must be provided to tenants. Missing any of these can result in fines, prosecution, invalidation of tenancy agreements, and severe reputational damage.

For an agency managing a handful of properties, keeping track of compliance deadlines might be manageable with a calendar and some diligence. For an agency managing hundreds, it is a systemic challenge that requires a proper system.

A CRM with compliance tracking capabilities turns a complex, high-stakes administrative burden into a managed, automated process.

## The compliance landscape

The specific compliance requirements vary by jurisdiction, but the categories are broadly consistent across most markets.

**Gas safety.** Properties with gas appliances require annual safety inspections by a qualified engineer. The resulting certificate must be provided to tenants at the start of the tenancy and within a defined period of the inspection.

**Electrical safety.** Electrical installations must be inspected at regular intervals, typically every five years, by a qualified electrician. Any remedial work identified must be completed within specified timeframes.

**Energy performance.** An energy performance certificate is required before a property is marketed for rent. These certificates are valid for ten years but must meet minimum efficiency standards that may change over time.

**Smoke and carbon monoxide detection.** Properties must have working smoke alarms on every floor and carbon monoxide alarms in rooms with solid fuel appliances. Testing requirements vary by jurisdiction.

**Legionella risk assessment.** A risk assessment for legionella bacteria should be carried out for each property, with preventive measures implemented where risks are identified.

**Fire safety.** For HMOs and certain other property types, additional fire safety requirements apply, including fire doors, fire alarms, and emergency lighting.

**Licensing.** Some properties require a licence — mandatory licensing for larger HMOs, selective licensing in certain areas, or additional licensing schemes. Licences have expiry dates and renewal requirements.

Each of these has its own timeline, its own documentation requirements, and its own consequences for non-compliance.

## Why manual tracking fails

The challenge is not understanding what needs to be done. It is ensuring that it gets done, on time, for every property, every time. This is where manual systems — spreadsheets, calendar reminders, paper files — consistently fall short.

Spreadsheets require someone to check them regularly and to act on what they find. In a busy agency, this checking happens sporadically. Calendar reminders are tied to individual people — if that person is on leave or leaves the company, the reminder goes with them. Paper files are difficult to search, easy to misfile, and impossible to query at scale.

The result is predictable: some certificates get renewed on time, some are renewed late, and some are missed entirely. The missed ones are usually discovered during an audit, an inspection, or — worst of all — after an incident.

## Building a compliance system in your CRM

A CRM-based compliance system centralises every certificate, inspection, and deadline in one place, linked to the relevant property and landlord. Here is how to structure it.

### Document the requirements

For each property type in your portfolio, document the compliance requirements that apply. A standard single-let residential property has a different set of requirements from an HMO, which has different requirements from a commercial property.

Create a compliance template for each property type that lists every required certificate, the inspection frequency, the responsible party, and the lead time needed for renewal.

### Record every certificate

For each property, record every current compliance certificate — the type, the date of issue, the expiry date, and the name of the engineer or inspector who carried it out. Attach a digital copy of the certificate to the property record.

This creates a single, searchable repository of compliance documents. When a landlord asks about the status of their gas safety certificate, or when a tenant requests a copy of the EPC, the answer is a few clicks away.

### Set automated reminders

The key to compliance is timing. You do not want to renew a certificate on the day it expires — you want to start the renewal process weeks or months in advance, so there is time to schedule the inspection, deal with any remedial work, and issue the new certificate before the old one lapses.

Set automated reminders at multiple intervals. A first reminder at 90 days before expiry gives you plenty of time. A second at 60 days adds urgency. A third at 30 days escalates to a manager. A fourth at 14 days should trigger emergency action.

Each reminder should generate a task in the CRM, assigned to the responsible team member, with the property and landlord details attached.

### Track remedial work

Some inspections — particularly electrical — may identify issues that require remedial work. The CRM should track these issues separately, with their own deadlines and task assignments. Remedial work often has a shorter deadline than the inspection itself, and missing it can be just as serious as missing the initial inspection.

### Monitor the dashboard

Your compliance dashboard should provide a summary of the compliance status across your entire portfolio. How many certificates are current? How many are due for renewal in the next 30, 60, and 90 days? Are there any that have already expired?

This dashboard should be one of the first things the compliance manager checks each morning. It should also be reviewed in weekly team meetings, ensuring that compliance is not treated as one person's responsibility but as a shared priority.

## Landlord communication

Compliance is a shared responsibility between the agency and the landlord. While the agency typically manages the process, the landlord is legally responsible for ensuring that their property is compliant.

Your CRM should support clear communication with landlords about compliance matters. When a certificate is approaching expiry, the landlord should be informed — both as a courtesy and to ensure they understand their responsibilities.

If remedial work is needed, the landlord should be presented with the options and the costs. If a landlord refuses to authorise necessary compliance work, the CRM should record that refusal and the advice given — protecting the agency in the event of a later dispute.

## Regulatory changes

Compliance requirements change over time. Minimum energy efficiency standards increase. New inspection requirements are introduced. Existing requirements are extended to additional property types.

When regulations change, your CRM's compliance templates need to be updated to reflect the new requirements. This is one area where a purpose-built property management CRM has a significant advantage over a generic tool — the vendor is more likely to update compliance templates in response to regulatory changes, reducing the burden on the agency.

## The cost of non-compliance

The financial penalties for non-compliance vary by jurisdiction and offence, but they are consistently severe. Fines for missing a gas safety inspection can run to thousands. Failure to protect a deposit correctly can cost up to three times the deposit amount. Letting a property that falls below the minimum energy efficiency standard can result in substantial penalties.

Beyond financial penalties, non-compliance damages the agency's reputation. A landlord whose property is found to be non-compliant will blame the agency — and rightly so, if the agency was responsible for managing compliance. A tenant who discovers that required safety certificates are missing will lose trust in both the agency and the landlord.

## Building a culture of compliance

Compliance tracking is a tool, not a solution. The tool is only effective if the agency builds a culture where compliance is taken seriously by everyone, not just the person responsible for checking the spreadsheet.

This means regular training, clear procedures, and accountability. It means treating a compliance near-miss — a certificate renewed just before it lapsed — as seriously as an actual lapse, because the process that allowed the near-miss could just as easily have produced a failure.

CRM platforms like [Relentify](/crm) provide the infrastructure for compliance tracking — automated reminders, document storage, dashboard monitoring, and audit trails. But the infrastructure only works if the agency commits to using it consistently and acting on what it reveals.

Compliance is not optional, it is not glamorous, and it is not something that can be left to chance. A systematic approach, powered by a capable CRM, is the only way to manage it reliably at scale.
