When an employee calls in sick, your immediate concern is probably the impact on the team and the workload. But there is also a payroll obligation to think about: Statutory Sick Pay. SSP is a legal requirement for most UK employers, and getting it right means understanding who qualifies, how much to pay, and how long the obligation lasts.

This guide covers everything you need to know about SSP as an employer.

## What is Statutory Sick Pay?

Statutory Sick Pay is a minimum amount that employers must pay to employees who are off work due to illness. It is not a benefit paid by the government — you pay it through your payroll, just like normal wages.

SSP is payable for up to 28 weeks per period of sickness. After that, the employee may be eligible for benefits paid directly by the state.

The current weekly rate of SSP is set by the government and updated each tax year. It is a flat rate — the same regardless of the employee's normal earnings — as long as the employee earns above the Lower Earnings Limit.

## Who qualifies for SSP?

To qualify for SSP, an employee must meet all of the following conditions:

- **They are classed as an employee**: Self-employed workers, agency workers (paid by the agency), and directors without a contract of employment may not qualify.
- **They have done some work under their contract**: SSP begins from the first day of employment, provided they have actually started work.
- **They are sick for four or more consecutive days**: This includes weekends and non-working days. The first three qualifying days are "waiting days" during which SSP is not paid.
- **They earn at least the Lower Earnings Limit**: If their average weekly earnings are below this threshold, they do not qualify for SSP. You should direct them to claim benefits directly from the government instead.
- **They have notified you of their sickness**: They must follow your notification procedure, or if you do not have one, inform you within seven days.

Employees on maternity leave, those within a disqualification period, or those who have already received 28 weeks of SSP in a linked period of sickness do not qualify for further SSP.

## Qualifying days

SSP is only paid for "qualifying days" — the days on which the employee would normally work. If an employee works Monday to Friday, their qualifying days are Monday to Friday. If they work a shift pattern, only the days they are scheduled to work count.

The first three qualifying days of any period of sickness are waiting days. SSP is not paid for these. From the fourth qualifying day onwards, SSP is payable.

If an employee has been sick within the last eight weeks and their current sickness is linked to the previous one (meaning the gap between the two was eight weeks or less), the waiting days from the first period of sickness carry over. This means SSP starts immediately in the second period.

## How to calculate SSP

SSP calculation involves a few steps:

1. **Determine qualifying days**: Identify which days in the week are qualifying days for the employee.
2. **Identify the period of incapacity for work (PIW)**: The PIW is the block of four or more consecutive days of sickness.
3. **Apply waiting days**: No SSP for the first three qualifying days (unless linked to a previous PIW).
4. **Calculate the daily rate**: Divide the weekly SSP rate by the number of qualifying days in the week.
5. **Pay SSP for each qualifying day of sickness**: After the waiting days, pay the daily rate for each qualifying day the employee is absent.

For example, if an employee works five days a week and the weekly SSP rate is £116.75:

- Daily SSP rate: £116.75 ÷ 5 = £23.35
- If they are off sick for two weeks (10 qualifying days), SSP is paid for 7 days (10 minus 3 waiting days)
- Total SSP: 7 × £23.35 = £163.45

SSP is subject to income tax and National Insurance deductions, just like normal pay. It should be processed through your payroll and reported via RTI.

## Evidence of sickness

For the first seven days of sickness, the employee can self-certify. They do not need a doctor's note — a simple statement confirming they were unwell is sufficient. Many employers provide a self-certification form, but there is no legal requirement for a specific format.

From the eighth day of sickness, the employee must provide a fit note (also known as a Statement of Fitness for Work) from their doctor. The fit note may say the employee is:

- **Not fit for work**: They should remain off work.
- **May be fit for work**: The doctor suggests adjustments that might allow the employee to return, such as amended duties, altered hours, or workplace adaptations.

If the fit note says "may be fit for work" and you cannot accommodate the suggested adjustments, treat the employee as not fit for work and continue paying SSP.

## Occupational sick pay

Many employers offer occupational sick pay that is more generous than SSP — for example, full pay for a certain number of weeks, followed by half pay, then SSP only.

If you offer occupational sick pay, SSP is included within it (not paid on top). So if your policy offers full pay for four weeks, the SSP entitlement is covered within that full pay. You only need to pay SSP separately once the employee has exhausted their occupational sick pay entitlement.

Your sick pay policy should clearly explain what employees receive during sickness absence, including the relationship between occupational sick pay and SSP.

## When SSP ends

SSP stops in any of these circumstances:

- The employee returns to work
- The employee has received 28 weeks of SSP in the current period of sickness (or linked periods)
- The employee's contract of employment ends
- The employee is taken into legal custody
- The employee starts receiving Maternity Allowance or Statutory Maternity Pay

When SSP ends after 28 weeks, you must issue the employee with an SSP1 form, which they can use to apply for Employment and Support Allowance (ESA) or Universal Credit from the government.

## Record keeping

You must keep records of SSP payments for a minimum of three years. Your records should include:

- Dates of each period of sickness for every employee
- The qualifying days for each employee
- Payments of SSP made, including dates and amounts
- Any periods of sickness where SSP was not paid, and the reason
- Fit notes and self-certification forms

Good record keeping is essential not just for compliance, but for managing absence patterns. If an employee has frequent short absences, your records will show the pattern clearly, allowing you to address it through your absence management process.

## Processing SSP through payroll

SSP should be included in your normal payroll run. On the pay period that covers the employee's absence:

- Replace their normal pay with SSP for the days they were absent (unless you offer occupational sick pay that exceeds SSP)
- Deduct income tax and National Insurance from SSP as normal
- Report SSP through your RTI submission

Your [payroll software](/payroll) should handle SSP calculations automatically once you input the sickness dates. Most systems will calculate the waiting days, apply the correct daily rate, and include SSP in the employee's payslip alongside any normal pay for days they did work.

## Employees who do not qualify

If an employee does not qualify for SSP — for example, because their earnings are below the Lower Earnings Limit — you must tell them within seven days of their sickness notification. Provide them with an SSP1 form so they can claim alternative benefits.

You cannot refuse SSP simply because you believe the employee is not genuinely sick. If they meet the qualifying conditions and provide the required evidence, you must pay SSP. If you suspect the employee is not being honest, address it through your absence management and disciplinary procedures, not by withholding SSP.

## Key points for employers

SSP is one of those obligations that most employers encounter infrequently enough that it feels unfamiliar each time. The key takeaways are:

- SSP is your responsibility to pay — it comes from your payroll, not the government
- The qualifying conditions are specific — check them each time
- Waiting days apply to the first three qualifying days
- Evidence requirements change at day eight (self-certification to fit note)
- Keep records for at least three years
- Process SSP through your normal payroll, subject to tax and NI
