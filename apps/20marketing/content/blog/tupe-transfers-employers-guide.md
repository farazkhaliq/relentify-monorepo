TUPE — the Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations — is one of the most complex areas of UK employment law. It applies when a business or part of a business transfers from one employer to another, and it exists to protect employees whose employer changes as a result.

If you are buying a business, taking over a contract, or outsourcing a function, TUPE may apply. This guide explains what that means in practice.

## When does TUPE apply?

TUPE applies in two main situations:

### Business transfers
When a business or part of a business transfers to a new owner. This includes:

- Buying a business as a going concern
- Merging two businesses
- Transferring a department or function from one company to another

The key test is whether an "economic entity" — an organised grouping of resources that has the objective of pursuing an economic activity — transfers and retains its identity after the transfer.

### Service provision changes
When a service currently provided by one employer is taken over by another. This includes:

- **Outsourcing**: A company contracts out a function previously done in-house (e.g., cleaning, IT support, catering)
- **Insourcing**: A company brings a previously outsourced function back in-house
- **Retendering**: A contract is awarded to a new provider, replacing the current one

For a service provision change to trigger TUPE, there must be an organised grouping of employees whose principal purpose is to carry out the activities being transferred.

### When TUPE does not apply

TUPE does not apply to:
- The transfer of shares (the employer remains the same company)
- The supply of goods (as opposed to services)
- One-off or short-term contracts

The question of whether TUPE applies is not always straightforward. If you are in doubt, seek legal advice before proceeding.

## What happens when TUPE applies?

### Employees transfer automatically

The most important effect of TUPE is that affected employees transfer automatically from the old employer (the transferor) to the new employer (the transferee). You do not need to issue new contracts. The employees' terms and conditions — including pay, holiday, working hours, and length of service — transfer intact.

From the transfer date, the new employer is responsible for the employees as if it had always been their employer. This includes any liabilities arising from the old employer's actions, such as outstanding pay, unfair dismissal claims, or discrimination claims.

### Terms and conditions are preserved

Employees' existing terms and conditions are protected. You cannot change them simply because of the transfer. If you want to change terms, you must have a valid reason unrelated to the transfer, or the change must be for an economic, technical, or organisational reason (known as an ETO reason) that involves a change in the workforce.

Changes that are solely or mainly because of the transfer are void, even if the employee agrees to them.

### Continuity of employment

The employees' continuous service is preserved. Their service with the old employer counts as service with the new employer for all purposes, including:
- Statutory redundancy pay calculations
- Unfair dismissal protection
- Holiday entitlement accrual
- Long-service benefits

### Dismissals connected to the transfer

Dismissing an employee because of the transfer (or for a reason connected to it) is automatically unfair. The only exception is if the dismissal is for an ETO reason that involves a change in the workforce — for example, a genuine redundancy situation arising from the reorganisation of the combined business.

## Obligations of the outgoing employer (transferor)

The outgoing employer must:

### Provide employee liability information
At least 28 days before the transfer, provide the incoming employer with specified information about each affected employee, including:

- Name, age, and length of service
- Terms and conditions of employment
- Details of any disciplinary or grievance proceedings in the last two years
- Details of any legal actions brought by the employee in the last two years
- Details of any collective agreements that apply

Failure to provide this information on time can result in a compensation award of at least a minimum fixed amount per employee.

### Inform and consult
Both the outgoing and incoming employers must inform and consult with employee representatives about the transfer. The information provided must include:

- The fact that the transfer is happening and the approximate date
- The reasons for the transfer
- The legal, economic, and social implications for affected employees
- Any measures the employer plans to take in connection with the transfer

If there are no existing employee representatives, you must arrange for representatives to be elected.

Consultation must take place with a view to reaching agreement. If the new employer plans to make changes to the workforce (such as redundancies), the consultation must cover those measures.

### Transfer records and data
Hand over relevant employee records to the incoming employer, including [payroll](/payroll) data, pension information, and any other records needed for a smooth transition.

## Obligations of the incoming employer (transferee)

The incoming employer must:

### Accept the transferring employees
You cannot pick and choose which employees to accept. All employees assigned to the transferring entity or service come across automatically.

### Maintain terms and conditions
Honour the existing terms and conditions of employment. If an employee had 25 days of holiday with the old employer, they have 25 days with you.

### Set up payroll
Add the transferring employees to your [payroll](/payroll) system, ensuring their pay, tax codes, pension arrangements, and other deductions continue correctly from the transfer date. Modern payroll platforms like Relentify can accommodate mid-year transfers, preserving year-to-date figures and ensuring continuity.

### Handle pensions
Occupational pension rights do not transfer under TUPE (this is a specific exception in the regulations). However, the incoming employer must offer the transferring employees a qualifying pension arrangement.

### Inform and consult
If you plan to take any measures affecting the transferring employees (such as changing their work location, reorganising teams, or making redundancies), you must consult with employee representatives before the transfer.

## Practical tips for managing a TUPE transfer

### Start early
TUPE compliance takes time. Begin the process as early as possible — at least two to three months before the planned transfer date.

### Get the employee data
Request the employee liability information from the outgoing employer promptly. You need this data to set up payroll, assess costs, and plan for the integration of new employees.

### Communicate clearly
Employees going through a TUPE transfer are anxious about their jobs, terms, and future. Clear, honest communication reduces anxiety and builds trust. Tell them what is happening, when, and what it means for them.

### Do not rush changes
Resist the temptation to immediately harmonise terms and conditions. Changes connected to the transfer are void. Plan any changes carefully, ensuring they are for a genuine ETO reason and implemented with proper consultation.

### Keep records
Document every step of the TUPE process — the information provided, the consultation meetings, the decisions made, and the communications sent. This protects you if any aspect of the transfer is later challenged.

## Common mistakes

### Assuming TUPE does not apply
Many employers assume TUPE only applies to large acquisitions. In fact, it can apply to relatively small service contracts. If in doubt, assume it applies and take legal advice.

### Changing terms immediately after transfer
This is the most common legal pitfall. Even if the employee agrees to the change, it can be void if connected to the transfer.

### Failing to consult
The duty to inform and consult is often underestimated. Failure to consult properly can result in compensation awards of up to 13 weeks' pay per affected employee.

### Ignoring pension obligations
While occupational pension rights do not transfer, you still have an obligation to provide a qualifying pension. Ignoring this creates a compliance gap.

### Not updating payroll
Transferring employees must be paid correctly from day one with the new employer. Setting up payroll late or incorrectly creates immediate problems and erodes trust.

## The bottom line

TUPE exists to protect employees when their employer changes. As either the outgoing or incoming employer, your obligations are specific and legally enforceable. Start early, communicate openly, consult properly, and get expert advice when the situation is complex. A well-managed TUPE transfer sets the foundation for a successful working relationship with your new team members.
