Support rarely happens in isolation. A billing question might need input from the finance team. A technical bug might require an engineer to investigate. A refund decision might need a manager's approval. In every case, the support agent needs to bring someone else into the conversation.

The challenge is doing this without confusing the customer. If you CC a colleague on the ticket reply, the customer sees the internal back-and-forth. If you send a separate email, the context is disconnected from the ticket. If you walk over to someone's desk (or ping them on Slack), the conversation happens off the record entirely.

Side conversations and internal notes solve this by giving agents structured ways to collaborate within the ticket without the customer ever seeing the internal discussion.

## Internal notes: the simplest collaboration tool

Internal notes are messages attached to a ticket that are visible only to your team. The customer never sees them.

### When to use internal notes

**Documentation.** When you investigate an issue, document what you found in an internal note. "Checked the billing system — customer was charged twice on March 15 due to a payment gateway retry. Refund of $49 is appropriate."

**Handoff context.** When you need to pass a ticket to another agent, an internal note provides context so they do not have to start from scratch. "Customer is frustrated — they have contacted us three times about this. The issue is a sync failure between our system and their ERP. Engineering ticket #5423 is tracking the fix."

**Decision tracking.** When a manager approves a refund or a policy exception, the approval should be recorded in an internal note for audit purposes.

**Status updates for the team.** "Waiting on the customer to provide their order number. Will follow up tomorrow if we do not hear back."

### Best practices for internal notes

**Be specific.** "Spoke to engineering" is less useful than "Spoke to Sarah in engineering — the fix will be deployed in version 4.2.3, expected next Tuesday."

**Timestamp your actions.** "Called the customer at 2:15 PM, went to voicemail. Left message requesting callback."

**Keep them professional.** Internal notes are not private diaries. They may be reviewed during quality assurance, audits, or if the ticket is escalated. Write them as you would any professional communication.

**Use them proactively.** Do not wait until you need to hand off the ticket. Adding notes as you work creates a running record that helps anyone who touches the ticket later.

## Side conversations: bringing others into the ticket

Internal notes are great for one-way documentation, but they do not facilitate discussion. Side conversations take collaboration further by allowing agents to start a separate thread within the ticket — typically via email, Slack, or an internal messaging tool — that is linked to the ticket but invisible to the customer.

### How side conversations work

From within the ticket, the agent initiates a side conversation and selects the recipient — a colleague, a team, a manager, or an external partner. The message includes context from the ticket (automatically or manually) and is sent through the chosen channel.

The recipient replies to the side conversation. Their response is attached to the ticket as part of the side conversation thread, not the customer-facing thread. The agent can see both threads and decides what, if anything, to communicate back to the customer.

### Common side conversation scenarios

**Consulting engineering about a bug.** The agent shares the error details and reproduction steps with the engineering team. Engineering investigates and responds with a diagnosis and ETA. The agent then translates the technical response into customer-friendly language for the reply.

**Getting manager approval.** The agent describes the situation and the proposed resolution (a refund, a credit, or a policy exception). The manager approves or suggests an alternative. The agent proceeds with the approved action.

**Contacting a third-party vendor.** If the issue involves an external partner (a payment processor, a shipping company, a software integration), the agent can email the vendor from within the ticket. The vendor's response is captured in the side conversation, keeping everything in one place.

**Looping in a specialist.** A general agent encounters an issue outside their expertise. Instead of reassigning the entire ticket, they start a side conversation with a specialist to get guidance while maintaining ownership of the customer relationship.

### Advantages over reassignment

The traditional approach to getting help on a ticket is to reassign it to another team. This works, but it has drawbacks:

- The customer may receive a response from a different person, breaking continuity
- The new assignee may not have the full context and needs time to catch up
- Response time increases because of the handoff overhead
- The original agent loses visibility into the ticket

Side conversations avoid all of these problems. The original agent stays in control of the customer relationship. The specialist contributes their expertise without being burdened with full ticket ownership. The customer experiences a seamless interaction with one person, unaware of the collaboration happening behind the scenes.

## Structuring your internal collaboration

### Define when to use notes vs side conversations

- **Internal notes** — Use for documentation, status updates, and information that does not require a response
- **Side conversations** — Use when you need input, approval, or action from someone else

### Create clear escalation paths

Agents should know exactly who to loop in for different types of issues:

- Billing questions requiring exceptions → Finance manager
- Technical bugs → Engineering team lead
- Refund requests above a threshold → Support manager
- Legal or compliance questions → Legal team
- Third-party integration issues → Partner support contact

Document these paths in your team handbook so agents do not waste time figuring out who to contact.

### Set expectations for response times

Internal side conversations need their own SLA expectations. If an agent is waiting on engineering to diagnose a bug, how long should they wait before following up? If a manager has not approved a refund after four hours, what is the escalation path?

Without these expectations, side conversations can stall indefinitely, delaying the customer's resolution.

### Keep the customer informed

While the internal discussion is happening, do not leave the customer in the dark. If you are waiting on a colleague's input, let the customer know: "I am consulting with our technical team to get you the most accurate answer. I expect to have an update within a few hours."

This is far better than silence while the side conversation unfolds behind the scenes.

## Avoiding common pitfalls

### Do not let side conversations become bottlenecks

If tickets routinely stall because agents are waiting on side conversation responses, the problem is not the feature — it is the process. Common fixes include:

- Assigning side conversation response targets (respond within 2 hours)
- Using automation to remind the recipient if they have not responded
- Empowering agents to make decisions independently for lower-stakes issues

### Do not over-rely on side conversations

Not every question needs a side conversation. If the agent can answer the question themselves with a quick knowledge base search or by checking the customer's account, a side conversation is unnecessary overhead.

### Keep side conversations on-record

The whole point of side conversations within a helpdesk is that they are documented and linked to the ticket. If agents are collaborating on tickets via Slack DMs, email threads outside the system, or verbal conversations, that context is lost.

Encourage agents to use the built-in side conversation feature rather than ad hoc communication channels. Platforms like [Relentify Connect](/connect) support side conversations that are fully integrated into the ticket record, ensuring nothing is lost.

### Watch the customer-facing thread carefully

When switching between the internal and external views of a ticket, be careful not to accidentally send an internal message to the customer. This is a common mistake that can be embarrassing or damaging. Most helpdesks use clear visual indicators (colour coding, labels) to distinguish internal and external messages — pay attention to them.

## Measuring collaboration effectiveness

Track these indicators to understand how well your team collaborates within tickets:

- **Side conversation frequency** — How often are agents looping in colleagues? Very high rates might indicate training gaps. Very low rates might indicate agents are not seeking help when they should.
- **Side conversation resolution time** — How long does it take for the internal party to respond?
- **Customer wait time during collaboration** — How long are customers waiting while the internal discussion happens?
- **First contact resolution rate** — A well-functioning collaboration system should improve this metric because agents get expert input without reassigning the ticket

## The outcome

Effective internal collaboration is invisible to the customer. They contact you with a problem and receive a thoughtful, accurate response from one consistent person. They do not know that three people contributed to that response, that a manager approved the resolution, or that an engineer diagnosed the root cause.

That is the goal. The customer sees one seamless conversation. Your team, behind the scenes, has all the tools they need to collaborate quickly, keep everything on the record, and resolve issues that no single agent could handle alone.
