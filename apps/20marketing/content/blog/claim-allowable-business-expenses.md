As a business owner, every pound you spend on allowable business expenses reduces your taxable profit — and therefore reduces the amount of tax you pay. Yet many businesses, especially in their early years, miss legitimate deductions simply because they did not know they could claim them.

The general rule is simple: an expense is allowable if it is incurred "wholly and exclusively" for business purposes. But applying this rule to specific situations is where it gets interesting. This guide provides a comprehensive list of common allowable expenses and guidance on how to claim them correctly.

## The golden rule

HMRC allows you to deduct expenses that are incurred "wholly and exclusively" for the purposes of your trade or business. This means:

- The expense must have a genuine business purpose
- If the expense has both a personal and business element, you may only claim the business proportion
- The expense must be revenue in nature (not capital — capital spending is handled through capital allowances)

If you are unsure whether an expense qualifies, ask yourself: "Would I have incurred this cost if I were not running this business?" If the answer is no, it is likely allowable.

## Office and premises costs

- **Rent** for business premises
- **Business rates** and council tax (business portion)
- **Electricity, gas, and water** for business premises
- **Property insurance**
- **Office maintenance and repairs** (but not improvements — improvements are capital)
- **Cleaning costs**
- **Security costs**

### Working from home

If you work from home, you can claim a proportion of your household costs:

**Simplified method:** Claim a flat rate based on hours worked per month:
- 25-50 hours: £10/month
- 51-100 hours: £18/month
- 101+ hours: £26/month

**Actual costs method:** Calculate the business proportion of your actual costs (mortgage interest or rent, utilities, insurance, broadband). This is typically based on the number of rooms used for business and the time spent working.

The actual costs method usually gives a higher deduction but requires more record-keeping.

## Travel and transport

- **Public transport** for business journeys (train, bus, taxi)
- **Business mileage** at HMRC approved rates (45p/mile first 10,000, then 25p/mile)
- **Parking fees** for business travel
- **Congestion charges** for business journeys
- **Hotel accommodation** for business trips
- **Flights** for business travel
- **Subsistence** — Meals during business travel (must be away from your normal place of work)

### What does not qualify

- Commuting from home to your regular workplace
- Any private mileage or travel
- Fines and penalties (parking tickets, speeding fines)

## Staff costs

- **Salaries and wages**
- **Employer's National Insurance contributions**
- **Employer pension contributions**
- **Staff training** directly related to business activities
- **Recruitment costs** — Advertising, agency fees
- **Staff welfare** — Reasonable staff entertainment, Christmas parties (up to £150 per head annually)
- **Temporary staff and subcontractor costs**

## Professional and financial services

- **Accountancy fees** — Tax return preparation, bookkeeping, financial advice
- **Legal fees** — Related to business operations (not capital purchases or disputes about personal matters)
- **Professional subscriptions** — Membership of professional bodies relevant to your trade
- **Consultancy fees** — Business advice, marketing strategy, IT consultancy
- **Bank charges and interest** — On business accounts and business loans
- **Credit card fees** — Annual fees and interest on business credit cards
- **Debt collection costs**

## Marketing and sales

- **Advertising** — Online ads, print advertising, social media marketing
- **Website costs** — Hosting, domain names, design, content
- **Business cards and stationery**
- **Trade shows and exhibitions** — Fees, stand costs, materials
- **Promotional materials** — Brochures, flyers, samples
- **PR costs**
- **Email marketing platforms**

## Technology and software

- **Software subscriptions** — Accounting software, CRM, project management tools
- **Cloud services** — Hosting, storage, SaaS subscriptions
- **Phone costs** — Business phone line, business proportion of personal phone
- **Internet** — Business broadband, or business proportion of home broadband
- **Domain names and hosting**
- **Computer consumables** — Printer ink, cables, peripherals under the capital threshold

Tools like [Relentify](/accounting) are themselves tax-deductible business expenses — software subscriptions that help you run your business are fully allowable.

## Insurance

- **Professional indemnity insurance**
- **Public liability insurance**
- **Employer's liability insurance** (compulsory if you have employees)
- **Business contents insurance**
- **Business interruption insurance**
- **Key person insurance** (if it protects business revenue)
- **Cyber insurance**

## Equipment and assets (capital allowances)

Capital purchases are not deducted as expenses. Instead, you claim capital allowances:

- **Computers and laptops**
- **Office furniture** — Desks, chairs, shelving
- **Machinery and equipment**
- **Vehicles** (with restrictions on personal use)
- **Tools**

The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) allows you to deduct up to £1 million of qualifying capital expenditure in the year of purchase — more than enough for most small businesses.

## Training and development

- **Training courses** directly related to your current business activities
- **Professional development** — Conferences, seminars, workshops
- **Books and publications** related to your business

### What does not qualify

- Training to enter a new field (not related to your current trade)
- Courses primarily for personal interest

## Bad debts

If a client does not pay their invoice and you have made reasonable efforts to collect, you can write off the debt as an allowable expense. The key requirements:

- The income was previously included in your taxable profits
- The debt is genuinely irrecoverable
- You have made reasonable efforts to collect

## Charity donations

- **Donations to registered charities** — Deductible for corporation tax purposes
- **Gift Aid donations** — Additional tax relief may be available
- **Sponsorship** — If there is a commercial benefit (advertising, promotion), it may be treated as marketing

## Commonly overlooked expenses

Many business owners miss these legitimate deductions:

- **Bank charges and interest** on business accounts
- **Professional subscriptions** and membership fees
- **Small tools and equipment** below the capital threshold
- **Protective clothing and uniforms** required for work
- **Business use of personal phone** (claim the business proportion)
- **Home office costs** (even the simplified flat rate adds up)
- **Software subscriptions** — Every business tool you pay for
- **Postage and courier costs**
- **Stationery and printing**
- **Business books and publications**

## How to claim properly

### Keep receipts

Every expense needs a receipt or invoice as evidence. Digital copies are acceptable — photograph receipts as soon as you receive them.

### Categorise accurately

Record expenses in the correct category in your accounting software. This makes tax filing easier and ensures you claim the right amounts.

### Separate personal and business

If an expense is partly personal and partly business, calculate and claim only the business proportion. Document how you calculated the split.

### Claim in the right period

Expenses should be claimed in the period they relate to, not when you happen to get around to recording them.

### Be reasonable

HMRC applies a "reasonableness" test. A business meal for two at a reasonable restaurant is fine. A five-course dinner for ten at a luxury venue may attract questions. Claim what is genuinely necessary for your business.

## What you cannot claim

- **Client entertainment** — Meals, drinks, and events for clients are not deductible (this is a common misconception)
- **Personal expenses** — Clothing (unless it is a uniform or protective), personal travel, personal phone use
- **Fines and penalties** — Parking fines, HMRC penalties, court fines
- **Political donations**
- **Provisions for future expenditure** (you can only claim costs actually incurred)

## The bottom line

Claiming all your allowable expenses is not aggressive tax planning — it is simply paying the correct amount of tax. Every legitimate expense you miss is money you are giving away unnecessarily.

Set up a good system for capturing expenses, categorise them properly, and review the list above periodically to make sure you are not missing anything. The cumulative tax saving over the life of your business can be substantial.
