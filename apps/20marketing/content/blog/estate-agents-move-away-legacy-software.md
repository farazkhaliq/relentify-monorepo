Many estate agencies are running on software that was installed years ago and has barely changed since. The interface looks like it was designed in a different era — because it was. The workflows are rigid, requiring agents to adapt their processes to the software rather than the other way around. Integrations are limited or nonexistent. Mobile access is an afterthought.

The agency has invested time, money, and training into the system. The data is there. The team knows how to use it, even if they complain about it regularly. The idea of switching feels disruptive, risky, and expensive.

This is the legacy trap. And the longer you stay in it, the more it costs you.

## What legacy software really costs

The direct cost of legacy software — the licence fee or subscription — is usually the least significant expense. The real costs are hidden in lost productivity, missed opportunities, and accumulated workarounds.

### Productivity costs

Legacy systems are slow. Loading screens that take ten seconds. Processes that require twelve clicks when a modern system needs three. Reports that take minutes to generate. These delays are individually small but cumulatively enormous. An agent who loses fifteen minutes per day to slow software loses over sixty hours per year — more than a week and a half of productive time.

### Workaround costs

When software cannot do what you need, people create workarounds. A spreadsheet to track something the CRM cannot. A manual email reminder because the system does not have automation. A separate folder for documents because the system's storage is inadequate. Each workaround adds time, introduces error risk, and creates a data silo.

### Training costs

Legacy systems often have steep learning curves, not because they are sophisticated, but because they are unintuitive. Training new hires takes longer. Experienced staff develop muscle memory for navigating clunky interfaces, and this knowledge leaves when they do.

### Opportunity costs

Modern CRM features — automation, integration, mobile access, advanced reporting — are not luxuries. They are capabilities that your competitors are using to work faster, serve clients better, and win more business. Every month you stay on legacy software is a month where you are falling further behind agencies that have already made the switch.

### Security costs

Older software often receives fewer security updates, and some systems reach end-of-life entirely — no longer receiving patches for newly discovered vulnerabilities. Running unpatched software is a security risk, particularly for systems that hold sensitive personal data like tenant identification documents and landlord bank details.

## Signs that your software is holding you back

Not sure whether your current system qualifies as legacy? Here are the indicators.

The software runs on a desktop installation rather than in a web browser. Mobile access is limited or unavailable. There is no API or integration capability. The vendor's update cycle is measured in years rather than weeks. The interface looks significantly different from the other tools your team uses daily. Your team uses spreadsheets or other tools alongside the system because it cannot do everything they need.

If three or more of these apply, your software is legacy — regardless of what the vendor calls it.

## Overcoming the barriers to switching

The most common objections to switching software are fear of data loss, disruption to the team, and the cost of transition. Each is legitimate but manageable.

### Data migration

Modern CRM vendors understand that migration is a critical step. Most offer migration tools or services that import your existing data — contacts, properties, tenancies, documents — into the new system. The key is to plan the migration carefully, validate the imported data, and run both systems in parallel for a short period to catch any issues.

Not all data needs to be migrated. Historical data that is no longer actively used can be archived separately. The focus should be on current, active data that the team needs for daily work.

### Team disruption

Any software change involves a learning curve. But modern systems are designed to be intuitive — significantly more so than most legacy systems. The adjustment period is typically measured in days or weeks, not months.

Involving the team in the selection process helps with adoption. If people have had input into choosing the new system, they are more invested in making it work.

### Cost

The cost of switching includes the new software subscription, migration effort, and training time. Set this against the ongoing costs of the legacy system — licence fees, productivity losses, workaround maintenance, and opportunity costs. In most cases, the switch pays for itself within the first year.

## What to look for in a replacement

When evaluating modern alternatives to your legacy system, prioritise the following.

**Cloud-based architecture.** Accessible from any device, anywhere, with automatic updates and backups.

**Intuitive interface.** A system that new team members can learn quickly and existing team members find pleasant to use.

**Automation capabilities.** Workflow automation, automated reminders, and task management that reduce manual admin.

**Integration support.** The ability to connect with your email, accounting software, property portals, and other tools.

**Flexible data model.** Custom fields, configurable pipelines, and the ability to adapt the system to your workflow rather than adapting your workflow to the system.

**Fair pricing.** Transparent pricing that does not penalise growth — no per-property charges that make scaling expensive, no feature lock-outs on lower tiers.

**Active development.** A vendor that regularly releases improvements, responds to user feedback, and invests in the product's future.

## The right time to switch

The right time to switch is before the pain of the current system becomes a crisis. Switching during a crisis — when the system has failed, when a key integration has broken, or when a security incident has occurred — is stressful and rushed.

Switching proactively — when you have time to evaluate options, plan the migration, and train the team — is smoother and more successful.

The legacy system you are running served its purpose when it was built. It got you to where you are today. But it is not the tool that will take you where you need to go next. The sooner you acknowledge that, the sooner you can start building on a foundation that supports your ambitions rather than constraining them.
