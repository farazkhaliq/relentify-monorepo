Shared properties — whether HMOs, flatshares, or houses let to groups — require a more structured inventory approach than single-tenant lets. Multiple tenants mean multiple check-ins, multiple check-outs (often at different times), shared communal areas where responsibility is divided, and a higher risk of disputes about who caused what.

A well-structured inventory process handles all of these complexities. A poorly structured one creates confusion, weak evidence, and disputes that are difficult to resolve fairly.

## The core challenge

In a single-tenant property, one person or household is responsible for everything. The inventory covers the entire property, the deposit is held against the entire property, and any deductions are straightforward to attribute.

In a shared property, each tenant typically occupies one room (their exclusive space) and shares access to communal areas (kitchen, living room, bathrooms, hallways, garden). The challenge is documenting individual and communal spaces in a way that allows fair assessment when tenants leave at different times.

## Structuring the inventory

### Individual room inventories

Each lettable room should have its own complete inventory, treated as a self-contained document. This room inventory should include:

- Room identification (number, name, or location)
- Full condition assessment — walls, ceiling, floor, door, window, light fittings, radiator, sockets
- All furniture and contents provided by the landlord — bed, mattress, desk, chair, wardrobe, drawers, shelving
- Condition and description of every item
- En-suite bathroom (if applicable)
- Photographs of everything

The room inventory is linked to the specific tenant who occupies that room. When that tenant checks out, only their room inventory is compared — they are not assessed against the condition of other tenants' rooms.

### Communal area inventory

The shared spaces should be documented in a single communal inventory that covers:

- Kitchen — units, worktops, appliances, shared equipment and utensils
- Living room — furniture, fittings, electronics
- Shared bathrooms — all fixtures and fittings
- Hallways and stairs — flooring, walls, handrails
- Utility areas — boiler, fuse box, storage
- External areas — garden, bins, parking, bike storage

This communal inventory applies to all tenants. Each tenant should acknowledge it at check-in, and it forms the basis for communal area assessments at check-out.

## Handling staggered move-ins

In many shared properties, tenants do not all start their tenancies on the same date. One tenant might move in on 1 September, another on 15 September, and a third on 1 October. Each needs their own check-in process.

### For each new tenant

1. Conduct a room inventory for their specific room
2. Walk them through the communal area inventory — showing them the current condition and the documentation
3. Have them acknowledge both their room inventory and the communal area inventory
4. Provide copies of both documents

### Communal area updates

Each time a new tenant checks in, it is worth doing a quick update of the communal areas. This creates a fresh baseline and helps track when any changes occurred. If the kitchen was in good condition when Tenant A moved in but showed damage by the time Tenant B arrived two weeks later, the updated record narrows the responsibility.

## Handling staggered move-outs

This is where the complexity really emerges. When one tenant leaves while others remain, you need to assess:

1. Their individual room — straightforward, using the room inventory comparison
2. The communal areas — more complex, because other tenants are still using them

### Communal area assessment at partial check-out

There are several approaches:

**Proportional responsibility**: The outgoing tenant is held responsible for a proportional share of any communal area issues. If there are four tenants and the kitchen is in worse condition than at check-in, the outgoing tenant bears 25% of the cost. This is simple but may be unfair — the other tenants may have caused all the damage.

**Evidence-based assessment**: If there is specific evidence linking damage to the outgoing tenant (for example, a mid-tenancy inspection report that predates their notice period), the deduction can be attributed accordingly.

**Clean exit standard**: The outgoing tenant is expected to leave communal areas in a reasonable condition at the point of departure. The inspection focuses on whether they have cleaned up after themselves and not left obvious damage, rather than comparing against the original check-in.

**Deferred assessment**: Communal area deductions are deferred until the final tenant leaves, at which point the cumulative damage is assessed. This is the simplest approach for the landlord but can feel unfair to the last tenant, who bears the burden of all accumulated wear.

The best approach depends on the tenancy structure (joint tenancy vs individual agreements), the property type, and the expectations set at the start.

## Documentation best practices

### Photograph communal areas at every transition

Every time a tenant moves in or out, photograph the communal areas. This creates a timeline of conditions that helps attribute responsibility. If the kitchen floor was clean when Tenant A checked in but stained when Tenant B checked in a month later, the timeline points to Tenant A.

### Use consistent room numbering

Assign clear, permanent identifiers to each room. "Room 1 — ground floor front" is better than "the room with the blue curtains" (curtains can change). Use the same identifiers in tenancy agreements, inventories, and all correspondence.

### Maintain a master property file

Keep a centralised record for each property that includes:
- All individual room inventories
- The communal area inventory (with all updates)
- All check-in and check-out reports
- Mid-tenancy inspection reports
- Any correspondence about condition or maintenance

This file tells the complete story of the property's condition over time.

### Set clear expectations at check-in

When tenants move into shared properties, explain:
- Their individual responsibility for their room
- Shared responsibility for communal areas
- How communal area deductions work
- The importance of reporting damage and maintenance issues
- What happens at check-out

This clarity prevents misunderstandings and encourages tenants to take shared responsibility seriously.

## Managing deposits in shared properties

### Joint tenancy deposits

If all tenants are on a single joint tenancy agreement with a single deposit, all tenants are jointly and severally liable for the entire property. Deductions can be made against the joint deposit for both individual room damage and communal area issues. The tenants then sort out between themselves who owes what.

### Individual room deposits

If each tenant has their own tenancy agreement and their own deposit, the situation is more nuanced. Room-specific deductions are straightforward — deducted from that tenant's deposit. Communal area deductions need to be allocated across the relevant tenants' deposits, which requires a clear and fair allocation method.

### Communal area deposits

Some landlords take a separate communal area deposit from each tenant, specifically for shared space issues. This ring-fences the communal costs and avoids the need to allocate communal deductions across individual room deposits.

## The bottom line

Multi-tenant properties are more complex to inventory, but the principles remain the same: document everything, photograph everything, and maintain a clear record of conditions at every transition point. The additional structure of separate room inventories and regular communal area updates is what makes the system work fairly for everyone involved.

Get the structure right at the start, maintain it through every transition, and the evidence will be there when you need it.
