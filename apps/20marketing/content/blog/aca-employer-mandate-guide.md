The Affordable Care Act (ACA) fundamentally changed employer obligations regarding health insurance in the United States. The employer shared responsibility provision — commonly called the employer mandate — requires certain employers to offer affordable, minimum-value health coverage to their full-time employees or face potential penalties.

Understanding whether the mandate applies to your business, what it requires, and how to comply is essential for any growing US company. This guide explains the rules in practical terms.

## Who does the employer mandate apply to?

The mandate applies to Applicable Large Employers (ALEs). An ALE is any employer that employed an average of 50 or more full-time employees (including full-time equivalent employees) during the prior calendar year.

### How to count employees

**Full-time employees:** Anyone who works an average of 30 or more hours per week, or 130 or more hours per month.

**Full-time equivalent (FTE) employees:** Part-time employees' hours are combined to create FTE counts. Add up the total monthly hours of all part-time employees (capped at 120 hours per employee) and divide by 120. The result is your FTE count.

**The calculation:** Add your full-time employee count to your FTE count for each month. Average this across all twelve months. If the result is 50 or more, you are an ALE for the following year.

**Example:** A business has 35 full-time employees and 40 part-time employees who each work an average of 60 hours per month. The FTE count is (40 x 60) / 120 = 20. Total: 35 + 20 = 55. This business is an ALE.

### Controlled groups and affiliated companies

If you own or control multiple businesses, their employees may be combined for purposes of determining ALE status. This includes parent-subsidiary groups, brother-sister groups, and affiliated service groups. Even if each individual business has fewer than 50 employees, the combined total across all entities determines your status.

### Seasonal worker exception

There is a limited exception for seasonal workers. If your workforce exceeds 50 full-time employees (including FTEs) for 120 days or fewer during the year, and the employees who pushed you over the threshold were seasonal workers, you are not considered an ALE.

## What the mandate requires

If you are an ALE, you must offer health insurance coverage that meets two criteria:

### Minimum essential coverage (MEC)

The plan must provide minimum essential coverage, which generally means it covers a core set of health benefits. Most employer-sponsored group health plans meet this requirement.

### Minimum value

The plan must cover at least 60% of the total allowed cost of benefits that are expected to be incurred under the plan. This is sometimes called the "60% actuarial value" test. Most standard employer health plans meet this threshold.

### Affordability

The employee's required contribution for self-only coverage must not exceed a specified percentage of the employee's household income. Since employers generally do not know an employee's household income, the IRS provides three safe harbors:

**W-2 safe harbor:** The employee's contribution does not exceed the affordability percentage of their W-2 wages (Box 1) for that year.

**Rate of pay safe harbor:** The employee's contribution does not exceed the affordability percentage of their hourly rate multiplied by 130 hours per month (or their monthly salary for salaried employees).

**Federal poverty line (FPL) safe harbor:** The employee's contribution does not exceed the affordability percentage of the federal poverty line for a single individual.

Using any of these safe harbors simplifies the affordability determination.

## The penalties

ALEs that fail to comply face two types of penalties, assessed on a monthly basis:

### Penalty A — No offer of coverage

If you do not offer MEC to at least 95% of your full-time employees (and their dependents), and at least one full-time employee receives a premium tax credit through a Marketplace plan, you owe a penalty for every full-time employee (minus the first 30).

This is the larger penalty and is assessed based on your total full-time employee count, not just the employees who were not offered coverage.

### Penalty B — Inadequate or unaffordable coverage

If you do offer coverage to at least 95% of full-time employees but the coverage is not affordable or does not meet minimum value, and at least one employee receives a premium tax credit, you owe a penalty for each employee who received a credit.

This penalty is calculated per affected employee rather than across the entire workforce, so it is typically smaller than Penalty A per employee — but it can still be substantial.

### How penalties are assessed

The IRS determines penalties based on information reported on Forms 1094-C and 1095-C. Penalty notices (Letter 226-J) are typically issued one to two years after the tax year in question. Employers have the right to respond and dispute the proposed assessment.

## Reporting requirements

### Form 1095-C

ALEs must furnish Form 1095-C to each full-time employee by the IRS deadline each year. This form reports:

- Whether coverage was offered each month
- The employee's share of the lowest-cost, self-only premium
- Whether the employee enrolled in coverage
- Any applicable safe harbor or other codes

### Form 1094-C

Form 1094-C is the transmittal form filed with the IRS along with all 1095-C forms. It reports aggregate employer-level information, including total employee counts and whether you are part of a controlled group.

### Filing deadlines

Forms must be furnished to employees and filed with the IRS by specified annual deadlines. Electronic filing is mandatory for employers filing 10 or more forms.

## Compliance strategies for small to mid-size employers

### Track employee hours carefully

Accurate hour tracking is the foundation of ACA compliance. You need to know which employees qualify as full-time (averaging 30+ hours per week) and calculate your FTE count accurately.

Reliable timesheet data from your payroll or timekeeping system feeds directly into this calculation. Businesses using an integrated platform like Relentify can pull hour data from timesheets and connect it to payroll records, ensuring consistent counts across all reporting.

### Use measurement periods

The ACA allows employers to use look-back measurement periods to determine full-time status for variable-hour employees. Instead of tracking hours in real time, you measure hours over a defined period (typically 6 to 12 months) and then apply the result for a subsequent stability period.

This approach provides predictability for both employers and employees and reduces the administrative burden of monthly hour tracking.

### Choose an affordable plan design

Work with your insurance broker to design a plan that meets affordability and minimum value requirements at a cost your business can sustain. The goal is to offer coverage that satisfies the mandate without overcommitting your budget.

### Maintain documentation

Keep records of:
- Employee hour records and FTE calculations
- Coverage offers and employee responses
- Plan documents showing minimum value and premium costs
- Safe harbor calculations used for affordability
- Forms 1094-C and 1095-C as filed

### Respond to penalty notices promptly

If you receive a Letter 226-J, review it carefully. Errors in IRS data are not uncommon, and you may have a valid basis for disputing the proposed penalty. Respond within the specified timeframe with supporting documentation.

## What if you are not an ALE?

Employers with fewer than 50 full-time employees (including FTEs) are not subject to the employer mandate. There is no penalty for not offering health insurance.

However, small employers may still choose to offer coverage:

- **Small Business Health Options Program (SHOP):** Employers with 1 to 50 employees can purchase group coverage through SHOP Marketplace plans.
- **Small employer tax credit:** Employers with fewer than 25 full-time equivalent employees, paying average wages below a set threshold, may qualify for a tax credit covering up to 50% of premium costs paid through SHOP.
- **Individual coverage HRA (ICHRA):** As an alternative to group coverage, employers of any size can offer an ICHRA that reimburses employees for individual health insurance premiums.

## Staying compliant

ACA compliance is an ongoing responsibility, not a one-time setup. As your workforce grows, your ALE status, plan affordability, and reporting obligations may change. Review your employee counts, coverage offers, and premium costs annually — and invest in systems that make accurate tracking and reporting as automated as possible.

The employer mandate adds complexity to running a business, but it is manageable with the right processes and tools in place.
