Your live chat widget is one of the most visible elements on your website. It sits on every page, follows visitors as they scroll, and is often the first point of interaction between your business and a potential customer. If it looks generic, clashes with your design, or behaves in unexpected ways, it undermines the professionalism of your entire site.

Customising your chat widget is not vanity. It is about creating a seamless experience where the chat feels like a natural extension of your website rather than a third-party tool bolted on as an afterthought.

## Why customisation matters

First impressions are formed quickly. When a visitor sees a chat widget that matches your brand colours, uses your logo, and speaks in your tone of voice, they instinctively trust it more than a generic widget with default colours and placeholder text.

A branded widget signals that this is your communication channel, maintained by your team, not a random chatbot from a platform they have never heard of. This trust translates directly into engagement. Visitors are more likely to start conversations when the widget feels like part of the business they are already considering.

Beyond trust, customisation affects usability. A widget that is too small is hard to interact with on mobile devices. One that is too large obscures important content. A widget placed in the wrong corner might conflict with other page elements. Getting these details right ensures the chat is accessible and unobtrusive across all devices and screen sizes.

## Colours and branding

### Match your brand palette

Most chat platforms let you set primary and secondary colours for the widget. The primary colour typically determines the header background and the send button, while the secondary colour affects accents and hover states.

Choose colours that align with your existing brand palette. If your website uses a dark navy header, a bright lime green chat widget will look out of place. If your brand is warm and earthy, a cold blue widget creates visual dissonance.

The simplest approach is to use your primary brand colour for the widget header and a complementary colour for interactive elements. This creates visual consistency without requiring complex design decisions.

### Add your logo

If your platform supports it, add your business logo to the chat widget header. This reinforces brand recognition and helps visitors immediately identify who they are talking to. A small, clean version of your logo works best since the widget header is compact and overly detailed logos will not render well at small sizes.

### Choose a chat icon

The default chat bubble icon works for most situations, but some platforms allow you to replace it with a custom icon or image. If your brand has a recognisable mascot or icon, using it as the chat bubble can increase visibility and brand recognition.

Keep the icon simple and clear. It needs to be recognisable at small sizes, typically around 50 to 60 pixels, so fine details will be lost.

## Widget text and language

### Welcome message

The welcome message is the first thing visitors see when they open the chat widget. It should be friendly, clear, and consistent with your brand voice.

A professional services firm might use: "Hello. How can we assist you today?"
A casual direct-to-consumer brand might use: "Hey there. Got a question? We are here to help."

The tone should match the rest of your website copy. If your marketing language is conversational and informal, a stiff, corporate chat greeting will feel jarring.

### Placeholder text

The text input field usually has placeholder text that tells the visitor where to type. Default placeholders like "Type a message..." are functional but generic. Consider something more specific: "Ask us anything..." or "Type your question here..." These small touches contribute to an overall impression of attention to detail.

### Offline message

When no agents are available, the widget should display a message that manages expectations. Customise this to tell visitors what will happen next: "We are currently away. Leave your details and we will get back to you within a few hours."

The offline message is an opportunity to capture leads rather than lose them. Make it clear, specific, and reassuring.

### Language

If your audience spans multiple languages, choose a platform that supports multilingual widgets. The widget text, including greetings, button labels, and system messages, should be available in the visitor's language. Some platforms detect browser language automatically and adjust accordingly.

## Position and placement

### Which corner?

The standard position for a chat widget is the bottom-right corner of the screen. This convention is so widespread that visitors instinctively look there when they want to start a conversation.

There are reasons to deviate. If your website has a persistent element in the bottom-right corner, such as a cookie banner, a back-to-top button, or a floating action button, the chat widget might overlap with it. In this case, moving it to the bottom-left corner is a reasonable alternative.

Avoid placing the widget anywhere other than the bottom of the screen. Top-positioned widgets are unusual and can feel intrusive. Centre-positioned widgets block content.

### Desktop versus mobile

On desktop, a small chat bubble that expands into a larger conversation window works well. On mobile, the widget needs more careful consideration. Screen space is limited, and a poorly sized widget can block content or be difficult to interact with.

Most modern chat platforms handle responsive behaviour automatically, reducing the widget size on mobile and expanding conversations to fill the screen when opened. Verify that your platform handles this well by testing on actual devices, not just browser resize tools.

### Page-specific visibility

You do not have to show the chat widget on every page. Some platforms let you configure which pages display the widget and which do not.

For example, you might hide the chat widget on a long-form blog post where it would be distracting but show it prominently on product, pricing, and contact pages where visitors are most likely to have questions.

Be deliberate about these choices. Every page where the widget is hidden is a page where you cannot engage visitors through chat.

## Agent profiles

When a visitor starts a conversation, they typically see an agent name and avatar. Customising these details makes the experience more personal.

### Real names and photos

Using real names and photos of your team members makes conversations feel human. Visitors are more comfortable and more engaged when they feel they are talking to a real person rather than a generic "Support Agent."

If privacy is a concern, first names with a professional photo work well. You do not need to use full names.

### Team display

Some widgets display a group of agent avatars before the conversation starts, showing the visitor that a real team is available. This can increase the likelihood that visitors start a conversation, as it signals responsiveness and availability.

## Behaviour settings

### Auto-open versus click to open

Some businesses configure the widget to open automatically after a few seconds, showing the welcome message without the visitor clicking anything. This increases visibility but can feel intrusive, particularly on mobile where an auto-opened widget takes up significant screen space.

A middle ground is to show a small badge or preview message above the chat bubble without fully opening the widget. This catches attention without disrupting the browsing experience.

### Sound notifications

When a new message arrives, the widget can play a notification sound. This is useful when the visitor has minimised the widget or switched to another tab but can be annoying if overused. Make this configurable, and consider defaulting to silent with an option for the visitor to enable sounds.

### Chat persistence

If a visitor navigates to a different page, the chat conversation should persist. Nobody wants to start their question over because they clicked a link. Most modern platforms handle this automatically, but verify that conversations survive page navigation on your site.

## Testing your customisation

After configuring your widget, test it systematically.

**Visual consistency.** Does the widget look natural alongside your website design? Check on both light and dark backgrounds if your site uses a dark mode toggle.

**Mobile experience.** Open the widget on a phone. Is it easy to tap? Does the conversation window obscure too much content? Is the text readable?

**Loading performance.** The widget script should load asynchronously so it does not slow down your page. Verify using your browser's developer tools that the chat widget does not add significant load time.

**Cross-browser testing.** Check the widget in Chrome, Firefox, Safari, and Edge. CSS rendering differences can cause visual inconsistencies.

Platforms like [Relentify Chat](/chat) provide a preview mode where you can see your customisations before they go live on your website, making it easy to iterate on the design without affecting your visitors.

## Balancing visibility with subtlety

The best chat widgets are visible enough to be found when needed but subtle enough not to dominate the page. They match the brand, they sit in an expected position, and they provide a smooth transition from browsing to conversation.

Over-customisation can be as problematic as no customisation. A widget with animated effects, attention-seeking colours, and aggressive auto-open behaviour might get more clicks, but it damages the user experience and can make your site feel less professional.

Aim for a widget that a visitor barely notices until they need it, and then finds exactly where they expect it, looking exactly like it belongs there.
