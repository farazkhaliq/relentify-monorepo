# Mental Health Days at Work: An Employer's Guide to Getting It Right

The conversation around mental health in the workplace has shifted dramatically. What was once a taboo subject is now openly discussed, and employees increasingly expect their employers to take mental wellbeing seriously. One question that comes up repeatedly is whether businesses should offer dedicated mental health days — and if so, how to structure them without creating a system that is difficult to manage or open to misuse.

This guide helps employers navigate the practical and cultural aspects of mental health days, from policy design to implementation.

## The Case for Mental Health Days

Mental health conditions are one of the leading causes of workplace absence globally. Anxiety, depression, stress, and burnout affect millions of workers, and the cost to businesses in lost productivity, absenteeism, and staff turnover is enormous.

The traditional approach — waiting until someone is too unwell to work and then managing them through sick leave — is reactive and costly. By the time an employee takes extended sick leave for mental health reasons, the situation has usually deteriorated significantly. Recovery takes longer, the impact on the team is greater, and the risk of the employee not returning at all increases.

Mental health days offer a proactive alternative. They give employees permission to take a day off specifically for mental wellbeing — before they reach crisis point. The theory is that a day of rest, recovery, and self-care early on prevents more serious and prolonged absence later.

Research supports this approach. Organisations that offer mental health days report lower overall absence rates, higher employee engagement, and better retention. The cost of a few additional days off is more than offset by reduced long-term absence and turnover.

## Designing Your Policy

There is no single right way to implement mental health days. The best approach depends on your organisation's size, culture, and operational requirements. Here are the key decisions you need to make.

### Dedicated Days vs Flexible Leave

**Option A: Dedicated mental health days.** Allocate a specific number of days per year (typically 2-5) that can be used exclusively for mental wellbeing. These are separate from annual leave and sick leave.

**Option B: Flexible personal days.** Provide additional personal days that can be used for any reason — mental health, family needs, personal appointments — without requiring a specific justification.

**Option C: Enhanced sick leave with explicit mental health inclusion.** Expand your existing sick leave policy to explicitly include mental health as a valid reason, ensuring employees know they can use sick days for mental health without stigma.

Option A sends the strongest signal that mental health matters but can feel prescriptive. Option B provides flexibility without requiring employees to label their reason. Option C requires the least policy change but depends on cultural acceptance of mental health as a legitimate sick leave reason.

### Notice Requirements

Standard sick leave typically requires notification on the day of absence. Mental health days can work similarly, but consider the nature of mental health episodes.

A reasonable approach: employees can take a mental health day at short notice (same-day notification to their line manager) but are encouraged to plan ahead where possible. If someone knows they are struggling and a quiet day next week would help, booking it in advance is better for everyone.

Do not require medical evidence for mental health days. Requiring a doctor's note for a single day undermines the purpose and adds unnecessary barriers. For extended absence (three or more consecutive days), your standard absence management process applies.

### Frequency and Limits

If offering dedicated mental health days, set a clear annual allocation — typically 2-5 days. This provides a meaningful benefit without creating an open-ended entitlement that is difficult to manage.

Consider whether unused mental health days carry over to the next year. Generally, it is better that they do not — the point is to encourage their use when needed, not to accumulate a bank of days.

### Communication and Culture

A policy is only as good as the culture surrounding it. If employees feel they will be judged, penalised, or viewed as weak for taking mental health days, they will not use them — regardless of what the policy says.

Leadership modelling is powerful. When senior leaders openly acknowledge that they have taken mental health days, it normalises the practice across the organisation.

Train line managers to respond supportively when someone requests a mental health day. The response should be brief and non-intrusive: "Thank you for letting me know. Take care of yourself, and let me know if there is anything I can do."

## Addressing Common Concerns

### "People will abuse the system."

This is the most common concern, and it is largely unfounded. Studies consistently show that mental health day policies do not increase overall absence rates. Most employees use them sparingly and genuinely.

A small minority may take advantage, but this is true of any leave policy. If you suspect misuse by a specific individual, address it through your standard absence management process — do not punish the majority by removing a beneficial policy.

### "How do we manage workload when someone takes a mental health day?"

The same way you manage when someone calls in sick. If a single day's absence creates an operational crisis, the issue is insufficient cover and resilience in your team structure, not the mental health day policy.

Build contingency into your operational planning. Cross-train team members. Document processes so anyone can cover essential tasks. These are good management practices regardless of mental health days.

### "What if someone is taking mental health days frequently?"

Frequent use of mental health days is a signal, not a problem in itself. It may indicate that the employee is dealing with a significant mental health challenge, is experiencing workplace stress, or has personal circumstances that need support.

Have a supportive conversation. Express concern for their wellbeing, ask how you can help, and signpost support resources (Employee Assistance Programme, occupational health, counselling services). Do not treat frequent mental health days as a disciplinary matter.

### "We are a small business — we cannot afford additional days off."

Small businesses often feel the impact of absence more acutely. But the cost of not addressing mental health is higher: prolonged sick leave, recruitment costs to replace people who leave, and the hidden cost of presenteeism (people at work but not functioning effectively).

Even 2-3 mental health days per year per employee is a modest investment. For a team of 10, that is 20-30 additional days off per year — roughly equivalent to one extra person being away for a month. The reduction in long-term absence and improved retention typically more than compensates.

## Supporting Mental Health Beyond Days Off

Mental health days are one tool in a broader wellbeing strategy. On their own, they are not enough. Complement them with:

**Employee Assistance Programmes:** Confidential counselling and support services available to all employees and often their family members.

**Mental health training:** Train managers to recognise signs of mental health difficulties and respond appropriately. Mental health first aid courses are widely available and highly effective.

**Workload management:** The most common cause of work-related mental health problems is excessive workload. Use [timesheet data](https://relentify.com/features/timesheets) and regular check-ins to monitor workload and prevent burnout before it happens.

**Flexible working:** The ability to adjust working hours or location to manage mental health needs can be more valuable than additional days off.

**Open conversations:** Create regular opportunities for employees to discuss their wellbeing — whether through one-to-ones, team check-ins, or anonymous surveys.

## Measuring Impact

Track the following metrics to assess whether your mental health day policy is working:

- **Mental health day usage:** How many days are being used, by whom, and when? Low usage might indicate stigma. Very high usage might indicate broader wellbeing issues.
- **Overall absence rate:** Is total absence decreasing as employees address issues earlier?
- **Employee engagement scores:** Are engagement and satisfaction improving?
- **Retention:** Is staff turnover decreasing?
- **Return on investment:** Compare the cost of additional days off against reductions in long-term absence, recruitment costs, and productivity improvements.

## Getting Started

If you are ready to implement mental health days, follow these steps:

1. **Decide your approach:** Dedicated days, flexible personal days, or enhanced sick leave
2. **Draft the policy:** Keep it simple, clear, and supportive in tone
3. **Train managers:** Ensure they understand the policy and can respond appropriately
4. **Communicate to all staff:** Explain the policy, its purpose, and how to use it
5. **Lead from the top:** Encourage senior leaders to model healthy behaviours
6. **Review after 12 months:** Assess usage, impact, and any adjustments needed

Mental health days are not a luxury or a trend — they are a practical response to a genuine workplace challenge. Implemented thoughtfully, they demonstrate that your organisation values its people, reduce the stigma around mental health, and create a healthier, more productive workplace for everyone.
