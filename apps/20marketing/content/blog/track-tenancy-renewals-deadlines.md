Tenancy renewals are one of the most time-sensitive processes in a letting agency. Miss the window and you risk an unplanned void period, a tenant who decides to leave, or a tenancy that rolls onto a periodic basis without any deliberate decision having been made.

For agencies managing dozens or hundreds of properties, keeping track of every renewal date — along with the actions that need to happen weeks or months before — is a genuine operational challenge. It is also one of the areas where the difference between a well-organised agency and a disorganised one is most visible.

## Why renewals get missed

The most common reason renewals get missed is that the timeline is too long for human memory to manage reliably. A tenancy agreement signed in January with a twelve-month term does not need renewal action until October or November. That is nine or ten months during which the renewal date exists only in the tenancy agreement document — a document that nobody is actively reviewing.

Many agencies rely on a spreadsheet or a diary entry to track renewal dates. Both methods work in theory but fail in practice. Spreadsheets need to be checked regularly, and in a busy office, "regularly" quickly becomes "occasionally." Diary entries are tied to individual staff members, and if that person is on leave or has left the agency, the reminder goes with them.

The result is a predictable pattern: renewals are noticed too late, conversations with landlords and tenants are rushed, and decisions are made reactively rather than strategically.

## The renewal timeline

A well-managed renewal process starts three months before the tenancy end date. This gives you enough time to consult the landlord, assess the market, present options to the tenant, negotiate terms, and prepare documentation — all without time pressure.

### Three months before expiry

At this point, the CRM should generate an alert and create a task for the assigned agent. The first action is to review the tenancy and the property. Has the tenant been a good tenant? Are there any outstanding maintenance issues? Is the current rent in line with the market?

Contact the landlord to discuss their intentions. Do they want to renew on the same terms? Do they want to propose a rent increase? Are they considering selling the property? This conversation is much more productive when it happens three months out than three weeks out.

### Two months before expiry

By now, the landlord's instructions should be clear. If they want to renew, prepare the renewal offer and present it to the tenant. If they want to increase the rent, explain the rationale — ideally supported by market data.

Give the tenant a reasonable deadline to respond — typically two to four weeks. If the tenant declines or does not respond, you need time to begin the re-letting process.

### One month before expiry

If the tenant has accepted the renewal, prepare and send the new tenancy agreement for signature. Ensure that any updated terms — rent changes, additional conditions — are reflected correctly.

If the tenant is leaving, the re-letting process should already be underway. Marketing the property, arranging viewings, and processing applications all take time, and the earlier you start, the shorter the void period.

### Two weeks before expiry

Final checks. Is the new agreement signed? Has the deposit been re-protected if required? Are all compliance certificates still current? Is the landlord updated?

If the tenancy is not being renewed, confirm the move-out date with the tenant, arrange the check-out inspection, and ensure that the incoming tenant's onboarding is on track.

## Building a renewal tracker

The simplest version of a renewal tracker is a filtered view in your CRM that shows all tenancies with an end date in the next three months. This view should be reviewed at least weekly — ideally as part of a team meeting where upcoming renewals are discussed and actions are assigned.

A more sophisticated approach uses the CRM's automation features to trigger tasks and reminders at each stage of the renewal timeline. When a tenancy record is created, the system automatically schedules a reminder for three months before expiry, two months, one month, and two weeks. Each reminder creates a task, assigns it to the property's managing agent, and sets a due date.

This approach ensures that renewals are surfaced proactively, regardless of how many properties you manage. Whether you have twenty tenancies or two thousand, the system flags what needs attention and when.

## Handling rent reviews

Many tenancy renewals include a rent review. This is a sensitive conversation that benefits from data and preparation.

Your CRM should give you access to the information you need to support a rent review discussion. What is the current rent? How does it compare to similar properties in the area? Has the rent increased at previous renewals? What is the tenant's payment history?

If your CRM tracks market data or integrates with valuation tools, even better. Being able to show a landlord that the proposed rent is in line with current market rates — or that a modest increase is justified — makes the conversation factual rather than emotional.

For tenants, a well-supported rent review feels fair even when the rent is increasing. "The market has moved and comparable properties are now letting for X" is a stronger position than "the landlord wants more money."

## Managing periodic tenancies

When a fixed-term tenancy expires without being renewed, it typically becomes a periodic tenancy — rolling on a month-by-month or week-by-week basis. This is not necessarily a problem, but it should be a deliberate choice rather than an accidental one.

A periodic tenancy gives both the landlord and the tenant more flexibility, but it also means less certainty. The landlord does not know how long the tenant will stay. The tenant does not know if the landlord will seek possession.

Your CRM should flag periodic tenancies separately so that you can review them regularly. In some cases, converting a periodic tenancy back to a fixed term is in everyone's interest. In others, the flexibility is exactly what the landlord or tenant wants. Either way, it should be a conscious decision documented in the system.

## Renewal reporting

Over time, your renewal data becomes a valuable source of insight. What percentage of tenancies are being renewed versus ending? What is the average rent increase at renewal? How many tenancies are rolling periodically without a deliberate renewal decision?

These metrics tell you a lot about the health of your portfolio and the satisfaction of your tenants. A high renewal rate suggests that tenants are happy and landlords are well-served. A declining renewal rate may indicate problems — with property condition, rent levels, or the service your agency provides.

Tracking renewal outcomes also helps you forecast. If you know that historically twenty percent of tenancies are not renewed, you can anticipate the number of void periods in the coming quarter and plan your re-letting resources accordingly.

## The cost of getting it wrong

A missed renewal deadline does not just result in an administrative headache. It has real financial consequences.

If a tenant leaves because the renewal conversation happened too late, the landlord faces a void period. Even a single month of lost rent on a property that generates 1,200 per month is a significant cost — and it is one that the landlord will rightly attribute to the agency's failure to manage the process.

If a tenancy rolls into a periodic arrangement without the landlord's knowledge, the landlord may feel that their agent is not paying attention. Trust erodes, and the landlord starts considering other agencies.

If a rent review is not conducted at renewal, the landlord misses the opportunity to adjust the rent to market levels. Over multiple years, this can add up to thousands in lost income.

## Making it systematic

The key to successful renewal management is treating it as a process, not an event. Renewals are not something that happens at the end of a tenancy. They are something that is managed throughout the tenancy, with specific actions at specific milestones.
