Maintenance is one of the most visible aspects of property management. When a boiler breaks on a winter evening or a pipe starts leaking, the speed and professionalism of the agency's response shapes the tenant's entire perception of the service. Get it right consistently and tenants stay, landlords are happy, and your agency builds a reputation for reliability. Get it wrong and complaints multiply, reviews suffer, and landlords start looking elsewhere.

Yet in many agencies, maintenance is managed through a combination of emails, phone calls, text messages, and notebooks. A tenant calls to report a dripping tap. The agent scribbles a note. The note gets buried under other paperwork. A week later, the tenant calls again, frustrated. The agent scrambles to find a plumber. The landlord is not informed until they receive the invoice.

A CRM-based approach to maintenance management replaces this chaos with a structured, trackable process.

## The maintenance lifecycle

Every maintenance request follows a predictable lifecycle, and each stage needs to be tracked.

**Report.** The tenant reports an issue. The details — what the problem is, where it is, how urgent it is — need to be captured accurately. Photographs are invaluable.

**Triage.** The agency assesses the report and determines the appropriate response. Is this an emergency that requires immediate attention? Is it a routine repair that can be scheduled? Does it require landlord approval before proceeding?

**Assignment.** A contractor is selected and the job is assigned. The contractor needs to know what the problem is, where the property is, how to gain access, and any relevant contact details.

**Execution.** The contractor attends the property and carries out the repair. The agency needs to know when the work is scheduled, whether the contractor attended, and what was done.

**Completion.** The work is finished, the tenant confirms satisfaction, and the cost is recorded against the property. The landlord is updated.

Each of these stages involves communication between multiple parties — the tenant, the agent, the contractor, and the landlord. Without a central system to track it all, information gets lost between stages.

## Capturing requests properly

The quality of a maintenance request depends on the information captured at the outset. A vague report — "something is wrong with the kitchen" — leads to wasted time and multiple visits. A detailed report — "the mixer tap in the kitchen is dripping from the spout when turned off, approximately one drip per second" — gives the contractor everything they need.

Your CRM should provide a structured way for tenants to report issues. This might be a form on your website, an email template, or a feature within your tenant portal. The key fields are the property address, the location within the property, a description of the issue, photographs if possible, and the tenant's availability for contractor access.

When the request is logged in the CRM, it should automatically link to the property record and the tenant's profile, giving anyone who views it the full context.

## Triage and prioritisation

Not all maintenance requests are equally urgent. A broken boiler in winter is an emergency. A sticking door is a routine repair. A cosmetic crack in a ceiling might not need immediate attention at all.

Your CRM should support a prioritisation system — emergency, urgent, routine, and low priority — with each level having a defined response time. Emergencies might require a response within two hours. Urgent issues within 24 hours. Routine repairs within a week. Low-priority items within a month.

This prioritisation ensures that resources are allocated appropriately and that tenants have realistic expectations about when their issue will be addressed.

## Landlord authorisation

Many maintenance jobs require landlord approval before work can proceed, especially those above a certain cost threshold. Your CRM should support a landlord authorisation workflow — sending the landlord a summary of the issue and the proposed solution, and tracking their response.

Speed matters here. A landlord who takes a week to approve a repair is a landlord whose tenant spends a week waiting. Your CRM can help by sending automated reminders to landlords who have not responded within a defined timeframe.

Some agencies agree a standing authorisation with their landlords — the authority to proceed with any repair below a certain value without seeking specific approval. If this arrangement exists, it should be recorded in the CRM so that the team knows to proceed without delay.

## Contractor management

Your CRM should maintain a database of approved contractors, organised by trade, area, availability, and quality rating. When a job needs to be assigned, the agent can quickly identify the most appropriate contractor and assign the job.

Tracking contractor performance over time — response time, quality of work, tenant feedback — helps you make better assignment decisions and maintain a roster of reliable tradespeople. If a particular plumber is consistently slow to respond, the data makes that visible long before it becomes a crisis.

Once a job is assigned, the CRM should track its progress. Has the contractor acknowledged the assignment? Have they scheduled a visit? Have they attended? Have they completed the work?

## Keeping everyone informed

One of the most common complaints in maintenance management is lack of communication. The tenant does not know what is happening. The landlord does not know a repair was needed. The agent does not know whether the contractor has attended.

A CRM solves this by providing a shared timeline of every action taken on each maintenance request. When the contractor is assigned, the tenant is notified. When the visit is scheduled, the tenant is notified. When the work is completed, both the tenant and the landlord are notified.

This proactive communication reduces inbound calls — tenants are not calling to ask for updates because they are already receiving them — and builds confidence that the agency is managing the process professionally.

## Cost tracking and invoicing

Every maintenance job has a cost, and that cost needs to be recorded accurately. Your CRM should log the invoice amount against the property record, making it easy to see the total maintenance spend for each property over time.

This data is useful for several purposes. It helps with landlord reporting — you can provide a clear breakdown of maintenance costs at the end of each quarter or year. It helps with budgeting — if a property consistently requires expensive repairs, it may be time to discuss a major refurbishment with the landlord. And it helps with identifying patterns — if multiple properties in the same building are reporting the same issue, the root cause might need a different approach.

## Emergency maintenance

Emergency maintenance — burst pipes, gas leaks, security breaches — requires a different process. The response time is measured in hours, not days, and the normal authorisation process may need to be bypassed.

Your CRM should have a separate workflow for emergencies that prioritises speed. The contractor should be notified immediately. The landlord should be informed as soon as possible, but not at the expense of delaying the repair. The tenant should receive confirmation that help is on the way.

After the emergency is resolved, the CRM records what happened, what was done, and what it cost — ensuring that the landlord is fully informed and the property record is up to date.

## Preventive maintenance

The best maintenance strategy is not reactive. It is preventive. Regular property inspections, seasonal checks, and scheduled servicing can catch issues before they become emergencies.

Your CRM can support preventive maintenance by scheduling recurring tasks — annual boiler servicing, gutter clearing before winter, smoke alarm testing. These tasks are assigned to contractors and tracked through the same system as reactive repairs, ensuring that preventive work actually gets done rather than being perpetually postponed.

## Reporting and analysis

Over time, your maintenance data becomes a valuable analytical tool. Which properties generate the most maintenance requests? Which types of issues are most common? Which contractors deliver the best value? How does your response time compare to your targets?

These insights allow you to improve your maintenance operation continuously. If a particular property type consistently generates plumbing issues, you can advise landlords on preventive measures. If your response time for urgent issues is slipping, you can investigate why and address the bottleneck.
