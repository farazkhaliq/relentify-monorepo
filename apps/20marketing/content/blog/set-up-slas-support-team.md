Without a service level agreement, customer support operates on gut feeling. Agents respond when they can, managers hope things are moving fast enough, and customers are left wondering when — or if — they will hear back.

An SLA changes this by setting explicit, measurable targets for how quickly your team responds to and resolves customer issues. It creates accountability, gives managers a benchmark to measure performance against, and gives customers a clear expectation of what "good service" looks like.

But SLAs only work if they are realistic, well-structured, and actually enforced. A target that nobody can meet is worse than no target at all — it demoralises your team and undermines trust in the system.

## What an SLA covers

In the context of customer support, an SLA typically defines two things:

**First response time** — how long between when a customer submits a request and when they receive their first human reply. This is the metric customers feel most strongly about. A fast acknowledgment, even if it does not contain a resolution, significantly reduces customer anxiety.

**Resolution time** — how long from when the ticket is created until the issue is fully resolved. This metric captures the overall efficiency of your support operation.

Some teams add additional SLA metrics:

- **Next response time** — how long between subsequent replies during an ongoing conversation
- **Time to assign** — how long before a ticket is picked up by an agent
- **Escalation time** — how long before an unresolved high-priority ticket is automatically escalated

## Setting realistic targets

The biggest mistake in SLA design is setting aspirational targets that your team cannot consistently meet. An SLA should reflect what your team can reliably achieve, not what you wish they could do.

### Consider your channel mix

Different channels have different speed expectations. Live chat demands responses in seconds or minutes. Email responses within a few hours are generally acceptable. Phone calls should be answered promptly or returned quickly. Your SLAs should reflect these differences.

A reasonable starting point for many businesses:

| Channel | First response | Resolution |
|---------|---------------|------------|
| Live chat | Under 2 minutes | Under 30 minutes |
| Email | Under 4 hours | Under 24 hours |
| Phone | Under 3 rings or 30 seconds | Varies |
| Social messaging | Under 1 hour | Under 8 hours |

These are guidelines, not rules. Your targets should be based on your team's capacity and your customers' expectations.

### Factor in business hours

Decide whether your SLAs run on business hours or calendar hours. If your team works Monday to Friday, 9 to 5, an SLA clock that runs 24/7 means tickets submitted on Friday evening will already be approaching breach by Monday morning — before your team even logs in.

Most helpdesks allow you to define business hours and pause the SLA clock outside those hours. This is a more honest and achievable approach for teams that do not offer 24/7 support.

### Differentiate by priority

Not every ticket deserves the same urgency. A customer who cannot log into their account needs a faster response than someone asking about a feature that might be useful in six months.

Define SLA targets for each priority level:

| Priority | First response | Resolution |
|----------|---------------|------------|
| Urgent | 30 minutes | 4 hours |
| High | 2 hours | 8 hours |
| Normal | 4 hours | 24 hours |
| Low | 8 hours | 48 hours |

The key is that priority assignment must be consistent. If every ticket is marked "urgent," your prioritisation system is not working.

### Differentiate by customer tier

Some businesses offer different SLA tiers based on the customer's plan or account value. Enterprise customers might get a one-hour first response guarantee, while free-tier users get 24 hours. This is common in SaaS and B2B contexts.

If you take this approach, be transparent about it. Customers understand that higher-paying plans come with faster support — but they do not appreciate discovering it only when they experience slow service.

## Implementing SLAs in your helpdesk

### Step 1: Define your SLA policies

Before touching any software, write down your SLA targets. For each combination of priority and channel, define the first response time and resolution time. Get buy-in from your team — they need to believe these targets are achievable.

### Step 2: Configure SLA rules in your platform

Most helpdesk platforms let you create SLA policies that automatically apply to tickets based on criteria. When a new ticket comes in, the system evaluates the ticket's properties (priority, channel, customer tier) and applies the appropriate SLA with its countdown timers.

### Step 3: Set up SLA breach alerts

An SLA that nobody monitors is just a number. Configure alerts that notify agents and managers when a ticket is approaching its SLA deadline. A common pattern is a warning at 75 percent of the elapsed time (giving the agent a chance to respond before breach) and an alert at breach.

### Step 4: Define escalation paths

What happens when an SLA is breached? The system should escalate — reassigning the ticket to a senior agent, notifying a manager, or bumping the ticket to the top of the queue. Automated escalation ensures that breaches are addressed immediately rather than discovered during a weekly review.

### Step 5: Exclude paused time

SLA clocks should pause when the ball is in the customer's court. If an agent asks the customer for more information, the SLA timer should stop until the customer replies. Otherwise, agents are penalised for customer delays, which skews your metrics and frustrates your team.

## Measuring SLA performance

### SLA attainment rate

The percentage of tickets resolved within their SLA target. Track this as a single number (overall attainment) and break it down by priority, channel, and agent. A healthy target for SLA attainment is above 90 percent. Below 80 percent indicates a systemic problem.

### Breach volume and patterns

Look at when and where breaches happen. Are they concentrated on Mondays (weekend backlog)? In a particular category (suggesting the need for specialist knowledge)? From a particular channel (suggesting staffing gaps)?

### Average time to breach

When breaches occur, how far past the SLA deadline are they? A ticket that breaches by five minutes is very different from one that breaches by five hours. The severity of breaches matters as much as their frequency.

### Agent-level performance

SLA metrics at the agent level help identify coaching opportunities. An agent who consistently meets SLAs on email but struggles with chat might need training on chat-specific workflows. An agent who meets first-response SLAs but misses resolution targets might be taking on too many tickets at once.

## Common SLA mistakes

### Setting SLAs without the resources to meet them

An SLA is a commitment. If you set a two-hour first response target but only have one agent working part-time, you are setting up for failure. SLAs must be matched with appropriate staffing.

### Not accounting for weekends and holidays

If your SLA clock runs on calendar hours, weekends and holidays will inflate your breach rate. Configure business hours properly and set customer expectations about out-of-hours support.

### Treating all tickets equally

A password reset request and a data loss incident should not have the same SLA. Prioritisation exists for a reason — use it, and make sure your team understands the criteria for each priority level.

### Using SLAs as a punitive tool

SLAs should drive improvement, not fear. If agents are gaming the system — closing tickets prematurely to meet targets, or deprioritising complex issues — the problem is with the incentive structure, not the agents.

### Ignoring the resolution quality

Fast resolutions are meaningless if the issue is not actually resolved. Track reopen rates alongside SLA metrics. A ticket that is resolved within SLA but reopened three times is not a success story.

## Communicating SLAs to customers

For B2B businesses and SaaS companies, publishing your SLA commitments can be a competitive advantage. It signals confidence in your support operation and gives customers a clear expectation.

When communicating SLAs externally:

- **Be honest about business hours** — do not imply 24/7 support if you do not provide it
- **Specify what "response" means** — a first response is not the same as a resolution
- **Explain priority levels** — help customers understand how urgency is determined
- **Include any exclusions** — planned maintenance, force majeure, third-party dependencies

## Evolving your SLAs over time

SLAs are not permanent. As your team grows, your processes improve, and your tooling evolves, your SLA targets should tighten. Review them quarterly. If you are consistently hitting 98 percent attainment, your targets might be too generous. If you are struggling to reach 85 percent, something needs to change — whether that is headcount, process, or the targets themselves.
