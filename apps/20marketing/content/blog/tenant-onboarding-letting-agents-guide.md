The period between a tenant accepting an offer and moving into a property is one of the most critical phases in any tenancy. Get it right and you set the foundation for a smooth, professional relationship. Get it wrong and you start the tenancy with frustrated tenants, confused landlords, and a backlog of admin that follows you for months.

Yet many letting agencies treat onboarding as an ad hoc process — a series of tasks handled by whoever happens to be available, with no consistent structure or timeline. The result is missed steps, duplicated effort, and an experience that feels chaotic to everyone involved.

This guide walks through every stage of the tenant onboarding process and explains how to turn it into a repeatable, reliable system.

## Why onboarding matters

First impressions last. A tenant who has a smooth onboarding experience — clear communication, timely paperwork, a well-prepared property — is far more likely to be a good tenant throughout the tenancy. They pay rent on time because they respect the agency. They report maintenance issues early because they trust you will handle them. They renew their tenancy because the experience has been positive from the start.

Conversely, a tenant who moves into a dirty property, receives contradictory instructions, or has to chase for information is starting the relationship on the wrong foot. That frustration tends to compound over time.

## Stage 1: Offer acceptance and referencing

Once a tenant's offer has been accepted by the landlord, the clock starts. The onboarding process should be triggered immediately — ideally automatically through your CRM or property management system.

### Referencing

Most agencies use a third-party referencing service. The key is speed. Send the referencing request to the tenant on the same day the offer is accepted. Include clear instructions about what information they need to provide and what the timeline looks like.

Common referencing checks include employment verification, previous landlord references, credit checks, and affordability assessments. If the tenant has a guarantor, their referencing runs in parallel.

Track the status of each reference in your system. If a reference is taking longer than expected, follow up proactively rather than waiting. Delays at this stage push back the entire move-in timeline.

### Holding deposit

Collect the holding deposit at the point of offer acceptance. This secures the property and demonstrates the tenant's commitment. Ensure the tenant receives written confirmation of the holding deposit terms — how long it is held, under what circumstances it is refundable, and what happens next.

## Stage 2: Documentation and compliance

While referencing is in progress, prepare the tenancy documentation. This is the stage where thoroughness prevents problems later.

### Tenancy agreement

Prepare the tenancy agreement with all the correct details — tenant names, property address, rent amount, deposit amount, tenancy start and end dates, break clauses, and any special conditions agreed with the landlord.

Review the agreement before sending it to the tenant. Errors in the tenancy agreement — a wrong date, a misspelled name, an incorrect rent figure — are surprisingly common and can cause legal issues down the line.

### Prescribed information

When collecting a tenancy deposit, you are legally required to provide prescribed information to the tenant within a specific timeframe. This includes details of the deposit protection scheme, the amount protected, the circumstances under which deductions may be made, and the dispute resolution process.

Set up a task in your CRM to ensure this is completed within the required window. Missing this deadline can expose the landlord to penalties.

### Right to rent checks

Conduct right to rent checks before the tenancy starts. Record the documents checked, the date of the check, and retain copies as required. Your CRM should log this against both the tenant profile and the property record.

### Compliance certificates

Before a tenant moves in, verify that all compliance certificates for the property are current — gas safety, electrical installation, energy performance certificate, and any others required by local regulations. If anything is due for renewal, arrange it before the tenancy begins.

## Stage 3: Pre-move-in preparation

With paperwork underway, attention turns to preparing the property and the tenant for move-in day.

### Property inspection

Conduct a pre-tenancy inspection of the property. Check that everything is clean, functional, and in the condition described to the tenant. Test appliances, check for maintenance issues, ensure utilities are connected, and confirm that the property matches the inventory report.

If the previous tenant has just moved out, coordinate any cleaning, repairs, or redecoration before the new tenant arrives. A property that is not ready on move-in day is a guaranteed source of complaints.

### Inventory and check-in report

Prepare a detailed inventory and schedule of condition. This document records the state of every room, fixture, and fitting at the start of the tenancy. Include photographs.

The inventory protects both the landlord and the tenant in the event of a deposit dispute at the end of the tenancy. It should be thorough, objective, and ideally signed by the tenant on the day they move in.

### Utility transfers

Notify utility providers of the change of tenancy. Record final meter readings from the outgoing tenant and opening readings for the incoming tenant. If the landlord is responsible for certain utilities, ensure those accounts remain in the correct name.

This is another task that is easy to overlook and time-consuming to fix after the fact.

## Stage 4: Move-in day

Move-in day should be anticlimactic. If the previous stages have been handled properly, the tenant arrives at a clean, compliant property with all their paperwork complete.

### Key handover

Hand over keys and any access devices — fobs, garage remotes, communal entry codes. Record what has been issued, including the number and type of keys.

### Welcome pack

Provide the tenant with a welcome pack containing essential information about the property. This should include emergency contact numbers, instructions for heating and hot water systems, waste collection schedules, parking arrangements, and details of how to report maintenance issues.

A good welcome pack answers the questions that tenants inevitably ask in their first week. Providing this information upfront reduces inbound calls and helps the tenant settle in faster.

### Walk-through

If possible, walk through the property with the tenant. Point out key features, demonstrate any appliances that might not be immediately intuitive, and note any existing marks or issues that are documented in the inventory.

## Stage 5: Post-move-in follow-up

The onboarding process does not end when the tenant collects their keys. A follow-up in the first week — a quick call or email to check that everything is satisfactory — goes a long way.

This is your opportunity to catch any issues early, before they become complaints. It also reinforces the impression that your agency is proactive and professional.

Set up an automated task in your CRM to trigger a follow-up seven days after move-in. If the tenant reports any issues, log them and address them promptly.

## Systematising the process

The steps above are not complicated individually. The challenge is doing all of them, in the right order, every time, for every tenancy. When you are managing dozens of move-ins each month, it is easy for steps to be missed.

This is where a CRM becomes invaluable. By creating a standardised onboarding workflow — a checklist of tasks triggered automatically when a new tenancy is confirmed — you ensure that every tenant receives the same thorough, professional experience.

Each task should have a due date, an assigned team member, and a status. The CRM dashboard gives you visibility of every onboarding in progress, highlighting anything that is overdue or stuck.

## The payoff

A well-executed onboarding process reduces void periods, minimises early-tenancy complaints, protects the agency legally, and sets the tone for a positive landlord-tenant relationship. It also frees up your team's time — when the process is systematised, they spend less time chasing paperwork and more time on activities that grow the business.

Tenant onboarding is one of those processes that every agency does but few do well. The agencies that get it right stand out — not through flashy marketing, but through the simple, consistent delivery of a professional experience from the very first interaction.
