Block management — the management of communal areas, shared services, and collective obligations in multi-unit buildings — is one of the most operationally complex areas of property management. It involves multiple leaseholders, a freeholder or residents' management company, shared service charges, communal maintenance, insurance, and a regulatory framework that differs significantly from standard residential lettings.

For agencies that offer block management alongside traditional lettings, the challenge is managing two fundamentally different workflows within the same operation. A CRM that is flexible enough to handle both provides a significant operational advantage.

## What makes block management different

In a standard residential letting, the relationships are relatively simple: one landlord, one property, one or more tenants. The agent manages the property on behalf of the landlord and interfaces with the tenant.

In block management, the relationships are more complex. A single building may contain dozens of units, each owned by a different leaseholder. The building as a whole is owned by a freeholder or managed by a residents' management company. The agent manages the building — not individual units — and interfaces with all leaseholders, the freeholder, contractors, insurers, and sometimes tenants as well.

The financial model is also different. Instead of collecting rent and deducting management fees, the agent collects service charges from leaseholders, manages a shared budget, and accounts for expenditure on communal areas and services.

## Adapting your CRM for block management

A CRM designed for residential lettings may not handle block management natively, but many can be adapted with the right configuration. The key adaptations include the following.

### Building-level records

In block management, the primary record is the building, not the individual unit. Your CRM should have a record for each managed building that captures the building name and address, the number of units, the freeholder or management company details, the insurance policy, and the communal areas and shared facilities.

Individual units within the building should be linked to the building record, each with their own leaseholder details, lease terms, and service charge obligations.

### Leaseholder management

Each leaseholder is a contact who owns a unit within the building. Their record should include their contact details, unit number, lease start and end dates, service charge amount, ground rent details, and payment history.

Some leaseholders live in their unit. Others have let it to tenants. The CRM should accommodate both scenarios, linking the tenant to the unit and the unit to the building.

### Service charge tracking

Service charges are the financial backbone of block management. Each leaseholder pays a share of the building's running costs — maintenance, insurance, cleaning, utilities, management fees — and the agent is responsible for collecting these charges, managing the budget, and providing annual accounts.

Your CRM or associated accounting system should track service charge demands, payments, arrears, and budget allocations. It should also support the creation of annual service charge accounts, showing leaseholders how their money has been spent.

### Communal maintenance

Maintenance in block management involves both individual units (the leaseholder's responsibility) and communal areas (the management company's responsibility). The CRM should distinguish between these two types and track communal maintenance requests, contractor assignments, and costs against the building record rather than an individual unit.

Major works — roof repairs, external decoration, lift replacement — require a different process, often involving consultation with leaseholders, multiple quotes, and a formal decision-making process. The CRM should support this by tracking the consultation timeline, storing quotes and decisions, and recording leaseholder responses.

## Compliance in block management

Block management carries its own set of compliance obligations, many of which are more complex than those in standard residential lettings.

**Fire safety.** Multi-unit buildings have specific fire safety requirements — fire risk assessments, fire doors, emergency lighting, alarm systems, and evacuation plans. The CRM should track fire risk assessment dates, remedial actions, and inspection records.

**Building insurance.** The building insurance policy must be current at all times, and leaseholders have the right to request sight of the policy. The CRM should store the insurance certificate, track the renewal date, and record any claims.

**Health and safety.** Communal areas must be maintained in a safe condition. Risk assessments for shared spaces — car parks, gardens, corridors, lifts — should be recorded and monitored.

**Section 20 consultation.** For major works above a certain threshold, leaseholders must be formally consulted before the work proceeds. The consultation process has strict legal requirements regarding timelines and documentation. Your CRM should track each stage of the consultation and store the associated correspondence.

## Communication with leaseholders

Block management involves frequent communication with a large number of stakeholders. Building-wide notices, individual service charge reminders, AGM invitations, and consultation letters all need to be distributed efficiently.

Your CRM should support bulk communication to all leaseholders within a building, while also maintaining individual communication histories. When a leaseholder contacts you about their unit, you should be able to see both their individual correspondence and any building-wide communications that have been sent.

## Financial transparency

Leaseholders care deeply about where their service charge money goes. Transparency is not just good practice — it is a legal requirement. Your CRM and accounting integration should support the production of clear, detailed financial reports showing income received, expenditure incurred, and the balance of the service charge fund.

These reports should be available to leaseholders upon request and should be produced formally at least once per year as part of the annual accounts process.

## The block management opportunity

For agencies already managing residential lettings, adding block management services can be a significant revenue opportunity. Blocks generate recurring fee income, often with long-term management contracts that provide stability.

However, the operational complexity means that attempting block management without appropriate systems is risky. The compliance requirements are strict, the financial accounting is detailed, and the stakeholder management is demanding.

A CRM that supports both residential lettings and block management — or that can be configured to handle both — allows you to expand your services without duplicating your technology stack.
