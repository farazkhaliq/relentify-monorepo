Every employer in the United States is required to verify the identity and employment eligibility of every person they hire. This is done through Form I-9, Employment Eligibility Verification. The form itself is straightforward, but the rules around it are strict, the deadlines are tight, and the penalties for non-compliance are significant.

Whether you are hiring your first employee or your fiftieth, understanding the I-9 process is essential. This guide explains what you need to do, when you need to do it, and how to avoid common mistakes.

## What is Form I-9?

Form I-9 is a federal form published by U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS). It requires both the employee and the employer to complete specific sections to verify that the employee is authorized to work in the United States.

**Every employer must complete an I-9 for every employee hired after November 6, 1986.** This applies regardless of the employee's citizenship status. US citizens, permanent residents, and authorized non-citizens all require I-9 verification.

The I-9 is not filed with the government. Instead, employers retain completed forms and make them available for inspection if requested by authorized government agencies.

## The three-step process

### Step 1: Employee completes Section 1

The employee must complete Section 1 of Form I-9 no later than the first day of employment (the day they actually begin work for pay). This section collects:

- Full legal name
- Other last names used (if any)
- Address
- Date of birth
- Social Security number (mandatory only if the employer participates in E-Verify; otherwise voluntary)
- Citizenship or immigration status (US citizen, noncitizen national, lawful permanent resident, or authorized alien)
- Signature and date

**Important:** The employee — not the employer — completes Section 1. The employer may assist but cannot dictate answers or fill in the section on the employee's behalf (unless the employee has a disability or is illiterate, in which case a preparer or translator may help).

### Step 2: Employee presents documents

The employee must present original, unexpired documents from the Lists of Acceptable Documents to prove both identity and employment authorization. The lists are:

**List A documents** — Establish both identity and employment authorization. Examples include a US passport, permanent resident card, or foreign passport with a work authorization endorsement. A single List A document satisfies both requirements.

**List B documents** — Establish identity only. Examples include a driver's license, state-issued ID card, or school ID with a photograph.

**List C documents** — Establish employment authorization only. Examples include a Social Security card (unrestricted), birth certificate, or employment authorization document.

An employee presenting a List B document must also present a List C document. An employee presenting a List A document needs nothing else.

**Critical rule: The employee chooses which documents to present.** The employer cannot specify which documents to provide or reject valid documents because they prefer to see a different one. Doing so can constitute document abuse, which is a form of employment discrimination.

### Step 3: Employer completes Section 2

The employer must physically examine the employee's documents and complete Section 2 within three business days of the employee's first day of work.

Section 2 requires the employer to:
- Record the document title, issuing authority, document number, and expiration date
- Certify that the documents appear genuine and relate to the employee
- Sign and date the form

**Examination standard:** The employer must examine the documents in the employee's physical presence. Documents must appear to be genuine and to relate to the person presenting them. The employer is not required to be a document fraud expert — the standard is whether the documents "reasonably appear on their face to be genuine."

## Deadlines

| Action | Deadline |
|--------|----------|
| Employee completes Section 1 | No later than first day of employment |
| Employee presents documents | Within three business days of first day of employment |
| Employer completes Section 2 | Within three business days of first day of employment |
| Reverification (Section 3) | Before the employee's work authorization or document expires |

**The first day of employment** is the day the employee begins work for pay, not the date the offer was made or accepted.

## Reverification (Section 3)

When an employee's work authorization or employment authorization document has an expiration date, the employer must reverify before it expires. This is done in Section 3 of the form.

**What to reverify:** Only employment authorization. You do not reverify identity documents or documents that do not expire (such as a US passport or permanent resident card).

**When to reverify:** Before the expiration date recorded on the I-9. Start the reverification process early enough to allow the employee time to provide updated documentation.

**What you cannot reverify:** US citizens and noncitizen nationals do not require reverification. Lawful permanent residents whose permanent resident cards expire may be reverified, but it is unlawful to reverify based solely on the expiration of a permanent resident card if the employee's status is permanent.

## Retention requirements

Employers must retain completed I-9 forms for the later of:

- Three years after the date of hire, or
- One year after the date employment ends

**Example:** If you hire an employee on January 1, 2027 and they leave on June 1, 2028, you must retain the I-9 until the later of January 1, 2030 (three years from hire) or June 1, 2029 (one year from termination). In this case, January 1, 2030 is later, so that is the retention date.

Forms can be retained on paper, microfilm, microfiche, or electronically, provided the storage system meets federal requirements and forms can be produced upon request.

## E-Verify

E-Verify is an online system that allows employers to confirm the employment eligibility of newly hired employees by comparing I-9 information against government databases.

E-Verify is mandatory for:
- Federal contractors and subcontractors
- Employers in certain states that have enacted mandatory E-Verify laws
- Employers hiring under specific visa categories

For most private employers, E-Verify is voluntary but increasingly common. Even where voluntary, using E-Verify provides a rebuttable presumption that you acted in good faith in verifying employment eligibility.

## Common mistakes

### Missing the three-day deadline

Section 2 must be completed within three business days of the employee's start date. Late completion is a common violation found during audits.

**Solution:** Build I-9 completion into your onboarding checklist. Make it a non-negotiable part of the first-day process. HR and onboarding workflows within your business platform can include automatic reminders to ensure no step is missed.

### Specifying which documents to accept

Asking an employee to show "a passport" or "a driver's license and Social Security card" is unlawful. The employee has the right to choose which acceptable documents to present.

**Solution:** Provide the employee with the Lists of Acceptable Documents and let them choose. Accept any valid combination without expressing a preference.

### Accepting expired documents

All documents presented must be unexpired at the time of examination (with limited exceptions for certain receipts).

**Solution:** Check expiration dates on every document before recording them in Section 2.

### Not completing the form at all

Some employers, particularly small businesses hiring informally, simply skip the I-9. This is a violation for every employee missing a form.

**Solution:** Complete an I-9 for every employee, every time, no exceptions.

### Making copies inconsistently

There is no requirement to photocopy documents, but if you choose to make copies, you must do so for all employees. Copying documents for some employees but not others could be considered discriminatory.

### Backdating or late-completing forms

If an auditor finds forms with inconsistent dates or evidence of late completion, it raises credibility concerns for your entire I-9 program.

## Penalties

I-9 violations can result in substantial fines:

**Civil penalties per violation** range from hundreds to thousands of dollars per form, depending on the severity and whether it is a first offense or repeat violation. Penalties are adjusted annually.

**Patterns of violations** or knowingly hiring unauthorized workers can result in significantly higher penalties, debarment from government contracts, and in some cases criminal prosecution.

**Discrimination penalties** for document abuse or citizenship-status discrimination are assessed separately and can also be substantial.

## Best practices for I-9 management

1. **Train everyone involved in hiring** on proper I-9 procedures
2. **Use the most current version of Form I-9** — check the USCIS website for updates
3. **Complete Section 2 on time, every time** — no exceptions
4. **Let the employee choose their documents** — never specify or suggest
5. **Store forms securely** with controlled access
6. **Set up expiration tracking** for work authorization documents requiring reverification
7. **Conduct periodic self-audits** to identify and correct errors before a government audit
8. **Retain forms for the required period** and properly destroy them afterward
9. **Integrate I-9 tracking into your onboarding workflow** using your HR or payroll platform

Platforms like Relentify that combine onboarding, payroll, and HR functions can help ensure I-9 deadlines are tracked and completion is recorded as part of the standard new-hire process.

## Getting it right

The I-9 process is one of those compliance obligations that seems simple but catches employers off guard when done incorrectly. The rules are specific, the deadlines are firm, and the penalties are real. Build it into your standard onboarding process, train your team, and review your forms periodically. The cost of compliance is minimal compared to the cost of getting it wrong.
