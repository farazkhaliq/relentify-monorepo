Every CRM comes with a set of standard fields — name, email, phone number, address. These cover the basics, but they do not capture the nuances of how your specific agency operates. The questions you ask landlords during onboarding, the property attributes that matter in your market, the internal classifications your team uses to organise their work — these are unique to your agency, and they require custom fields to capture.

Custom fields are one of the most underused features of modern CRM systems. They are also one of the most powerful. Used well, they transform a generic tool into a system that feels like it was built specifically for your business.

## What custom fields are

A custom field is an additional data point that you add to a record type in your CRM. If the standard contact record has fields for name, email, and phone, a custom field might add "acquisition source" (how the landlord found you), "communication preference" (email, phone, or text), or "fee tier" (basic, standard, premium).

Custom fields can be added to any record type — contacts, properties, tenancies, maintenance requests, deals. They can be text fields, dropdown menus, date fields, checkboxes, number fields, or multi-select options.

The result is a CRM that captures the data your agency actually uses to make decisions, not just the data that someone at the CRM company thought would be useful.

## When to use custom fields

Custom fields are appropriate when you need to capture information that is not covered by the CRM's standard fields, and that information is used for filtering, reporting, or decision-making.

### For contacts

Standard CRM fields cover the obvious — name, email, phone. Custom fields might capture landlord type (accidental, portfolio, overseas), preferred contact time (mornings only, after 5pm), tax reference (for financial reporting), or pet policy (relevant for matching applicants to properties).

### For properties

Standard property fields typically include address, type, and status. Custom fields might capture parking spaces, garden (yes/no/shared), furnished status (unfurnished, part-furnished, fully furnished), council tax band, broadband availability, or a specific internal rating that your team uses.

### For tenancies

Standard tenancy fields include start date, end date, and rent amount. Custom fields might capture break clause date, rent review date, permitted occupants, or any special conditions agreed between the landlord and tenant.

### For maintenance requests

Standard maintenance fields include description and status. Custom fields might capture whether landlord authorisation was required, the cost band, the satisfaction rating from the tenant, or whether the issue was a repeat of a previous problem.

## Designing custom fields well

The temptation with custom fields is to add them whenever someone thinks of a data point they would like to capture. Resist this temptation. Every custom field adds complexity — it needs to be populated, maintained, and understood by everyone who uses the system.

### Start with the questions you ask

The best custom fields are answers to questions you are already asking. If every landlord intake conversation includes the question "how did you hear about us?", that is a natural custom field. If your team regularly needs to know whether a property has off-street parking, that is another.

Review your intake forms, your standard questions, and your reporting requirements. The custom fields you need will become obvious.

### Use structured inputs, not free text

Where possible, use dropdown menus, checkboxes, or predefined options rather than free-text fields. A dropdown with options "Referral," "Portal," "Website," "Walk-in," "Networking" is far more useful for reporting than a free-text field where agents type "ref from John," "found us online," "walked past the office," and seventeen other variations of the same information.

Structured inputs ensure consistency, enable filtering, and make reporting reliable. Free-text fields are appropriate for notes and descriptions, but not for data you want to analyse.

### Keep the list manageable

A contact record with forty custom fields is intimidating and will not be fully populated. Aim for five to ten custom fields per record type — the ones that genuinely inform decisions. You can always add more later if needed.

### Review and prune regularly

As your business evolves, some custom fields become irrelevant. A field that was useful three years ago may no longer serve any purpose. Review your custom fields annually and archive any that are no longer used. This keeps the interface clean and the data relevant.

## Using custom fields for segmentation

Custom fields are the foundation of effective segmentation. When your landlord records include a "landlord type" field with options for "single property," "portfolio," and "overseas," you can instantly filter your database to see only overseas landlords — and send them targeted communication about property management for non-resident owners.

When your applicant records include a "pet owner" field, you can immediately match pet owners to properties that allow pets. When your property records include a "furnished status" field, you can filter listings for applicants who need a furnished property.

This segmentation capability is only possible when the data is captured consistently in structured fields.

## Using custom fields for reporting

Custom fields feed directly into your CRM's reporting capabilities. If you track "acquisition source" for every landlord, you can produce a report showing which channels generate the most instructions. If you track "contractor satisfaction rating" for every maintenance job, you can identify your best and worst contractors.

These reports drive better decisions. If networking events generate twice as many instructions as portal advertising, you know where to invest your time. If a particular contractor consistently receives low satisfaction ratings, you know it is time to find an alternative.

## Practical examples

Here are some of the most useful custom fields that letting agencies commonly implement.

**Landlord: Acquisition source.** How the landlord found your agency. Used for marketing ROI analysis.

**Landlord: Fee tier.** The management fee level. Used for revenue analysis and segmentation.

**Property: Furnished status.** Whether the property is furnished, part-furnished, or unfurnished. Used for applicant matching.

**Property: Parking.** Whether off-street parking is available. Used for applicant matching and marketing.

**Tenancy: Break clause date.** When the tenant can exercise a break clause. Used for renewal planning.

**Applicant: Budget range.** The minimum and maximum rent the applicant can afford. Used for property matching.

**Maintenance: Landlord authorised.** Whether the landlord has approved the work. Used for workflow management and audit.

Each of these captures a specific piece of information that influences how the agency operates. Together, they create a CRM that reflects the reality of your business rather than a generic approximation.
