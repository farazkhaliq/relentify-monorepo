More businesses fail because of cash flow problems than because of lack of profit. You can be profitable on paper and still run out of money to pay your bills, your staff, or your suppliers. The gap between earning revenue and actually collecting it — combined with expenses that do not wait — is where businesses get into trouble.

A cash flow forecast is the tool that prevents this. It shows you, week by week or month by month, how much cash you expect to have. Done well, it gives you early warning of problems and time to take action.

## What is a cash flow forecast?

A cash flow forecast is a projection of the money you expect to receive (inflows) and the money you expect to pay out (outflows) over a future period. The difference between inflows and outflows gives you your net cash position for each period.

Unlike a profit and loss statement, which deals with revenue and expenses when they are earned or incurred, a cash flow forecast deals exclusively with when cash actually moves. An invoice issued in March but paid in May is a May inflow, not a March one.

## Building your cash flow forecast

### Step 1: Choose your time horizon and intervals

Most small businesses forecast 12 months ahead, broken into monthly intervals. If cash is very tight, weekly forecasts for the next 13 weeks (one quarter) give you more granular control.

### Step 2: Determine your opening cash balance

Start with how much cash you have right now — your current bank balance plus any cash on hand.

### Step 3: Forecast your cash inflows

List all the sources of cash you expect to receive:

**Sales receipts** — This is usually the biggest inflow. Base it on your sales pipeline and historical payment patterns. If you invoice on Net 30 terms and your average client pays in 35 days, use 35 days in your forecast, not 30.

**Other income** — Interest, rental income, grants, tax refunds, loan proceeds, or any other cash receipts.

Be realistic. Optimistic sales forecasts are the most common reason cash flow forecasts fail. Use your actual historical data, not your targets.

### Step 4: Forecast your cash outflows

List all the payments you expect to make:

**Fixed costs** — Rent, insurance, loan repayments, salaries, and other costs that are the same every month. These are the easiest to forecast because they are predictable.

**Variable costs** — Supplier payments, materials, shipping, commissions, and other costs that fluctuate with your sales volume. These should be linked to your revenue forecast.

**One-off costs** — Equipment purchases, tax payments, annual subscriptions, or any other non-recurring expenses. Check your calendar and prior year records for these.

**Tax payments** — Do not forget quarterly or annual tax payments. These can be large and catch businesses off guard if not planned for.

### Step 5: Calculate net cash flow

For each month:

**Net cash flow = Total inflows minus total outflows**

### Step 6: Calculate closing cash balance

For each month:

**Closing balance = Opening balance + net cash flow**

The closing balance for one month becomes the opening balance for the next.

### Step 7: Identify problem months

Look for any month where your closing balance goes negative or drops below a comfortable level. These are the months where you need to take action — either by increasing inflows, decreasing outflows, or arranging additional funding.

## A simple example

| | Jan | Feb | Mar | Apr |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Opening balance | 10,000 | 8,000 | 3,000 | 7,500 |
| **Inflows** | | | | |
| Sales receipts | 15,000 | 12,000 | 18,000 | 16,000 |
| Other income | 0 | 0 | 500 | 0 |
| **Total inflows** | 15,000 | 12,000 | 18,500 | 16,000 |
| **Outflows** | | | | |
| Rent | 2,000 | 2,000 | 2,000 | 2,000 |
| Salaries | 8,000 | 8,000 | 8,000 | 8,000 |
| Suppliers | 4,000 | 5,000 | 3,000 | 4,000 |
| Insurance | 0 | 2,000 | 0 | 0 |
| Tax payment | 3,000 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| Equipment | 0 | 0 | 1,000 | 0 |
| **Total outflows** | 17,000 | 17,000 | 14,000 | 14,000 |
| **Net cash flow** | -2,000 | -5,000 | 4,500 | 2,000 |
| **Closing balance** | 8,000 | 3,000 | 7,500 | 9,500 |

This forecast shows February is tight — the closing balance drops to 3,000. If any client pays late or an unexpected expense hits, you could have a problem. Knowing this in advance lets you prepare.

## Improving your cash flow

### Accelerate inflows

**Invoice immediately** — Do not wait days or weeks after completing work to send the invoice. Invoice the same day if possible.

**Shorten payment terms** — If you currently offer Net 30, consider Net 14 for new clients. Even shortening by a week can make a difference.

**Offer early payment incentives** — A small discount for paying within 7 days can significantly accelerate collections.

**Chase promptly** — Follow up on overdue invoices immediately, not weeks later. Automated payment reminders help enormously.

**Request deposits** — For large projects, request a deposit or milestone payments rather than billing everything at completion.

**Diversify payment methods** — Accept card payments, bank transfers, and online payment links. The easier it is to pay, the faster you get paid.

### Delay outflows (responsibly)

**Negotiate longer terms with suppliers** — If your suppliers offer Net 30 but your clients pay on Net 14, your cash flow is naturally healthy. If it is the other way around, negotiate.

**Time large purchases** — If you have a choice about when to make a major purchase, align it with a period when cash is plentiful rather than tight.

**Use payment terms strategically** — Pay on the due date, not before. Early payment is generous but not always wise when cash is constrained.

### Reduce outflows

**Review subscriptions and recurring costs** — Audit your regular payments. Cancel anything you are not using. Negotiate better rates on what you keep.

**Manage inventory carefully** — Do not tie up cash in stock you will not sell quickly. Order based on demand, not optimism.

**Control discretionary spending** — Marketing, travel, entertainment, and other discretionary costs should be reviewed regularly and aligned with cash availability.

### Build a buffer

**Maintain a cash reserve** — Aim to keep at least one to three months of fixed costs as a cash buffer. This absorbs unexpected expenses and delayed payments.

**Arrange a credit facility** — A business overdraft or line of credit provides a safety net for temporary cash shortfalls. Arrange it when you do not need it — banks are more willing to lend when your finances look healthy.

## Updating your forecast

A cash flow forecast is only useful if you keep it current. Update it regularly:

- **Weekly** if cash is tight or your business is seasonal
- **Monthly** at minimum, as part of your regular financial review
- **Immediately** when significant changes occur (a large order, a lost client, an unexpected expense)

Compare your forecast with actual results each month. Where were you accurate? Where were you off? This feedback loop improves the accuracy of future forecasts.

## Common forecasting mistakes

### Being too optimistic about sales

Base your inflows on realistic expectations, not targets. Use historical data and consider seasonal patterns. If you are forecasting a significant increase in sales, make sure you have a concrete reason to expect it.

### Forgetting irregular expenses

Annual insurance premiums, quarterly tax payments, equipment replacements, and other irregular costs can create cash flow holes if they are not in the forecast. Review your prior year's records to identify all recurring but infrequent payments.

### Not accounting for payment timing

Revenue is not cash until the client pays. If you forecast based on when you expect to invoice rather than when you expect to be paid, your forecast will be overly optimistic.

### Forecasting too far ahead with too much precision

A monthly forecast for the next 12 months is useful. A daily forecast for the next 3 years is not. The further out you go, the less accurate the forecast, so do not waste time on excessive detail for distant months.

Cash flow forecasting is not about predicting the future perfectly. It is about seeing problems coming and having time to respond. Even a rough forecast is infinitely better than no forecast at all.
