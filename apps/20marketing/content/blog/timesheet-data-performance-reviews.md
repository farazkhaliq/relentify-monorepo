# Using Timesheet Data in Performance Reviews: A Manager's Guide

Performance reviews work best when they are based on evidence rather than impressions. Timesheet data provides one of the richest sources of objective information about how an employee spends their working time — but it must be used carefully. Wielded poorly, time data in reviews feels like surveillance and destroys trust. Used thoughtfully, it provides insights that help both managers and employees understand performance patterns and identify opportunities for growth.

This guide explains how to incorporate timesheet data into performance reviews in a way that is fair, constructive, and genuinely useful.

## What Timesheet Data Can Tell You

Before using time data in reviews, understand what it can and cannot reveal.

**What it shows:**
- How an employee allocates their time across projects, clients, and activities
- Their utilisation rate (for billable-hour environments)
- Patterns in working hours (early starts, late finishes, weekend work)
- Time spent on different types of work (strategic, operational, administrative)
- Attendance and punctuality patterns
- Project delivery relative to estimated time

**What it does not show:**
- Quality of work produced
- Creativity, initiative, or problem-solving ability
- Collaboration effectiveness
- Client satisfaction
- Why time was spent in a particular way

Time data is one input among many. It provides context for a conversation, not a verdict on performance.

## Preparing for the Review

### Gather the Data

Pull timesheet reports for the review period — typically six months or a year. Focus on:

**Time allocation summary:** Where did their time go? Break it down by client, project, activity type, and billable/non-billable categories.

**Utilisation trends:** Plot their utilisation rate monthly to see trends. Is it stable, improving, or declining? Are there seasonal patterns?

**Project performance:** For completed projects, compare actual hours to estimates. Identify projects that came in under budget and those that over-ran.

**Working pattern:** Are there patterns in their daily hours? Consistent early starts? Regular late finishes? Weekend work? These may indicate dedication, poor time management, or unsustainable workload.

### Look for Stories, Not Numbers

Raw numbers without context are meaningless. A utilisation rate of 60% might be excellent for a manager with team responsibilities or poor for a delivery consultant. Context matters.

Before the review, ask yourself: What story do these numbers tell? Do they suggest someone who is effectively managing their workload, or someone who is struggling? Are there patterns that might indicate a problem — or an opportunity?

Prepare questions to explore the data with the employee, not conclusions to present at them.

### Cross-Reference With Other Data

Timesheet data is most valuable when combined with other performance indicators:

- Client feedback and satisfaction scores
- Quality metrics (error rates, revision requests)
- Peer and team feedback
- Project outcomes (on time, on budget, client retained)
- Personal development objectives and progress

An employee with moderate utilisation but exceptional client feedback and project outcomes is performing well — the numbers just do not tell the whole story.

## Conducting the Review

### Share the Data Openly

Bring the timesheet summary to the review and share it with the employee. Better yet, give them access to their own data before the meeting so they can review it and come prepared with their own observations.

Transparency builds trust. If employees feel that data is being used behind their back or selectively, they will disengage from both the review process and the timesheet system.

### Ask Questions, Do Not Make Accusations

Present data as a starting point for discussion, not as evidence of wrongdoing.

**Instead of:** "Your utilisation was only 55% last quarter, which is below target."

**Try:** "I notice your utilisation was lower last quarter than the previous one. Can you help me understand what was happening? Were there specific factors that affected your billable time?"

The employee might explain that they were heavily involved in a major pitch (non-billable but valuable), were training a new team member, or were waiting for delayed client approvals. These are legitimate explanations that the numbers alone do not reveal.

### Identify Positive Patterns

Do not use time data only to highlight problems. Look for positive stories too.

"I can see you delivered the Henderson project in 30% less time than estimated. That is impressive — what did you do differently that we could learn from?"

"Your time allocation shows you have been spending more time mentoring junior staff. I really appreciate that investment, and I can see the team's output improving as a result."

Recognising positive patterns reinforces good behaviour and shows that you are using the data fairly.

### Explore Problem Areas Constructively

When the data suggests a problem, approach it as a shared challenge to solve rather than a failing to address.

"The data shows that revision time on your projects has been higher than average. Do you think that is a briefing issue, a process issue, or something else? How can we work together to reduce it?"

"I notice you have been consistently logging long hours — 50+ hours most weeks. I am concerned about sustainability. Is there too much on your plate, or are there process improvements we could make?"

The goal is to understand root causes and find solutions, not to assign blame.

### Set Data-Informed Objectives

Use timesheet data to set specific, measurable objectives for the next review period.

"Based on what we have discussed, let us aim for your utilisation to reach 70% next quarter, with the additional capacity coming from reducing the time spent on internal administration."

"I would like to see your project delivery more closely aligned with estimates. Let us review your estimation process and see if there are adjustments we can make."

These objectives are specific and measurable because they are grounded in data. Both parties can track progress objectively.

## What Not to Do

### Do Not Use Time Data as the Sole Basis for Performance Assessment

An employee who works fewer hours but produces higher-quality output, maintains better client relationships, and mentors colleagues effectively is a strong performer — regardless of what the timesheet says.

### Do Not Compare Individuals Publicly

"Sarah logs 20% more billable hours than you" is destructive. Individual comparisons breed resentment and competition rather than collaboration. If you use benchmarks, use team or role averages rather than named individuals.

### Do Not Penalise Honest Reporting

If an employee honestly reports that a project took longer than expected, that honesty is valuable. If the response is punishment, they will underreport next time, corrupting your data and hiding real problems.

### Do Not Ignore the Context

Numbers without context are dangerous. Low utilisation during a month when the employee was on approved training, covering for a sick colleague in a non-billable role, or dealing with a personal issue is not a performance problem.

### Do Not Surprise People

Never present time data in a review that the employee has not had the opportunity to see beforehand. Surprise data creates defensiveness and shuts down productive conversation.

## Building a Healthy Culture Around Time Data

The most successful organisations treat time data as a management tool, not a disciplinary one. They create cultures where:

- Everyone tracks time honestly because the data is used fairly
- Time data informs conversations rather than dictating conclusions
- Over-runs are treated as learning opportunities, not failures
- Sustainable working patterns are valued over raw output
- Data is transparent and accessible to the people it describes

This culture starts with how managers use the data in reviews. If the first experience an employee has of their time data being discussed is constructive and fair, they will engage positively with the process going forward.

If the first experience is punitive or accusatory, they will resist time tracking and disengage from reviews — losing the benefits of both.
