You can track first response time, resolution time, and tickets per agent down to the second. But none of those metrics tell you whether the customer actually felt helped. A ticket resolved in four hours means nothing if the customer walked away frustrated by the experience.

Customer Satisfaction (CSAT) surveys close this gap. They ask the one question that all the operational metrics dance around but never directly answer: was the customer happy with the support they received?

## What CSAT is and how it works

CSAT is a metric that measures customer satisfaction with a specific interaction, typically expressed as a percentage. The survey is sent after a support ticket is resolved (or at another defined trigger point), and the customer rates their experience.

The most common format is a simple question: "How would you rate your experience?" followed by a scale. The scale varies by implementation:

- **1 to 5 stars** — The most intuitive format
- **1 to 5 numeric scale** — Where 1 is very dissatisfied and 5 is very satisfied
- **Good/Bad binary** — A thumbs up or thumbs down, the simplest possible format
- **Emoji scale** — Increasingly common for its visual immediacy

The CSAT score is calculated as the percentage of respondents who gave a positive rating. If you use a 5-point scale, positive typically means 4 or 5. If you use a binary scale, positive means thumbs up.

**Formula:** CSAT = (Number of positive responses / Total responses) x 100

A score of 85 percent means 85 percent of customers who responded were satisfied.

## Why CSAT matters

### It measures what metrics cannot

Operational metrics measure efficiency. CSAT measures effectiveness. A team can be fast but unhelpful, or slow but deeply satisfying. CSAT captures the customer's actual perception of the experience.

### It identifies systemic problems

A dip in CSAT across the team suggests a process or policy issue. A dip for a specific agent suggests a training or performance issue. A dip for a specific product area suggests a product issue. CSAT data, when segmented, reveals patterns that no other metric can surface.

### It drives improvement

Teams that measure CSAT and act on it consistently outperform those that do not. The simple act of measuring creates accountability and gives agents a clear signal about what good looks like.

### It provides early warning

CSAT often drops before other metrics deteriorate. Customers become dissatisfied before they churn. Catching that dissatisfaction early gives you a window to intervene.

## Designing your CSAT survey

### Keep it short

The survey should take less than 30 seconds to complete. One rating question and one optional text field for comments is the standard. Every additional question reduces response rates.

**Recommended format:**
1. "How would you rate your support experience?" (star rating or binary)
2. "Any additional feedback?" (optional text field)

That is it. Resist the temptation to add more questions. You can always follow up with dissatisfied customers individually.

### Choose your scale

**Binary (Good/Bad):** Highest response rates, simplest to analyse. Best for teams that want maximum participation and do not need granular data.

**5-point scale:** More granular data, slightly lower response rates. Useful for identifying the difference between "satisfied" and "very satisfied" or "dissatisfied" and "very dissatisfied."

**3-point scale (Positive/Neutral/Negative):** A middle ground that captures more nuance than binary without the complexity of 5 points.

For most support teams, the binary or 3-point scale provides enough signal with maximum response rates.

### Write a clear question

The question should be specific to the interaction, not general. "How would you rate your experience with our support team?" is better than "How satisfied are you with our company?" The former measures what you can control. The latter is influenced by product quality, pricing, and a dozen other factors.

### Make the text field optional

The comments field captures qualitative feedback that explains the rating. A 1-star rating with the comment "Had to explain my issue three times" is actionable. A 1-star rating with no comment is less useful. But making the field required will reduce response rates significantly.

## When to send the survey

### After ticket resolution

The most common trigger. The survey is sent when the ticket is marked as resolved, giving the customer a chance to rate the complete interaction.

**Timing:** Send the survey within an hour of resolution. Response rates drop significantly if you wait a day or more. The experience is freshest — and the customer is most motivated to respond — right after the interaction concludes.

### After a specific event

Some teams trigger surveys after specific events rather than all resolutions:

- After a phone call
- After a live chat session ends
- After a certain number of interactions on a ticket
- After a VIP customer's ticket is resolved

### Avoid survey fatigue

If a customer contacts you frequently, do not send a survey after every single interaction. Set a minimum interval — for example, no more than one survey per customer per week, or per month. Over-surveying leads to lower response rates and annoyance.

## Acting on CSAT data

Collecting CSAT scores without acting on them is a waste of everyone's time. Here is how to make the data useful.

### Review scores regularly

Set a cadence for reviewing CSAT data: weekly for trends, monthly for deeper analysis. Look at the overall score, but also segment by:

- **Agent** — Are some agents consistently scoring higher or lower?
- **Channel** — Is chat satisfaction different from email satisfaction?
- **Issue type** — Are certain categories of tickets generating lower satisfaction?
- **Customer tier** — Are enterprise customers more or less satisfied than others?
- **Time of day** — Are after-hours interactions less satisfying?

### Follow up on negative ratings

Every negative CSAT response is an opportunity to recover the relationship. Set up a workflow that alerts a manager or senior agent when a customer gives a negative rating. Reach out personally to understand what went wrong and, where possible, make it right.

This follow-up has a dual benefit: it sometimes turns a dissatisfied customer into a loyal one, and it always provides valuable feedback about what your team can improve.

### Share results with the team

Agents should see their own CSAT scores and understand how they compare to the team average. This transparency drives self-improvement and creates healthy accountability.

Share positive feedback widely — it is a powerful motivator. Share negative feedback privately and constructively.

### Identify and fix root causes

When CSAT drops, investigate why. Common root causes include:

- **Long wait times** — Customers waited too long for a first response
- **Multiple transfers** — The customer was bounced between agents or teams
- **Inaccurate information** — The answer they received was wrong or incomplete
- **Tone issues** — The response felt cold, dismissive, or formulaic
- **Unresolved issues** — The ticket was closed but the problem was not actually fixed

Each root cause requires a different intervention: staffing changes, training, knowledge base updates, or process improvements.

## CSAT benchmarks

Benchmarks vary by industry and channel, but general guidelines are:

- **90%+ CSAT** — Exceptional. Your team is consistently exceeding expectations
- **80-90% CSAT** — Good. Solid performance with room for improvement
- **70-80% CSAT** — Average. Significant opportunities to improve
- **Below 70% CSAT** — Concerning. Systemic issues likely need addressing

More important than the absolute number is the trend. An 85 percent score that is improving is better than a 90 percent score that is declining.

## Beyond CSAT: complementary metrics

CSAT is powerful but not complete on its own. Consider pairing it with:

**NPS (Net Promoter Score)** — Measures overall loyalty ("How likely are you to recommend us?"). Less granular for support but useful for overall brand health.

**CES (Customer Effort Score)** — Measures how easy it was for the customer to get help ("How much effort did you have to put in to resolve your issue?"). CES often predicts future behaviour better than CSAT.

**First contact resolution rate** — The percentage of issues resolved in a single interaction. Strongly correlated with CSAT.

## Common mistakes

**Sending surveys too late.** A survey sent three days after resolution gets poor response rates and inaccurate data because the customer has already moved on.

**Over-surveying.** Bombarding customers with surveys after every interaction leads to fatigue and lower response rates over time.

**Ignoring the data.** Collecting CSAT without acting on it wastes the customer's time and erodes trust. If you ask for feedback, demonstrate that you use it.

**Averaging without segmenting.** An overall CSAT of 85 percent might hide a serious problem in a specific channel, team, or issue type. Always segment.

**Treating CSAT as the only metric.** CSAT is one dimension of a complete picture. Use it alongside operational metrics for a balanced view of performance.

## Getting started

If you are not currently measuring CSAT, start simple:

1. Enable post-resolution surveys in your helpdesk
2. Use a binary (good/bad) scale for maximum response rates
3. Include an optional comments field
4. Review results weekly
5. Follow up on every negative rating
6. Share results with your team

Even this basic setup provides more insight into customer sentiment than any operational metric alone. From there, you can refine the survey design, add segmentation, and build more sophisticated workflows around the data.

The most important step is the first one. Start measuring, and the rest follows.
