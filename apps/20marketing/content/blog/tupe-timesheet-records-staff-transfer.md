When a business changes hands, a contract is awarded to a new provider, or services are brought in-house, the employees who perform that work may transfer to the new employer. In many jurisdictions, this transfer is governed by specific regulations — most notably TUPE (Transfer of Undertakings, Protection of Employment) in the UK and similar provisions across the EU, Australia, and other countries.

These regulations exist to protect workers' rights during a change of employer. But they also create specific obligations around record-keeping — and timesheet records are a central part of that picture. This guide explains what happens to timesheet data during a staff transfer and what both the outgoing and incoming employers need to do.

## What is TUPE (and its equivalents)?

TUPE regulations apply when a business or part of a business transfers from one employer to another. Common scenarios include:

- A company is sold, and its employees transfer to the buyer
- A client awards a service contract to a new provider, and the workers who deliver that service transfer to the new contractor
- A company brings outsourced services back in-house, and the outsourced workers become direct employees

The key principle is that affected employees transfer to the new employer on their existing terms and conditions — including their pay, working hours, holiday entitlement, and continuity of service.

Similar protections exist in other jurisdictions under different names: Acquired Rights Directives in the EU, business transfer provisions in Australia and New Zealand, and various state-level protections elsewhere.

## Why timesheet records matter in a transfer

### Continuity of employment

Transferred employees retain their continuity of service. This means that their accrued rights — holiday entitlement, notice period, redundancy eligibility — carry over to the new employer. Timesheet records provide evidence of hours worked and service duration with the previous employer.

### Holiday entitlement

Accrued but unused holiday must be honoured by the new employer. Accurate records of leave taken and leave remaining are essential for calculating what the new employer inherits. Timesheet data — showing days worked and days absent — supports this calculation.

### Working patterns

The new employer takes on the employees with their existing working patterns. If a worker has been consistently working four days per week (evidenced by timesheet records), the new employer cannot unilaterally change this to five days. Timesheet records establish the pattern.

### Pay calculations

If there are disputes about pay rates, overtime entitlements, or shift patterns after the transfer, historical timesheet data provides the evidence. What hours were typically worked? Was overtime regular or occasional? Were weekends or bank holidays a normal part of the pattern?

### Compliance evidence

Both the outgoing and incoming employer may need to demonstrate compliance with working time regulations during the transfer period. Complete timesheet records for the period before, during, and after the transfer are essential.

## What the outgoing employer must provide

Under most transfer regulations, the outgoing employer (the "transferor") is required to provide the incoming employer with certain employee information. While the specific requirements vary by jurisdiction, they typically include:

- Employee identity and age
- Terms and conditions of employment
- Information about disciplinary and grievance actions
- Information about any legal actions
- Details of any collective agreements

Beyond these mandatory disclosures, the outgoing employer should also provide:

### Timesheet records

Complete time records for at least the period required by local legislation (often two to six years). These records should show:

- Hours worked per day and per week
- Start and end times
- Break records
- Overtime hours
- Absence records (holiday, sickness, other)

### Leave balances

The current holiday balance for each transferring employee — days accrued, days taken, days remaining. This may include carried-over days from previous leave years.

### Working pattern data

Documentation of each employee's established working pattern — standard hours, shift pattern, regular overtime, and any flexible or variable arrangements.

### Payroll records

Pay information for the reference period, including basic pay, overtime pay, shift premiums, and any other regular payments. This helps the new employer set up payroll correctly.

## What the incoming employer must do

### Receive and verify records

The incoming employer should request all available timesheet and employment records from the outgoing employer well in advance of the transfer date. Verify the data for completeness — are all employees covered? Are there gaps in the records?

### Set up time tracking

Before the transfer date, set up the transferring employees in your timesheet system. Import their details, assign them to the correct sites or roles, and ensure they can clock in from day one of the new arrangement.

If you use a platform like [Relentify](/timesheets), this setup involves creating worker profiles, assigning them to sites, and configuring any specific shift patterns or overtime rules that carry over from their previous employment.

### Honour existing patterns

Do not change working patterns unilaterally after the transfer. If the records show that employees worked specific shifts, had particular overtime arrangements, or followed established break patterns, these should continue under the new employer — at least until any changes are agreed through proper consultation.

### Track the transition

Maintain detailed timesheet records for the period immediately after the transfer. This is a high-risk period for disputes, and complete records provide the evidence needed to resolve them.

## Common problems during transfers

### Incomplete records from the outgoing employer

The most frequent problem is receiving incomplete or poorly organised records from the previous employer. Paper timesheets may be missing, digital records may be in an incompatible format, or records may simply not exist for some workers.

**Mitigation:** Request records as early as possible. Specify the format and detail you need. If records are incomplete, document the gap and request whatever supplementary information the outgoing employer can provide.

### Disputes about working patterns

Transferred employees may claim that their working pattern differed from what the records show — for example, that they regularly worked overtime that was not recorded. Without timesheet evidence, these disputes are difficult to resolve.

**Mitigation:** If the outgoing employer's records are unreliable, consider asking transferring employees to confirm their typical working pattern in writing before or shortly after the transfer.

### Holiday entitlement disagreements

The most contentious area is often holiday. If the outgoing employer's records are unclear about how much leave has been taken, the incoming employer may inherit a liability they cannot quantify.

**Mitigation:** Reconcile holiday records before the transfer date. Get written confirmation of each employee's leave balance from the outgoing employer. If there is a dispute, err on the side of the employee — it is easier to manage a small additional cost than a tribunal claim.

### Payroll setup errors

If the incoming employer does not have accurate pay and hours data, the first payroll after the transfer may contain errors — incorrect rates, wrong hours, or missing overtime. This is damaging to employee relations at the most sensitive time.

**Mitigation:** Run a parallel calculation before the first payroll: calculate what each employee should receive based on the outgoing employer's data and compare it to what your system produces. Resolve discrepancies before the paycheques go out.

## Best practices for transfers

### Start early

Begin requesting and reviewing records at least one month before the transfer date. Two months is better. Rushed data transfers lead to incomplete records and errors.

### Document everything

Keep a record of what information was requested, what was received, and any gaps. This documentation protects the incoming employer if problems arise later.

### Communicate with employees

Transferring employees are often anxious about the change. Reassure them that their working patterns, pay, and leave entitlements will be honoured. Explain how the new timesheet system works and provide training or support as needed.

### Maintain the outgoing records

Even after the transfer, the outgoing employer should retain copies of employee records for the legally required period. The incoming employer should also retain the records received, as they form part of the ongoing employment record.

## Summary

Staff transfers under TUPE and similar regulations are high-stakes events where accurate records make the difference between a smooth transition and a costly dispute. Timesheet records — showing hours worked, overtime, breaks, absences, and working patterns — are central to this process.

Both the outgoing and incoming employer have responsibilities: one to provide complete, accurate records, and the other to receive, verify, and build upon them. The businesses that handle transfers well are those that treat timesheet data as a strategic asset, not an administrative afterthought.
