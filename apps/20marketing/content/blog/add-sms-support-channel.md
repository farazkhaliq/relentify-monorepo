Email gets buried. Chat requires the customer to be on your website. Phone calls put people on hold. But a text message? It lands on the lock screen, gets read within minutes, and requires no app downloads, no browser tabs, and no waiting on hold.

SMS is one of the oldest digital communication channels, yet it remains one of the most effective for customer support. With open rates consistently above 95 percent and response rates that dwarf email, text messaging offers a direct line to customers that no other channel can match.

Despite this, many support teams overlook SMS entirely. They invest in live chat, social messaging, and phone support while ignoring the one channel that works on every mobile phone ever made — no smartphone required, no data connection needed.

## Why SMS works for support

### Universal accessibility

SMS works on every mobile phone. Not every customer has WhatsApp. Not every customer uses Facebook Messenger. Not every customer has reliable internet access. But virtually every customer with a phone number can send and receive text messages.

This makes SMS particularly valuable for reaching demographics that are less likely to use app-based messaging: older customers, customers in rural areas with limited connectivity, and customers in markets where smartphone adoption is lower.

### Immediate attention

Text messages create a sense of immediacy that email cannot match. Studies consistently show that the majority of text messages are read within three minutes of delivery. For time-sensitive support interactions — appointment confirmations, delivery updates, security alerts — there is no faster way to reach a customer.

### Asynchronous and convenient

Like messaging apps, SMS is asynchronous. Customers can reply when it suits them, without needing to stay on a call or keep a chat window open. This convenience makes it ideal for support interactions that span hours or days, where the customer needs to gather information or check something before responding.

### No app required

Every other messaging channel requires the customer to have a specific app installed. SMS requires nothing beyond the default messaging app that comes with every phone. There is no friction, no download, no account creation.

## Setting up SMS support

### Choose your SMS provider

SMS support requires a telephony provider that connects your helpdesk to the SMS network. Common providers include Twilio, MessageBird, Vonage, and Plivo. Your helpdesk platform may have built-in integrations with one or more of these.

When selecting a provider, consider:

- **Coverage** — Does the provider support the countries where your customers are located?
- **Pricing** — SMS costs vary by country. Understand the per-message pricing for both sending and receiving
- **Number types** — Do you need a local number, a toll-free number, or a short code?
- **Reliability** — Check the provider's uptime track record and delivery rates
- **Two-way capability** — Ensure the provider supports both sending and receiving messages, not just one-way notifications

### Get a dedicated support number

You will need a phone number dedicated to customer support. This can be a local number (which looks familiar to customers in your area), a toll-free number (which signals professionalism and accessibility), or a short code (which is easy to remember but more expensive to set up).

Some businesses use the same number for both voice and SMS support. This simplifies the customer experience — one number for everything — but requires your system to handle both channels.

### Connect to your helpdesk

Like any other channel, SMS should flow into your helpdesk so that text messages create tickets, agents respond from the unified interface, and conversations are tracked with the same tools and SLAs as email or chat.

When properly integrated:

- An incoming text creates a new ticket or adds to an existing conversation with that phone number
- Agents reply from the helpdesk, and the response is sent as a text message
- The full conversation history is visible in the ticket, regardless of how many messages have been exchanged
- Routing rules and automations apply to SMS tickets just like other channels

## Managing SMS conversations

### Keep messages short

SMS has a 160-character limit per segment. While modern phones handle longer messages by splitting and reassembling them, the cost per message increases with length. More importantly, the medium itself favours brevity. Customers expect short, clear messages — not paragraphs.

Train your team to be concise. Replace a three-paragraph email response with a two-sentence text message and a link to a help article if more detail is needed.

### Respect the channel's expectations

Customers who text you expect a conversational, informal tone. Use plain language, avoid jargon, and write as you would speak. At the same time, maintain professionalism and include all necessary information.

Good: "Hi Sarah, your refund of $45.00 has been processed. You should see it in your account within 3-5 business days."

Bad: "Dear Valued Customer, We wish to inform you that your refund request reference #RF-2026-0412 has been approved and processed in accordance with our standard refund policy. Please allow 3 to 5 business days for the credit to appear on your statement."

### Manage opt-in and opt-out

SMS is subject to stricter regulations than most other channels. In many jurisdictions, you must obtain explicit consent before sending text messages to customers, and you must provide a clear way to opt out.

- Include opt-out instructions in your first message: "Reply STOP to unsubscribe"
- Honour opt-out requests immediately
- Maintain records of consent for compliance purposes
- Never send marketing messages through your support SMS number unless the customer has explicitly opted in

### Handle multimedia

While traditional SMS is text-only, MMS (Multimedia Messaging Service) allows you to send and receive images, videos, and files. If your provider and helpdesk support MMS, customers can text you photos of damaged products, screenshots of errors, or documents that need review.

This capability turns SMS from a simple text channel into something much more versatile.

## Proactive SMS support

SMS is not just for receiving inbound requests. It is also highly effective for proactive outreach.

**Appointment reminders.** A text message the day before an appointment reduces no-shows by up to 40 percent.

**Delivery notifications.** "Your order has been dispatched and will arrive tomorrow between 2 PM and 4 PM" — practical information delivered directly to the customer's pocket.

**Service alerts.** If you are experiencing a known issue, a proactive text to affected customers reduces inbound volume because customers do not need to contact you to find out what is happening.

**Follow-up surveys.** A simple "How was your experience? Reply 1-5" after ticket resolution collects feedback with minimal effort from the customer.

**Ticket updates.** "Your support ticket #4521 has been resolved. Reply if you need further help" — keeps the customer informed without requiring them to log in to a portal.

## SMS and automation

Automation works particularly well with SMS because of the channel's simplicity.

**Auto-acknowledgement.** When a customer texts your support number, an automated reply confirms receipt and provides an estimated response time.

**Keyword routing.** If the customer's message contains specific keywords (like "billing" or "cancel"), the ticket can be automatically routed to the appropriate team.

**Status updates.** When a ticket moves from "open" to "in progress" to "resolved," automated text messages keep the customer informed at each stage.

**Chatbot integration.** Simple chatbots can handle routine SMS queries entirely — checking order status, confirming business hours, or collecting information before handing off to a human agent.

## Measuring SMS performance

Track SMS as a distinct channel in your reporting:

- **Volume** — How many SMS conversations are you handling?
- **First response time** — Are you meeting your targets for this channel?
- **Resolution time** — How does SMS compare to other channels?
- **Customer satisfaction** — Survey responses from SMS interactions
- **Opt-out rate** — If this is climbing, you may be messaging too frequently or at inappropriate times
- **Delivery rate** — What percentage of your messages are successfully delivered?

## Cost considerations

SMS is not free. Unlike email or web chat, each text message carries a per-message cost that varies by country. In some markets, sending a single message costs fractions of a penny. In others, it can cost several pence per message.

For most support teams, the per-message cost is modest relative to the value of the interaction. A support conversation that takes ten messages costs far less than a phone call, and the asynchronous nature means agents can handle multiple SMS conversations simultaneously — unlike phone calls, which demand full attention.

Budget for SMS costs and monitor them as part of your channel economics. If SMS volume is high, negotiate volume discounts with your provider.

## Getting started

Adding SMS to your support operation is typically a three-step process:

1. Select an SMS provider and obtain a phone number
2. Connect the provider to your helpdesk
3. Configure automated messages, routing rules, and SLA targets

The technical setup is straightforward. The strategic decisions — which number type to use, what response time targets to set, how to handle opt-in compliance — deserve more thought.

SMS is a deceptively simple channel with outsized impact. Its universal accessibility, immediacy, and convenience make it one of the most effective ways to communicate with customers. Adding it to your support operation fills a gap that no other channel quite covers.
