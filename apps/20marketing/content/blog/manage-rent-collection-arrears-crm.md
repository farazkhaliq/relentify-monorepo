Rent collection is the most fundamental service a letting agency provides. If the rent does not get collected, nothing else matters — not the compliance tracking, not the maintenance management, not the market reports. The landlord's income depends on it, and the agency's relationship with the landlord depends on delivering it reliably.

Most of the time, rent collection is straightforward. The standing order is set up, the payment arrives on the due date, and the process runs smoothly. But when a payment does not arrive — whether because of a tenant's financial difficulties, a banking error, or simple oversight — the agency needs to act quickly, consistently, and professionally.

A CRM that tracks rent payments and automates the arrears management process ensures that no late payment goes unnoticed and no follow-up is delayed.

## Tracking rent payments

Your CRM should maintain a payment record for every tenancy, showing the expected rent amount, the due date, and the actual payments received. When a payment is received, it should be recorded against the relevant tenancy automatically — through bank feed integration — or manually by the accounts team.

The system should highlight any discrepancies immediately. If the expected payment for a property is 1,200 and only 1,000 has been received, the shortfall should be flagged. If no payment has been received by the end of the due date, the arrears process should be triggered automatically.

## The arrears escalation process

Rent arrears should be managed through a defined escalation process with clear stages, timelines, and actions. A structured process ensures that every arrears case is handled consistently, regardless of which team member is responsible.

### Day one: Automated notification

On the day rent is due but not received, an automated notification should alert the responsible team member. At this stage, the cause might be a banking delay rather than a genuine arrears issue. No action is needed beyond awareness.

### Day three: First contact

If the payment has still not arrived by day three, make contact with the tenant. A phone call or email — friendly and professional — is appropriate. "We noticed that your rent payment for this month has not yet arrived. Could you please check that the standing order has been processed?"

This first contact resolves the majority of arrears cases. The tenant forgot to set up the standing order after changing banks, or there was a processing delay, or they had a temporary cash flow issue and can pay immediately.

### Day seven: Formal reminder

If the payment remains outstanding after seven days, send a formal written reminder. This should reference the tenancy agreement, state the amount owed, and request payment within a specified period. The tone should be firm but professional.

### Day fourteen: Landlord notification and guarantor contact

At this stage, the landlord should be informed that rent is overdue and that the agency is actively pursuing collection. If the tenant has a guarantor, the guarantor should be contacted with a formal demand for payment.

### Day twenty-one and beyond: Escalation

If rent remains unpaid after twenty-one days, further escalation may be necessary — formal demand letters, potential mediation, or the beginning of a legal process. The specific actions depend on the jurisdiction and the circumstances of the case.

Throughout this process, every action — every call, every email, every letter — should be logged in the CRM against the tenant's record and the tenancy. This creates a complete audit trail that is invaluable if the matter escalates to a legal proceeding.

## Automating the process

The escalation process described above follows a predictable pattern: if payment is not received by date X, do action Y. This makes it an ideal candidate for CRM automation.

An automated arrears workflow can generate tasks at each escalation stage, send template communications, notify the responsible team member, and update the arrears status. The team member's role is to handle the human interactions — the phone calls, the sensitive conversations, the judgment calls — while the system handles the scheduling, the reminders, and the record-keeping.

## Partial payments

Partial payments add complexity. If a tenant owes 1,200 and pays 800, the system should record the payment, calculate the outstanding balance, and apply the arrears process to the remaining 400.

Your CRM should handle partial payments gracefully, maintaining a running balance for each tenancy and tracking the arrears amount accurately over time.

## Rent payment patterns

Over time, your CRM accumulates data about each tenant's payment patterns. Does this tenant consistently pay on time? Do they pay a few days late every month? Did they have one late payment two years ago and have been perfect since?

This pattern data informs how you handle individual arrears cases. A tenant who has paid on time for three years and is suddenly late deserves empathy and investigation — something may have changed in their circumstances. A tenant who is habitually late may need a different conversation about expectations and consequences.

## Landlord reporting

Landlords want to know that their rent has been collected — and they want to know quickly when it has not been. Your CRM should provide landlords with regular rent statements and immediate notification of any arrears.

Proactive communication about rent arrears builds landlord confidence. "We wanted to let you know that this month's rent has not yet arrived. We have contacted the tenant and will update you within 48 hours" is a message that demonstrates professionalism. Hearing about the arrears only when the monthly statement arrives is a message that erodes trust.

## Prevention strategies

The best arrears management is prevention. Several CRM-supported practices can reduce the frequency and severity of rent arrears.

**Thorough referencing.** Catching affordability issues before the tenancy begins is the first line of defence. Your CRM should track referencing outcomes and flag any concerns.

**Clear onboarding.** Ensuring that the standing order is set up correctly at the start of the tenancy, and verifying the first payment, prevents many early-tenancy arrears.

**Early intervention.** The automated day-one notification ensures that arrears are caught immediately, before a small problem becomes a large one.

**Regular communication.** Tenants who have a good relationship with their agent are more likely to communicate proactively if they are facing financial difficulties. This gives the agency time to work with the tenant on a solution before arrears accumulate.
