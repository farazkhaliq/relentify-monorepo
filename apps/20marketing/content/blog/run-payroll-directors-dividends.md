Running payroll for company directors is different from running it for regular employees. Directors have unique rules for National Insurance, they often split their income between salary and dividends, and the optimal payment structure changes with each tax year.

This guide explains how director payroll works, the tax-efficient salary and dividend approach, and the pitfalls to watch out for.

## Directors are employees too

A company director who receives a salary is an employee of the company for payroll purposes. Even if you are a sole director of your own limited company, you need to run payroll, report to HMRC through RTI, and comply with all the same rules as any other employer.

The key differences are in how National Insurance is calculated and how directors typically structure their remuneration.

## The salary and dividend approach

Most directors of owner-managed companies do not take all their income as salary. Instead, they take a relatively low salary and draw the rest as dividends from company profits.

### Why?

Dividends are not subject to National Insurance contributions — neither employee NI nor employer NI. Salary is subject to both. This means the combined tax burden on dividends is lower than on salary at most income levels.

The typical approach is:

1. **Pay a salary** up to a level that optimises tax efficiency (usually around the Primary Threshold for NI, or the personal allowance for income tax)
2. **Draw dividends** from remaining company profits after corporation tax

The optimal salary level depends on several factors and changes with each tax year as thresholds adjust.

### Optimal salary levels

There are generally two strategies:

**Strategy 1: Salary at the NI Primary Threshold**
- The director earns enough salary to qualify for State Pension credits
- No employee NI is payable (earnings are at or below the threshold)
- No employer NI is payable (earnings are at or below the Secondary Threshold)
- Income tax may be payable if the Primary Threshold is above the personal allowance

**Strategy 2: Salary at the personal allowance**
- The director uses their full personal tax-free allowance through salary
- No income tax on the salary
- Employee and employer NI may be payable on the portion between the NI threshold and the personal allowance
- Slightly higher total NI cost, but the salary is a deductible business expense for corporation tax purposes

The right choice depends on the company's corporation tax rate, the NI thresholds, and the personal allowance in the current tax year. Consult your accountant for the optimal figure for your specific circumstances.

## Directors' National Insurance

Directors have a special NI calculation method. Unlike regular employees, whose NI is calculated on each pay period independently, directors' NI can be calculated on an annual basis.

### Annual calculation method

Under the annual method:
- The annual NI thresholds are applied to the director's cumulative earnings for the tax year
- NI is recalculated at each pay date based on total earnings to date
- This prevents overpayment of NI when the director takes irregular payments

This method is particularly useful for directors who take a large payment in one month and nothing in others. Without the annual method, a single large payment could push them above the NI threshold unnecessarily.

### Alternative method

You can choose to calculate directors' NI on a standard cumulative per-period basis, the same as regular employees. This is simpler but may result in higher NI if the director's pay varies significantly between periods.

Choose the method at the start of the tax year and apply it consistently. Your [payroll software](/payroll) should offer both options.

## Setting up director payroll

### Step 1: Register as an employer
If you have not already, register your company with HMRC as an employer. You will receive a PAYE reference and an Accounts Office reference.

### Step 2: Set up payroll software
Enter the director as an employee with the "director" flag enabled. This ensures the software applies the correct NI calculation method.

### Step 3: Choose a pay frequency
Most directors pay themselves monthly, but some prefer quarterly or even annually. Monthly is the simplest for RTI compliance and pension auto-enrolment assessment.

### Step 4: Set the salary level
Enter the agreed annual salary, divided by the number of pay periods.

### Step 5: Run payroll regularly
Even if the salary amount is the same each month, you must run payroll and submit an FPS to HMRC on or before each payday.

## Dividends: outside payroll

Dividends are not processed through payroll. They are a distribution of company profits to shareholders and are handled through the company's accounts.

To pay a dividend:
1. **Check the company has sufficient distributable profits**: You cannot pay dividends if the company does not have enough retained profit after tax.
2. **Hold a board meeting** (or pass a written resolution) to declare the dividend.
3. **Issue a dividend voucher**: A written record showing the date, amount, and who received it.
4. **Pay the dividend**: Transfer the money from the company account to the director's personal account.

Dividends are taxed on the director's personal tax return at the dividend tax rates, which are lower than income tax rates on employment income. Each person also has a tax-free dividend allowance.

Directors must report dividend income on their self-assessment tax return and pay any tax due by 31 January following the end of the tax year.

## Common mistakes

### Not running payroll at all
Some sole directors believe they do not need payroll because they only take dividends. But if you take any salary — even a small amount — you must run payroll and report to HMRC. Failure to do so can result in penalties.

### Taking dividends without sufficient profits
Dividends can only be paid from distributable profits. If the company has made a loss or has already distributed all its profits, further dividends are unlawful. Your accountant should prepare accounts showing distributable profits before each dividend is declared.

### Forgetting Employment Allowance restrictions
Sole director companies with no other employees cannot claim the Employment Allowance. If you add an employee, you may become eligible.

### Using the wrong NI calculation method
If you calculate directors' NI per period instead of annually, you may over-collect NI during high-payment months. This gets corrected at year-end but creates unnecessary cash flow issues.

### Not keeping dividend records
Every dividend payment needs a voucher and a board minute. Without these records, HMRC may challenge the payment or treat it as salary, which has significantly higher tax implications.

### Ignoring pension auto-enrolment
Even directors can trigger auto-enrolment requirements if they earn above the earnings trigger. If you are a sole director, you are generally exempt, but if you employ other people and you earn above the threshold, you may need to auto-enrol yourself.

## Year-end for directors

At the end of the tax year:
- Issue yourself a P60 (if you were on the payroll on 5 April)
- Finalise your RTI submissions
- Review your annual NI calculation to ensure the correct total has been deducted
- File your personal self-assessment tax return including both salary and dividend income
- Ensure the company's accounts correctly reflect salary costs and dividend payments

## Working with an accountant

Most director-owners work with an accountant who advises on the optimal salary and dividend split each year. The accountant can also help with:

- Calculating corporation tax implications
- Preparing dividend vouchers and board minutes
- Filing company accounts and corporation tax returns
- Self-assessment tax returns

## Key takeaways

- Directors must run payroll if they take any salary
- The salary and dividend split is the most tax-efficient approach for most director-owners
- Directors' NI is calculated annually unless you choose the alternative method
- Dividends are outside payroll but must be properly documented
- The optimal salary level changes each tax year — review it annually with your accountant

Getting director payroll right from the start saves time, reduces your tax burden, and keeps you compliant with HMRC requirements.
