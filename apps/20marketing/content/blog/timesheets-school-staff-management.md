# Timesheets for School Staff: Managing Teachers, TAs, and Support Workers

Schools are complex employers. A single school might have salaried teachers on term-time-only contracts, hourly-paid teaching assistants, casual supply staff, lunchtime supervisors working 90 minutes a day, breakfast and after-school club workers, cleaning teams, catering staff, and peripatetic music teachers who visit weekly. Each category has different working patterns, pay arrangements, and contractual terms.

Managing this diverse workforce without a proper timesheet system leads to payroll errors, compliance gaps, and frustration for staff who feel their hours are not recorded fairly. This guide covers how schools can implement effective time tracking that works for all staff types.

## Why Schools Need Better Time Tracking

Many schools still rely on paper sign-in books, manual registers, or the assumption that salaried staff work their contracted hours. While this might work for a small school with stable staffing, it breaks down as complexity increases.

**Payroll accuracy:** Hourly-paid staff — teaching assistants, lunchtime supervisors, casual workers — need accurate hour records for correct pay. Manual tracking errors lead to overpayments, underpayments, and time-consuming corrections.

**Supply staff management:** When regular staff are absent, supply cover must be tracked for both the absent person (to manage sick leave records) and the cover staff (for payment).

**Overtime and additional hours:** Teaching assistants who cover after-school clubs, support staff who work during school holidays for deep cleaning, and administrators who put in extra hours during admissions season all need their additional hours recorded.

**Working time compliance:** Schools must comply with working time regulations, including rest period requirements and maximum weekly hours. Without time records, demonstrating compliance is impossible.

**Budget management:** Staff costs typically represent 70-80% of a school's budget. Understanding exactly where those costs go — broken down by role, department, and activity — enables better financial planning.

## Different Staff Types, Different Approaches

### Salaried Teachers

Full-time salaried teachers typically do not need to record their daily hours — they are paid an annual salary regardless of the specific hours worked each day. However, there are scenarios where teacher time tracking is valuable:

**Directed time:** Many teacher contracts specify a maximum number of directed hours per year. Tracking meetings, training days, parents' evenings, and other directed activities ensures the school does not exceed the contractual limit.

**PPA time:** Planning, preparation, and assessment time is a contractual entitlement. Tracking that teachers receive their full PPA allocation protects both the school and the teacher.

**Absence recording:** Sick days, personal leave, and other absences should be recorded systematically for HR management and supply cover budgeting.

### Teaching Assistants and Support Staff

Many TAs and support staff are paid hourly or work term-time-only contracts with specific daily hours. These staff need accurate time recording.

Implement a simple clock-in/clock-out system — either through a fixed terminal in the staff room or via a mobile app on their phone. Record start time, end time, and any break periods.

For TAs who split their time between classes or who cover for absent colleagues in different roles, record the specific assignment alongside the hours. This helps with both payroll (if different roles carry different rates) and resource analysis.

### Casual and Supply Staff

Supply teachers, exam invigilators, sports coaches, and other casual workers present the biggest tracking challenge because they work irregularly and may not be familiar with school systems.

Create a streamlined process: the office records the casual worker's attendance each day, noting arrival time, departure time, and the role covered. This can be done through a visitor/staff sign-in system or by having the covering teacher report to the office at start and end of day.

### Lunchtime and Break Supervisors

These staff often work very short shifts — perhaps 60-90 minutes at lunchtime. Despite the short duration, their hours must be recorded accurately for payroll and compliance purposes.

A simple daily register, signed by the supervisor and countersigned by a senior staff member, works well for these roles. Alternatively, include them in the same clock-in/clock-out system used by other hourly staff.

### Catering and Cleaning Staff

Kitchen and cleaning teams often work split shifts or hours that differ from the main school day. Cleaners might work 6am-8am and 4pm-6pm. Catering staff might work 7am-2pm.

Their [timesheet system](https://relentify.com/features/timesheets) needs to accommodate these non-standard patterns and accurately capture the actual hours worked, not just the scheduled hours.

## Setting Up the System

### Choose a Proportionate Solution

A small primary school with 15 staff does not need enterprise workforce management software. A paper register with monthly collation might suffice. A large secondary school with 150+ staff, including dozens of part-time and casual workers, needs a digital system.

For mid-sized schools, a digital timesheet system that offers mobile clock-in, simple approval workflows, and export to payroll provides the right balance of functionality and simplicity.

### Define Work Patterns

Before implementing any system, document the standard work patterns for each staff category:

- Teachers: term dates, directed time budget, PPA allocation
- TAs: daily contracted hours, term-time-only dates, any flexibility arrangements
- Support staff: contracted hours, any shift patterns
- Casual staff: rate per hour or day, booking process

These patterns form the basis of your timesheet configuration and make it easy to spot anomalies (a TA clocking in two hours early, for example).

### Approval Workflows

Implement a simple approval process:

1. Staff record their hours (or hours are recorded for them)
2. Their line manager reviews and approves weekly or fortnightly
3. Approved hours go to payroll

For most schools, a two-step process (record and approve) is sufficient. Adding more layers slows things down without adding value.

### Integration With Payroll

If your school uses a payroll bureau or local authority payroll service, ensure your timesheet data can be exported in a compatible format. Many schools manually re-key timesheet data into payroll systems — a duplication of effort that introduces errors.

A digital timesheet system that exports directly to your payroll format eliminates this step and improves accuracy.

## Managing Absences and Cover

One of the most operationally important uses of time tracking in schools is managing staff absences and arranging cover.

When a teacher or TA calls in sick, several things need to happen quickly:

1. Record the absence against the individual's record
2. Arrange cover (supply teacher, internal cover, or cover supervisor)
3. Record the cover arrangement
4. Track the cost of cover

Over time, this data reveals patterns — which days have the highest absence rates, which terms are worst, which roles are hardest to cover — enabling proactive planning.

Track the total cost of supply cover monthly and compare it to budget. Supply costs can spiral quickly, and early visibility gives you the chance to investigate causes and implement solutions (wellness programmes, flexible working arrangements, or building an internal cover pool).

## Reporting and Analysis

Timesheet data from schools supports several important management functions.

**Budget monitoring:** Compare actual staff costs to budget monthly. Break costs down by department, role, and funding source (especially important for staff funded by specific grants or pupil premium).

**Utilisation analysis:** For support staff, understand how their time is allocated between classes, year groups, and activities. This ensures resources are directed where they are most needed.

**Absence reporting:** Generate absence reports by individual, team, and school-wide. Compare to benchmarks and identify trends that need attention.

**Compliance reporting:** Demonstrate that working time regulations are being met, that PPA entitlements are being honoured, and that directed time budgets are within limits.

## Practical Tips

**Make it easy:** The simpler the system, the higher the compliance. Staff who find timesheets burdensome will resist using them or submit inaccurate data.

**Communicate the purpose:** Explain to staff why time tracking is being implemented. Frame it around fair pay and resource management, not surveillance.

**Be consistent:** Apply the same standards to all staff types. If TAs must clock in, senior leaders should record their hours too (even if they are salaried).

**Review regularly:** Use the data. Share relevant reports with governors and senior leadership. If the data is not informing decisions, the effort of collecting it is wasted.

**Respect the data:** Time tracking data is personal information. Store it securely, limit access to those who need it, and retain it only for the required period.

Effective time tracking in schools is not about bureaucracy — it is about ensuring that every member of staff is paid fairly, that resources are allocated wisely, and that the school can demonstrate compliance with its obligations. The right system, implemented thoughtfully, achieves all of this with minimal burden on the people it is designed to support.
