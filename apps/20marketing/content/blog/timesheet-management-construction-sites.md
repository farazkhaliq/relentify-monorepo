Construction is one of the most timesheet-intensive industries. A typical project involves multiple trades, subcontractors, and varying skill levels — all working across different phases of the build, often at multiple sites simultaneously. Labour is usually the largest single cost on a construction project, and without accurate time records, that cost is impossible to manage.

Yet construction is also one of the industries most likely to rely on paper timesheets, manual records, or informal tracking. The result is predictable: payroll disputes, inaccurate project costing, compliance gaps, and invoicing errors that strain relationships with clients and subcontractors.

This guide explains what construction contractors need from a timesheet system and how to implement one that fits the realities of site-based work.

## Why construction timesheets are complex

### Multiple workforce types

A construction site may have a mix of:

- Direct employees paid by the contractor
- Subcontractors billing for their own hours or a fixed price
- Agency workers supplied by staffing firms
- Self-employed individuals engaged under various arrangements

Each type has different pay structures, tax implications, and record-keeping requirements. The timesheet system needs to accommodate all of them.

### Changing sites

Workers often move between sites during a project or between projects entirely. A plasterer might spend three days on one site and two on another in the same week. Each site may have a different client, budget, and cost code. Time needs to be recorded per site to support accurate project costing and client billing.

### Weather and delays

Construction work is weather-dependent. Rain can halt exterior work, frost can prevent concrete pours, and extreme heat can trigger mandatory breaks. These interruptions create non-standard timesheets — partial shifts, early finishes, weather-related standby time — that need to be recorded accurately.

### Phased work

As a project progresses, different trades arrive and depart. Groundworkers are first on site, followed by bricklayers, electricians, plumbers, roofers, and finally decorators. The workforce composition changes weekly, and the timesheet system needs to handle workers who may only be on site for a few days.

## What contractors need from a timesheet system

### Site-based clock-in with GPS

Workers should clock in and out at each site they visit. GPS verification confirms they were physically present, which matters for:

- **Client billing**: Proving that workers were on site for the hours invoiced
- **Subcontractor verification**: Confirming that subcontractor labour was delivered as claimed
- **Compliance**: Meeting record-keeping requirements for health and safety and employment law

Tools like [Relentify](/timesheets) allow contractors to set up geofences for each site, so clock-ins are automatically associated with the correct project and client.

### Cost code allocation

Every hour worked on a construction project should be allocable to a cost code — a specific phase, trade, or budget line. This allows the project manager to compare actual labour hours against the estimate and identify areas where the project is over or under budget.

The timesheet system should allow workers or managers to tag each entry with a cost code, either at the point of clock-in or during approval.

### Subcontractor timesheet management

If you use subcontractors who bill by the hour, you need verified records of their hours to check against invoices. A shared timesheet system — where subcontractors clock in using the same platform as direct employees — simplifies this verification and eliminates the back-and-forth of paper timesheets and manual comparisons.

### Overtime and premium tracking

Construction work frequently involves overtime, weekend work, and bank holiday shifts — all of which may attract premium rates. The system should track these hours separately and apply the correct rates automatically, based on the worker's contract and the project's rate structure.

### Daily site reports

Many clients and main contractors require daily reports showing who was on site, their trade, and the hours worked. This is partly for project management and partly for health and safety — the site needs to know who is present in case of an emergency.

A timesheet system that records real-time attendance can generate these reports automatically, replacing the manual sign-in sheets that are still common on many sites.

## Implementing timesheets on a construction site

### Step 1: Set up your sites

Create a record for each active site in the system. Include the site address, the client or main contractor, the project reference, and the expected duration.

### Step 2: Configure worker types

Set up different categories for your workforce — direct employees, subcontractors, agency workers — with the appropriate pay rules and reporting requirements for each.

### Step 3: Define cost codes

Work with your project managers or quantity surveyors to define the cost codes used on each project. Map these into the timesheet system so that hours can be allocated from day one.

### Step 4: Equip workers

Ensure every worker has access to the clock-in system. For most construction sites, a mobile app on the worker's smartphone is the most practical approach. For sites where personal phones are not practical (restricted areas, safety concerns), a shared tablet at the site entrance serves as a kiosk.

### Step 5: Brief the team

Construction workers are generally pragmatic — if the system is quick and simple, they will use it. Show them how to clock in and out (one tap), explain why it matters (accurate pay, correct site records), and address any concerns about GPS tracking (only at clock-in, not continuous).

### Step 6: Integrate with project management

Feed timesheet data into your project management and costing tools. This creates a feedback loop where actual labour hours inform future estimates, project progress reports, and client valuations.

## Common challenges and solutions

### Workers arriving before the site is open

On busy sites, workers may arrive early and want to clock in before the official start time. Decide whether to allow this (and pay for the extra time) or restrict clock-ins to the scheduled start time. The system should support whichever policy you choose.

### Multiple trades, one site

When several subcontractors are working on the same site, each with their own workforce, the site manager needs a unified view of attendance. A shared timesheet platform avoids the fragmentation of each subcontractor using a different system.

### Short-duration workers

Temporary workers who are on site for just a few days need to be onboarded quickly. The system should allow fast setup — name, trade, phone number — without extensive configuration.

### Disputed hours between contractor and client

When a client disputes the hours invoiced, GPS-verified timesheet records provide objective evidence. A record showing that five electricians clocked in at 7:02am and clocked out at 4:58pm, with GPS coordinates matching the site, is difficult to argue with.

## The financial impact

Labour typically represents 40 to 60 percent of a construction project's cost. Even small inaccuracies in time recording have a meaningful financial impact:

- **5 workers x 15 minutes unrecorded overtime per day = 6.25 hours per week** of labour that is either unbilled to the client or underpaid to the workers
- **Inaccurate cost code allocation** means the project manager cannot identify which phases are over budget until it is too late to correct
- **Unverified subcontractor hours** may result in paying for time that was not actually worked

Accurate timesheet data is not an overhead — it is a tool for protecting margins, managing costs, and maintaining commercial relationships.

## Health and safety considerations

Construction sites have specific health and safety requirements around workforce tracking:

- **Site induction records**: Workers must complete an induction before starting work. Linking induction status to the timesheet system ensures that un-inducted workers cannot clock in.
- **Emergency roll call**: In the event of an evacuation, the site needs to know who is present. Real-time clock-in data provides an instant headcount.
- **Working time compliance**: Construction workers, particularly those working on multiple sites or for multiple contractors, may exceed safe working hours without anyone noticing. A centralised timesheet system flags these risks.

## Summary

Construction timesheet management is more complex than in most industries, but the principles are the same: record hours accurately, approve them promptly, and use the data for payroll, billing, and project management.

The key is choosing a system designed for the realities of site work — mobile, GPS-verified, multi-site, and capable of handling the diverse workforce that characterises construction projects. Get this right, and the benefits flow through every aspect of the business: accurate pay, defensible invoices, reliable project costing, and compliance peace of mind.
