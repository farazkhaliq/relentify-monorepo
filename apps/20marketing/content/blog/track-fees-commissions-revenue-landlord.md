Every landlord in your portfolio generates revenue for your agency — but not every landlord generates the same amount. Some have multiple properties, premium fee agreements, and make regular use of ancillary services. Others have a single property on your lowest fee tier. Understanding this distribution is essential for informed management.

Yet many agencies have no clear picture of the revenue each landlord generates. Management fees are calculated monthly, but they are often aggregated in the accounting system without being attributed back to individual landlords. The result is a blind spot — you do not know which landlords are your most valuable, which are barely profitable, and where the growth opportunities lie.

A CRM that tracks fees, commissions, and revenue at the landlord level provides this visibility.

## Why revenue attribution matters

### Prioritising service

When you know that one landlord generates 15,000 per year in management fees while another generates 1,200, you can allocate your resources accordingly. This does not mean providing poor service to the lower-value landlord — it means ensuring that the high-value landlord receives the level of attention that their contribution to your business warrants.

High-value landlords should receive proactive communication, quarterly portfolio reviews, and priority access to senior staff. These service touches are not expensive, but they require intentional prioritisation — and that requires knowing who your highest-value clients are.

### Identifying growth opportunities

Revenue tracking also reveals where growth is possible. A landlord who currently has two properties under management but has mentioned interest in purchasing a third represents a tangible revenue opportunity. A landlord whose properties are on a basic management fee might be open to upgrading to a premium service that includes regular inspections and detailed reporting.

Without revenue data, these opportunities are invisible. With it, you can target your growth efforts where they are most likely to succeed.

### Fee structure analysis

Tracking revenue per landlord helps you evaluate your fee structure. Are your fees competitive? Are your ancillary charges being applied consistently? Is there a segment of landlords who are unprofitable when you factor in the time they consume?

This analysis might reveal that a particular fee tier is too low to cover the service it promises, or that a specific ancillary service is underpriced relative to the effort involved.

## What to track

### Management fees

The core of your revenue — the percentage or fixed fee charged for managing each property. Track this monthly for each property, attributed to the landlord who owns it.

### Letting fees

When a property is let or re-let, the letting fee generates a one-off revenue event. Track these against the landlord and the property, noting the date and the amount.

### Renewal fees

If your agency charges for tenancy renewals, track these in the same way as letting fees — attributed to the landlord and the property.

### Ancillary fees

Many agencies charge additional fees for specific services — property inspections, inventory preparation, maintenance coordination, compliance certificate arrangement, reference processing. Each of these should be tracked individually.

### Commissions

If your agency earns commissions from referrals — insurance, utilities, mortgage referrals — attribute these to the landlord whose property or tenant generated them.

### Total revenue per landlord

The sum of all fee types for each landlord gives you a total revenue figure. This is the number that matters for prioritisation, segmentation, and strategic planning.

## Building a revenue dashboard

Your CRM should provide a dashboard view of revenue by landlord, filterable by time period, fee type, and branch.

The most useful views include a ranked list of landlords by total revenue, a breakdown of revenue by fee type, a trend showing revenue changes over time, and a segmentation of landlords into value tiers (high, medium, low).

This dashboard should be accessible to agency directors and senior managers, providing the financial visibility needed for strategic decisions.

## Revenue-based segmentation

Once you have revenue data, you can segment your landlord base and tailor your service accordingly.

**Top tier.** The landlords who generate the most revenue — typically a small percentage of your total landlord base but a large percentage of your total revenue. These landlords should receive your highest level of service, including named account management, regular reviews, and priority communication.

**Mid tier.** The bulk of your landlord base — generating moderate, steady revenue. These landlords should receive consistent, reliable service with regular check-ins and responsive communication.

**Growth tier.** Landlords who currently generate modest revenue but have the potential for more — perhaps they own other properties managed by a competitor, or they have expressed interest in expanding their portfolio. Invest in these relationships with a view to future growth.

**Low tier.** Landlords who generate minimal revenue and consume disproportionate management time. These relationships may need to be re-evaluated — either by adjusting fees to reflect the true cost of service, or by having an honest conversation about expectations.

## Landlord lifetime value

Revenue per year is useful, but lifetime value — the total revenue a landlord generates over their entire relationship with your agency — is even more powerful.

A landlord who has been with you for ten years and generates 3,000 per year has a lifetime value of 30,000. A new landlord who generates 5,000 per year but leaves after two years has a lifetime value of only 10,000.

This perspective changes how you think about retention. Keeping an existing landlord is almost always more valuable than winning a new one, because the relationship has already been built and the revenue compounds over time.

## Using revenue data in landlord conversations

Revenue data is for internal use — you would not present a landlord with a breakdown of how much money they make you. But it informs how you approach conversations.

When a high-value landlord raises a concern, you know that resolving it quickly is a priority — not because other landlords matter less, but because the business impact of losing this landlord is greater. When a landlord asks for a fee reduction, you can evaluate the request in the context of their total revenue contribution and the cost of their potential departure.

Revenue data also helps with fee increase conversations. If a landlord has been on the same fee rate for five years while your costs have increased, the data supports a conversation about bringing the fee into line with your current pricing.
