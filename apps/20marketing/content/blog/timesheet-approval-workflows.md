A timesheet without approval is just a claim. It might be accurate, or it might contain errors — honest mistakes, forgotten breaks, accidental double entries, or occasionally something more deliberate. The approval step is what turns raw time data into verified records that can safely feed into payroll, invoicing, and compliance documentation.

Yet many small businesses either skip approval entirely or implement it as a rubber-stamp exercise. Neither approach serves the business well. This article explains how to design a timesheet approval workflow that is effective without being burdensome.

## Why approval matters

### Catching errors before payroll

The most immediate benefit of approval is error detection. A manager who knows the team's schedule can quickly spot a timesheet that shows ten hours when the shift was eight, or a clock-in at 6am on a site that does not open until 9am. These errors, caught before payroll processing, are trivial to fix. Discovered after paycheques have been issued, they become disputes.

### Establishing accountability

When a manager approves a timesheet, they are taking responsibility for its accuracy. This accountability encourages managers to actually review the data rather than just clicking "approve all." It also provides an audit trail — if a payroll discrepancy is later discovered, you can identify who approved the timesheet and when.

### Supporting compliance

Employment records are only as reliable as the process that creates them. If your records consist of unreviewed, self-reported timesheets, they carry little weight in a dispute. Approved timesheets, by contrast, represent a verified record that both the worker and the manager have agreed to.

### Reducing fraud

While most timesheet errors are genuine mistakes, approval is also a deterrent against deliberate manipulation. Workers who know their timesheets will be reviewed by someone familiar with the schedule are less likely to inflate hours or misrepresent their attendance.

## Designing the workflow

An effective approval workflow answers three questions: who approves, what they review, and when the cycle runs.

### Who approves

The approver should be someone with direct knowledge of the work being done. In most businesses, this is the line manager or site supervisor. They know the schedule, they know who was present, and they can identify discrepancies.

**Common structures:**

- **Single approver**: One manager approves all timesheets for their team. Simple and effective for small teams.
- **Site-based approval**: Each site has a designated approver. Good for multi-site businesses where a central manager cannot observe every location.
- **Two-tier approval**: A supervisor does the initial review, and a senior manager or payroll administrator gives final sign-off. This adds a safety net but also adds time.
- **Self-approval with audit**: Workers submit timesheets that are automatically approved but subject to random audit. This works in high-trust environments but offers less protection against errors.

For most small businesses, single approver per team or site is the right balance. It is simple enough to sustain and thorough enough to catch problems.

### What to review

Managers should not need to re-calculate every timesheet from scratch. A good system highlights the items that need attention:

- **Shifts that exceed normal duration**: If the standard shift is eight hours, a timesheet showing twelve hours should be flagged automatically.
- **Missing breaks**: If a worker did not record a break on a shift where one is required, the system should highlight it.
- **Late or early clock-ins**: If a worker clocked in significantly before or after their scheduled start time, that is worth reviewing.
- **Unmatched entries**: A clock-in without a corresponding clock-out (or vice versa) indicates either a forgotten entry or a system glitch.
- **GPS anomalies**: If GPS verification is enabled, clock-ins from unexpected locations should be flagged.

The goal is to make the approval process focused rather than exhaustive. Managers should spend their time on the exceptions, not on reviewing routine entries that match expectations.

### When to run the cycle

The approval cycle should align with your payroll cycle:

- **Weekly payroll**: Timesheets should be submitted by end of day on the last working day of the week and approved by the following morning.
- **Fortnightly payroll**: Same principle, but with a two-week submission window.
- **Monthly payroll**: Workers submit at month-end, approval within the first two working days of the new month.

The key is to leave enough time between submission and payroll processing for managers to review and for any queries to be resolved. A tight cycle — submit Friday, approve Monday, process Tuesday — keeps the data fresh and reduces the chance of disputes.

### Automatic reminders

Workers forget to submit timesheets. Managers forget to approve them. Automatic reminders solve both problems:

- **Submission reminders**: Sent to workers who have not submitted by the deadline
- **Approval reminders**: Sent to managers who have pending approvals
- **Escalation**: If approval has not occurred within a defined period, the notification escalates to a senior manager or payroll administrator

These reminders should be built into the system, not managed manually. Manual follow-up is time-consuming and unreliable.

## Handling exceptions

### Disputed entries

If a manager queries a timesheet entry, the worker should have the opportunity to explain or correct it. The system should support a simple back-and-forth:

1. Manager flags the entry with a note (e.g., "Your timesheet shows 10 hours but the shift was scheduled for 8. Can you confirm?")
2. Worker responds (e.g., "I stayed late to finish a job — it was agreed with the site supervisor")
3. Manager approves or rejects based on the explanation

This dialogue should be recorded in the system as part of the audit trail.

### Late submissions

Define a policy for late timesheet submissions. Options include:

- Accept with a warning
- Accept but delay payment to the next payroll cycle
- Reject and require the worker to submit a manual correction

Whatever your policy, communicate it clearly and enforce it consistently. Late submissions disrupt the approval cycle and delay payroll.

### Manual adjustments

Sometimes a timesheet needs to be corrected — a worker forgot to clock out, or clocked in at the wrong site. The system should allow managers to make manual adjustments, but every adjustment should be logged with a reason. This audit trail protects both the manager and the worker.

## Common mistakes

### Approving without reviewing

If managers routinely approve entire batches without checking individual entries, the approval process is providing false assurance. It creates the illusion of control without actually catching errors.

**Fix:** Make the approval interface highlight anomalies so managers can focus their attention. If the system shows that nine out of ten timesheets are routine, the manager only needs to spend time on the tenth.

### Making the process too slow

If approval takes days, it delays payroll and frustrates workers. The goal is quick review, not forensic analysis.

**Fix:** Set clear deadlines and enforce them with reminders. Most timesheet approvals should take minutes, not hours.

### Not having a process at all

Some businesses treat timesheet approval as optional — workers submit hours and they go straight to payroll. This is a significant risk. One undetected error can cascade into an incorrect paycheque, a compliance failure, or a financial loss.

**Fix:** Implement approval as a mandatory step before payroll processing. Even a quick review is better than none.

### One person approving everything

In a growing business, the owner or office manager often becomes the bottleneck — approving timesheets for every worker across every site. This does not scale, and the approver lacks the context to spot errors at sites they never visit.

**Fix:** Delegate approval to site managers or team leaders who are closer to the work.

## The approval workflow in practice

Here is what a well-functioning weekly approval cycle looks like:

**Friday evening:** Workers are reminded to review and submit their timesheets for the week.

**Saturday morning:** The system flags any unsubmitted timesheets and sends reminders.

**Monday morning:** Managers receive a notification that timesheets are ready for approval. The dashboard shows pending timesheets with anomalies highlighted.

**Monday midday:** Most timesheets are approved. The manager has queried two entries and is waiting for worker responses.

**Monday afternoon:** Queries are resolved. All timesheets are approved.

**Tuesday:** Approved hours are exported to payroll. The payroll team processes payments with confidence that the data is verified.

This cycle takes managers perhaps fifteen to twenty minutes on Monday. In exchange, the business gets accurate payroll, clean records, and a documented process that stands up to scrutiny.

## Summary

Timesheet approval is not bureaucracy — it is quality control. It catches errors, establishes accountability, supports compliance, and deters fraud. The key is designing a workflow that is effective without being onerous: the right people reviewing the right things at the right time, supported by a system that highlights exceptions and automates reminders.

Get the approval workflow right, and everything downstream — payroll, invoicing, compliance, reporting — benefits automatically.
