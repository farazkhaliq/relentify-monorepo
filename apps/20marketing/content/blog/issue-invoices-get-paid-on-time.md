Late payment is not always about unwilling clients. Often, it is about invoices that are easy to ignore, confusing to process, or missing the information the client needs to pay you. The way you create and deliver your invoices has a direct impact on how quickly the money arrives.

Businesses that follow invoicing best practices consistently get paid faster than those that do not. And the good news is that most of these practices are simple to implement.

## Send the invoice immediately

The single most impactful thing you can do is invoice as soon as the work is done or the goods are delivered. Every day you delay is a day added to your collection timeline.

If you complete a project on Monday and send the invoice on Friday, you have already added four days to your payment cycle. If payment terms are Net 30, the client now has until day 34 from project completion to pay — and in practice, many will take even longer.

Invoice on the same day if possible. If your invoicing process is too complicated to do immediately, simplify it.

## Include everything the client needs

Missing information is one of the top reasons invoices get held up. Make sure every invoice includes:

### Your details
- Business name and address
- Contact email and phone number
- Tax registration number (VAT number, if applicable)

### Client details
- Correct legal entity name (not just the contact person)
- Billing address
- Purchase order number (if required)
- Client reference number (if applicable)

### Invoice specifics
- Unique invoice number
- Invoice date
- Due date (not just "Net 30" — calculate the actual date)
- Description of goods or services
- Quantity and unit price for each line item
- Subtotal, tax, and total
- Currency

### Payment information
- Bank account details (account name, number, sort code / routing number)
- Payment link (if you accept online payments)
- Accepted payment methods
- Reference to use when paying (so you can match the payment to the invoice)

If even one piece of information is missing — especially a PO number for corporate clients — the invoice may sit in someone's inbox until they get around to asking you for it.

## Make the total and due date prominent

Your invoice should be scannable in seconds. The recipient should be able to see:

1. How much they owe
2. When it is due
3. How to pay

These three pieces of information should be the most visually prominent elements on the invoice. If the total is buried in a table and the due date is in small print at the bottom, you are making it harder than it needs to be.

## Use clear, specific descriptions

Vague descriptions invite questions and delays. Compare:

**Vague:** "Services rendered — 3,000"

**Clear:** "Website redesign — homepage, about page, and contact page. Includes design, development, and two rounds of revisions. Project completed 15 November 2026 — 3,000"

The clear version tells the client exactly what they are paying for. There is no ambiguity, no need to check back through emails, and no reason to query the invoice.

## Offer convenient payment methods

The fewer obstacles between the client and payment, the faster you get paid.

**Bank transfer** is standard but requires the client to log into their bank, enter your details, and make the transfer manually. It works but has friction.

**Online payment links** let the client click a link and pay immediately by card. This dramatically reduces the steps and is particularly effective for smaller invoices.

**Direct debit** for recurring billing means the client only sets it up once, and payments happen automatically. This is the gold standard for regular billing.

Modern invoicing platforms like [Relentify](/accounting) embed payment links directly in the invoice, so the client can pay with a single click. This removes the friction of manual bank transfers and consistently results in faster payment.

## Set the right payment terms

Your payment terms should balance your cash flow needs with client expectations:

- **Net 7-14** for new clients, small invoices, or clients with no payment history
- **Net 30** for established clients with good payment records
- **Net 60+** only if required by the client and your cash flow can handle it

Whatever terms you set, include the specific due date on the invoice, not just "Net 30." A date is concrete and unambiguous.

## Send to the right person

This is a surprisingly common problem. You send the invoice to your day-to-day contact, but they are not the person who processes payments. The invoice sits in their inbox until they remember to forward it to accounts payable.

Ask your client: "Who should I send invoices to?" Get the accounts payable email address and send invoices directly there. Copy your main contact if needed.

For larger organisations, ask about their invoicing requirements:
- Do they need a PO number?
- Is there a specific portal for submitting invoices?
- Do they require invoices in a particular format?

Meeting their requirements upfront prevents delays.

## Follow up systematically

Even well-crafted invoices need follow-up. Set up a structured reminder process:

- **5 days before due:** Friendly reminder that the invoice is coming due
- **1 day after due:** Polite follow-up noting the invoice is now past due
- **7 days after due:** Firmer reminder requesting payment
- **14 days after due:** Phone call or escalation

Automate these reminders through your accounting software. Manual follow-up is inconsistent — you will skip it when you are busy, which is exactly when you most need the cash.

## Build relationships, not just invoices

The businesses that get paid fastest are often the ones with the best client relationships. When your client knows you, trusts you, and values the relationship, your invoice moves to the top of the pile rather than the bottom.

This does not mean you cannot be firm about payment. It means that the combination of professionalism, clear communication, and good work creates an environment where paying you promptly is the natural thing to do.

## Common invoicing mistakes

### Inconsistent invoice numbering

Use a sequential numbering system and stick to it. Gaps or duplicates in your invoice numbers create confusion and can be flagged in audits.

### No terms stated

If your invoice does not state payment terms, the client has no clear deadline. Always include both the terms and the specific due date.

### Sending invoices as attachments only

Some email systems flag invoice attachments or route them to spam. Consider sending the invoice both as an attachment and with key details (amount, due date, payment link) in the body of the email.

### Not following up

Sending the invoice and hoping for the best is not a strategy. Follow up consistently and promptly.

### Billing after long delays

If you wait weeks after completing the work to invoice, the client may have moved on mentally. They are less motivated to pay promptly for work that feels like ancient history.

## Tracking and improving

Monitor your average days to payment. If it is consistently longer than your terms, something in your invoicing process needs to change. Test different approaches — payment links vs bank transfer, Net 14 vs Net 30, email vs portal — and measure the results.

The goal is not just to issue invoices but to issue invoices that get paid. Every improvement to your invoicing process translates directly into better cash flow and less time spent chasing money.
