Flexible working has moved from a niche perk to a mainstream expectation. Employees increasingly want to adjust their working hours, location, or pattern — and in many jurisdictions, they have the legal right to request it. As a small business, handling these requests well is important for retention, recruitment, and compliance.

This guide explains how to manage flexible working requests fairly and practically.

## What is flexible working?

Flexible working is any arrangement that departs from the standard working pattern. Common forms include:

- **Remote or hybrid working**: Working from home for some or all of the week
- **Flexible hours**: Choosing start and finish times (sometimes called flexitime)
- **Compressed hours**: Working full-time hours over fewer days (e.g., four 10-hour days instead of five 8-hour days)
- **Part-time working**: Reducing the number of hours or days worked
- **Job sharing**: Two people sharing the responsibilities of one full-time role
- **Staggered hours**: Different start, finish, and break times from other employees
- **Annualised hours**: Working a set number of hours over the year, with flexibility in when they are worked
- **Term-time working**: Working only during school terms

## The legal position

In many jurisdictions, employees have the legal right to request flexible working from their first day of employment. The specific rules vary, but the general framework is:

- The employee makes a written request specifying what change they want, when they want it to start, and what impact they think it will have on the business
- The employer must consider the request in a reasonable manner
- The employer must respond within a specified timeframe (commonly two to three months)
- The employer can only refuse the request on specific business grounds
- The employee can appeal a refusal

The right is to request, not to demand. You can refuse, but only for legitimate business reasons and after genuinely considering the request.

## Grounds for refusal

Most legislation specifies the grounds on which you can refuse a flexible working request. Common permitted grounds include:

- The burden of additional costs
- Detrimental effect on the ability to meet customer demand
- Inability to reorganise work among existing staff
- Inability to recruit additional staff
- Detrimental impact on quality or performance
- Insufficient work during the periods the employee proposes to work
- Planned structural changes

These grounds must be genuine and specific to the request. A blanket "we do not do flexible working" is not a valid response.

## Handling a request: step by step

### Step 1: Acknowledge the request
When you receive a flexible working request, acknowledge it in writing. Confirm what the employee has asked for and explain the next steps, including the timeline for your decision.

### Step 2: Consider the request

Assess the request against the realities of the business:

**Operational impact**: Can the work be done effectively under the proposed arrangement? Think about:
- Coverage during business hours
- Impact on team collaboration
- Customer or client-facing requirements
- Supervision and management needs

**Cost impact**: Will the arrangement create additional costs? Consider:
- Equipment for home working
- Changes to shift patterns that require additional staff
- Administrative costs of managing the arrangement

**Fairness**: If you grant this request, will it create issues with other employees? Consider precedent and consistency.

**Trial period**: If you are uncertain, consider agreeing to a trial period of three to six months. This allows both parties to assess whether the arrangement works in practice.

### Step 3: Meet with the employee

Arrange a meeting to discuss the request. This gives you the opportunity to:

- Understand why the employee wants the change
- Explore the practical details
- Discuss any concerns you have
- Consider alternative arrangements that might address both parties' needs
- Agree modifications to the original request if needed

The employee has the right to be accompanied at the meeting in some jurisdictions.

### Step 4: Make your decision

After considering the request, decide whether to approve, modify, or refuse it.

**If approving**: Confirm the new arrangement in writing, including:
- The specific changes to working hours, location, or pattern
- The start date
- Whether it is permanent or for a trial period
- Any conditions (e.g., maintaining availability for team meetings)
- How the arrangement will be reviewed

**If refusing**: Provide the refusal in writing with:
- A clear explanation of which business ground(s) apply
- Specific details of why those grounds apply to this request
- The employee's right to appeal

### Step 5: Implement and review

If the request is approved:
- Update the employee's employment contract or issue a variation letter
- Update your [payroll](/payroll) system to reflect any changes to hours, pay, or working pattern
- Inform the team about the new arrangement (with the employee's agreement)
- Schedule a review after three to six months

## Practical considerations

### Managing remote workers
If you approve home or hybrid working:
- Ensure the employee has a suitable workspace and equipment
- Establish clear expectations about availability, communication, and deliverables
- Consider data security and provide any necessary VPN or security tools
- Check insurance implications (both yours and the employee's home insurance)
- Agree on which days require office presence, if any

### Tracking hours for flexible workers
When employees work flexible or compressed hours, accurate [time tracking](/timesheets) becomes important. You need to ensure:
- Working time regulations are observed (maximum hours, rest breaks)
- Overtime is correctly identified and compensated
- Holiday entitlement is calculated correctly for the new pattern

### Impact on pay and benefits
Changes to working hours usually affect pay proportionally. If an employee moves from full-time to part-time, their salary is pro-rated. Benefits may also be affected — check your pension scheme and any other benefits for implications.

### Team dynamics
A flexible working arrangement should not unfairly burden the rest of the team. If one person works from home on Fridays, but that means everyone else must cover their desk duties, resentment can build. Design arrangements that work for the team, not just the individual.

## Common mistakes

### Refusing without genuine consideration
Even if you are inclined to say no, you must genuinely consider the request. Going through the motions is not enough — and an employee who feels their request was dismissed without thought is more likely to disengage or leave.

### Inconsistent decisions
Approving flexible working for one employee and refusing a similar request from another, without clear business reasons for the difference, creates fairness issues and potential discrimination claims.

### Not updating contracts and payroll
An agreed change to working hours is a contractual change. Update the contract, update the payroll, and ensure everything is aligned.

### Ignoring the review
A flexible working arrangement that is not reviewed can drift into problems. Schedule a review and actually conduct it.

## The business case for flexibility

Flexible working is not just an employee benefit — it can benefit the business too:

- **Retention**: Employees who can work flexibly are less likely to leave.
- **Recruitment**: Offering flexibility widens your talent pool.
- **Productivity**: Many employees report being more productive with some flexibility in how they work.
- **Cost reduction**: Hybrid working can reduce office space requirements.
- **Resilience**: A team that is accustomed to flexible working adapts more easily to disruption.

For small businesses, being flexible is often easier than for large corporations. You have fewer layers of bureaucracy, faster decision-making, and the ability to tailor arrangements to individual needs. Use that to your advantage.

## The bottom line

Flexible working requests are a normal part of modern employment. Handle them professionally: consider each request genuinely, make decisions based on business reality, communicate clearly, and update your systems when arrangements change. A fair approach to flexibility makes your business a better place to work.
