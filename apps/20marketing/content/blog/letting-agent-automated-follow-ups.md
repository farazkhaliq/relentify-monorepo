In a letting agency, the to-do list never ends. There are tenants to follow up with, landlords to update, viewings to confirm, references to chase, certificates to renew, and rent arrears to pursue. On any given day, a single agent might have dozens of outstanding follow-up tasks — each one important, each one easy to forget.

The problem is not that agents do not care. The problem is that there are too many things to remember, and when the office gets busy, the less urgent tasks get pushed to tomorrow. Then tomorrow gets busy too, and before long, a simple follow-up has become an overdue complaint.

Automation solves this not by replacing the agent, but by ensuring that the right reminder reaches the right person at the right time.

## Why manual follow-ups fail

Manual follow-up systems — whether they rely on memory, notebooks, or shared calendars — share a common weakness: they depend on the person remembering to check them. In the flow of a busy day, checking a to-do list is the first thing to be skipped.

Even when follow-ups are logged in a shared calendar, they compete with appointments, meetings, and other time-blocked commitments. A reminder to "chase reference for 14 Oak Road" sits alongside a viewing at 2 pm and a landlord meeting at 3 pm. By the end of the day, the reference chase has been pushed to tomorrow.

The consequence is inconsistency. Some follow-ups happen on time. Others are delayed. Some are missed entirely. And the longer a follow-up is delayed, the less effective it becomes. An applicant who does not hear back within 24 hours assumes you are not interested. A landlord who has to chase for an update assumes you are not on top of things.

## What should be automated

Not every follow-up needs to be automated. A sensitive conversation with a landlord about rent arrears, for example, requires human judgment and empathy. But the trigger — the reminder that the conversation needs to happen — absolutely should be automated.

Here are the most common follow-up types that benefit from automation in a letting agency.

### New enquiry response

When an applicant enquires about a property, speed matters. An automated acknowledgement — sent immediately upon receipt of the enquiry — confirms that you have received their message and sets expectations about when they will hear back. This buys time without leaving the applicant in silence.

A secondary reminder to the assigned agent — sent if the enquiry has not been personally responded to within two hours — ensures that no enquiry sits unattended for long.

### Post-viewing follow-up

After every viewing, there should be a follow-up with the applicant. Did they like the property? Do they want to proceed? Do they have any questions?

This follow-up should happen within 24 hours of the viewing. An automated task created at the time the viewing is logged ensures that this happens consistently, regardless of how busy the agent's day becomes.

### Referencing chases

Referencing is one of the most common sources of delay in the lettings process. Applicants forget to submit documents. Employers do not respond to verification requests. Previous landlords do not return calls.

Automated reminders — sent to the applicant on day 2, day 5, and day 7 if their documents have not been received — keep the process moving without requiring the agent to manually track every outstanding reference.

### Tenancy renewal reminders

Tenancy renewals need to be initiated well before the current agreement expires. A reminder sent three months before expiry gives the agent time to discuss options with the landlord, present the renewal offer to the tenant, and negotiate terms.

A second reminder at two months, and a third at one month, ensures that renewals are never left to the last minute.

### Compliance certificate expiries

Gas safety certificates, electrical inspections, and energy performance certificates all have expiry dates. An automated reminder sent 60 days before expiry — with escalating reminders at 30 days and 14 days — ensures that renewals are arranged in good time.

### Rent arrears alerts

When rent is overdue, the follow-up process should start immediately. An automated alert on the day rent is due but not received prompts the agent to investigate. A structured sequence — a gentle reminder on day 1, a formal notification on day 7, and an escalation on day 14 — ensures that arrears are addressed promptly and consistently.

### Landlord check-ins

Regular communication with landlords is one of the best ways to build loyalty and prevent churn. But in a busy office, proactive landlord calls are the first thing to be deprioritised.

An automated quarterly reminder to "check in with [landlord name]" ensures that no landlord goes too long without hearing from you. The conversation itself is personal and human. The reminder that triggers it is automated.

## Setting up automation in your CRM

Most modern CRM systems support some form of task automation. The specifics vary between platforms, but the core concepts are consistent.

### Trigger events

An automation begins with a trigger — an event that initiates the workflow. Common triggers include a new contact being created, a tenancy end date approaching, a viewing being logged, or a compliance certificate reaching its renewal window.

### Actions

When the trigger fires, the system performs one or more actions. This might be creating a task and assigning it to a team member, sending an email to a contact, updating a status field, or sending a notification to a manager.

### Conditions

Conditions add logic to your automations. You might want to send a referencing reminder only if the applicant has not yet submitted their documents. You might want to escalate a rent arrears alert only if the balance is above a certain threshold. Conditions ensure that automations are relevant, not just frequent.

### Timing

Most automations include a time element — either a delay (send this email 24 hours after the viewing) or a schedule (check for expiring certificates every Monday morning). Getting the timing right is important. Too many reminders too quickly feels like spam. Too few, and things still slip through.

## Avoiding automation fatigue

One of the risks of automation is creating so many reminders that people start ignoring them. If an agent receives fifty automated task notifications before lunch, they will stop paying attention to any of them.

The solution is to be selective about what you automate. Focus on high-impact, time-sensitive follow-ups — the ones where a missed deadline has real consequences. Leave lower-priority tasks to be managed through regular pipeline reviews rather than individual reminders.

It also helps to design your automations with escalation in mind. The first reminder is a gentle nudge. If it is not actioned, the second reminder is more urgent. If that is also ignored, a third reminder goes to the manager. This graduated approach ensures that important tasks eventually get the attention they need without overwhelming the team from the start.

## Measuring the impact

Automation is only valuable if it produces results. Track the metrics that matter.

How has your average enquiry response time changed since implementing automated follow-ups? How many tenancy renewals are being initiated on time versus being rushed at the last minute? How many compliance certificates have lapsed?

These metrics will tell you whether your automations are working and where they need to be adjusted.

## Getting started

You do not need to automate everything at once. Start with the one process that causes you the most pain. For many agencies, this is enquiry follow-up — it is high-volume, time-sensitive, and directly linked to revenue.

Set up an automated acknowledgement for new enquiries and a reminder for the assigned agent. Measure the impact over a month. Then add the next automation — perhaps post-viewing follow-ups or referencing chases. Build gradually, and adjust as you learn what works.
