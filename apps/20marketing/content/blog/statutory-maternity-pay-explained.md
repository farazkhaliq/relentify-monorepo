When an employee tells you they are expecting a baby, one of the first things you need to work out is their entitlement to Statutory Maternity Pay. SMP is a legal obligation for qualifying employees, and as the employer, you are responsible for calculating it, paying it through payroll, and recovering the cost from HMRC.

This guide explains the rules clearly so you can handle maternity pay with confidence.

## What is Statutory Maternity Pay?

Statutory Maternity Pay is a weekly payment made by the employer to an employee who is on maternity leave. It is paid for up to 39 weeks. The first six weeks are paid at a higher rate (90% of average weekly earnings), and the remaining 33 weeks are paid at a flat rate set by the government, or 90% of average weekly earnings — whichever is lower.

SMP is paid through your normal payroll process. It is subject to income tax and National Insurance, just like regular pay.

## Who qualifies for SMP?

An employee qualifies for SMP if they meet all of the following conditions:

- **They have been employed by you continuously for at least 26 weeks** by the end of the 15th week before the expected week of childbirth (known as the "qualifying week").
- **They earn at least the Lower Earnings Limit** on average over the eight-week period ending with the qualifying week.
- **They give you proper notice** of their intention to take maternity leave — at least 15 weeks before the expected week of childbirth.
- **They provide proof of pregnancy** — usually a MATB1 certificate issued by their midwife or doctor at or after the 20th week of pregnancy.
- **Their maternity leave has started** — SMP cannot be paid before the leave begins.

If the employee does not qualify for SMP — for example, because they have not been employed long enough or their earnings are too low — you must give them form SMP1 within seven days. This allows them to apply for Maternity Allowance directly from the government.

## The qualifying week and relevant period

Two dates matter for SMP calculations:

- **The qualifying week (QW)**: The 15th week before the expected week of childbirth. This is the date you use to check whether the employee has 26 weeks of continuous service.
- **The relevant period**: The eight weeks (or two months for monthly-paid employees) ending with the last normal pay day on or before the last day of the qualifying week. This period is used to calculate average weekly earnings.

Getting these dates right is essential because they determine both eligibility and the payment amount. Most payroll software will calculate them automatically if you enter the expected week of childbirth.

## Calculating SMP

SMP is calculated in two phases:

### First six weeks: earnings-related rate
Pay 90% of the employee's average weekly earnings. There is no cap on this amount — if the employee earns well, the 90% figure can be significantly higher than the flat rate.

### Remaining 33 weeks: flat rate or 90% (whichever is lower)
Pay the flat weekly rate set by the government, or 90% of average weekly earnings if that is lower. The flat rate is updated each April.

### Average weekly earnings
Calculate this by taking the total gross pay earned during the relevant period and dividing by the number of weeks (or months) in that period. Include regular salary, bonuses, overtime, and any statutory payments made during that period.

For monthly-paid employees, the relevant period is two months. Divide total earnings by two to get average monthly earnings, then multiply by 12 and divide by 52 to get the weekly figure.

## When SMP starts

SMP can start from the 11th week before the expected week of childbirth — the employee chooses when within this window. In practice, most employees start their maternity leave either at the expected due date or a few weeks before.

SMP starts automatically if the employee is off work for a pregnancy-related illness in the four weeks before the expected week of childbirth. In this case, SMP begins on the day after the first complete day of absence.

SMP also starts on the day after the birth if the baby arrives before the planned start date.

## Paying SMP through payroll

SMP is processed through your normal payroll. On each pay date during the maternity leave period:

- Calculate the SMP due for that pay period
- Deduct income tax and employee National Insurance
- Include SMP on the payslip
- Report it through your RTI submission

The employee does not receive their normal salary during maternity leave unless you offer an enhanced maternity pay scheme that exceeds SMP. If you do offer enhanced pay, SMP is included within that amount — it is not additional.

Some employers offer occupational maternity pay (OMP) that tops up SMP. A common arrangement is full pay for a set number of weeks (with SMP offset), followed by SMP only, followed by unpaid leave. Whatever your policy, make sure it is clearly documented and communicated to the employee before their leave starts.

## Recovering SMP from HMRC

The good news for employers is that you can recover most or all of the SMP you pay from HMRC. The recovery rate depends on your size:

- **Small employers** (those who qualify for Small Employers' Relief): Can recover 100% of SMP plus an additional compensation amount (currently 3% of SMP paid).
- **All other employers**: Can recover 92% of SMP.

You qualify for Small Employers' Relief if your total employer and employee National Insurance contributions in the previous tax year were below a specified threshold.

SMP recovery is claimed through your Employer Payment Summary (EPS) as part of your RTI submissions. You offset the SMP amount against your PAYE and NI liabilities. If the SMP exceeds your PAYE liability for the period, HMRC will pay you the difference.

## Notice and evidence requirements

The employee must give you at least 15 weeks' notice before the expected week of childbirth. They must confirm:

- That they are pregnant
- The expected week of childbirth
- When they want their maternity leave to start

You must respond within 28 days, confirming the date their maternity leave will end (52 weeks from the start date).

The employee must also provide a MATB1 certificate as proof of pregnancy. This is issued by the midwife or GP at around 20 weeks of pregnancy. You need this before you can start paying SMP.

## Keeping in touch days

During maternity leave, the employee can work up to 10 "keeping in touch" (KIT) days without losing SMP for that week. These are optional — neither the employer nor the employee can insist on them.

KIT days must be agreed between both parties. Payment for KIT days is by agreement — there is no statutory rate. Many employers pay the employee's normal daily rate minus the SMP they would receive for that day.

KIT days are useful for helping the employee stay connected with the workplace, attend training, or ease their return. However, any work done beyond 10 KIT days will result in the loss of SMP for the weeks in which the additional days fall.

## Maternity leave and return to work

Employees are entitled to up to 52 weeks of maternity leave: 26 weeks of Ordinary Maternity Leave and 26 weeks of Additional Maternity Leave. SMP covers 39 of those weeks — the remaining 13 weeks are unpaid.

The employee must give at least eight weeks' notice if they want to return to work before the end of their maternity leave. If they return during the SMP period, SMP stops on the date they return.

Employees have the right to return to the same job (or a similar one if that is not reasonably practicable after Additional Maternity Leave). Dismissing or treating an employee unfavourably because of pregnancy or maternity leave is automatically unfair dismissal and discrimination.

## Record keeping

Keep records of SMP payments for at least three years after the end of the tax year in which SMP was paid. Records should include:

- The expected week of childbirth and the MATB1 certificate
- The start and end dates of SMP payments
- Amounts paid each pay period
- Average weekly earnings calculation
- Any weeks where SMP was not paid, and the reason
- Recovery calculations

## Key points for employers

Statutory Maternity Pay requires careful attention to dates, calculations, and process. The main things to remember:

- Check eligibility early — the qualifying week is 15 weeks before the due date
- Calculate average weekly earnings over the correct relevant period
- Pay through payroll with normal tax and NI deductions
- Recover SMP from HMRC via your EPS submissions
- Keep detailed records for at least three years
