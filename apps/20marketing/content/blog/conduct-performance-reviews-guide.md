Performance reviews have a bad reputation. Employees dread them, managers rush through them, and many businesses treat them as an annual box-ticking exercise that changes nothing. But done well, performance reviews are one of the most powerful management tools available — a structured opportunity to recognise good work, address problems, align expectations, and plan development.

This guide explains how to make performance reviews actually work.

## Why most performance reviews fail

Before fixing the process, it helps to understand why reviews go wrong:

- **They happen too infrequently**: An annual review means feedback is months old by the time it is delivered. The employee cannot remember the context, and the manager cannot remember the details.
- **They are too vague**: "Good job this year" and "needs to improve communication" are meaningless without specifics.
- **They are one-directional**: The manager talks, the employee listens (or pretends to). There is no genuine conversation.
- **There is no follow-through**: Goals are set and immediately forgotten until the next review.
- **They are linked to pay**: When salary is on the table, the conversation becomes a negotiation rather than a genuine discussion about performance and development.

## A better approach

### Frequency
Conduct formal reviews at least twice a year — quarterly is better. Supplement these with informal check-ins every two to four weeks. Regular feedback eliminates the shock factor that makes annual reviews uncomfortable.

### Separation from pay
If possible, separate the performance conversation from the salary conversation. When employees know their pay review is in a different meeting, they are more likely to be open about challenges and development needs.

### Two-way conversation
A review should be a dialogue, not a lecture. The employee should talk at least as much as the manager. Ask questions, listen to the answers, and be willing to learn something.

## Preparing for the review

### For the manager

**Review the evidence**:
- Look at the goals set in the last review. What was achieved? What was not?
- Review any notes from regular check-ins
- Consider specific examples of good performance and areas for improvement
- Gather feedback from colleagues if relevant (with their permission)

**Plan the conversation**:
- Identify the two to three most important points to discuss
- Prepare specific examples for each point
- Think about development opportunities and future goals
- Consider the employee's career aspirations and how this role fits

### For the employee

Give the employee time to prepare too. Send them a self-assessment form or a list of reflection questions a week before the review:

- What are you most proud of since the last review?
- What goals did you achieve? Which ones did you miss, and why?
- What challenges have you faced?
- What support do you need?
- What would you like to focus on in the next period?

An employee who has reflected on their own performance before the meeting will contribute more meaningfully to the conversation.

## Structuring the review conversation

A good review follows a consistent structure:

### 1. Set the tone (5 minutes)
Start positively and explain the purpose. "This is a conversation about how things are going, what is working well, and what we can improve. I want to hear your perspective too."

### 2. Review the period (15-20 minutes)
Walk through the goals from the last review:
- What was achieved? Acknowledge successes with specific examples.
- What was not achieved? Understand the reasons without blame.
- What unexpected contributions did the employee make?
- What challenges arose?

Use the employee's self-assessment as a starting point. Ask them to share their perspective first, then add yours.

### 3. Feedback (10-15 minutes)
Provide specific, constructive feedback:

**For positive feedback**: Be specific about what was good and why it mattered. "Your handling of the client complaint last month was excellent — you identified the root cause, resolved it quickly, and the client specifically thanked us" is far more useful than "good job."

**For improvement areas**: Focus on behaviour and outcomes, not personality. "I have noticed that project updates are often submitted a few days late, which delays the team's planning. What can we do to address this?" opens a conversation. "You are disorganised" shuts it down.

Use the SBI model (Situation, Behaviour, Impact):
- **Situation**: "During last month's product launch..."
- **Behaviour**: "...you took the initiative to coordinate between sales and marketing without being asked..."
- **Impact**: "...which meant the launch materials were ready a week early and the sales team felt much more prepared."

### 4. Development discussion (10 minutes)
Discuss the employee's growth:
- What skills do they want to develop?
- Are there training opportunities or stretch assignments?
- How does this role fit into their longer-term career plans?
- What support do they need from you?

### 5. Set goals (10 minutes)
Agree on goals for the next period. Good goals are:

- **Specific**: Clear enough that both parties know what success looks like
- **Measurable**: With a defined outcome or metric
- **Achievable**: Challenging but realistic
- **Relevant**: Connected to the team's objectives and the employee's development
- **Time-bound**: With a deadline or review date

Three to five goals per review period is usually the right number. More than that and focus is diluted.

### 6. Summarise and agree next steps (5 minutes)
Recap the key points, agreed goals, and any actions. Confirm when you will check in next. Both parties should leave with a clear understanding of what was discussed and what happens next.

## After the review

### Document the conversation
Write a brief summary of the review: key points discussed, goals agreed, and any actions for either party. Share this with the employee so you both have the same record.

Store the review documentation alongside the employee's other records in your HR or [payroll](/payroll) system. This creates a history of performance conversations that is invaluable for future reviews, promotions, or — if necessary — performance management.

### Follow through on commitments
If you promised to arrange training, provide a resource, or make a change, do it. Nothing undermines a review process faster than a manager who does not follow through on their own commitments.

### Continue with regular check-ins
The review is not the end of the conversation — it is a checkpoint. Continue with regular informal check-ins to track progress on goals, provide ongoing feedback, and address issues as they arise.

## Common mistakes

### Recency bias
Focusing only on the last few weeks rather than the entire review period. Counteract this by reviewing notes and evidence from the full period.

### Avoiding difficult conversations
If you have concerns about an employee's performance, the review is the place to raise them — clearly and constructively. Avoiding the issue does the employee a disservice and allows problems to grow.

### Rating inflation
Giving everyone a top rating to avoid conflict makes the review meaningless. Be honest and differentiate between good, adequate, and below-standard performance.

### No employee input
If the employee sits in silence while the manager delivers a monologue, the review is not a conversation. Create space for the employee to share their perspective, challenges, and ideas.

### Making it personal
Feedback should address behaviour and outcomes, not personality traits. "Your reports have contained several errors recently" is actionable. "You are careless" is not.

## Making it work in a small business

You do not need a complex HR system to run effective performance reviews. Start with:

- A simple template for the review conversation
- A shared document for goals and follow-up notes
- Calendar reminders for reviews and check-ins
- A commitment to doing it consistently

Platforms like Relentify can help by storing review records alongside employee profiles and [payroll](/payroll) data, creating a single view of each employee's history. But the most important tool is a manager who takes the time to prepare, listen, and follow through.

## The bottom line

Performance reviews work when they are specific, regular, two-directional, and followed by action. They fail when they are vague, infrequent, one-sided, and forgotten the next day. Choose to do them well, and they become one of the most valuable management habits in your business.
