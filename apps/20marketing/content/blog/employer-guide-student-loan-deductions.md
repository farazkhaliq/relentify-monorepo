If you employ anyone who attended university or took out a postgraduate loan, you may need to deduct student loan repayments from their pay. Student loan deductions are a mandatory payroll obligation — similar to tax and National Insurance — and you cannot avoid or defer them once you have been notified.

This guide explains the different loan plans, how to calculate deductions, and what your responsibilities are as an employer.

## How student loan repayments work

Student loans in the UK are repaid through a combination of direct payments and payroll deductions. As an employer, you only deal with the payroll deduction element.

When an employee earns above a specific threshold, you deduct a percentage of their earnings above that threshold and send it to HMRC alongside your PAYE payments. HMRC then passes the money to the Student Loans Company.

The thresholds and rates depend on which loan plan the employee is on. There are currently several plans in operation, and some employees may be on more than one.

## The loan plans

### Plan 1
Plan 1 covers loans taken out before September 2012 in England and Wales, and all loans from Student Awards Agency Scotland and Student Finance Northern Ireland.

- **Threshold**: Set annually (check current year's figure)
- **Rate**: 9% of earnings above the threshold
- **Typical employees**: Those who started university before 2012

### Plan 2
Plan 2 covers loans taken out from September 2012 onwards in England and Wales.

- **Threshold**: Higher than Plan 1 (check current year's figure)
- **Rate**: 9% of earnings above the threshold
- **Typical employees**: Those who started university from 2012

### Plan 4
Plan 4 covers Scottish student loans issued from April 2021 onwards (replacing Plan 1 for Scottish students).

- **Threshold**: Similar to Plan 1 but set independently
- **Rate**: 9% of earnings above the threshold
- **Typical employees**: Scottish students who started courses from 2021

### Plan 5
Plan 5 covers loans taken out from September 2023 onwards in England.

- **Threshold**: The highest of all plans
- **Rate**: 9% of earnings above the threshold
- **Typical employees**: Students starting university from 2023

### Postgraduate Loan
A separate loan for postgraduate master's or doctoral study.

- **Threshold**: Set annually
- **Rate**: 6% of earnings above the threshold
- **Key difference**: Deducted alongside (not instead of) any undergraduate loan

An employee can be on both an undergraduate plan and a postgraduate plan simultaneously, in which case you make two separate deductions.

## How you know to make deductions

HMRC will notify you to start making student loan deductions in one of two ways:

1. **Start notice (SL1)**: HMRC sends you a notice through your payroll software or by post, telling you which plan type to deduct and from which date.
2. **New starter declaration**: When a new employee joins and does not provide a P45, they complete a starter declaration. One of the questions asks whether they have a student loan. If they answer yes, you begin deductions based on the plan type they declare.

You must not start deductions without one of these triggers. Even if you know an employee went to university, you cannot deduct student loan repayments unless HMRC has instructed you to do so or the employee has declared the loan on their starter form.

Similarly, you must not stop deductions unless HMRC sends you a stop notice (SL2). Even if the employee tells you their loan is paid off, continue deducting until HMRC confirms.

## Calculating deductions

The calculation is straightforward:

1. **Determine the employee's gross earnings for the pay period** (before tax and NI, but after pension deductions if using net pay arrangement)
2. **Convert the annual threshold to a per-period figure** (divide by 12 for monthly, 52 for weekly)
3. **If earnings exceed the threshold, calculate the deduction**: (Earnings − Threshold) × Rate
4. **Round down to the nearest whole penny**

### Worked example (monthly pay, Plan 2)

Assume the annual threshold is £27,295 and the employee earns £2,800 per month:

- Monthly threshold: £27,295 ÷ 12 = £2,274.58
- Earnings above threshold: £2,800 − £2,274.58 = £525.42
- Deduction: £525.42 × 9% = £47.29

The £47.29 is deducted from the employee's pay and included in your PAYE payment to HMRC.

### Multiple loans

If an employee has both an undergraduate and postgraduate loan:

- Calculate each deduction separately using its own threshold and rate
- The undergraduate deduction uses 9%
- The postgraduate deduction uses 6%
- Both are deducted in the same pay period

## Reporting through RTI

Student loan deductions are reported to HMRC through your Real Time Information submissions. Your Full Payment Submission (FPS) includes fields for:

- The student loan plan type
- The amount deducted in the pay period
- Year-to-date student loan deductions

Make sure you are using the correct plan type in your RTI submissions. Using the wrong plan type can result in deductions being applied to the wrong loan account, causing problems for the employee.

## Common questions

### What if the employee says they do not have a student loan?

If HMRC has sent you a start notice, you must make deductions regardless of what the employee tells you. Direct them to contact the Student Loans Company to resolve any discrepancy.

### What if the employee has already repaid their loan?

Continue deducting until you receive a stop notice from HMRC. If the employee has overpaid, the Student Loans Company will refund them directly. It is not your responsibility to verify the loan balance.

### What if I hire someone from abroad who has a student loan from another country?

You only need to make deductions for UK student loans administered by the Student Loans Company. Foreign student loans are not collected through UK payroll.

### What about directors?

Directors' student loan deductions are calculated on an annual basis (similar to directors' NI), unless you choose to use period-by-period calculation. The annual method prevents over-deduction in months where the director takes a larger payment.

### What about employees with multiple jobs?

Each employer deducts independently based on the earnings paid by that employer. The thresholds are applied per employment, not in aggregate.

## Payroll software and student loans

Most [payroll software](/payroll) handles student loan deductions automatically once you enter the loan plan type and start date. The software:

- Applies the correct threshold and rate for each plan type
- Calculates the deduction each pay period
- Includes the deduction in RTI submissions
- Tracks year-to-date deductions
- Handles multiple concurrent loan types

When you receive a start notice from HMRC, simply enter the plan type and effective date in your payroll system. The calculations are automatic from that point.

## Key responsibilities

As an employer, your student loan obligations are:

- Start deductions when instructed by HMRC or declared by the employee
- Use the correct plan type, threshold, and rate
- Deduct from every pay period where earnings exceed the threshold
- Report deductions accurately through RTI
- Pay deductions to HMRC alongside PAYE and NI
- Stop deductions only when HMRC sends a stop notice

Student loan deductions are one of the simpler payroll calculations, but they require attention to the correct plan type and threshold. Get the setup right, and your payroll software will handle the rest.
