Petty cash is the small fund of physical cash that a business keeps on hand for minor, everyday expenses — postage, office supplies, taxi fares, refreshments for meetings, and other small purchases where using a card or raising a purchase order would be impractical.

It sounds simple, and conceptually it is. But petty cash is one of the most commonly mismanaged areas of business finance. Without a clear system, money disappears, receipts go missing, and the balance never quite adds up.

Here is how to manage petty cash properly.

## Setting up your petty cash system

### Determine the float amount

Your petty cash float is the starting balance — the amount you top up to each time you replenish. It should be large enough to cover a normal period's small expenses but small enough that losing or misplacing it would not be a significant problem.

For most small businesses, a float of 50 to 200 is reasonable. Larger businesses with more frequent small purchases might need 500 or more.

### Assign a custodian

One person should be responsible for the petty cash at all times. This person (the custodian) controls access to the cash, approves disbursements, maintains the log, and handles replenishment.

Having a single custodian creates accountability. If multiple people have access to the cash without clear responsibility, tracking becomes impossible.

### Get a secure container

Keep petty cash in a locked box or drawer. This is not about distrust — it is about preventing casual access and establishing that petty cash is business money, not communal change.

### Set spending limits

Define the maximum amount that can be paid from petty cash for a single purchase. Anything above this limit should go through the normal purchasing process (company card, bank transfer, purchase order). A typical limit is 25 to 50 per transaction.

### Define allowable expenses

Be clear about what petty cash can and cannot be used for. Typical allowable expenses include:

- Postage and stamps
- Office supplies (pens, paper, printer ink)
- Cleaning supplies
- Taxi fares and parking
- Tea, coffee, and refreshments for the office
- Small tools or parts
- Emergency sundries

Petty cash should not be used for:

- Personal expenses
- Regular, recurring purchases (set up an account with the supplier instead)
- Purchases above the spending limit
- Cash advances or loans to staff

## The petty cash process

### Step 1: Someone needs to make a small purchase

An employee needs to buy something small — a pack of envelopes, for example.

### Step 2: Get approval (if required)

Depending on your process, the purchase may need pre-approval from the custodian or a manager. For very small amounts, this might be informal.

### Step 3: Disburse the cash

The custodian gives the employee cash from the petty cash box. The employee should sign a petty cash voucher or slip noting the date, amount, purpose, and their name.

### Step 4: Make the purchase and get a receipt

The employee makes the purchase and obtains a receipt. This is non-negotiable — no receipt, no reimbursement from petty cash.

### Step 5: Return the receipt and any change

The employee returns the receipt and any unused cash to the custodian. The custodian updates the petty cash log with the actual amount spent.

### Step 6: File the receipt

The receipt is attached to the petty cash voucher and filed. Some businesses number their vouchers sequentially to make tracking easier.

## Maintaining the petty cash log

The petty cash log is a running record of every transaction. It should include:

| Date | Voucher # | Description | Amount Out | Amount In | Balance |
|------|-----------|-------------|------------|-----------|---------|
| 1 Jun | — | Float established | — | 100.00 | 100.00 |
| 3 Jun | PC-001 | Postage stamps | 12.60 | — | 87.40 |
| 5 Jun | PC-002 | Printer paper | 8.99 | — | 78.41 |
| 8 Jun | PC-003 | Taxi to client meeting | 15.00 | — | 63.41 |

At any point, the physical cash in the box plus the total of all vouchers should equal the original float amount. If it does not, there is a discrepancy that needs to be investigated.

## Reconciling petty cash

### Regular reconciliation

At least once a month (more often if you have high petty cash activity), reconcile:

1. Count the physical cash in the box
2. Total the vouchers/receipts for the period
3. Check that cash + vouchers = float amount
4. Investigate any discrepancies
5. Record the expenses in your accounting system

### What to do about discrepancies

Small discrepancies (a few pence or cents) happen — coins get miscounted, change is rounded. Record these as "petty cash over/short" in your accounts.

Larger or recurring discrepancies need investigation. Common causes:

- Missing receipts (someone forgot to return one)
- Incorrect change given or received
- Unrecorded disbursements
- In the worst case, theft

If discrepancies become a regular problem, tighten your controls: require pre-approval for all disbursements, count the cash more frequently, or reduce the float.

## Replenishing petty cash

When the cash runs low, replenish it back to the float amount:

1. Total all the vouchers for the period
2. Write a cheque or make a bank withdrawal for that amount
3. Add the cash to the petty cash box
4. The balance should now be back to the original float
5. Record the replenishment in your accounting system

The replenishment is the point at which petty cash expenses enter your main accounting records. Each voucher is categorised to the appropriate expense account.

## The imprest system

The method described above is called the imprest system, and it is the standard approach to petty cash management:

1. Start with a fixed float
2. Spend from it (with receipts)
3. At any time: cash + receipts = float amount
4. Replenish back to the float amount
5. Repeat

The imprest system is self-balancing by design. If your cash plus receipts do not equal the float, something has gone wrong and you know immediately.

## Petty cash in the digital age

With the rise of company debit cards, payment apps, and expense management tools, some businesses have eliminated petty cash entirely. Every small purchase goes through a card, and the receipt is captured digitally.

### Should you eliminate petty cash?

**Arguments for keeping petty cash:**
- Some purchases still require cash (certain market stalls, vending machines, parking meters)
- Not every employee has a company card
- It is simpler for very small, infrequent purchases

**Arguments for eliminating it:**
- Every transaction is automatically recorded by the card issuer
- Digital receipts are easier to manage than paper ones
- No risk of cash loss or theft
- Better audit trail

For many small businesses, the answer is to reduce rather than eliminate petty cash. Keep a small float for genuinely cash-only situations and route everything else through a card.

## Tax and petty cash

Petty cash expenses are tax-deductible just like any other business expense, provided they are:

- Genuine business expenses (not personal)
- Supported by receipts
- Recorded in your accounts

The key is documentation. A tax authority will not disallow a legitimate expense just because it was paid in cash, but they will expect the same level of evidence as any other expense: who, what, when, where, and why.

## Common petty cash mistakes

### No receipts

This is the most common problem. Without receipts, you cannot prove the expense was genuine, and the claim may not be tax-deductible. Insist on receipts for every petty cash transaction.

### Infrequent reconciliation

If you only reconcile petty cash at year end, you have no chance of finding the cause of a discrepancy from six months ago. Reconcile monthly at minimum.

### Too many people with access

The more people who can take cash from the box, the harder it is to maintain accountability. Assign one custodian and stick to it.

### Using petty cash for personal expenses

Even if the intention is to pay it back later, using petty cash for personal purposes is a misuse of business funds. Keep it strictly for business expenses.

### Float too large

A large float is an unnecessary temptation and risk. Keep only as much cash as you realistically need for the replenishment period.

Petty cash management is not glamorous, but it is a basic internal control that every business with cash expenses needs. Set up the system, assign responsibility, insist on receipts, reconcile regularly, and it will take care of itself.
