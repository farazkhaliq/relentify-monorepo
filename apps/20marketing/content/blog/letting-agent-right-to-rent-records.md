Right to rent checks — verifying that a prospective tenant has the legal right to rent residential property — are a mandatory part of the letting process. Agents who fail to conduct these checks, or who fail to keep adequate records, face significant penalties.

The checks themselves are straightforward. The record-keeping and ongoing monitoring requirements are where many agencies fall short — not through lack of diligence, but through lack of system. When you are processing dozens of tenancies, each with its own documentation requirements, it is easy for records to become incomplete or disorganised.

A CRM that integrates right to rent checks into your tenancy setup workflow ensures that every check is conducted on time, every document is recorded properly, and every follow-up action is triggered automatically.

## What the checks involve

The process has three components: obtaining the tenant's original documents, checking that the documents are genuine and relate to the person presenting them, and making and retaining copies.

Acceptable documents include passports, national identity cards, residence permits, and other immigration documents as specified by the relevant authority. The specific list of acceptable documents depends on the jurisdiction and may change over time.

For tenants with time-limited permission to rent, a follow-up check is required before the permission expires. This follow-up is one of the most commonly missed steps, because the deadline may be months or years in the future and is easy to forget without a systematic reminder.

## Recording checks in your CRM

Each right to rent check should be recorded against the tenant's contact record in your CRM, with the following information.

The date the check was conducted. The documents that were presented and checked. Copies of those documents, stored digitally and linked to the tenant record. The identity of the person who conducted the check. The outcome — whether the tenant has an unrestricted right to rent, a time-limited right, or no right to rent.

For tenants with time-limited permission, the CRM should also record the expiry date of that permission and trigger an automated reminder to conduct a follow-up check before the expiry.

## Integrating checks into the tenancy workflow

Right to rent checks should not be a separate process that agents have to remember. They should be an integrated step in the tenancy setup workflow — as automatic as collecting a deposit or signing the agreement.

When a new tenancy is created in your CRM, the workflow should include a task for right to rent verification, with a deadline based on the tenancy start date. The task should not be marked as complete until the check has been conducted, the documents have been uploaded, and the outcome has been recorded.

This integration ensures that no tenancy is set up without a right to rent check, regardless of how busy the office is or how many tenancies are being processed simultaneously.

## Follow-up checks

For tenants with time-limited permission, follow-up checks are mandatory. The CRM should calculate the follow-up date based on the expiry of the tenant's current permission and create an automated reminder well in advance — typically 28 days before the follow-up is due.

If the follow-up check reveals that the tenant's permission has expired and has not been renewed, the agent must take appropriate action. The CRM should guide this process, linking to the relevant procedures and recording the actions taken.

Follow-up checks are the area of greatest risk for most agencies. The initial check is integrated into the tenancy setup process, so it rarely gets missed. But a follow-up that is due eighteen months later, when the original urgency has faded, is easily overlooked. Automated reminders eliminate this risk.

## Common mistakes

### Accepting photocopies

The original documents must be seen. Photocopies, scans, or photographs of documents are not sufficient for the initial check. The person conducting the check must see the physical document (or conduct an online check through an approved digital identity service).

### Incomplete records

Recording that a check was conducted but not recording what documents were presented or retaining copies is insufficient. The records must demonstrate not just that a check happened, but what it involved.

### Conducting checks after the tenancy starts

The check must be conducted before the tenancy begins. A check conducted after the tenant has moved in does not satisfy the requirement and does not provide a statutory excuse against any penalty.

### Missing follow-up checks

As discussed above, follow-up checks for tenants with time-limited permission are frequently missed. This is perhaps the most common compliance failure in this area.

### Discriminatory practices

Right to rent checks must be conducted equally for all applicants, regardless of their apparent nationality, ethnicity, or background. Conducting checks only for applicants who "look foreign" or who have non-local names is discriminatory and illegal.

Your CRM can help ensure non-discriminatory practice by making the check a mandatory step in every tenancy setup, with no option to skip or bypass it.

## The penalty regime

The penalties for non-compliance vary by jurisdiction but are consistently severe. Civil penalties for a first offence can run to thousands per tenant. Repeat offences carry even higher penalties and potential criminal prosecution.

Beyond financial penalties, non-compliance damages the agency's reputation and its relationship with the landlord. If a landlord faces a penalty because their agent failed to conduct a check, the landlord will not blame the regulation — they will blame the agent.

## Building a culture of compliance

Right to rent compliance is not a one-off task — it is an ongoing obligation that applies to every new tenancy and every follow-up check for the duration of every time-limited tenancy. Building a culture where these checks are taken seriously, conducted properly, and recorded thoroughly is essential.

Training is the foundation. Every team member involved in the letting process should understand the requirements, the acceptable documents, and the consequences of non-compliance. Refresher training should be conducted annually, and updates should be communicated whenever the requirements change.

Your CRM reinforces the culture by making compliance visible. Dashboard indicators showing the status of right to rent checks across your portfolio — completed, pending, overdue — keep the team focused and accountable.
