When you work as a freelancer or independent contractor in the United States, nobody withholds taxes from your pay. That means the full amount hits your bank account — which feels great until you realize the IRS still expects its share, and it expects it throughout the year, not just at tax time.

Quarterly estimated tax payments are the mechanism for this. They ensure you pay income tax and self-employment tax as you earn, rather than accumulating a massive bill on April 15. Missing or underpaying your estimated taxes results in penalties and interest, regardless of whether you eventually pay everything you owe.

## Who must pay estimated taxes?

You generally must make quarterly estimated tax payments if:

- You expect to owe at least $1,000 in tax for the year after subtracting withholding and credits
- Your withholding and credits will be less than the smaller of: (a) 90 percent of the tax shown on this year's return, or (b) 100 percent of the tax shown on last year's return (110 percent if your adjusted gross income exceeded $150,000)

Most freelancers with meaningful income will meet this threshold.

### Exceptions

You may not need to pay estimated taxes if:

- You had no tax liability for the prior year (and were a US citizen or resident for the entire year)
- You have sufficient withholding from other income sources (like a W-2 job) to cover your freelance tax liability

## What taxes are you paying?

Quarterly estimated payments cover two types of tax:

### Federal income tax

Just like employees, you owe income tax on your net self-employment income. Your tax rate depends on your total taxable income and filing status, following the standard progressive federal tax brackets.

### Self-employment tax

This is the freelancer's equivalent of the employee and employer shares of Social Security and Medicare taxes. The total rate is 15.3 percent, broken down as:

- **12.4 percent** for Social Security (on net earnings up to the annual wage base)
- **2.9 percent** for Medicare (on all net earnings)
- **0.9 percent** additional Medicare tax on net earnings above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly)

As a freelancer, you pay both the employee and employer portions. However, you can deduct the employer-equivalent portion (half of the self-employment tax) when calculating your adjusted gross income.

## Quarterly payment deadlines

The tax year is divided into four unequal payment periods:

| Period | Income earned | Payment due |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Q1 | January 1 – March 31 | April 15 |
| Q2 | April 1 – May 31 | June 15 |
| Q3 | June 1 – August 31 | September 15 |
| Q4 | September 1 – December 31 | January 15 (following year) |

Note that Q2 covers only two months while Q3 covers three. If a due date falls on a weekend or holiday, the deadline moves to the next business day.

## Calculating your estimated tax

### Method 1: Prior year safe harbor

The simplest approach is to pay at least 100 percent of last year's total tax liability, divided into four equal quarterly payments. If your adjusted gross income exceeded $150,000 ($75,000 if married filing separately), you must pay 110 percent of last year's tax.

**Advantage:** Simple to calculate. As long as you pay enough, you avoid underpayment penalties regardless of how much you actually owe this year.

**Disadvantage:** If your income is significantly higher this year, you will still owe a large balance at filing time.

### Method 2: Current year estimate

Estimate your current year's income and calculate the expected tax. Divide by four for quarterly payments.

**Steps:**
1. Estimate your total freelance income for the year
2. Subtract estimated business deductions to get net self-employment income
3. Calculate self-employment tax (15.3 percent of 92.35 percent of net SE income)
4. Calculate federal income tax on your total taxable income
5. Add SE tax and income tax together
6. Subtract any credits or withholding from other sources
7. Divide the result by four

**Advantage:** Payments are proportional to your actual income.

**Disadvantage:** Requires estimating your annual income, which can be difficult for freelancers with variable income.

### Method 3: Annualized income installment method

If your income is highly variable throughout the year, you can calculate each quarter's payment based on actual income earned during that period. This avoids overpaying in slow quarters.

This method requires more complex calculations and is documented on IRS Form 2210, Schedule AI. Consider using tax software or a professional if you choose this approach.

## Making payments

### IRS Direct Pay

Pay directly from your bank account at irs.gov/directpay. Free, no registration required.

### Electronic Federal Tax Payment System (EFTPS)

Register at eftps.gov for ongoing estimated payments. You can schedule payments in advance, which helps with planning.

### IRS2Go app

The IRS mobile app allows payments through Direct Pay or credit/debit card.

### Credit or debit card

Pay through IRS-approved processors. Note that processors charge a convenience fee (typically 1.87-1.99 percent for credit cards, or a flat fee for debit).

### Check or money order

Mail with a completed Form 1040-ES voucher to the address specified for your state.

### Record your payments

Record every estimated tax payment in your [accounting software](/accounting) as it is made. Note the date, amount, and payment method. You will need this information when filing your annual return.

## State estimated taxes

Many states also require quarterly estimated tax payments for self-employment income. The rules, rates, deadlines, and payment methods vary by state. If you live in a state with income tax, check whether you need to make state estimated payments as well.

Some states follow the federal quarterly schedule. Others have different deadlines.

## Avoiding underpayment penalties

The IRS charges a penalty for underpayment of estimated taxes. To avoid it:

- Pay at least 90 percent of your current year tax liability through estimated payments, OR
- Pay at least 100 percent of your prior year tax liability (110 percent if AGI exceeded $150,000)

Meeting either threshold protects you from penalties, even if you owe additional tax when you file.

### If your income is irregular

Freelance income is rarely predictable. Some strategies for managing variable income:

- **Use the prior year safe harbor** to ensure penalty protection regardless of income fluctuations
- **Adjust payments quarterly** as your actual income becomes clearer
- **Front-load payments** — If you have a strong start to the year, make larger payments early
- **Set aside a percentage of every payment** — Transfer 25-30 percent of every client payment into a dedicated tax savings account immediately

## Tracking and planning

### Maintain accurate income and expense records

Your [accounting software](/accounting) is your primary tool for estimated tax calculations. At any point during the year, you should be able to see:

- Total freelance income received year-to-date
- Total business expenses year-to-date
- Net self-employment income
- Estimated tax payments already made

This data lets you adjust your remaining quarterly payments if your income is running significantly above or below your initial estimate.

### Separate your tax money

Open a dedicated savings account for tax payments. When you receive freelance income, immediately transfer your estimated tax percentage to this account. This prevents you from spending money that belongs to the IRS.

### Review quarterly

Before each estimated payment deadline, review your year-to-date financials and adjust your payment if needed. A quick review takes fifteen minutes and prevents surprises at filing time.

## The bottom line

Quarterly estimated taxes are not optional for freelancers with meaningful income. Treat them as a regular business obligation — calculate your payments, set aside the money, and pay on time. The discipline of quarterly payments prevents the stress of a large annual tax bill and keeps you in good standing with the IRS.
