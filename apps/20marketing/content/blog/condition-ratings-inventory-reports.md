One of the most common reasons inventory reports fail under scrutiny is inconsistency. A report might describe a carpet as "good" in one room and "fair" in another, even though both are in a similar state. Or two clerks working for the same company might grade the same wall damage differently — one calls it "minor wear," the other calls it "damage requiring attention."

When a deposit dispute reaches adjudication, the adjudicator is looking for clarity and consistency. If your condition ratings shift in meaning from room to room or report to report, the entire document loses credibility. The fix is straightforward: adopt a standardised grading system and apply it the same way every time.

This guide explains how to build a consistent condition rating framework, train yourself or your team to use it reliably, and avoid the common pitfalls that undermine otherwise thorough inventory reports.

## Why consistency matters more than precision

It is tempting to focus on making condition descriptions as detailed as possible. And detail is important — but it is secondary to consistency. A simple three-point scale applied uniformly across every report is more useful than a ten-point scale that different clerks interpret differently.

Here is why. In a deposit dispute, the adjudicator compares the check-in report against the check-out report. They are looking for changes. If the check-in report says a kitchen worktop is in "good" condition and the check-out report says it is in "fair" condition, the adjudicator needs to understand what that shift means. If "good" and "fair" are clearly defined and consistently used, the shift is meaningful evidence. If the terms are vague or applied inconsistently, the evidence collapses.

Consistency also protects the clerk. If a landlord or tenant challenges your report, you can point to a documented rating system and explain exactly what each grade means. That is much stronger than saying "I used my professional judgement" — which, while valid, is harder to defend when the other party disagrees.

## Building a condition rating scale

Most inventory professionals use a scale of three to five grades. Fewer than three lacks nuance. More than five introduces ambiguity. Here is a practical five-point scale that works well for residential property inventories:

### Suggested five-point scale

**1 — New or as new.** The item or surface shows no signs of use. It appears to be newly installed, replaced, or professionally restored. There are no marks, stains, scratches, or wear of any kind.

**2 — Good.** The item or surface is in very good condition with only minimal signs of normal use. There may be the faintest marks or light wear, but nothing that would be considered damage or that detracts from the overall appearance.

**3 — Fair.** The item or surface shows clear signs of normal use and age-appropriate wear. There may be visible marks, light scratching, minor staining, or fading. These are consistent with fair wear and tear for the age and type of the item.

**4 — Poor.** The item or surface shows significant wear, damage, or deterioration beyond what would be expected from normal use. This might include deep scratches, heavy staining, chips, dents, or functional issues.

**5 — Very poor or damaged.** The item or surface is in a state of disrepair. It may be broken, heavily soiled, missing parts, or otherwise unusable or unsightly. Replacement or significant repair is likely needed.

### When three points are enough

If a five-point scale feels like overkill for your workflow, a three-point version works well for many agencies:

- **Good** — minimal or no wear beyond brand new
- **Fair** — normal wear and tear, age-appropriate condition
- **Poor** — damage or deterioration beyond normal wear

The key is to choose one scale, define each grade clearly, and use it across every report.

## Applying ratings room by room

A common mistake is to give a single condition rating per room. "Bedroom 1 — Good condition" tells an adjudicator almost nothing. If there is a dispute about a stain on the carpet, a crack in a window, or a mark on the wall, a blanket room rating offers no evidence for or against any specific claim.

Instead, apply your condition rating to individual elements within each room:

- Ceiling — Good
- Walls — Fair (light scuff marks near door frame)
- Flooring (carpet) — Fair (minor flattening in high-traffic area)
- Window — Good
- Door and frame — Good
- Light fitting — Good
- Radiator — Fair (minor paint chips on top edge)

This level of detail, combined with consistent ratings, makes the report genuinely useful in a dispute. The adjudicator can compare the check-in and check-out ratings for the specific item in question, rather than trying to interpret a vague overall impression.

## Anchoring ratings with descriptions and photographs

A condition rating on its own is a label. What gives it weight is the supporting description and photographic evidence.

For each element you rate, include a brief description that explains the rating. This does not need to be lengthy — a single sentence is usually sufficient:

- "Walls — Fair. Light scuff marks near door frame, approximately 10cm, consistent with normal furniture movement."
- "Carpet — Good. No visible stains or wear. Recently cleaned."

The description anchors the rating to a specific observation. Without it, "Fair" is open to interpretation. With it, the meaning is clear.

Photographs serve the same anchoring function visually. A time-stamped photo of the scuff marks described above removes any ambiguity about what "Fair" means in this context. The combination of rating, description, and photograph creates a three-layer evidence package that is very difficult to challenge.

## Training your team for consistency

If you work alone, consistency is a matter of personal discipline. If you manage a team of clerks, it becomes a training challenge. Two people can look at the same wall and honestly disagree about whether it is "Good" or "Fair." That is not a failure of professionalism — it is a natural result of subjective judgement.

The solution is calibration. Here are practical steps to align your team:

### Use reference photographs

Create a library of reference images for each grade on your scale. What does a "Good" wall look like? What does a "Fair" carpet look like? When new clerks can compare what they see in a property against a visual benchmark, their ratings become more consistent.

### Run calibration exercises

Periodically, have two or more clerks independently rate the same property (or the same set of photographs). Compare their results. Where ratings diverge, discuss why and agree on the correct grade. This is not about finding fault — it is about aligning interpretation.

### Review reports for drift

Over time, individual clerks may gradually shift their standards. Someone who started strict may become more lenient, or vice versa. Regular report reviews catch this drift before it becomes a pattern.

### Document your rating definitions

Write down your rating scale and keep it accessible to every team member. Include the definitions, the reference photographs, and any notes from calibration exercises. This document becomes your quality standard, and it should be updated as new edge cases arise.

## Handling fair wear and tear

The concept of fair wear and tear is where most rating disagreements happen. A tenant moves out after three years. The carpet shows flattening in the hallway and some fading near the window. Is that "Fair" or "Poor"?

The answer depends on context. Three years of normal use in a hallway will naturally flatten a standard carpet. That is fair wear and tear, and a condition rating of "Fair" is appropriate. But the same flattening after six months in a bedroom might indicate something unusual, and a rating of "Poor" could be justified.

When applying condition ratings, always consider:

- **Age of the item.** A five-year-old carpet is expected to show more wear than a new one.
- **Quality of the item.** A builder-grade paint finish will mark more easily than a premium one.
- **Duration of the tenancy.** Longer tenancies naturally produce more wear.
- **Location within the property.** High-traffic areas like hallways and kitchens will show more wear than spare bedrooms.

If your rating system accounts for these factors — even briefly, in the supporting description — the report becomes much more defensible.

## Common mistakes to avoid

### Mixing rating systems between reports

If your check-in report uses a five-point scale and your check-out report uses a three-point scale (or no scale at all), comparison becomes unreliable. Use the same system for both.

### Using vague terms without definition

Words like "acceptable," "reasonable," or "average" mean different things to different people. Either define them precisely in your rating scale or avoid them entirely.

### Rating condition differently for furnished versus unfurnished items

Your rating system should work the same way whether you are grading a built-in wardrobe or a freestanding bookcase. The condition of an item is independent of whether the landlord or tenant owns it.

### Forgetting to rate cleanliness separately

Condition and cleanliness are different things. A carpet can be in good structural condition but poorly cleaned. Rate them as separate attributes to avoid confusion during check-out comparisons.
