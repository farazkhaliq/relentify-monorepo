The photographs in an inventory report are often more important than the written descriptions. An adjudicator reviewing a deposit dispute can read a description of a "mark on the wall" in many different ways. But a clear photograph of that mark — showing its size, location, and nature — leaves very little room for interpretation.

Good inventory photography is not about artistic composition or expensive equipment. It is about consistently capturing clear, well-lit, properly framed images that document what you need them to document. This guide covers the practical techniques that produce useful inventory photos.

## Equipment

### Camera choice

A modern smartphone is the best tool for inventory photography. Smartphones are always with you, they integrate with inventory software, and their cameras are more than capable of producing the image quality needed for inventory reports.

Dedicated cameras are unnecessary for most inventory work. They add bulk, require separate file management, and do not integrate with reporting software. The exception might be very high-end properties where the client expects exceptional photographic quality.

### Lens cleaning

It sounds trivial, but a dirty phone lens is one of the most common causes of blurry or hazy inventory photos. Get in the habit of wiping your lens before each inspection. Carry a microfibre cloth or use your shirt — just make sure the lens is clean.

### Battery management

A full day of inventory photography can drain your phone battery quickly. Start each day with a full charge and carry a portable charger. Running out of battery mid-inspection is not just inconvenient — it means missing photos that you cannot retake later.

## Lighting

### Use natural light where possible

Natural daylight is the best light source for inventory photography. It is even, neutral, and shows colours accurately. When entering a room, open curtains and blinds to maximise natural light before taking photos.

### Turn on room lights

In addition to natural light, turn on all room lights. This fills shadows and illuminates darker areas that natural light may not reach. The combination of natural and artificial light produces the most evenly lit images.

### Avoid using flash

Flash creates harsh shadows, washes out nearby surfaces, and produces an uneven exposure. It can make surfaces look different from how they appear in person, which undermines the accuracy of the photograph.

The only exception is when a room is very dark (no windows, no working lights) and flash is the only way to capture an image. In that case, take one photo with flash and one without (using any available light) for comparison.

### Difficult lighting situations

Some common difficult situations and how to handle them:

- **Rooms with no windows**: Turn on all lights. Use your phone's torch as a supplementary light source. Take photos slowly to allow the camera to adjust.
- **Rooms with very bright windows**: If shooting toward a window, the room may appear dark while the window is blown out. Try shooting from an angle that avoids including the window directly. Or take multiple shots — one exposing for the window area, one for the room interior.
- **Basements and cupboards**: Your phone torch is your friend. Hold it at an angle to avoid direct glare.

## Angles and composition

### Room overview shots

Every room should start with one or two overview shots taken from the doorway. These establish the room layout, show the general condition, and provide context for the detail shots that follow.

Stand in the doorway and photograph the room at a natural standing height. Hold the phone level — do not tilt up or down unless you are specifically capturing the ceiling or floor. Try to capture as much of the room as possible in a single frame.

For larger rooms, take two overview shots from opposite corners to cover the entire space.

### Wall-by-wall coverage

After the overview, photograph each wall individually. Stand at a distance that captures the entire wall in one frame. This provides a clear record of the wall condition that can be compared at check-out.

### Floor shots

Photograph the floor in each room. For carpet, get close enough to show the pile condition and any stains or marks. For hard flooring, capture the surface condition including any scratches, chips, or wear patterns.

Shooting floors can be tricky — shooting straight down shows detail but not context; shooting at an angle shows context but may miss detail. Take both: one shot from standing height looking down at the floor area, and one closer shot at an angle to show surface texture and condition.

### Close-up detail shots

Every specific condition that might be relevant to a deposit dispute needs a close-up shot:

- Marks, stains, or damage on any surface
- Scratches on doors, floors, or furniture
- Chips or cracks in tiles, glass, or surfaces
- Wear patterns on carpets or upholstery
- Inside appliances (ovens, fridges, washing machines)
- Meter readings
- Keys

For close-ups, get near enough that the detail is clearly visible. Most smartphone cameras focus well at about 15 to 30 centimetres for detail shots. If the image is blurry, pull back slightly.

### Furniture and contents

For furnished properties, photograph each piece of furniture:

- An overall shot showing the item in context
- Close-ups of any wear, marks, or damage
- For upholstered items, shots of the seats, arms, and any visible wear points

### External areas

Gardens and external areas benefit from wide shots showing the overall condition, supplemented by detail shots of specific features (fences, patios, sheds, etc.).

## Consistency

### Same angles at check-in and check-out

This is one of the most important principles in inventory photography. At check-out, try to replicate the check-in photographs as closely as possible — same room, same angle, same distance. When an adjudicator sees the same view at two different dates, changes are immediately obvious.

### Same order throughout

Photograph rooms in the same order every time. This creates a predictable structure that makes reports easy to navigate and check-out comparisons straightforward.

A typical order might be:

1. Property exterior (front)
2. Entrance/hallway
3. Living room
4. Kitchen
5. Bedrooms (in order)
6. Bathrooms (in order)
7. Utility areas
8. External areas (rear)
9. Meters
10. Keys

### Same level of coverage

Every room should receive the same level of photographic attention. Do not photograph the kitchen in detail and then rush through the bedrooms. Inconsistent coverage creates gaps in your evidence.

## Common photography mistakes

### Not enough photos

This is the most frequent error. When in doubt, take more photos. The cost of an extra photograph is zero. The cost of not having the right photograph when you need it for a dispute can be significant.

Aim for a minimum of:
- 2 to 3 overview shots per room
- 1 photo per wall
- 1 photo of the floor
- Close-ups of any notable conditions
- Inside and outside of each appliance

### Blurry images

Always check each photo on screen after taking it. If it is blurry, retake it immediately. A blurry photo is worse than no photo — it suggests you took the photo but could not be bothered to check the quality.

### Too far away

Overview shots are useful for context, but they do not show detail. If the only photo of a wall is taken from across the room, a small mark or hole will not be visible. Combine overview shots with closer shots for each area.

### Inconsistent angles

If your check-in photos are taken from standing height and your check-out photos are taken from floor level, the comparison is harder. Maintain consistent angles across all inspections.

### Not photographing items in good condition

It is tempting to only photograph damage and issues. But items in good condition at check-in need to be photographed too. Without a check-in photo of an undamaged door, you have no evidence to prove the door was undamaged before the tenancy.

## The bottom line

Good inventory photography is a skill, but it is a simple one. Clean your lens, use available light, cover every room and every notable condition, check your images as you go, and maintain consistency between inspections. These habits, applied consistently, produce photographic evidence that does its job when it matters most.

The goal is not beautiful photos. The goal is clear, dated, comprehensive evidence that shows exactly what the property looked like at a specific moment in time. Get that right, and your photographs will speak for themselves.
