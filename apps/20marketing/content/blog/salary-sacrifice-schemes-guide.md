Salary sacrifice is one of the most effective ways to offer tax-efficient benefits to your employees while reducing your own employer costs. Yet many small businesses either do not know about it or assume it is too complex to set up.

This guide explains how salary sacrifice works, which schemes are available, and how to implement them in your business.

## What is salary sacrifice?

Salary sacrifice (sometimes called "salary exchange") is an arrangement where an employee agrees to give up a portion of their gross salary in exchange for a non-cash benefit. Because the benefit replaces salary rather than being paid on top of it, both the employee and the employer can save on National Insurance contributions.

The key mechanism is simple: the employee's contractual salary is reduced by the value of the benefit. They receive the benefit instead of that portion of their pay. Because their gross salary is lower, they pay less income tax and employee National Insurance. The employer also pays less employer National Insurance on the reduced salary.

Everyone wins — the employee gets the benefit at a lower cost than buying it themselves, and the employer saves on NI contributions.

## How it works: a pension example

Pension salary sacrifice is the most common arrangement. Instead of the employee making pension contributions from their net pay, the employer makes the contribution directly from the sacrificed salary.

### Without salary sacrifice
An employee earning £30,000 wants to contribute £150 per month to their pension.
- £150 is deducted from their net pay (after tax and NI)
- The actual cost to the employee is £150
- The pension provider claims basic rate tax relief, adding £37.50 (at 20%) to the pension pot
- Total pension contribution: £187.50

### With salary sacrifice
The employee's salary is reduced by £187.50 per month. The employer pays this directly into the pension.
- The employee's gross salary drops from £2,500/month to £2,312.50/month
- The employee saves NI on the £187.50 (approximately £25 at the 13.25% NI rate)
- The employer saves employer NI on the £187.50 (approximately £25.88 at 13.8%)
- Total pension contribution: £187.50 (same amount, but cheaper for both parties)

The employee gets the same pension contribution but takes home more money each month. The employer saves on NI costs. Many employers choose to pass their NI savings into the employee's pension as well, making the arrangement even more beneficial.

## Which benefits work with salary sacrifice?

Since April 2017, the tax advantages of salary sacrifice are limited to specific benefits. The following schemes still benefit from favourable tax treatment:

### Pension contributions
The most popular salary sacrifice arrangement. Works with any qualifying workplace pension scheme. Both employer and employee save on NI.

### Cycle to work
Employees sacrifice salary to lease a bicycle and cycling equipment for commuting. The benefit is exempt from income tax and NI. At the end of the lease period, the employee can usually purchase the equipment at a reduced price.

### Childcare vouchers (closed to new entrants)
While no longer open to new applicants, existing participants can continue until they leave the scheme or change employers. Employees sacrifice salary in exchange for vouchers to pay for registered childcare.

### Ultra-low emission vehicles
Company cars with CO2 emissions of 75g/km or less qualify for favourable benefit-in-kind rates under salary sacrifice. Electric vehicles are particularly tax-efficient under current rules, with benefit-in-kind rates significantly lower than for petrol or diesel cars.

### Technology schemes
Some employers offer salary sacrifice for personal technology purchases (laptops, tablets, phones). However, these do not receive the same tax advantages as the schemes above — the benefit is taxed as a benefit in kind at full value.

## Setting up a salary sacrifice scheme

### Step 1: Choose the benefit
Decide which salary sacrifice benefits to offer. Pensions and cycle to work are the most common for small businesses.

### Step 2: Amend employment contracts
Salary sacrifice requires a genuine contractual change. The employee must formally agree to a lower salary in exchange for the benefit. This is not optional — without a contractual change, HMRC may not recognise the arrangement.

Draft an agreement (or an addendum to the existing contract) that specifies:
- The amount of salary being sacrificed
- The benefit being received in exchange
- The start and end dates of the arrangement
- What happens if the employee wants to opt out

### Step 3: Update payroll
Reduce the employee's gross salary by the sacrificed amount. Ensure your [payroll software](/payroll) handles the reduction correctly — NI and tax should be calculated on the reduced salary.

### Step 4: Arrange the benefit
For pensions, increase the employer contribution by the sacrificed amount. For cycle to work, set up the lease arrangement with a scheme provider. For ultra-low emission vehicles, arrange the car lease.

### Step 5: Communicate to employees
Explain the arrangement clearly. Employees need to understand what they are giving up, what they are receiving, and how it affects their take-home pay, pension contributions, and other calculations.

## Important considerations

### National Minimum Wage
Salary sacrifice must not reduce an employee's cash pay below the National Minimum Wage. If the sacrifice would take their effective hourly rate below the legal minimum, the arrangement is not permitted.

### Impact on other benefits
A reduced salary can affect entitlements that are based on earnings, such as:
- Statutory Maternity Pay (calculated on average weekly earnings)
- Statutory Sick Pay (qualification threshold based on earnings)
- Mortgage applications (lenders look at gross salary)
- Life insurance (if death-in-service benefit is a multiple of salary)

Consider these impacts carefully and communicate them to employees. Some employers mitigate this by using "reference salary" (the pre-sacrifice amount) for calculating death-in-service benefits and other earnings-related entitlements.

### Opting out
Employees should have the option to opt out of salary sacrifice at defined intervals — typically annually, or on a "life event" such as marriage, divorce, or a significant change in financial circumstances. The contract should specify these terms.

### HMRC compliance
For HMRC to recognise a salary sacrifice arrangement, it must be:
- A genuine contractual change (not just a payroll adjustment)
- Prospective (applied to future earnings, not retrospectively)
- Clearly documented with the employee's written agreement

If HMRC does not accept the arrangement, the tax and NI savings may be clawed back.

## The employer perspective

Salary sacrifice is one of the few employee benefits that actually saves the employer money. The NI savings on pension salary sacrifice alone can be significant — for an employer with 20 employees each sacrificing £200 per month, the annual employer NI saving is roughly £6,600.

Many employers use this saving to:
- Increase pension contributions (enhancing the employee benefit at no net cost)
- Fund the administration of other benefit schemes
- Simply reduce their overall employment costs

From an HR perspective, offering salary sacrifice demonstrates that you care about your employees' financial wellbeing. It is a relatively low-effort way to provide a meaningful benefit.

## Implementing with your payroll system

Your payroll system needs to:
- Reduce the employee's gross salary by the sacrificed amount
- Increase the employer pension contribution (for pension sacrifice)
- Calculate tax and NI on the reduced salary
- Track the arrangement start and end dates
- Report correctly through RTI

Most modern payroll platforms handle salary sacrifice without difficulty. Platforms like Relentify integrate salary sacrifice into the standard [payroll](/payroll) workflow, so you set up the arrangement once and it processes automatically each pay period.

## Key takeaways

Salary sacrifice is a powerful tool for small businesses:

- It saves both employers and employees on National Insurance
- Pension sacrifice is the most impactful and simplest to implement
- Cycle to work and ultra-low emission vehicles offer additional tax-efficient benefits
- A genuine contractual change is required for HMRC compliance
- Watch for impacts on NMW, statutory payments, and other earnings-based calculations

If you are not yet offering salary sacrifice for pensions, it is worth exploring. The savings are real, the setup is manageable, and your employees will appreciate the boost to their take-home pay and retirement savings.
