No matter how carefully you vet your clients or how diligently you chase invoices, some debts will never be collected. A client goes out of business. A dispute drags on until both parties lose interest. A small invoice is not worth the cost of pursuing.

Bad debt is an unavoidable reality of business. What matters is how you handle it in your accounts — because incorrect treatment can distort your financial reports, create tax problems, and mask the true health of your business.

## What is bad debt?

A bad debt is an amount owed to you that you do not expect to collect. It has gone from being an asset (accounts receivable — money you are owed) to being a loss (an expense that reduces your profit).

The distinction between a bad debt and a slow-paying client is important. A client who pays three months late is not a bad debt — they are a late payer. A bad debt is one where there is no realistic prospect of payment.

## When to write off a bad debt

There is no fixed rule about when a debt becomes "bad," but common indicators include:

- The client has gone into administration, liquidation, or bankruptcy
- You have exhausted all reasonable collection efforts and the client is not responding
- The client is disputing the debt and you have no realistic legal remedy
- The cost of pursuing the debt exceeds the amount owed
- The debt is very old (over 12 months with no payment or communication)

Use judgement. Writing off a debt too quickly means accepting a loss that might have been recoverable. Writing it off too slowly means your accounts overstate what you are owed.

## How to write off a bad debt

### Step 1: Document the decision

Before writing off any debt, document why you believe it is irrecoverable:

- What collection efforts have you made?
- What was the client's response (or lack thereof)?
- Is there any evidence of the client's financial difficulty?
- Why do you believe further collection efforts would be futile?

This documentation supports the write-off in your accounts and may be needed for tax purposes.

### Step 2: Record the write-off

The accounting entry for a bad debt write-off is:

- **Debit:** Bad Debt Expense (this reduces your profit)
- **Credit:** Accounts Receivable (this removes the debt from what you are owed)

After this entry, the invoice no longer appears in your accounts receivable. Your total assets decrease by the amount written off, and your profit decreases by the same amount.

In accounting software like [Relentify](/accounting), this is typically handled through a write-off function that creates the correct entries automatically and marks the invoice as written off.

### Step 3: Handle the tax implications

Bad debts that you write off are generally tax-deductible — they reduce your taxable profit. However, requirements vary:

- The income must have been previously included in your taxable profits
- You must be able to demonstrate that the debt is genuinely irrecoverable
- You must have made reasonable efforts to collect

If you are registered for VAT and the original invoice included VAT, you may be able to reclaim the VAT element of the bad debt (subject to conditions — the debt must be at least six months old and must have been written off in your accounts). This is called bad debt relief.

## Bad debt provisions

A bad debt provision (also called a doubtful debt provision or allowance for doubtful accounts) is an estimate of the debts you think may not be collected, made before you are certain they are bad.

### Why provision?

Provisions match the expected loss to the period in which the sales were made, giving a more accurate picture of your profitability. Without provisions, your profit looks artificially high until the moment you write off the bad debt, when it drops suddenly.

### How to calculate a provision

There are two common methods:

**Specific provision:** Review each outstanding invoice individually and assess the likelihood of collection. Invoices from clients in financial difficulty, disputed invoices, and very old invoices might warrant a provision.

**General provision:** Apply a percentage to your total receivables based on historical experience. For example, if historically 2% of your receivables become bad debts, you might provision 2% of your current receivables.

Most businesses use a combination: specific provisions for known problem debts and a general provision for the remainder.

### Recording the provision

The accounting entry for creating or increasing a bad debt provision is:

- **Debit:** Bad Debt Expense
- **Credit:** Provision for Doubtful Debts (a contra-asset account that reduces your net receivables)

The provision appears on the balance sheet as a deduction from accounts receivable, showing the net amount you realistically expect to collect.

### Adjusting the provision

At each reporting date, review your provision and adjust it up or down:

- If more debts look doubtful, increase the provision
- If previously doubtful debts have been collected, decrease it
- If a provisioned debt is confirmed bad, write it off against the provision

## Bad debt recovery

Sometimes debts you have written off are subsequently paid — the client resurfaces, gets back on their feet, or a liquidator distributes partial funds.

When this happens, record the recovery:

- **Debit:** Cash/Bank (the money received)
- **Credit:** Bad Debt Recovered (income)

This correctly recognises the recovery as income in the period it occurs, rather than adjusting the period when the write-off was made.

## Preventing bad debt

Prevention is always better than cure:

### Credit checks

For new clients or large orders, run a credit check before extending credit. A small upfront investment in due diligence can prevent a large write-off later.

### Clear payment terms

Set and communicate your payment terms clearly from the start. Include them in your contracts, proposals, and invoices.

### Deposits and milestone payments

For large projects, require a deposit before starting work and milestone payments during the project. This limits your exposure to the final payment rather than the entire project value.

### Credit limits

Set a maximum amount of credit for each client. Do not allow their outstanding balance to exceed this limit without review.

### Prompt invoicing and chasing

Invoice immediately after completing work. Chase promptly when payment is overdue. The longer a debt ages, the less likely it is to be collected.

### Monitor your aged debtors report

Review your aged debtors report weekly or monthly. Any invoice moving into the older aging buckets needs attention before it becomes a potential write-off.

## The financial impact of bad debt

Bad debt directly reduces your profit. If your profit margin is 10% and you write off a 5,000 debt, you need 50,000 in additional revenue just to replace the lost profit. This makes bad debt prevention one of the most cost-effective things you can do.

Beyond the direct financial impact, significant bad debts can:

- Strain your cash flow
- Reduce the value of your accounts receivable (important for financing)
- Indicate problems with your client vetting or collection processes
- Affect your relationship with lenders (who monitor your receivables quality)

## Reporting and disclosure

In your financial statements, disclose:

- Total bad debts written off during the period
- The bad debt provision and how it was calculated
- Any significant individual bad debts
- Changes in the provision from the prior period

Your accountant can advise on the appropriate level of disclosure for your business.

Bad debt is a cost of doing business. Handling it properly — with timely recognition, correct accounting, and honest disclosure — keeps your financial statements accurate and your tax position correct. But the real goal is to minimise it through prevention, so your write-offs are small and infrequent rather than large and painful.
