Every UK employer is required to provide a workplace pension for eligible employees and automatically enrol them into it. This duty applies regardless of business size — whether you have one employee or a thousand. Getting auto-enrolment right is not optional, and The Pensions Regulator has the power to issue fines for non-compliance.

This guide explains what you need to do, step by step.

## What is auto-enrolment?

Auto-enrolment is the legal requirement for employers to automatically enrol eligible workers into a qualifying workplace pension scheme and make contributions to it. The employee does not need to opt in — they are enrolled by default, though they can choose to opt out.

The scheme was introduced to address the fact that millions of workers had no pension savings. By making enrolment automatic, the government shifted the default from "no pension" to "pension unless you actively choose not to."

## Who must be enrolled?

Not all workers need to be auto-enrolled. The rules depend on the worker's age and earnings:

### Eligible jobholders
These workers must be auto-enrolled. They are:
- Aged between 22 and State Pension age
- Earning above the earnings trigger (a threshold set annually by the government)
- Working in the UK

### Non-eligible jobholders
These workers are not auto-enrolled but have the right to opt in. They are:
- Aged between 16 and 74
- Earning above the Lower Earnings Limit but below the earnings trigger, OR
- Aged between 16 and 21 or between State Pension age and 74, earning above the earnings trigger

If a non-eligible jobholder opts in, you must make employer contributions to their pension.

### Entitled workers
These workers earn below the Lower Earnings Limit. They can ask to join the pension scheme, but you do not have to make employer contributions for them.

You must assess each employee's category on every pay period because their status can change as their earnings fluctuate.

## Choosing a pension scheme

Before you can enrol anyone, you need a qualifying pension scheme in place. A qualifying scheme must meet minimum contribution levels and be registered with The Pensions Regulator.

Options include:

- **NEST (National Employment Savings Trust)**: A government-backed scheme that must accept any employer. Often the simplest choice for small businesses.
- **Other multi-employer schemes**: Such as The People's Pension, NOW: Pensions, or Smart Pension. These are designed for auto-enrolment and offer straightforward administration.
- **Insurance company schemes**: Providers like Aviva, Scottish Widows, or Royal London offer workplace pension products with more investment options.
- **Self-invested personal pensions (SIPPs)**: Less common for auto-enrolment but possible for higher-earning workforces.

When choosing a scheme, consider:
- Setup and ongoing costs (some charge employers, others charge only members)
- Investment options and default funds
- Administration requirements and software compatibility
- Member communication and online access

Your scheme must be compatible with your payroll software so that contributions can be calculated and submitted efficiently.

## Minimum contribution levels

The minimum total contribution is split between employer and employee:

| Contributor | Minimum percentage |
|------------|-------------------|
| Employer | 3% of qualifying earnings |
| Employee | 5% of qualifying earnings |
| Total | 8% of qualifying earnings |

"Qualifying earnings" means earnings between the Lower Earnings Limit and the Upper Earnings Limit. Earnings below or above these thresholds are excluded from the contribution calculation.

Some employers use alternative contribution structures (such as contributions on total earnings from the first pound) which may satisfy the legal requirements differently. Check with your pension provider or adviser to confirm your arrangement qualifies.

## The auto-enrolment process

### Step 1: Assess your workforce
On each pay period, assess every worker to determine whether they are an eligible jobholder, non-eligible jobholder, or entitled worker.

### Step 2: Enrol eligible jobholders
You must enrol eligible jobholders within a specific window. There is a postponement option that allows you to delay enrolment by up to three months, which many employers use for new starters with a probation period.

### Step 3: Write to the employee
After enrolling an employee, you must write to them within six weeks. The letter must confirm:
- That they have been enrolled
- The name of the pension scheme
- When contributions will start
- How much they and you will contribute
- Their right to opt out

### Step 4: Start contributions
Deduct the employee's pension contribution from their pay and add your employer contribution. Submit both to the pension provider by the required deadline.

### Step 5: Handle opt-outs
Employees who do not want to be in the pension can opt out within one month of being enrolled. If they opt out within this window, any contributions already deducted must be refunded. You cannot encourage or incentivise employees to opt out — this is a criminal offence.

### Step 6: Re-enrolment
Every three years, you must re-enrol any eligible jobholders who previously opted out. This is called the "cyclical re-enrolment duty." You must also write to them again with the same information as the original enrolment letter.

## Processing pension contributions through payroll

Pension contributions are calculated as part of your payroll run:

1. Calculate the employee's qualifying earnings for the pay period
2. Apply the employee contribution rate and deduct from gross pay (for relief at source) or net pay (for net pay arrangements)
3. Calculate the employer contribution
4. Submit both contributions to the pension provider by the payment deadline

The tax treatment of pension contributions depends on the scheme type:

- **Relief at source**: You deduct the employee contribution after tax. The pension provider claims basic rate tax relief from HMRC and adds it to the employee's pot.
- **Net pay arrangement**: You deduct the employee contribution before calculating tax. The tax relief is automatic because the employee's taxable income is reduced.

Most auto-enrolment schemes for small employers use relief at source, which is simpler from a payroll perspective.

Your [payroll software](/payroll) should calculate contributions, generate the submission file for your pension provider, and track deadlines automatically.

## Common auto-enrolment mistakes

### Missing the assessment
You must assess workers on every pay period, not just when they join. An employee who was not eligible last month may become eligible this month if their earnings increase.

### Late enrolment
Enrolling employees late means you owe back-contributions. The Pensions Regulator can issue compliance notices and fixed penalty notices for persistent delays.

### Underpaying contributions
Calculating contributions on the wrong earnings figure, or at the wrong rate, is a common error. Always use qualifying earnings (between the thresholds) unless you have a certified alternative arrangement.

### Not communicating
The legal requirement to write to employees at specific points is often overlooked. Failure to communicate can result in penalties even if the actual enrolment and contributions are correct.

### Encouraging opt-outs
It is illegal for an employer to encourage or induce an employee to opt out of the pension. Even casual comments like "most people opt out" can constitute an offence. Present the information neutrally.

## The Pensions Regulator

The Pensions Regulator (TPR) oversees auto-enrolment compliance. TPR can:

- Issue compliance notices requiring you to take specific actions
- Issue fixed penalty notices (currently £400)
- Issue escalating daily penalties (from £50 to £10,000 per day depending on employer size)
- Prosecute employers who wilfully fail to comply

TPR is generally supportive of employers who are trying to comply but have made genuine mistakes. However, persistent non-compliance or deliberate avoidance will be dealt with firmly.

You must complete a Declaration of Compliance with TPR within five months of your duties start date, and every three years at re-enrolment.

## Key takeaways

Auto-enrolment is a permanent obligation for every UK employer. The rules are clear, the penalties for non-compliance are real, and the administrative work is ongoing. But with the right pension scheme and payroll setup, it can be managed efficiently:

- Choose a scheme that works with your payroll software
- Assess workers on every pay period
- Enrol eligible workers promptly
- Calculate and submit contributions accurately
- Communicate with employees at every required point
- Re-enrol opt-outs every three years

Getting it right from the start is far easier than trying to fix mistakes after the fact.
