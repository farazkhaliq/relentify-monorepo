Photographs are the strongest evidence in any property inventory. But a photograph without context — without a verifiable date, time, and connection to a specific property — is weaker than it could be. Time-stamped photos add that context, creating a record that is significantly harder to challenge.

When a deposit dispute goes to adjudication, one of the first things the adjudicator checks is whether the photographic evidence is dated. Can they confirm that the check-in photos were actually taken on the check-in date? Can they verify that the check-out photos correspond to the end of the tenancy? Timestamped photographs answer these questions definitively.

This guide covers what timestamping means in practice, the different methods available, and how to incorporate them into your inventory workflow.

## What is a timestamp and why does it matter?

A timestamp is a record of the date and time when a photograph was taken. In the context of property inventories, it serves as proof that the photo was captured at a specific moment — typically the check-in or check-out date.

Without a timestamp, the photograph's age is unverifiable. A landlord could theoretically submit photos taken weeks before the tenancy started, or a tenant could claim that damage shown in check-out photos occurred after they left. Timestamps remove this ambiguity.

Deposit protection adjudicators consistently give more weight to timestamped photographic evidence. It is not that untimestamped photos are rejected — they can still be useful — but timestamped photos carry inherently more credibility.

## Methods of timestamping

### EXIF metadata (built into your device)

Every digital photo taken on a smartphone or camera contains EXIF metadata — embedded data that includes the date, time, device model, and often the GPS coordinates where the photo was taken.

**Advantages:**
- Automatic — no extra steps required
- Includes GPS location data (proving the photo was taken at the property)
- Embedded in the file, not overlaid on the image

**Limitations:**
- EXIF data can be edited with readily available software
- Not visible when viewing the photo — you need to access the file properties
- Some platforms strip EXIF data when photos are uploaded or shared

**Best practice:** EXIF metadata is a good baseline, but because it can be edited, it should not be relied upon as the sole form of timestamping.

### Overlay timestamps (visible on the image)

Some camera apps and inventory software can overlay the date and time directly onto the photograph, usually in a corner. This makes the timestamp visible whenever the photo is viewed.

**Advantages:**
- Immediately visible — no need to inspect file properties
- Cannot be removed without visibly altering the image
- Easy to verify at a glance

**Limitations:**
- Takes up space on the image (may obscure detail if poorly placed)
- Can be faked with image editing software (though this is harder to do convincingly)
- May look unprofessional if not well-positioned

### Inventory software timestamps

Professional inventory platforms capture photos within the application and automatically embed timestamps, user identification, and sometimes GPS data into both the image metadata and the generated report.

**Advantages:**
- Integrated into the workflow — no extra steps
- Timestamps are part of a structured report, not just individual photos
- Audit trail is maintained by the platform
- Harder to manipulate because the data is stored server-side
- GPS verification confirms the photo was taken at the correct property

**Limitations:**
- Requires using specific software
- Depends on the platform's reliability and security

Platforms like [Relentify](/inventory) handle timestamping as part of the standard inspection workflow, embedding date, time, and location data automatically.

### Physical date cards

Some inventory clerks include a physical card showing the date in the first photograph of each inspection session. This is an old-school method but remains effective as a supplementary verification.

**Advantages:**
- Simple and low-tech
- Provides an independent physical reference
- Works alongside any other timestamping method

**Limitations:**
- Only proves the date for the photo containing the card — other photos could have been taken at different times
- Requires carrying and positioning the card for each inspection
- A card can be prepared with any date, so it is not foolproof on its own

## Best practice: use multiple methods

The strongest approach combines multiple timestamping methods:

1. **Capture photos within inventory software** that automatically records timestamps and GPS data
2. **Ensure your device's date and time are correct** so EXIF metadata is accurate
3. **Include a physical date card** in the first photo of each inspection as supplementary evidence
4. **Generate the report promptly** — a report dated on the inspection day, with embedded photos taken on the same day, is more credible than one compiled days later

## GPS data: the additional layer

Modern smartphones embed GPS coordinates in photo metadata by default (unless location services are disabled). For inventory purposes, this is valuable because it proves the photo was taken at the correct property.

If a landlord submits check-in photos with GPS data matching the property's address, it is strong evidence that the photos are genuine. If the GPS data shows a different location, or is absent entirely, it raises questions.

Make sure location services are enabled on your device when conducting inspections. If using inventory software, check that the app has permission to access location data.

## Workflow for timestamped inventory photos

### Before the inspection

- Confirm your device's date and time are correct
- Enable location services
- Ensure your inventory software is updated and functioning
- Prepare a physical date card if you use one

### During the inspection

- Take all photos within your inventory software where possible
- Start with a photo of the property exterior, establishing the location
- Include the date card in the first interior photo
- Follow your standard room-by-room approach, capturing all relevant areas
- Check photo quality on-screen after each capture — retake any that are blurry or dark

### After the inspection

- Generate the report as soon as possible, ideally the same day
- Verify that timestamps appear correctly in the report
- Store the report and original photos securely
- Back up the data to cloud storage

## Handling timestamp challenges

### Device date is wrong

If your device's date or time is incorrect, every photo will have an inaccurate timestamp. Always check before starting an inspection. Set your device to automatic date and time to avoid this issue.

### Photos taken over multiple days

If an inspection spans two days (unusual but possible for very large properties), note this in the report. The timestamps will show different dates, which is fine as long as the report explains the multi-day inspection.

### Tenant challenges the timestamp

If a tenant claims the photos were not taken on the reported date, the combination of EXIF metadata, inventory software timestamps, GPS data, and a physical date card provides multiple independent verification points. This is why using multiple methods is the strongest approach.

### Platform stores data

If you are using inventory software that stores data server-side, the platform's records serve as an independent verification. The timestamps are not just on your device — they are recorded by the platform at the time of capture.

## The impact on dispute outcomes

Timestamped photographic evidence consistently leads to faster, clearer dispute resolutions. Adjudicators spend less time questioning whether evidence is genuine and more time assessing the substantive issues.

For landlords, this means legitimate claims are more likely to succeed. For tenants, it means false claims are more easily identified and rejected. For everyone, it means the dispute process is fairer and more efficient.

The effort required to produce timestamped photos is negligible when using modern tools. The impact on the strength of your evidence is significant. Make timestamping a standard part of every inspection, and your inventory reports will carry the credibility they need when it matters most.
