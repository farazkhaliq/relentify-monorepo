Payroll taxes are one of the most complex and consequential responsibilities for US employers. Get them wrong, and you face penalties, interest, and potential legal liability. Get them right, and your employees are paid correctly, your tax obligations are met, and your business stays in good standing with both federal and state authorities.

This guide breaks down the payroll taxes that US employers need to understand, calculate, and remit — at both the federal and state levels.

## Federal payroll taxes: the basics

Every employer in the United States must withhold and remit several categories of federal payroll tax. These apply regardless of which state you operate in.

### Federal income tax withholding

Employers are required to withhold federal income tax from employee wages based on the information provided on the employee's W-4 form. The amount withheld depends on:

- The employee's filing status (single, married filing jointly, head of household)
- The number of allowances or adjustments claimed
- Any additional withholding the employee requests
- The employee's wage level

The IRS publishes withholding tables and provides the Percentage Method and Wage Bracket Method for calculating the correct amount. Most payroll software handles this calculation automatically based on the W-4 data you enter.

**Employer action:** Collect a W-4 from every new hire before their first paycheck. Update withholding when employees submit revised W-4 forms.

### FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare)

FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act) taxes fund Social Security and Medicare. Both the employer and the employee pay FICA, and the rates are split equally.

**Social Security tax:**
- Rate: 6.2% for the employee + 6.2% for the employer = 12.4% total
- Wage base limit: Applies only to wages up to the annual Social Security wage base (this limit is adjusted annually for inflation). Wages above this threshold are not subject to Social Security tax.

**Medicare tax:**
- Rate: 1.45% for the employee + 1.45% for the employer = 2.9% total
- No wage base limit — Medicare tax applies to all wages regardless of amount

**Additional Medicare tax:**
- Rate: 0.9% on employee wages exceeding $200,000 (single filers) or $250,000 (married filing jointly)
- This is an employee-only tax — the employer does not pay a matching amount
- The employer is responsible for withholding it once wages exceed $200,000 in a calendar year, regardless of the employee's filing status

**Employer action:** Calculate and withhold the employee portion of FICA from each paycheck. Pay the matching employer portion. Report and remit both portions to the IRS.

### FUTA (Federal Unemployment Tax Act)

FUTA funds federal unemployment programs and provides a framework for state unemployment insurance.

- Rate: 6.0% on the first $7,000 of each employee's wages per year
- Credit: Employers who pay state unemployment taxes on time receive a credit of up to 5.4%, reducing the effective FUTA rate to 0.6%
- This is an employer-only tax — it is not deducted from employee wages

**Employer action:** Calculate FUTA quarterly. If your FUTA liability exceeds $500 in a quarter, deposit it by the end of the following month. File Form 940 annually.

## State payroll taxes

In addition to federal obligations, most states impose their own payroll taxes. These vary significantly from state to state.

### State income tax withholding

Most states require employers to withhold state income tax from employee wages. The rates and methods vary by state:

- **No state income tax:** Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (limited), South Dakota, Tennessee (limited), Texas, Washington, Wyoming
- **Flat rate states:** Some states apply a single flat tax rate to all income
- **Progressive rate states:** Most states use a graduated rate structure similar to the federal system

Employers must register with each state where they have employees, obtain state withholding tables, and remit withheld taxes according to the state's schedule.

### State unemployment insurance (SUI)

Every state operates its own unemployment insurance program funded by employer-paid taxes. Key variables include:

- **Tax rate:** Varies by state and by employer. New employers typically pay a standard rate, which then adjusts based on their experience rating (essentially, how many former employees have claimed unemployment benefits).
- **Wage base:** Each state sets its own taxable wage base, ranging from under $10,000 to over $50,000 per employee per year.
- **Who pays:** Primarily an employer-only tax, though a few states require employee contributions as well.

### State disability insurance (SDI)

A handful of states require disability insurance contributions:

- California, Hawaii, New Jersey, New York, and Rhode Island currently mandate state disability insurance
- These programs provide partial wage replacement to employees who cannot work due to non-work-related illness or injury
- Contribution rates and methods vary by state

### Paid family and medical leave

An increasing number of states have enacted paid family and medical leave programs funded through payroll contributions:

- These programs provide wage replacement for employees taking leave for family or medical reasons
- Both employer and employee contributions may be required
- Each state sets its own rules for eligibility, benefit amounts, and contribution rates

### Local taxes

Some cities and municipalities impose additional payroll-related taxes, including:

- Local income taxes (common in parts of Pennsylvania, Ohio, and other states)
- Transit district taxes
- School district taxes

These add another layer of compliance that varies by location.

## Payroll tax filing deadlines

### Federal deadlines

- **Semi-weekly or monthly deposits:** Depending on your total tax liability, you must deposit withheld income tax and FICA taxes either semi-weekly or monthly
- **Form 941:** Filed quarterly, reporting wages, tips, and tax withholding
- **Form 940:** Filed annually for FUTA
- **W-2 forms:** Issued to employees and filed with the SSA by January 31 each year

### State deadlines

State filing frequencies and deadlines vary. Most states require quarterly reporting of wages and tax withholding, but the specific deadlines, forms, and electronic filing requirements differ.

## Common payroll tax mistakes

### Misclassifying workers

Treating employees as independent contractors to avoid payroll tax obligations is one of the most expensive mistakes an employer can make. The IRS and state agencies actively audit for worker misclassification, and the penalties include back taxes, interest, and substantial fines.

If you control when, where, and how a worker performs their duties, they are likely an employee, not a contractor.

### Missing deposit deadlines

Payroll tax deposits must be made on time. Late deposits trigger penalties that escalate based on how late the payment is — from two per cent for deposits one to five days late up to fifteen per cent for amounts still unpaid more than ten days after the first notice.

### Incorrect W-4 processing

Using outdated W-4 information or incorrectly applying the withholding tables results in under-withholding or over-withholding. Either creates problems — under-withholding leads to IRS penalties for the employer, while over-withholding frustrates employees.

### Not keeping up with rate changes

FICA wage bases, FUTA rates, state unemployment rates, and local tax rates change regularly. Using last year's rates creates cumulative errors throughout the year.

## Managing payroll tax compliance

### Use payroll software

Manual payroll tax calculations are error-prone and time-consuming. Payroll software automates calculations, generates pay stubs, handles tax filings, and keeps up with rate changes. For businesses operating across multiple states, this is essential rather than optional.

### Keep impeccable records

Maintain records of all payroll tax calculations, deposits, filings, and employee W-4 forms. The IRS can audit payroll records going back several years, and the burden of proof falls on the employer.

Good accounting software that integrates with payroll makes record-keeping automatic. Platforms like Relentify combine payroll, accounting, and reporting so that your payroll data flows directly into your financial records without manual reconciliation.

### Stay current on changes

Federal and state payroll tax laws change frequently. Subscribe to IRS updates, state tax authority newsletters, and payroll industry publications. Alternatively, work with a payroll provider or accountant who specializes in employment tax compliance.

### Register in every state where you have employees

If you have employees working in multiple states — including remote workers — you may have payroll tax obligations in each state. Each state requires separate employer registration, separate withholding, and separate reporting.
