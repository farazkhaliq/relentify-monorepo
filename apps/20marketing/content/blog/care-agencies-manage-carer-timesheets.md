Domiciliary care is one of the most operationally demanding sectors for timesheet management. Carers work alone, visiting multiple clients in their homes throughout the day. There is no central workplace, no single supervisor watching attendance, and no clock on the wall. Yet the need for accurate time records is acute — for payroll, for client billing, for regulatory compliance, and for the safety and welfare of the people receiving care.

This guide explains the specific timesheet challenges faced by care agencies and offers practical solutions that balance accuracy with the realities of mobile, client-facing work.

## Why care timesheets are different

Care work does not fit the standard model of time tracking. Several characteristics make it uniquely challenging:

### Multiple visits per day

A typical carer may visit four to eight clients per day, each visit lasting between fifteen minutes and two hours. Every visit needs to be recorded separately — the client, the start time, the end time, and often the tasks performed. A simple daily clock-in and clock-out is not sufficient.

### Lone working

Carers work alone in clients' homes. There is no colleague to verify their presence and no manager observing their attendance. The timesheet is the only record of what happened and when.

### Variable schedules

Care schedules change frequently. A new client is added, a hospital discharge creates an urgent visit, or another carer's absence requires cover. The schedule a carer starts the week with may look very different by Friday.

### Regulatory requirements

Care agencies are subject to inspection and regulation. Inspectors may ask to see records of visit times and durations as evidence that clients are receiving the care they are entitled to. Incomplete or inaccurate records can lead to compliance failures and, in serious cases, enforcement action.

### Client billing

Many care agencies are funded by local authorities or health bodies, which pay based on delivered visit hours. If the agency cannot demonstrate that visits occurred and lasted the required duration, payment may be withheld or clawed back.

## Building a care-specific timesheet system

### Visit-based recording

The fundamental unit of time tracking in domiciliary care is the visit, not the shift. Your system should allow carers to record:

- Client name or identifier
- Visit start time
- Visit end time
- Tasks completed during the visit (optional but valuable for compliance)
- Any notes or observations

This visit-based model produces a complete picture of the carer's day — not just total hours, but a detailed record of where they were and what they did.

### GPS-verified check-in

GPS verification is particularly valuable in domiciliary care. When a carer clocks into a visit, the system records their GPS coordinates and compares them against the client's address. This confirms that the carer was physically at the client's home at the recorded time.

This serves multiple purposes:

- **Payroll accuracy**: The agency can be confident that the recorded visit actually occurred
- **Client billing evidence**: Funders and commissioners accept GPS-verified records as evidence of visit delivery
- **Safeguarding**: If a client is not answering the door or if a visit is missed, the system can alert the agency immediately
- **Worker protection**: If a carer is accused of not attending a visit, GPS records provide objective evidence

Platforms like [Relentify](/timesheets) support GPS-verified check-ins for each visit, allowing care agencies to build a defensible record of every client interaction.

### Travel time tracking

Carers spend significant portions of their day travelling between visits. Whether travel time is paid depends on the agency's contract and local employment law, but it should always be tracked. The gap between one visit's check-out and the next visit's check-in represents travel time, and this data is useful for:

- Calculating total working hours (including travel) for working time compliance
- Identifying inefficient routes or unrealistic scheduling
- Supporting mileage claims where applicable

### Real-time monitoring

For the safety of both carers and clients, the agency office needs real-time visibility into visit status. A dashboard showing which carers are currently at visits, which visits have been completed, and which are overdue allows the coordinator to intervene quickly when something is wrong.

This is not just an operational convenience — it is a safeguarding requirement. If a vulnerable client's morning visit does not happen, the agency needs to know immediately, not when timesheets are submitted at the end of the week.

## Compliance and inspection readiness

Care regulators look for evidence that the agency is delivering care as planned and that accurate records are maintained. Key areas include:

### Visit duration

Are visits lasting the scheduled duration? A visit scheduled for thirty minutes that consistently shows as fifteen minutes may indicate that care is being rushed, which is a quality and safeguarding concern.

Timesheet data should show exact visit durations, and the agency should review this data regularly — not just for payroll purposes, but for care quality assurance.

### Missed visits

A missed visit in domiciliary care is a serious incident, particularly for clients who depend on visits for medication, personal care, or nutrition. Your system should flag any scheduled visit that was not checked into by the expected time, and alert the coordinator immediately.

### Continuity of care

Regulators often assess whether clients are receiving care from a consistent team. Timesheet data can demonstrate this by showing which carers visited which clients over time.

### Record retention

Time records for care visits should be retained for the period required by your regulator — often several years. Digital records stored in a secure system meet this requirement far more reliably than paper logs in a filing cabinet.

## Managing the workforce

### Scheduling around client needs

Care scheduling is driven by client need, not employer convenience. Visits must happen at specific times — medication rounds, meal preparation, bedtime routines. This means the schedule is relatively fixed, and the challenge is ensuring enough carers are available to cover all visits.

Timesheet data from previous weeks helps with scheduling by showing actual visit durations (which may differ from scheduled durations), travel times between clients, and patterns of absence or overtime.

### Mileage and expenses

Carers who use their own vehicles to travel between visits typically claim mileage. The timesheet system can simplify this by calculating the distance between consecutive visits and generating mileage reports automatically. This reduces admin for both the carer and the agency.

### Carer wellbeing

Carers are at high risk of burnout, particularly those working long hours with limited breaks and significant travel. Timesheet data can reveal warning signs: consistently long days, insufficient gaps between visits, or patterns of working through breaks. Monitoring this data and acting on it is both an ethical responsibility and a practical one — a burnt-out carer is more likely to leave, and recruitment in care is notoriously difficult.

## Practical implementation

### Start with the essentials

For a care agency implementing a timesheet system for the first time, focus on:

1. Visit-based clock-in and clock-out
2. GPS verification of visit location
3. A coordinator dashboard showing live visit status
4. Automated alerts for missed or late visits
5. Export functionality for payroll and billing

These five capabilities address the most critical needs: accurate records, safeguarding, and financial processes.

### Train carers thoroughly

Carers are busy, often technologically cautious, and working under time pressure. Training should be hands-on, brief, and focused on the minimal actions required: how to check in, how to check out, what to do if there is a problem.

Provide a simple reference card or one-page guide that carers can keep on their phone or in their bag.

### Engage with commissioners

If your agency is funded by local authorities or health commissioners, involve them in the process. Many commissioners welcome GPS-verified visit records as they provide assurance that funded care is being delivered. Some may even require them as a condition of the contract.

## The bigger picture

For domiciliary care agencies, timesheet management is not an administrative task — it is a core component of care delivery, safeguarding, and regulatory compliance. The data captured in every visit check-in and check-out feeds into pay, billing, quality assurance, and the safety net that protects vulnerable people.

Getting it right requires a system designed for the realities of mobile, visit-based care work — not a generic timesheet tool adapted to fit. The investment in the right system pays dividends across every dimension of the agency's operations.
