The Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) is one of the most important employment laws in the United States, yet it remains one of the most frequently misunderstood. It gives eligible employees the right to take unpaid, job-protected leave for specified family and medical reasons — and it places significant obligations on employers to administer leave correctly.

Getting FMLA compliance wrong can result in lawsuits, back pay, and damages. This guide explains the law in practical terms so you can manage leave requests confidently.

## Who is covered by the FMLA?

### Covered employers

The FMLA applies to:
- Private-sector employers with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius for at least 20 workweeks in the current or preceding calendar year
- All public agencies (federal, state, and local government employers) regardless of size
- Public and private elementary and secondary schools regardless of size

If your business has fewer than 50 employees within a 75-mile radius, the federal FMLA does not apply. However, many states have their own family and medical leave laws with lower thresholds.

### Eligible employees

To be eligible for FMLA leave, an employee must:
- Work for a covered employer
- Have worked for the employer for at least 12 months (these do not need to be consecutive)
- Have worked at least 1,250 hours during the 12 months before leave begins
- Work at a location where the employer has 50 or more employees within 75 miles

All four criteria must be met. If an employee does not qualify, you are not required to provide FMLA leave — though you may still choose to offer leave voluntarily.

## What does the FMLA provide?

### Leave entitlement

Eligible employees are entitled to up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave in a 12-month period for:

**Serious health condition.** The employee's own serious health condition that makes them unable to perform the essential functions of their job.

**Family member's serious health condition.** To care for the employee's spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition.

**Birth and bonding.** For the birth of a child and to bond with the newborn within one year of birth.

**Adoption or foster care.** For the placement of a child with the employee for adoption or foster care, and to bond with the newly placed child within one year of placement.

**Military qualifying exigency.** For qualifying exigencies arising from the employee's spouse, child, or parent being on active duty or called to active duty in the Armed Forces.

### Military caregiver leave

A separate provision allows eligible employees to take up to 26 weeks of leave in a single 12-month period to care for a covered servicemember with a serious injury or illness. This is an enhanced entitlement beyond the standard 12 weeks.

### Job protection

When the employee returns from FMLA leave, they must be restored to their same position or an equivalent position with equivalent pay, benefits, and other terms and conditions of employment.

### Benefits continuation

During FMLA leave, the employer must maintain the employee's group health insurance coverage under the same terms as if the employee had continued to work. The employee remains responsible for their share of the premium.

## Employer obligations

### Posting requirements

Covered employers must display an FMLA poster in a conspicuous place where employees and applicants can see it. This poster explains FMLA rights and is available from the Department of Labor.

### Eligibility notice

When an employee requests FMLA leave (or when the employer learns of a need for leave that may qualify), the employer must notify the employee of their eligibility within five business days. If the employee is not eligible, the notice must state at least one reason why.

### Rights and responsibilities notice

At the same time as the eligibility notice, the employer must provide a notice explaining the specific expectations and obligations of the employee, including:
- Whether a medical certification will be required
- Whether paid leave will be substituted for unpaid FMLA leave
- Requirements for premium payments during leave
- Requirements for fitness-for-duty certification before returning

### Designation notice

Once the employer has enough information to determine whether the leave qualifies as FMLA leave, they must provide a designation notice within five business days. This notice confirms whether the leave is designated as FMLA leave and specifies the amount of leave counted against the employee's entitlement.

### Record keeping

Employers must maintain records related to FMLA compliance for at least three years, including:
- Basic payroll and employee identification data
- Dates of FMLA leave taken
- Copies of employee FMLA notices
- Copies of all employer FMLA notices
- Documents describing employee benefits and policies
- Premium payment records during leave
- Records of any disputes regarding FMLA designation

## Medical certification

### Requesting certification

The employer may require the employee to provide a medical certification supporting the need for leave due to a serious health condition. The certification should be requested at the time of the rights and responsibilities notice, and the employee must be given at least 15 calendar days to provide it.

### What the certification should include

The certification form (WH-380-E for the employee's own condition, WH-380-F for a family member's condition) asks the healthcare provider to confirm:
- The medical facts related to the condition
- The expected duration of the condition
- Whether the employee is unable to perform essential job functions
- Whether the family member requires care

### Second and third opinions

If the employer has reason to doubt the validity of the initial certification, they may require a second opinion at the employer's expense. If the first and second opinions differ, a third opinion — from a provider mutually agreed upon — may be obtained, and this opinion is final.

## Intermittent and reduced schedule leave

FMLA leave does not have to be taken all at once. Employees may take leave intermittently (in separate blocks of time) or on a reduced schedule when medically necessary.

**Common examples:**
- Recurring medical appointments
- Chronic conditions with periodic flare-ups
- Reduced hours during recovery from surgery

Intermittent leave creates administrative challenges for employers. Track leave carefully using your payroll or HR system to ensure accurate accounting of FMLA hours used. ## Using the 12-month period

Employers can choose from four methods for measuring the 12-month FMLA leave period:

1. **Calendar year** — January 1 to December 31
2. **Fixed 12-month period** — Any fixed period (fiscal year, anniversary date, etc.)
3. **12 months from first FMLA leave** — Measured forward from the first day of leave
4. **Rolling 12-month period** — Measured backward from the date leave is used

The rolling method is generally most favorable to employers because it prevents employees from stacking leave at the end of one year and beginning of the next. Whichever method you choose, apply it consistently to all employees.

## Common FMLA mistakes

**Failing to recognize FMLA-qualifying requests.** An employee does not need to mention "FMLA" by name. Any request for leave that could be related to a qualifying reason triggers the employer's obligation to inquire further and provide notice.

**Retaliating against employees who take leave.** Disciplining, demoting, or terminating an employee because they exercised FMLA rights is illegal and frequently litigated.

**Counting FMLA leave against attendance policies.** Absences protected by FMLA cannot be counted as unexcused absences or used as a basis for disciplinary action under attendance policies.

**Not tracking intermittent leave accurately.** Without careful tracking, intermittent leave can be difficult to manage and employees may take more leave than their entitlement. Use time-tracking systems to record FMLA hours precisely.

**Failing to maintain health benefits.** The employer's obligation to maintain health coverage continues throughout the leave period, even if the employee is not actively working.

## State family leave laws

Many states have enacted their own family and medical leave laws that provide broader protections than the federal FMLA:

- Lower employer size thresholds (some cover employers with as few as one employee)
- Longer leave durations
- Broader definitions of family member
- Paid leave benefits
- Coverage for additional reasons (domestic violence, bereavement, etc.)

If your state has its own family leave law, you must comply with both the state law and the federal FMLA where applicable. When both apply, the employee receives the benefit of whichever law is more generous.

## Building a compliant FMLA process

1. Determine whether you are a covered employer
2. Train managers to recognize potential FMLA-qualifying leave requests
3. Establish a consistent process for providing required notices
4. Use standardized forms for medical certification
5. Track leave accurately using reliable timekeeping and HR systems
6. Maintain comprehensive records for at least three years
7. Review your policies annually to reflect any changes in federal or state law

FMLA compliance requires attention to detail and consistent processes, but the fundamentals are manageable. The key is to treat every leave request seriously, follow the notice and certification procedures, and maintain accurate records from start to finish.
