When an employee leaves your business — whether they resign, are dismissed, are made redundant, or reach the end of a fixed-term contract — there is a specific payroll process to follow. Getting the final pay wrong is one of the most common sources of employee complaints and, in some cases, legal disputes.

This guide walks you through the process step by step.

## Before the final pay run

As soon as you know an employee is leaving, start gathering the information you need for their final pay:

- **Leaving date**: Confirm the exact date. This determines how much of the final pay period they are paid for.
- **Outstanding holiday**: Calculate how much holiday the employee has accrued but not taken, and how much they have taken but not yet accrued.
- **Notice period**: Are they working their notice or being paid in lieu of notice (PILON)?
- **Outstanding expenses**: Any approved expense claims that have not yet been reimbursed.
- **Deductions**: Any amounts you are entitled to deduct from final pay, such as overpaid holiday, outstanding loans, or contractual deductions.
- **Company property**: While not a payroll issue, confirm that company equipment, keys, cards, and documents will be returned.

Gather this information early so you are not scrambling on the employee's last day.

## Step 1: Calculate final salary

The employee's final salary is based on how much of the final pay period they worked. If they leave partway through a monthly pay period, calculate their pay pro rata:

**Pro-rata pay = (Annual salary ÷ 365) × Number of calendar days worked in the period**

Or, more commonly:

**Pro-rata pay = (Monthly salary ÷ Working days in the month) × Working days worked**

For hourly workers, simply calculate hours worked up to and including their last day, multiplied by their hourly rate. Include any overtime, shift premiums, or other variable pay that applies.

## Step 2: Calculate holiday pay

This is where most final pay disputes arise. You need to determine whether the employee owes you for holiday taken in advance, or whether you owe them for holiday accrued but not taken.

### Calculating accrued holiday

**Holiday accrued to date = Annual entitlement × (Months worked in holiday year ÷ 12)**

Compare this to the holiday actually taken:

- **If accrued > taken**: The employee has untaken holiday. You must pay them for the difference.
- **If taken > accrued**: The employee has taken more holiday than they have earned. You may be able to deduct the excess from their final pay, but only if their contract allows this deduction.

### Paying for untaken holiday

Payment for untaken holiday is calculated at the employee's normal daily rate. For employees with variable hours or pay, use the average over the appropriate reference period.

Include this payment as a separate line item on the final payslip so it is clearly identifiable.

### Deducting overpaid holiday

If the employee has taken more holiday than they have accrued and their contract permits deduction, calculate the excess days and deduct the value from final pay. Be transparent about this — show it clearly on the payslip and explain it to the employee.

If the contract does not include a clause permitting holiday deductions, you may not be able to recover the overpayment. This is why it is important to include such a clause in all employment contracts.

## Step 3: Process notice pay

How you handle the notice period affects the final pay:

### Working their notice
The employee works normally during their notice period and is paid as usual for those days. Their final pay covers up to their last working day.

### Garden leave
The employee does not attend work during the notice period but remains employed and receives their normal pay and benefits. Process payroll as if they were working.

### Payment in lieu of notice (PILON)
The employee leaves immediately and receives a lump sum equivalent to the pay they would have received during the notice period.

PILON may be taxable depending on whether the contract includes a PILON clause. If the contract includes a specific PILON clause, the payment is treated as earnings and is subject to tax and social contributions. If there is no PILON clause and the employer chooses to make a payment in lieu, the treatment may be different — consult your payroll adviser or accountant.

## Step 4: Process any final bonuses or commission

If the employee is entitled to a bonus or commission payment that relates to work performed before their departure, this should be included in their final pay.

Check the employee's contract and any bonus or commission scheme rules to determine:
- Whether the employee is entitled to a payment if they leave before the payment date
- Whether the bonus is pro-rated based on time worked
- Whether there is a "good leaver" or "bad leaver" clause that affects entitlement

These payments are subject to normal tax and social contribution deductions.

## Step 5: Make any permitted deductions

You may need to deduct amounts from the final pay, such as:

- **Overpaid holiday**: As discussed above, if permitted by the contract.
- **Outstanding loans**: If the employee has a loan from the company, the outstanding balance can be deducted if the loan agreement permits it.
- **Training costs**: Some contracts include clauses requiring repayment of training costs if the employee leaves within a specified period.
- **Company property not returned**: Deductions for unreturned property are only permitted if the contract explicitly allows them.

Any deduction must be authorised — either by the employee's written agreement or by a specific contractual clause. Unauthorised deductions from pay can lead to legal claims.

## Step 6: Run the final payroll

Process the final pay through your normal payroll:

- Include the pro-rata salary, holiday pay, and any other payments
- Apply all deductions (tax, social contributions, pension, and any permitted deductions from final pay)
- Generate the final payslip

The payslip should clearly show each component of the final pay so the employee can see exactly what they are being paid and why.

## Step 7: Issue leaving documentation

Depending on your jurisdiction, you will need to provide specific documents:

- **Final payslip**: Showing all payments and deductions.
- **Tax form**: In the UK, this is the P45. In other jurisdictions, there will be an equivalent document showing year-to-date pay and tax information.
- **Reference letter**: Not legally required in all jurisdictions, but commonly expected.
- **Pension information**: Details of their pension scheme and what happens to their contributions.
- **Benefits information**: If they had company benefits (health insurance, etc.), details of when coverage ends and any continuation options.

Issue these documents promptly — ideally on or before the employee's last day.

## Step 8: Update your systems

After the final pay run:

- Mark the employee as a leaver in your payroll system
- Remove them from future payroll runs
- Update your pension provider (stop contributions)
- Cancel any benefits or subscriptions tied to their employment
- Archive their payroll records (keep for the legally required period)
- Report the leaver to your tax authority through your regular filing

Your [payroll software](/payroll) should handle most of this automatically when you process the leaver, but verify that all systems are updated.

## Redundancy payments

If the employee is being made redundant, there may be additional payments:

- **Statutory redundancy pay**: Calculated based on the employee's age, length of service, and weekly pay, subject to a statutory cap.
- **Enhanced redundancy pay**: Any contractual or discretionary redundancy payment above the statutory minimum.

Statutory redundancy pay is tax-free. Enhanced redundancy pay is also tax-free up to a threshold (in the UK, the first £30,000). Amounts above this are taxable.

Redundancy payments should be clearly separated from other final pay components on the payslip and in your accounts.

## Common mistakes when processing leavers

- **Forgetting to pay untaken holiday**: This is the most common error and the most likely to result in a complaint or claim.
- **Deducting overpaid holiday without contractual authority**: Check the contract before making any deduction.
- **Not issuing tax documentation on time**: Late documentation causes problems for the employee's next employer.
- **Incorrect notice pay tax treatment**: PILON tax treatment depends on the contract. Get advice if you are unsure.
- **Continuing payroll after the leaving date**: Ensure the employee is removed from future pay runs to avoid overpayment.
