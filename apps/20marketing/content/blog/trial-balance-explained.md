A trial balance is one of the most fundamental reports in accounting, yet it is one that many business owners have never looked at. Accountants use it as a starting point for preparing financial statements, and it serves as a basic check on the accuracy of your bookkeeping.

If you understand what a trial balance is and how to read it, you gain a much better understanding of how your financial reports are constructed — and how to spot errors before they become problems.

## What is a trial balance?

A trial balance is a list of all accounts in your general ledger, with their debit or credit balances, at a specific point in time. The fundamental test is simple: the total of all debit balances should equal the total of all credit balances.

If they are equal, the trial balance "balances." If they are not, there is an error somewhere in the bookkeeping.

### Why debits must equal credits

Double-entry bookkeeping requires that every transaction has at least one debit and one credit of equal value. If this rule has been followed correctly for every transaction, the totals must balance. An imbalance means at least one transaction has been entered incorrectly.

## The structure of a trial balance

A trial balance lists every account with a non-zero balance:

| Accounts payable | | 8,000 |

The totals balance at 152,000, which means (at a basic level) the bookkeeping is consistent.

### Understanding the debit and credit sides

The rule is:

- **Assets** normally have debit balances (they increase with debits)
- **Expenses** normally have debit balances
- **Liabilities** normally have credit balances (they increase with credits)
- **Equity** normally has credit balances
- **Revenue** normally has credit balances

If an account appears on the "wrong" side — an asset with a credit balance or a liability with a debit balance — it warrants investigation. It might be correct (a bank overdraft would make the bank account show a credit balance), but it might indicate an error.

## Types of trial balance

### Unadjusted trial balance

This is the trial balance before any year-end adjustments (depreciation, accruals, prepayments, provisions). It represents the raw data from your bookkeeping during the period.

### Adjusted trial balance

After year-end adjustments have been made, the adjusted trial balance reflects the true position. This is the version used to prepare the financial statements.

### Post-closing trial balance

After the books are closed for the year (revenue and expense accounts are reset to zero, with net profit transferred to retained earnings), the post-closing trial balance shows only balance sheet accounts. All income statement accounts have zero balances.

## What the trial balance can tell you

### Total assets and liabilities

While not formatted as nicely as a balance sheet, the trial balance contains all the information needed to construct one. You can quickly total the asset accounts and liability accounts to get a high-level view.

### Revenue and expense totals

Similarly, totalling the revenue and expense accounts gives you the ingredients for a P&L. The difference between revenue (credits) and expenses (debits) is your profit or loss.

### Account completeness

A trial balance shows every account with a balance. If an account you expect to see is missing (no marketing expense when you know you spent money on marketing), something has not been recorded.

### Unusual balances

Look for accounts with unexpected balances:
- An asset account with a credit balance (negative asset — might be an error)
- A revenue account with a debit balance (negative revenue — returns? error?)
- An expense account with a very large balance relative to prior periods

## What the trial balance cannot tell you

A balanced trial balance does not mean your books are error-free. It only confirms that debits equal credits. Several types of errors will not cause the trial balance to go out of balance:

### Errors of omission

A transaction that was not recorded at all will not cause an imbalance — both the debit and credit are missing, so the totals are still equal.

### Errors of commission

Posting a transaction to the wrong account within the same category (e.g., recording an expense in "Office Supplies" instead of "Marketing") keeps the totals balanced but misclassifies the expense.

### Errors of principle

Recording a capital purchase as an expense (or vice versa) does not affect the trial balance total but fundamentally misrepresents your financial position.

### Compensating errors

Two errors that cancel each other out — an overstatement in one account matched by an equal overstatement in another — keep the trial balance in balance while both accounts are wrong.

### Errors of original entry

Recording the wrong amount (e.g., 540 instead of 450) on both the debit and credit sides keeps the trial balance balanced but misrepresents the transaction value.

This is why the trial balance is a necessary but not sufficient test of accuracy. It catches single-sided errors and transposition mistakes, but a full review of individual transactions is needed to catch the rest.

## When the trial balance does not balance

If your totals do not match, finding the error can be straightforward or time-consuming depending on the nature of the mistake.

### Step 1: Check the arithmetic

Verify that the columns have been totalled correctly. In software, this is automatic, but in manual systems, addition errors happen.

### Step 2: Check for transposition errors

If the difference is divisible by 9, a transposition error is likely (e.g., 54 entered as 45 — the difference of 9 is divisible by 9). Check recent entries for swapped digits.

### Step 3: Check for single-entry transactions

Look for transactions where only the debit or credit was recorded, not both. This is rare in accounting software (which enforces double entry) but can happen with manual journal entries.

### Step 4: Halve the difference

If the difference is an even number, divide it by 2. Look for a transaction of that amount that might have been posted to the wrong side (debit instead of credit or vice versa).

### Step 5: Check the exact amount

The difference itself might be a transaction that was missed entirely from one side or a balance that was carried forward incorrectly.

### Step 6: Review recent entries

Start with the most recent entries and work backwards. Errors are more likely to be recent.

## Trial balances in practice

Most modern accounting software generates trial balances automatically and always balances (because it enforces double-entry on every transaction). The trial balance becomes less about finding mathematical errors and more about reviewing account balances for reasonableness.

## Using the trial balance for your accountant

When your accountant prepares your year-end accounts, the trial balance is typically their starting point. They will:

1. Review the unadjusted trial balance
2. Investigate any unusual balances
3. Make year-end adjustments (depreciation, accruals, etc.)
4. Produce the adjusted trial balance
5. Prepare the financial statements from the adjusted trial balance

Providing a clean, balanced trial balance with clearly categorised accounts saves your accountant time — and therefore saves you money.

The trial balance is not glamorous, but it is the bridge between your day-to-day bookkeeping and your formal financial statements. Understanding what it shows (and what it does not) helps you maintain better records and have more productive conversations with your accountant.
