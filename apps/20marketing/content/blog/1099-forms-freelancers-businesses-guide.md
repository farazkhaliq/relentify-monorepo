If you work as a freelancer, independent contractor, or run a business that pays non-employees, 1099 forms are part of your tax life. These forms report various types of income to the IRS and to the recipients of that income. Getting them right — whether you are issuing them or receiving them — is a legal requirement that carries real penalties for non-compliance.

This guide explains the most common 1099 forms, who needs to issue them, who receives them, and how to stay compliant.

## What is a 1099 form?

A 1099 form is an information return that reports income paid to someone other than an employee. While a W-2 reports wages paid to employees, a 1099 reports payments to independent contractors, freelancers, and other non-employee payees.

The IRS uses 1099 forms to match income reported by payers with income reported by recipients on their tax returns. If you receive 50,000 in 1099 income but only report 40,000 on your return, the IRS will notice the discrepancy.

## Common types of 1099 forms

### 1099-NEC (Nonemployee Compensation)

This is the most relevant form for freelancers and businesses that hire independent contractors. It reports payments of $600 or more made to non-employees for services performed during the tax year.

**Who issues it:** Businesses that pay independent contractors, freelancers, or other non-employees $600 or more during the year.

**Who receives it:** The freelancer or independent contractor who was paid.

**Common payments reported:**
- Freelance or consulting fees
- Professional service fees (lawyers, accountants, if not a corporation)
- Commissions to non-employees
- Payments to subcontractors

### 1099-MISC (Miscellaneous Information)

Previously used for nonemployee compensation (now handled by 1099-NEC), the 1099-MISC still reports certain other payments:

- Rent payments of $600 or more
- Prizes and awards
- Royalties of $10 or more
- Attorney payments (regardless of attorney's business structure)
- Crop insurance proceeds

### 1099-K (Payment Card and Third-Party Network Transactions)

Reports payments processed through payment card transactions or third-party payment networks (like PayPal, Stripe, or Venmo for business). The current threshold requires reporting of transactions exceeding $600 per year through these networks.

**Important:** If you receive a 1099-K, your gross payment volume is reported — before fees, refunds, and adjustments. You must reconcile this against your actual income.

### 1099-INT (Interest Income)

Reports interest income of $10 or more from banks and financial institutions.

### 1099-DIV (Dividends and Distributions)

Reports dividend income from investments.

## If you are a business issuing 1099s

### Who do you need to send 1099-NEC to?

You must issue a 1099-NEC to any person (including sole proprietors, partnerships, and LLCs taxed as sole proprietors or partnerships) to whom you paid $600 or more for services during the tax year.

**Exceptions — you generally do NOT need to issue 1099s for:**
- Payments to corporations (C-corps and S-corps) — with the exception of attorney fees
- Payments to employees (these go on W-2s)
- Payments for merchandise, inventory, or goods (not services)
- Payments made via credit card or payment network (these are reported on 1099-K by the processor)

### Collecting W-9 forms

Before paying any independent contractor, request a completed W-9 form. This provides:

- Their legal name and business name
- Their tax identification number (SSN or EIN)
- Their entity type (sole proprietor, LLC, corporation, etc.)

The entity type tells you whether a 1099 is required. Collect the W-9 before making the first payment — chasing down tax information from contractors after year-end is frustrating and often unsuccessful.

### Filing deadlines

- **January 31:** Furnish copies to recipients (the contractor or freelancer)
- **January 31:** File with the IRS (if filing electronically, which is required for 10 or more forms)

### Penalties for non-compliance

Penalties for failing to file 1099 forms or filing late include:

- $60 per form if filed within 30 days of the deadline
- $130 per form if filed more than 30 days late but by August 1
- $330 per form if filed after August 1 or not filed at all
- Increased penalties for intentional disregard of filing requirements

These penalties apply per form, so a business with 20 unreported contractors could face thousands in penalties.

## If you are a freelancer receiving 1099s

### Reporting income

All income reported on 1099 forms must be included on your tax return. But remember: you must report all income, whether or not you receive a 1099. If a client pays you $500, it falls below the $600 reporting threshold — no 1099 is required — but you must still report the $500 as income.

### Self-employment tax

Income reported on 1099-NEC is subject to self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare) in addition to income tax. The current self-employment tax rate is 15.3 percent on net self-employment income (12.4 percent for Social Security up to the annual wage base, plus 2.9 percent for Medicare).

This is often the biggest surprise for new freelancers — the self-employment tax can add significantly to their total tax burden.

### Deducting business expenses

As a freelancer, you can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses against your 1099 income, reducing your taxable profit. Common deductions include:

- Home office expenses
- Business equipment and software
- Professional development and training
- Business travel
- Health insurance premiums (under certain conditions)
- Professional fees (accounting, legal)
- Marketing and advertising

Track these expenses carefully in your [accounting software](/accounting) throughout the year.

### Estimated tax payments

Freelancers are generally required to make quarterly estimated tax payments covering both income tax and self-employment tax. These are due:

- April 15 (for January-March income)
- June 15 (for April-May income)
- September 15 (for June-August income)
- January 15 (for September-December income)

Failure to make estimated payments can result in underpayment penalties.

### What to do if a 1099 is incorrect

If you receive a 1099 with an incorrect amount, contact the issuer and request a corrected form (1099-C). If the issuer will not correct it, report the income as shown on the 1099 and explain the discrepancy on your tax return, or consult a tax professional.

## Record-keeping best practices

### For businesses issuing 1099s

- Collect W-9 forms from all contractors before first payment
- Track all payments to non-employees throughout the year by recipient
- Keep records of amounts paid, dates, and services provided
- Generate 1099 forms by January 31
- Retain copies for at least four years

### For freelancers receiving 1099s

- Keep records of all income received, regardless of 1099 status
- Track all business expenses with receipts
- Reconcile 1099s received against your own records
- File estimated quarterly tax payments
- Retain records for at least three years (longer if claiming depreciation)

## Do not leave it until January

The biggest 1099 headache is trying to do everything in January — collecting W-9s, totalling payments, and generating forms all under deadline pressure. Instead, collect W-9s before first payment, track payments throughout the year, and review your 1099 obligations in December. January then becomes a simple matter of generating and sending forms from your existing records.
