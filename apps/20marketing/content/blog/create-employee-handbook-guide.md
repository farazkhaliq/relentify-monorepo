An employee handbook is not just something big companies have. It is a practical tool that saves you time, reduces misunderstandings, and protects your business. Every time an employee asks "what is the policy on...?" and you do not have a clear answer, that is a situation a handbook would have prevented.

The good news is that you do not need an HR department to create one. This guide shows you how to build a useful handbook for a small business.

## What is an employee handbook?

An employee handbook is a document that brings together your company policies, procedures, and expectations in one place. It is not a contract — it supplements the employment contract by providing detail on how things work day to day.

Think of it as the answer to every question a new employee might ask in their first few months, and every question an existing employee might ask when something unusual happens.

## Why every small business needs one

### Consistency
Without a handbook, policies exist in people's heads. This means different managers give different answers to the same question. A handbook ensures everyone operates from the same information.

### Protection
When a dispute arises — and eventually one will — your handbook provides evidence that the employee was informed of the relevant policy. This is particularly important for disciplinary and grievance situations.

### Time saving
Every time you answer the same question — "how many days' notice do I need to book holiday?" — you are spending time that a handbook would save. Point people to the handbook, and you get that time back.

### Onboarding
A handbook gives new employees a single document to read that covers everything they need to know. It accelerates their integration and reduces the burden on their manager.

## What to include

Your handbook does not need to cover every conceivable scenario. Focus on the policies and information that employees actually need. Here is a practical structure:

### 1. Welcome and company overview
A brief introduction to your company — who you are, what you do, and your values. Keep it genuine and concise. This sets the tone for the rest of the document.

### 2. Employment basics
- Working hours and attendance expectations
- Pay dates and payslip information
- Probation period terms
- How to notify absence

### 3. Leave and time off
- Holiday entitlement and booking process
- Public holidays
- Sick leave and notification requirements
- Maternity, paternity, and parental leave
- Other leave types (compassionate, jury service, etc.)

### 4. Conduct and behaviour
- Expected standards of conduct
- Dress code (if applicable)
- Use of company equipment and technology
- Social media policy
- Substance abuse policy

### 5. Health and safety
- Your health and safety responsibilities
- Fire safety and emergency procedures
- Accident reporting
- Display screen equipment (if relevant)

### 6. Disciplinary and grievance procedures
- The steps in your disciplinary process
- What constitutes misconduct and gross misconduct
- How to raise a grievance
- The investigation and appeal process

These sections are particularly important because they demonstrate that you have a fair process. In many jurisdictions, failing to follow a proper procedure can make a dismissal automatically unfair.

### 7. Equal opportunities and anti-discrimination
- Your commitment to equal treatment
- Protected characteristics
- How to report discrimination or harassment
- What happens when a report is made

### 8. Data protection
- How you handle employee personal data
- Employee rights regarding their data
- Who to contact with questions

### 9. IT and security
- Acceptable use of company devices and email
- Password and security expectations
- Remote working security requirements

### 10. Expenses and company property
- How to submit expenses
- What is claimable
- Return of company property on leaving

### 11. Leaving the company
- Resignation process
- Notice periods
- Exit interview (if applicable)
- Return of property and final pay

## How to write it

### Use plain language
Write for clarity, not formality. Avoid legal jargon unless it is genuinely necessary (and if it is, explain what it means). The handbook is for your employees, not a courtroom.

### Be specific
"Employees should dress appropriately" is useless. "Smart casual is expected in the office. On client-facing days, business attire is required. On Fridays, casual dress is acceptable" gives people something they can actually follow.

### Keep it practical
For each policy, ask: "What does an employee actually need to do?" The answer to that question is what belongs in the handbook. Skip the corporate philosophy and focus on the practical steps.

### Use a consistent format
Each section should follow a similar structure: what the policy is, who it applies to, what the employee should do, and who to contact with questions. Consistency makes the document easier to navigate.

### Include examples where helpful
For complex policies like expenses or disciplinary procedures, a brief example or scenario can make the policy much clearer than abstract description alone.

## Common mistakes

### Making it too long
A 100-page handbook is a handbook nobody reads. Aim for 20-30 pages for a small business. If you need more detail on a specific topic, keep the handbook entry brief and link to a separate detailed policy document.

### Copying someone else's handbook
Every business is different. A handbook written for a 500-person company in a different industry will not suit a 10-person business. Use templates as inspiration, but write your own content that reflects your actual policies.

### Setting policies you do not follow
If your handbook says "no personal phone use during work hours" but everyone, including management, uses their phones freely, the policy is meaningless. Worse, it creates inconsistency that can undermine disciplinary action. Only include policies you intend to enforce.

### Not updating it
A handbook that was accurate two years ago but does not reflect current law or current practice is a liability. Review it at least annually and update it whenever a policy changes.

### Not distributing it
A handbook sitting in a filing cabinet is useless. Make sure every employee has access — either a physical copy, a digital document, or access through your HR system. Require new employees to acknowledge that they have received and read it.

## Keeping it current

Set a recurring reminder — annually at minimum — to review the handbook. Check for:

- Changes in employment law that affect your policies
- Policies that no longer reflect how you actually operate
- New policies that have been implemented since the last review
- Feedback from employees or managers about unclear sections

When you update the handbook, communicate the changes to all employees and ensure everyone has access to the latest version. Keep a version history so you can track what changed and when.

## Digital versus physical

Most small businesses now maintain their handbook digitally. This is easier to update, distribute, and search. Options include:

- A PDF stored on a shared drive or intranet
- A section within your HR or [payroll](/payroll) platform
- A dedicated page on your company website (internal access only)

Whatever format you choose, make sure employees can access it easily and that the version they see is always the most current one.

## Getting started

You do not need to write the entire handbook in one sitting. Start with the sections that address your most common questions and your most important legal obligations:

1. Working hours and attendance
2. Holiday and leave
3. Sickness absence
4. Disciplinary and grievance procedures
5. Equal opportunities

These five sections cover the majority of day-to-day situations. Add more sections as needed, and build the handbook over time rather than trying to make it perfect from the start.

The best handbook is the one that exists and that people actually read. Start simple, be practical, and improve it as your business grows.
