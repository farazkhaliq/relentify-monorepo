A purchase order is one of those documents that many small businesses skip because it feels like unnecessary paperwork. Why bother with a formal order when you can just email the supplier and pay the invoice when it arrives?

The answer is that purchase orders prevent problems. They prevent disputes about what was ordered, misunderstandings about pricing, budget overruns, and accounting errors. As your business grows and you have multiple people making purchases, they become essential for maintaining control.

## What is a purchase order?

A purchase order (PO) is a formal document sent from a buyer to a supplier that specifies exactly what goods or services are being ordered, at what price, in what quantity, and with what delivery expectations.

It is a commitment to purchase. When the supplier accepts your PO, it becomes a binding agreement — you have agreed to buy, and they have agreed to sell, at the specified terms.

## The purchase order workflow

The typical PO process follows these steps:

### 1. Identify the need

Someone in the business identifies a need for goods or services. This might be restocking inventory, buying new equipment, or engaging a subcontractor.

### 2. Create the purchase order

The PO is created with all the relevant details: supplier, items, quantities, prices, delivery date, and any special terms.

### 3. Approve the purchase order

Depending on the business size and the PO value, approval may be required from a manager, finance team, or business owner. Approval ensures the purchase is authorised and within budget.

### 4. Send to the supplier

The approved PO is sent to the supplier, who reviews it and confirms they can fulfil the order at the stated terms.

### 5. Receive goods or services

When the goods arrive or the service is delivered, you check them against the PO. Does the quantity match? Is the quality acceptable? Were the delivery terms met?

### 6. Match the invoice

When the supplier's invoice arrives, it is matched against the PO and the delivery receipt. This three-way match confirms that what was ordered, what was received, and what was invoiced all agree.

### 7. Process payment

Once the invoice is matched and approved, payment is processed according to the agreed terms.

## Why purchase orders matter

### Budget control

POs let you track committed expenditure before the money is actually spent. If you have approved 20,000 in POs this month but only received 12,000 in invoices, you know there is still 8,000 of committed spend to come. Without POs, you only see the spending when invoices arrive — by which time it is too late to adjust.

### Dispute prevention

A PO is your record of what was agreed. If the supplier delivers the wrong quantity, charges a different price, or sends the wrong items, the PO is your evidence of what was ordered. Without a PO, it is your word against theirs.

### Approval controls

POs create a formal approval step before spending happens. This is essential for businesses with multiple employees who can make purchases. Without approval controls, spending can quickly get out of hand.

### Accounts payable accuracy

Matching invoices to POs catches errors. If a supplier invoices you for 50 units but the PO was for 40, the mismatch is flagged immediately. Without POs, incorrect invoices might be paid without question.

### Audit trail

POs create a clear paper trail from the decision to purchase through to payment. This is valuable for internal audits, external audits, and simply understanding how money was spent.

### Forecasting

Approved POs give you visibility into future cash outflows, improving the accuracy of your cash flow forecasts.

## What goes on a purchase order?

A purchase order should include:

- **PO number** — Unique, sequential identifier
- **Date**
- **Your business details** — Name, address, contact information
- **Supplier details** — Name, address, contact information
- **Delivery address** — If different from your business address
- **Line items** — Description, quantity, unit price, and total for each item
- **Subtotal, tax, and total**
- **Delivery terms** — Expected delivery date, shipping method
- **Payment terms** — When and how you will pay
- **Special instructions** — Any specific requirements
- **Authorised signature** — Who approved the purchase

## POs for small businesses

### "Is this not overkill for a small business?"

It might seem that way when it is just you making purchases. But consider these scenarios:

- You order supplies from a supplier, and the invoice is 15% higher than expected. Without a PO, you have no leverage to dispute it.
- You have two staff members both ordering from the same supplier, resulting in duplicate orders.
- You are reviewing your accounts at year end and cannot work out why a particular expense is so high. POs would have shown you exactly what was ordered and when.

Even simple PO processes add clarity and control. You do not need a complex procurement system — just a consistent process.

### Starting simple

For a small business, your PO process might be as simple as:

1. Create a PO in your accounting software before placing any order above a certain threshold
2. Email the PO to the supplier with your order
3. When the invoice arrives, match it to the PO
4. Resolve any discrepancies before paying

### Setting a PO threshold

Not every purchase needs a PO. A mid-morning coffee does not warrant one. But a threshold — say, any purchase above 100 or 250 — keeps the process practical.

Below the threshold: use a company credit card or petty cash with simple receipt tracking.

Above the threshold: create a PO, get approval if needed, and follow the matching process.

## Three-way matching

Three-way matching is the gold standard for purchase order processing:

1. **Purchase order** — What was ordered
2. **Goods received note (GRN)** — What was actually delivered
3. **Invoice** — What the supplier is billing

All three should agree on quantities and prices. Discrepancies are investigated before payment is made.

**Two-way matching** (PO to invoice only) is simpler and sufficient for services where there is no physical delivery to check.

## Common PO problems

### Invoices without POs

Suppliers send invoices for goods or services that were never formally ordered. This can happen when employees bypass the PO process and order directly. Address this by making POs mandatory for all purchases above your threshold and communicating this requirement to suppliers.

### PO amendments

The original order changes — more items needed, different specifications, later delivery. Update the PO (or issue a revised PO) and get the amendment approved. Do not rely on verbal changes.

### Forgotten POs

POs are created but then forgotten. The goods arrive but nobody matches the invoice to the PO. Regular review of open POs ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

### Retrospective POs

Creating a PO after the purchase has already been made defeats the purpose. The PO is supposed to authorise spending before it happens. If retrospective POs become common, the approval process is broken.
