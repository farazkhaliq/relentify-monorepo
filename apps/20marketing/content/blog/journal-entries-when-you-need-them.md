In day-to-day bookkeeping, most transactions are recorded through standard processes — invoices, bills, bank transactions, and payments. But there are situations where the standard processes do not cover what needs to be recorded. That is where journal entries come in.

A journal entry is a manual adjustment to your accounts. It is the accounting equivalent of writing directly into the ledger, and it gives you the flexibility to record transactions and adjustments that do not fit neatly into the normal workflow.

## What is a journal entry?

A journal entry is a record of a financial transaction that is entered directly into the general ledger. It consists of at least two lines — a debit and a credit — that must balance (total debits must equal total credits).

Every journal entry follows the fundamental rule of double-entry bookkeeping: for every debit, there must be an equal and opposite credit. This ensures the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) always remains in balance.

### The anatomy of a journal entry

A journal entry includes:

- **Date** — When the entry is effective
- **Reference number** — A unique identifier
- **Description/narration** — An explanation of what the entry is for and why it is being made
- **Debit line(s)** — The account(s) being debited, with amounts
- **Credit line(s)** — The account(s) being credited, with amounts
- **Supporting documentation** — Evidence for the adjustment

Example:

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit | Description |
|------|---------|-------|--------|-------------|
| 31 Mar | Depreciation Expense | 500 | | Annual depreciation on office equipment |
| 31 Mar | Accumulated Depreciation | | 500 | Annual depreciation on office equipment |

The debits and credits balance (both 500), and the entry moves value from the asset (via accumulated depreciation) to the expense, reflecting the equipment's loss of value over time.

## When do you need journal entries?

### Year-end adjustments

The most common use of journal entries is for year-end (or month-end) adjustments that cannot be recorded through normal transaction processing:

**Depreciation** — Recording the decrease in value of fixed assets over time. Most businesses record depreciation monthly or annually via journal entries.

**Accruals** — Recording expenses that have been incurred but not yet invoiced. If you received a service in March but the invoice does not arrive until April, you accrue the expense in March with a journal entry.

**Prepayments** — If you paid for 12 months of insurance upfront, a journal entry allocates the correct portion to each month, moving the balance from "prepaid expense" (asset) to "insurance expense."

**Provisions** — Setting aside money for known future liabilities (bad debts, warranty claims, etc.).

### Correcting errors

If a transaction has been recorded in the wrong account, at the wrong amount, or in the wrong period, a journal entry corrects it without deleting the original transaction. This preserves the audit trail.

Example: An expense was coded to "Office Supplies" when it should have been "Marketing." The correcting journal entry debits Marketing and credits Office Supplies.

### Recording non-cash transactions

Some transactions do not involve cash but still need to be recorded:

- **Owner's drawings/contributions in kind** — An owner contributes a personal computer to the business, or takes business stock for personal use
- **Barter transactions** — You provide services in exchange for goods
- **Write-offs** — Writing off a bad debt that will never be collected

### Transfers between accounts

Moving money between accounts in your accounting system (not bank transfers, but accounting adjustments):

- Reclassifying an expense from one category to another
- Transferring retained earnings
- Allocating costs between departments or projects

### Opening balances

When you first set up your accounting software or switch from one system to another, you enter your opening balances as journal entries. This establishes the starting point for your new system.

### Reversals

If a previous journal entry needs to be undone (perhaps an accrual from last month that has now been replaced by an actual invoice), a reversing entry does this. It is the mirror image of the original entry — debits become credits and vice versa.

## How to create a journal entry

### Step 1: Identify the accounts affected

Determine which accounts need to be debited and which need to be credited. Remember:

- **Debits increase** assets and expenses
- **Credits increase** liabilities, equity, and revenue
- **Debits decrease** liabilities, equity, and revenue
- **Credits decrease** assets and expenses

### Step 2: Determine the amounts

Calculate the correct amounts for each line. Total debits must equal total credits.

### Step 3: Write a clear description

The description should explain not just what the entry is, but why it is being made. Future reviewers (including your future self and your accountant) need to understand the purpose without having to investigate.

Good: "Accrual for December electricity — invoice not yet received, estimated based on prior month's bill of 450"

Bad: "Adjustment"

### Step 4: Gather supporting documentation

Attach or reference the evidence for the entry. This might be:

- A calculation spreadsheet
- An email confirming an arrangement
- An asset register showing depreciation schedules
- A prior period's invoice used for estimation

### Step 5: Enter and review

Enter the journal entry in your accounting software. Most platforms like [Relentify](/accounting) include journal entry functionality that enforces the balancing rule — you cannot save an entry where debits do not equal credits. Review the entry carefully before posting.

### Step 6: Get approval (if required)

In businesses with separation of duties, journal entries should be reviewed and approved by someone other than the person who created them. This is a basic internal control.

## Common journal entry mistakes

### Unbalanced entries

Debits must equal credits. Always. If they do not, there is an error. Modern accounting software prevents this, but manual systems or spreadsheets can allow it.

### Missing descriptions

A journal entry without a clear description is a mystery for anyone who reviews the accounts later. Always explain the "why."

### Posting to the wrong period

Journal entries should be dated to the period they relate to, not the date you happen to be making the entry. A March accrual should be dated March, even if you are entering it in April.

### Forgetting to reverse

If you create an accrual or prepayment adjustment, it usually needs to be reversed in the following period. Forgetting to reverse leads to double-counting.

### Over-using journal entries

If you find yourself making lots of journal entries to record routine transactions, your normal processes may need improvement. Journal entries should be the exception, not the rule. Routine transactions should flow through invoices, bills, and bank transactions.

### Not getting approval

Journal entries are powerful — they can move any amount between any accounts. Without proper oversight, they can also be used to manipulate figures. Even in small businesses, a second pair of eyes on journal entries is good practice.

## Journal entries vs standard transactions

When should you use a journal entry versus a standard transaction?

| Scenario | Use |
|----------|-----|
| Invoicing a client | Invoice (not a journal entry) |
| Recording a supplier bill | Bill (not a journal entry) |
| Recording a bank payment | Bank transaction (not a journal entry) |
| Year-end depreciation | Journal entry |
| Correcting a miscategorised expense | Journal entry |
| Accruing for an expense not yet invoiced | Journal entry |
| Writing off a bad debt | Journal entry |
| Recording opening balances | Journal entry |

The rule of thumb: use standard transaction types for routine business transactions, and journal entries for adjustments, corrections, and non-standard items.

## Keeping journal entries organised

Over time, your business will accumulate many journal entries. Keep them organised:

- **Use sequential numbering** — JE-001, JE-002, etc.
- **Include the period** — Note which accounting period the entry relates to
- **Categorise by type** — Depreciation, accruals, corrections, etc.
- **Keep a journal entry register** — A log of all entries with their purpose and approval status

Journal entries are a fundamental part of accounting. They give you the flexibility to record anything that the standard processes cannot handle. Used properly, they keep your books accurate and complete. Used carelessly, they create confusion. The key is to make them clear, justified, and properly documented.
