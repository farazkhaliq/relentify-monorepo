Pet-friendly lettings are becoming more common, driven by changing attitudes, tenant demand, and evolving legislation in some jurisdictions. For landlords and agents, allowing pets creates specific inventory challenges that require thoughtful documentation.

Pet damage — scratches on doors, stains on carpets, odours embedded in soft furnishings — is among the most common and most contentious deposit deduction categories. A thorough inventory protects both the landlord (who needs evidence if damage occurs) and the tenant (who needs proof the property's pre-existing condition was not pristine).

## The inventory context for pet-friendly lets

### Why pets matter for inventories

Pets interact with a property differently from humans. Dogs scratch doors and skirting boards. Cats sharpen claws on carpet and furniture. Both can cause stains and odours that are difficult to remove. Birds, rabbits, and other small animals can also cause localised damage.

The inventory needs to capture the baseline condition of areas most likely to be affected by pets, so that any pet-related damage can be accurately assessed at check-out.

### The tenancy agreement sets the framework

Before the inventory begins, the tenancy agreement should already address:

- Whether pets are permitted (and which types)
- Any additional pet deposit or higher deposit
- The tenant's responsibility for pet damage
- Cleaning requirements at the end of the tenancy (including professional cleaning if required)
- Specific obligations (such as keeping the garden free of pet waste)

The inventory documents the physical condition; the tenancy agreement sets the contractual framework. Both are needed.

## What to document at check-in

### Floors and carpets

Pets are hardest on floors. Document:

- Carpet condition throughout — any existing stains, odours, or wear
- Hard floor condition — scratches on laminate or hardwood, chips in tiles
- Floor coverings near doors and access points (where pets are most active)
- Condition of floor edges and skirting boards (dogs often scratch these)

Take close-up photographs of floors in every room, especially near doorways, under windows, and in any areas where a pet is likely to rest.

### Doors and door frames

Scratching at doors is one of the most common forms of pet damage:

- Document the condition of every door — both sides
- Pay particular attention to the lower sections of doors (where pets scratch to be let in or out)
- Note the condition of door frames, especially at the base
- Document any existing scratches or wear

### Walls and skirting boards

- Check walls at pet height for marks, scuffs, or stains
- Document skirting board condition throughout (chewing and scratching marks)
- Note any existing areas where paint has been rubbed or worn (which could look like pet damage later)

### Furniture (furnished properties)

If the property is furnished, pet damage to furniture is a significant risk:

- Document the condition of all upholstered furniture — sofas, chairs, cushions
- Note any existing claw marks, hair, stains, or wear
- Check chair and table legs for scratching damage
- Document bed and mattress condition (if pets may access bedrooms)

### Garden and external areas

- Document lawn condition (pet activity can create worn patches and urine burns)
- Note fence condition, especially at ground level (dogs may dig underneath)
- Check gate latches and closures (relevant for pet containment)
- Document any existing pet-related wear in the garden

### Smell assessment

This is subjective but important. At check-in, note whether the property has any noticeable odours, including:

- Pet smell (from previous tenants or the property's history)
- Smoke smell
- Damp or musty odours
- Cleaning product smells

If the property smells clean and neutral at check-in, note it. This provides a baseline if pet odours are detected at check-out.

## Special photographic considerations

For pet-friendly lets, take additional photographs focused on the areas most at risk:

- **Every door, both sides** — especially the lower half
- **Skirting boards** throughout the property
- **Carpet close-ups** near doors, windows, and in the middle of rooms
- **Furniture legs and upholstery** (furnished properties)
- **Garden lawn and fence bases**
- **Any existing damage** that could be confused with pet damage later

The goal is to ensure that any future pet damage can be clearly identified as new — not attributed to pre-existing conditions.

## At check-out: assessing pet damage

### Common pet damage types

**Scratches**: On doors, door frames, skirting boards, furniture, and floors. Compare against check-in photos to determine whether scratches are new.

**Stains**: On carpets, flooring, and upholstery. Pet urine stains can penetrate deep into carpet and underlay. Compare against check-in photos and note any discolouration.

**Odours**: Pet odours can become embedded in carpets, curtains, and soft furnishings. If the property was odour-free at check-in and has a noticeable pet smell at check-out, this may warrant a professional cleaning deduction.

**Garden damage**: Worn lawn patches from pet activity, holes from digging, urine burns on grass. Compare against check-in garden photos.

**Flea infestation**: If the property has evidence of fleas at check-out, this is a tenant responsibility if they had pets. Professional flea treatment is a legitimate deduction.

### Fair wear and tear with pets

This is an important nuance. If the landlord agreed to pets in the property, some pet-related wear is arguably fair wear and tear. A single scratch on a door after a year of dog ownership is probably fair wear. Twenty deep scratches that have gouged through the paint and into the wood is damage.

The boundary depends on the severity and extent of the damage relative to the type of pet and the length of the tenancy. The check-in inventory provides the critical context.

## Calculating pet damage deductions

### Cleaning costs

If the tenancy agreement requires professional cleaning at the end (common for pet-friendly lets), the tenant is expected to arrange this. If they do not, or if the cleaning does not remove pet odours and stains, the landlord can claim the cost.

A standard professional clean may not address embedded pet odours. Specialist cleaning (including underlay treatment or replacement for urine-affected carpets) may be needed. Get quotes or invoices to support any deduction.

### Repair costs

Scratched doors may need sanding and repainting. Damaged skirting boards may need replacing. Gouged floors may need repair or replacement. As with all deposit deductions, apply betterment — the deduction should reflect the age and remaining life of the damaged item, not the full replacement cost.

### Replacement costs

If carpet, underlay, or furniture is damaged beyond repair, replacement may be necessary. Apply the same betterment principles as for any deposit deduction.

## Best practices for pet-friendly inventories

1. **Be thorough at check-in** — document every area a pet could affect, even if it is currently in perfect condition
2. **Photograph doors, skirting, and floors extensively** — these are the highest-risk areas
3. **Note the absence of pet odours** at check-in — this becomes evidence if odours are present at check-out
4. **Conduct mid-tenancy inspections** — catching pet damage early allows the tenant to address it before it worsens
5. **Communicate expectations clearly** — make sure the tenant understands their responsibility for pet damage
6. **Be fair at check-out** — some pet-related wear is inevitable if pets are permitted. Focus deductions on damage beyond reasonable use

## The bottom line

Pet-friendly lettings are here to stay, and inventories need to adapt. The key is thorough documentation of the areas most at risk, clear photography, and fair assessment at the end. When the evidence is strong and the expectations are clear, pet-related deposit disputes resolve the same way as any other — based on facts, not arguments.

Document the property as if a pet will definitely cause some damage. Then, if it does not, everyone is pleasantly surprised. And if it does, you have the evidence you need.
