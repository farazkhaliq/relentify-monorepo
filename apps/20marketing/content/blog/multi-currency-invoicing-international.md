Working with international clients is exciting for any growing business. It opens up new markets, diversifies your revenue, and often brings larger opportunities. But it also introduces a new layer of complexity: currency.

When you invoice a client in a different currency, every transaction involves an exchange rate — and that rate changes constantly. You need to decide which currency to invoice in, how to handle exchange rate fluctuations, and how to record everything accurately in your accounts.

Done well, multi-currency invoicing is manageable. Done poorly, it creates accounting headaches and unexpected gains or losses that distort your financial reports.

## Deciding which currency to invoice in

You generally have three options:

### Your local currency

Invoice in your home currency and let the client handle the conversion. This is simplest for you because there is no exchange rate risk — you receive exactly what you invoiced.

**Advantages:** No exchange rate risk, simpler accounting, predictable income.

**Disadvantages:** The client bears the conversion cost and exchange rate risk, which may make your pricing less attractive compared to competitors who invoice in the client's currency.

### The client's currency

Invoice in your client's local currency. They pay a round number they understand, and you receive whatever the exchange rate gives you at the time of payment.

**Advantages:** More attractive to international clients, competitive positioning, standard practice for many industries.

**Disadvantages:** You bear the exchange rate risk. The amount you receive in your home currency may be more or less than you expected when you set the price.

### A common currency

Some industries use a standard currency for international transactions (often USD or EUR) regardless of where either party is based.

**Advantages:** Both parties understand the pricing, and it is a standard approach in certain sectors.

**Disadvantages:** Both parties may need to convert, and neither is working in their home currency.

## Setting your exchange rate

When you create an invoice in a foreign currency, you need an exchange rate to record it in your accounts. There are several approaches:

### Spot rate at invoice date

Use the exchange rate on the day you issue the invoice. This is the most common approach and is straightforward.

### Average rate for the period

Some businesses use an average exchange rate for the month or quarter rather than the spot rate for each individual transaction. This smooths out daily fluctuations and simplifies bookkeeping when you have many transactions.

### Agreed fixed rate

For long-term contracts, you might agree a fixed exchange rate with your client. This eliminates exchange rate risk for both parties during the contract period, though it means neither party benefits from favourable rate movements.

### Central bank rate

Some jurisdictions require you to use the central bank rate published on a specific date. Check your local tax authority's guidance.

## Exchange rate gains and losses

Here is where multi-currency accounting gets interesting. Between the date you issue an invoice and the date you receive payment, the exchange rate changes. This creates an exchange rate gain or loss.

**Example:**

You invoice a client for 10,000 USD when the exchange rate is 1 USD = 0.80 of your home currency. You record the invoice as 8,000 in your home currency.

Two weeks later, the client pays. The exchange rate is now 1 USD = 0.82. You receive 8,200 in your home currency.

The 200 difference is an exchange rate gain. You received more than expected because the exchange rate moved in your favour.

If the rate had moved to 0.78, you would have received only 7,800 — an exchange rate loss of 200.

### Recording exchange rate differences

Exchange rate gains and losses should be recorded in a dedicated account in your chart of accounts (typically "Exchange Rate Gains/Losses" or "Foreign Currency Adjustments"). They appear on your profit and loss statement as a non-operating item.

This keeps your revenue and expenses clean — the original invoice amount stays in revenue, and the exchange rate impact is shown separately.

### Realised vs unrealised gains and losses

- **Realised** gains and losses occur when a transaction is completed (payment received or made). The difference between the original recorded amount and the actual amount received/paid is realised.
- **Unrealised** gains and losses occur at period end when you revalue outstanding foreign currency balances to the current exchange rate. These are paper gains or losses — they become realised when the transactions are eventually settled.

Most accounting standards require you to revalue foreign currency balances at each reporting date.

## Practical considerations

### Payment methods for international clients

Receiving international payments adds cost and complexity. Common methods include:

**International bank transfer (SWIFT)** — Traditional method. Banks charge fees on both the sending and receiving ends, and the exchange rate applied by the bank may be less favourable than the market rate. Transfers can take 2-5 business days.

**Multi-currency payment platforms** — Services like Wise, Payoneer, or similar platforms offer better exchange rates and lower fees than traditional banks. Many let you hold balances in multiple currencies.

**Credit card payments** — Accepting card payments in foreign currencies is convenient for clients but involves processing fees (typically 2-3%) plus a currency conversion fee.

**PayPal and similar services** — Convenient but often have unfavourable exchange rates and higher fees than dedicated currency platforms.

Consider the total cost of each method (fees + exchange rate spread) rather than just the headline fee.

### Holding foreign currency accounts

If you regularly work with clients in a particular currency, consider opening a bank account in that currency. This lets you:

- Receive payments without immediate conversion
- Pay suppliers in the same currency
- Choose when to convert, potentially getting a better rate
- Avoid double-conversion costs

### Pricing strategy

When setting prices for international clients, factor in:

- Exchange rate risk — Build a small buffer into your pricing to absorb rate fluctuations
- Payment method costs — If international transfers cost you fees, factor them into your pricing
- Local market rates — Research what competitors charge in the client's market

Some businesses quote a price range rather than a fixed price, or include a clause in their contracts that adjusts the price if the exchange rate moves more than a certain percentage.

## Accounting for multi-currency transactions

### At invoice creation

Record the invoice in both the foreign currency (for the client) and your home currency (for your accounts) using the exchange rate at the invoice date.

### At payment receipt

Record the payment in your home currency at the exchange rate when the money arrives. If this differs from the rate used at invoicing, record the exchange rate gain or loss.

### At period end

Revalue any outstanding foreign currency invoices and balances to the current exchange rate. Record unrealised gains or losses.

### Tax implications

Exchange rate gains are typically taxable, and exchange rate losses are typically deductible. The exact treatment varies by jurisdiction, so consult your accountant or tax advisor.

## Tips for managing multi-currency

1. **Choose your approach and be consistent** — Whatever method you use for exchange rates, apply it consistently
2. **Invoice promptly** — The longer the gap between earning and invoicing, the more exchange rate risk you accumulate
3. **Monitor exchange rates** — Major rate movements can significantly impact your margins
4. **Consider hedging for large contracts** — Forward contracts or options can lock in exchange rates for known future transactions
5. **Reconcile foreign currency accounts separately** — Each currency account needs its own reconciliation
6. **Review exchange rate impact regularly** — Monthly reviews help you understand whether currency movements are helping or hurting your business

Multi-currency invoicing adds complexity, but it also opens doors to a global client base. With the right processes and tools in place, it becomes a routine part of your financial management rather than a source of anxiety.
