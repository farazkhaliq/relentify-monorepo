Minimum wage in the United States is not a single number. It is a patchwork of federal, state, and local rates that varies dramatically depending on where your employees work. An employer in one state might pay the federal minimum, while an employer across the state line pays nearly double that amount.

For businesses operating in multiple locations or hiring remote workers, understanding minimum wage requirements is essential for payroll compliance. This guide explains how the system works, what employers must track, and how to stay compliant as rates change.

## The federal minimum wage

The federal minimum wage is set by the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) and applies to covered non-exempt employees nationwide. The federal rate has historically been adjusted by Congress at irregular intervals and has remained at the same level for extended periods between increases.

**Key point:** The federal minimum wage sets a floor. No covered employee in the United States can legally be paid below this rate, regardless of state law. However, the majority of states have enacted minimum wages that exceed the federal rate.

## How state minimum wage works

Each state has the authority to set its own minimum wage. When both federal and state minimum wages apply, the employee is entitled to the higher of the two rates.

### States with higher minimum wages

The majority of US states set minimum wages above the federal level. Rates vary significantly — some states have minimum wages that are more than double the federal rate, while others are only slightly above it.

States with the highest minimum wages tend to be those with higher costs of living, often concentrated on the West Coast and in the Northeast. Several states have enacted legislation to gradually increase their minimum wage to fifteen dollars per hour or more over a phased schedule.

### States that follow the federal minimum

A smaller number of states either have no state minimum wage law or set their state rate at or below the federal minimum. In these states, the federal rate applies to covered employees.

### States with automatic adjustments

Many states have tied their minimum wage to an inflation index, typically the Consumer Price Index (CPI). In these states, the minimum wage increases automatically each year without requiring new legislation. This means employers must check the updated rate annually, even if no new law has been passed.

**Automatic adjustment states include** a growing number of jurisdictions across the country. The indexed increases are usually announced in the fall for the following January.

## Local minimum wages

In addition to state rates, many cities and counties have enacted their own minimum wage ordinances that exceed both federal and state levels. This is particularly common in major metropolitan areas.

**Examples of localities with higher minimum wages:**
- Several cities in California
- Cities in Washington State
- New York City and surrounding areas
- Cities in Colorado, Minnesota, and other states

If your business operates in a jurisdiction with a local minimum wage, you must pay at least the local rate for employees working in that location.

**Preemption:** Some states have enacted preemption laws that prohibit cities and counties from setting minimum wages higher than the state rate. In these states, only the state rate applies regardless of any local ordinance. Check whether your state allows local minimum wage laws before assuming a local rate does not apply.

## Special minimum wage categories

### Tipped employees

The FLSA allows employers to pay a lower cash wage to tipped employees, provided the employee's tips bring their total hourly compensation to at least the applicable minimum wage. This is known as the tip credit.

**Federal tipped minimum:** The FLSA sets a lower cash minimum wage for tipped employees, with the employer claiming a tip credit for the difference between the cash wage and the standard minimum.

**State tipped minimums vary widely:**
- Some states require the full state minimum wage to be paid to tipped employees regardless of tips
- Others allow a tip credit but at a higher cash minimum than the federal rate
- Some follow the federal tipped minimum wage structure

Employers using the tip credit must ensure that the combination of cash wages and tips equals at least the applicable minimum wage for every hour worked. If tips fall short, the employer must make up the difference.

### Youth and training wages

The FLSA allows employers to pay a youth minimum wage (lower than the standard rate) to employees under 20 years of age during their first 90 consecutive calendar days of employment. Some states do not allow this sub-minimum wage.

### Student workers

Certain full-time students working in retail, service, agriculture, or at colleges and universities may be paid a sub-minimum wage under specific Department of Labor certificates.

### Workers with disabilities

Under certain DOL certificates, employers may pay workers with disabilities a sub-minimum wage based on their productive capacity relative to workers without disabilities. This program has been increasingly restricted and is being phased out in many jurisdictions.

## Compliance challenges for multi-location businesses

### Remote workers

If you have employees working remotely from different states, you may owe different minimum wages to different employees based on where they physically work — not where your business is headquartered.

**Rule of thumb:** The minimum wage that applies is typically determined by the employee's work location. An employee working from home in a high-minimum-wage state must be paid at least that state's rate, even if your business is based in a lower-wage state.

### Employees who travel between locations

Employees who regularly work in multiple locations may be subject to different minimum wages depending on where they perform their work each day. The rules for determining which rate applies can be complex and vary by jurisdiction.

### Tracking rate changes

With the federal rate, fifty state rates, and hundreds of local rates — many of which change annually — staying current requires deliberate effort.

**Best practices:**
- Subscribe to Department of Labor updates at both federal and state levels
- Set annual calendar reminders to check for rate changes (most take effect on January 1 or July 1)
- Use payroll software that automatically updates minimum wage rates by jurisdiction
- Review all employee pay rates against the applicable minimums at least annually

## Record keeping requirements

The FLSA requires employers to maintain records for each non-exempt employee, including:
- The basis on which wages are paid and the regular hourly pay rate
- Hours worked each workday and total hours each workweek
- Total wages paid each pay period

These records must be retained for at least three years. For tipped employees, additional records of tip credits and total compensation are required.

## Penalties for non-compliance

Paying below the applicable minimum wage exposes employers to:

**Back pay:** Employers must pay the difference between what was paid and what should have been paid for the affected period.

**Liquidated damages:** Under the FLSA, employees may recover an additional amount equal to the back pay owed (effectively doubling the damages).

**Civil penalties:** The DOL can assess civil money penalties per violation, with higher penalties for willful or repeated violations.

**State penalties:** State labor departments may impose additional fines, penalties, and interest under state minimum wage laws.

**Private lawsuits:** Employees can file private lawsuits for minimum wage violations, and successful plaintiffs are typically awarded attorney's fees in addition to back pay and damages.

## Wage notices and pay stub requirements

Many states require employers to provide written notice to employees at the time of hire specifying their pay rate, pay frequency, and the employer's identity. Some states also require detailed pay stubs showing hours worked, pay rate, deductions, and net pay.

These requirements vary by state and are separate from the federal record-keeping obligations. Failure to provide required notices can result in penalties independent of any wage violation.

## Practical steps for employers

1. **Identify every jurisdiction where your employees work** — headquarters, branch offices, remote locations, and travel destinations
2. **Determine the applicable minimum wage for each location** — federal, state, and local
3. **Apply the highest applicable rate** for each employee based on their work location
4. **Audit current pay rates** against applicable minimums at least annually
5. **Update payroll systems** whenever rates change
6. **Track tipped employee compensation** to ensure total hourly compensation meets the minimum
7. **Maintain compliant records** for at least three years
8. **Provide required wage notices** and pay stubs as specified by each state

Minimum wage compliance sounds straightforward — pay at least the legal minimum. But with fifty states, hundreds of local jurisdictions, automatic annual increases, and special categories for tipped and other workers, the details require attention. The cost of staying current is modest. The cost of getting it wrong is not.
