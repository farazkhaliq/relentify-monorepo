When your workforce is spread across multiple sites, client premises, or remote locations, a fundamental question arises: how do you know that workers are actually where they say they are when they clock in?

This is not about distrust. It is about accuracy. If you are paying workers based on hours recorded at specific locations, you need confidence that those records reflect reality. GPS-verified timesheets provide that confidence — and in doing so, they eliminate one of the most persistent problems in workforce management: time theft.

## What is time theft?

Time theft occurs when an employee is paid for time they did not actually work. It takes several forms:

- **Early clock-in**: A worker clocks in from home or in their car before arriving at the site
- **Late clock-out**: A worker leaves the site but does not clock out until later
- **Buddy punching**: One worker clocks in on behalf of another who has not yet arrived
- **Ghost hours**: A worker records hours at a location they never visited
- **Extended breaks**: A worker takes longer breaks than recorded

Most time theft is not malicious. It often starts as a minor habit — clocking in five minutes early while still in the car park, or forgetting to clock out at the end of a shift and doing it from home. But these small discrepancies add up. Industry estimates suggest that time theft costs businesses between two and seven percent of their payroll, depending on the sector and level of oversight.

## How GPS verification works

GPS-verified timesheets use the location services built into smartphones to record where a worker is when they clock in or out. The process is straightforward:

1. The employer sets up a geofence around each work site — a virtual boundary defined by GPS coordinates and a radius (typically 50 to 200 metres)
2. When a worker taps "clock in" on their phone, the app records the current time and the device's GPS coordinates
3. The system checks whether the coordinates fall within the geofence of an assigned site
4. If the worker is within the boundary, the clock-in is verified. If not, it may be flagged for review or rejected, depending on the employer's settings

The same process applies to clock-out, break start, and break end. The result is a complete, location-verified record of the worker's shift.

## What GPS verification prevents

### Clocking in from the wrong location

The most common form of time theft on remote sites is clocking in before arriving. A worker might clock in from the car while stuck in traffic, adding ten or fifteen minutes to their recorded shift. Over a five-day week, that is an hour of paid time with no work performed. Over a year, across a team of twenty workers, the cost is substantial.

GPS verification catches this immediately. If the worker is outside the geofence when they attempt to clock in, the system flags it. Managers can see exactly where the clock-in occurred and decide whether to accept or reject it.

### Buddy punching

In paper-based or PIN-based systems, one worker can easily clock in for a colleague. With GPS-verified mobile timesheets, each clock-in is tied to a specific device and location. Even if a worker could somehow access a colleague's phone, they would still need to be at the correct location — which eliminates most of the incentive.

### Ghost shifts

In businesses that serve multiple clients across different locations — cleaning, security, care — there is a risk that workers record hours at sites they never visited. GPS verification makes this virtually impossible. Every clock-in is stamped with coordinates that can be cross-referenced against the assigned site.

### Extended breaks

If break tracking is combined with GPS data, managers can see not only when breaks started and ended but where the worker was during the break. This is particularly relevant for workers at client sites, where taking an excessively long break reflects poorly on the business.

## The privacy question

GPS tracking is a sensitive topic, and rightly so. Workers have legitimate concerns about being monitored, and employers have a responsibility to use location data proportionately and transparently.

Here are the key principles for implementing GPS-verified timesheets ethically:

### Track only during working hours

GPS data should only be collected at the moment of clock-in and clock-out (and optionally during breaks). The system should not continuously track a worker's location throughout the day, and it absolutely should not track them outside of working hours.

### Be transparent

Tell workers exactly what data is collected, when, and why. Explain that GPS verification is about confirming they were at the right place at the right time — not about monitoring their movements. Most workers accept this once they understand the scope.

### Use it consistently

Apply GPS verification to all workers at a given level, not selectively. If only certain workers are tracked, it creates a perception of unfairness and suspicion.

### Store data securely

GPS coordinates are personal data. Store them in compliance with data protection regulations, restrict access to authorised personnel, and define retention periods. Do not keep location data longer than necessary.

### Allow exceptions

There will be legitimate cases where a worker needs to clock in from outside the geofence — for example, if the GPS signal is weak inside a building, or if the worker is arriving at a new site that has not yet been configured. Build a process for handling these exceptions without penalising the worker.

## Industries that benefit most

GPS-verified timesheets are valuable in any business with a distributed workforce, but they are particularly impactful in:

### Construction

Workers move between sites daily. Subcontractors may work across multiple projects for different clients. GPS verification confirms presence on site and provides evidence for client billing.

### Cleaning and facilities management

Cleaning companies often serve dozens of client sites with workers arriving early in the morning or late at night. GPS verification confirms that the contracted hours are being delivered.

### Domiciliary care

Care agencies send carers to visit clients in their homes. GPS-verified clock-in and clock-out times provide evidence that visits occurred and lasted the required duration — important for both regulatory compliance and client billing.

### Security

Security companies need to confirm that guards are on patrol at the correct times and locations. GPS-verified timesheets replace manual sign-in sheets and provide an auditable record of presence.

### Field services and maintenance

Engineers, technicians, and maintenance teams working across multiple client sites benefit from automatic location recording, which feeds into job costing and client invoicing.

## Implementing GPS-verified timesheets

Setting up GPS verification is straightforward with the right platform. Tools like [Relentify](/timesheets) allow you to define geofences for each site, assign workers to those sites, and automatically verify clock-in locations — all from a single dashboard.

Here is a practical implementation plan:

1. **Map your sites**: List every location where workers operate. For each site, define the centre point (address or coordinates) and a reasonable radius.
2. **Configure geofences**: Set up each site in your timesheet system with its geofence. Start with a generous radius (150-200 metres) and tighten it later if needed.
3. **Brief your team**: Explain what GPS verification is, how it works, and why it is being introduced. Address privacy concerns directly.
4. **Pilot with one team**: Run the system with a single team or site for two weeks before rolling it out more broadly.
5. **Review and adjust**: Check the data after the pilot. Are there false flags due to GPS drift? Are geofences too tight or too loose? Adjust settings based on real-world results.
6. **Roll out fully**: Once the system is calibrated, extend it to all sites and teams.

## The business case

The return on investment for GPS-verified timesheets is typically rapid. Even a modest reduction in time theft — say, five minutes per worker per day — delivers significant savings across a year:

- 20 workers x 5 minutes/day x 250 working days = 416 hours
- At a blended cost of $20/hour, that is $8,300 per year in recovered payroll

Add the value of more accurate client invoicing, reduced disputes, and stronger compliance records, and the business case becomes compelling.

GPS-verified timesheets are not about catching people. They are about building a system where the data is reliable enough to base business decisions on — from payroll to pricing to project planning. In a world where workforces are increasingly distributed, that reliability is not a luxury. It is a necessity.
