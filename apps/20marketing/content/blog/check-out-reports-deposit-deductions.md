The check-out report is where everything comes together. It is the document that compares the property's condition at the end of a tenancy against the check-in record, and it forms the basis for any deposit deductions. A well-prepared check-out report protects landlords who have legitimate claims. A poorly prepared one can cost thousands in lost deductions.

This guide covers what a check-out report should include, how to conduct one properly, and how to ensure your deductions are fair and defensible.

## What is a check-out report?

A check-out report is a detailed assessment of a property's condition at the end of a tenancy. It mirrors the structure of the check-in report, covering the same rooms, fixtures, contents, and external areas, so the two can be compared directly.

The purpose is straightforward: identify any changes that have occurred during the tenancy and determine whether those changes justify deposit deductions.

Changes fall into three categories:

- **Fair wear and tear** — normal deterioration from everyday use, which is the landlord's responsibility
- **Damage** — deterioration beyond what would be expected, caused by the tenant's actions or negligence
- **Missing items** — contents or fixtures that were present at check-in but are absent at check-out

Only damage and missing items warrant deductions. Fair wear and tear does not.

## When should the check-out happen?

The check-out should take place on or as close to the last day of the tenancy as possible. Ideally, it happens after the tenant has removed all their belongings and cleaned the property, but before they return the keys.

Timing matters. If the check-out is done too early, the tenant may cause further damage while moving out. If it is done too late, the landlord may have already started work on the property, making it harder to distinguish tenant damage from renovation work.

## Who should conduct the check-out?

The same person or company that prepared the check-in report should ideally handle the check-out. This ensures consistency in how conditions are described and assessed. If that is not possible, the check-out clerk should have access to the original check-in report and follow the same format.

Impartiality matters enormously at check-out. If the landlord conducts the check-out themselves, tenants may challenge the findings as biased. An independent inventory clerk provides the strongest position in any dispute.

## What the check-out report should include

### Room-by-room comparison

For every room, the check-out should assess:

- Walls, ceilings, and floors — noting any new marks, stains, holes, or damage since check-in
- Windows and doors — condition of glass, frames, locks, handles
- Light fittings and switches — working condition
- Radiators — condition and whether operational
- Curtains and blinds — condition and cleanliness

Each observation should reference the check-in record. Rather than simply noting "mark on living room wall," a good check-out report says "mark on living room wall — not present at check-in" or "mark on living room wall — noted at check-in, no change."

### Cleanliness assessment

Cleaning is one of the most common sources of deposit disputes. The check-out report should assess the cleanliness of every room and compare it against the standard recorded at check-in.

If the property was professionally cleaned before check-in, the tenant should return it to a professionally cleaned standard. If it was cleaned to a domestic standard, the expectation should match.

Specific areas to assess include:

- Kitchen appliances (inside and out)
- Bathroom fixtures — bath, shower, toilet, sink
- Windows — glass and frames
- Carpets and flooring
- Skirting boards and light switches
- Behind and under furniture (if furnished)

### Furniture and contents check

For furnished properties, every item listed on the check-in inventory must be accounted for. The check-out should confirm:

- Whether each item is still present
- Whether its condition has changed
- Whether any items have been added by the tenant and left behind

Missing or damaged items should be clearly documented with photographic evidence.

### Appliance condition

Each appliance should be tested and its condition compared to check-in:

- Does the oven heat properly?
- Does the fridge maintain temperature?
- Does the washing machine complete a cycle?
- Are there any new dents, scratches, or operational issues?

### Meters and utilities

Record the final meter readings for gas, electricity, and water. Photograph each meter. These readings help finalise utility accounts and can be relevant if there are concerns about tampering or excessive usage.

### Keys

Confirm all keys provided at check-in have been returned. Missing keys are a legitimate deduction, especially if locks need to be changed for security reasons.

### Photographic evidence

Every observation needs photographic support. The check-out photos should mirror the check-in photos as closely as possible — same rooms, same angles, same areas of detail. This makes comparison straightforward for anyone reviewing the reports.

## Fair wear and tear: the critical distinction

The concept that causes the most confusion and disagreement in deposit deductions is fair wear and tear. Understanding it is essential.

Fair wear and tear is the natural and reasonable deterioration that occurs through normal, everyday use of a property. It is not the tenant's responsibility.

**Examples of fair wear and tear:**

- Slight fading of paint or wallpaper from sunlight
- Small scuffs on skirting boards from vacuuming
- Minor carpet wear in high-traffic areas (hallways, doorways)
- Gradual dulling of kitchen worktops from regular use
- Loose door handles from repeated use

**Examples that are NOT fair wear and tear:**

- Large holes in walls from hanging heavy items without permission
- Burns or cuts on kitchen worktops
- Pet scratches on doors or floors
- Carpet stains from spills that were not cleaned
- Broken appliances due to misuse

The distinction depends on context. A five-year-old carpet will show more wear than a one-year-old one. A family of four will create more wear than a single occupant. Adjudicators consider the age and condition of items at check-in, the length of the tenancy, and the number of occupants when assessing whether deterioration is reasonable.

## How to calculate fair deductions

When damage is identified, the deduction should be fair and proportionate. You cannot charge for a brand-new replacement if the damaged item was already several years old.

The standard approach is to apply a depreciation or betterment calculation:

- **Carpet**: Typical lifespan of 5 to 10 years depending on quality. If a carpet was 3 years old at check-in and the tenancy lasted 2 years, the carpet is now 5 years old. The tenant should only be charged a proportion of the replacement cost that reflects the remaining useful life.
- **Decoration**: Typically lasts 3 to 5 years. If walls were freshly painted at check-in and the tenancy was 4 years, the paint has reached the end of its expected life, and the tenant should not be charged for repainting.
- **Appliances**: Typical lifespan varies (10 to 15 years for a fridge, 5 to 10 for a washing machine). Apply the same proportional approach.

A transparent, well-reasoned deduction is far more likely to be upheld than one that simply charges full replacement cost.

## Common check-out mistakes

### Comparing against the wrong baseline

The check-out must compare against the check-in report, not against the landlord's expectations or a previous tenancy's check-out. If the carpet was stained at check-in, you cannot deduct for that same stain at check-out.

### Claiming for fair wear and tear

This is the most common reason landlords lose deposit disputes. If damage falls within what is reasonable for the length and type of tenancy, it is not the tenant's liability.

### Insufficient evidence

A check-out report that says "property not cleaned to acceptable standard" without photographs, without comparing to the check-in cleaning standard, and without specifics, will not hold up. Be precise, be photographic, be comparative.

### Not giving the tenant the opportunity to put things right

Many tenancy agreements and deposit schemes expect that tenants are given a reasonable opportunity to address issues before deductions are made. If the property is not clean enough, offering the tenant a chance to re-clean is both fair and pragmatic.

### Delaying the check-out report

The sooner the check-out report is completed and shared with the tenant, the stronger it is. A report produced weeks after the tenancy ended is easier to challenge.

## After the check-out

Once the check-out report is complete, the next steps are:

1. Share the report with the tenant and landlord
2. Identify any proposed deductions with supporting evidence
3. Negotiate or agree on deductions
4. Return the agreed portion of the deposit
5. If there is a dispute, submit evidence to the deposit protection scheme

The check-out report is the centrepiece of this process. A good one makes everything smoother. A poor one invites disputes, delays, and losses.

Invest the time and care at check-out. It is the last step of the tenancy, but it is the one that determines whether your financial interests are protected.
