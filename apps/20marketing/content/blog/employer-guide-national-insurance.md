National Insurance contributions are one of the largest payroll costs for UK employers, yet they remain one of the most misunderstood. Unlike income tax, which most people have some grasp of, National Insurance operates under its own set of rules, thresholds, and categories — and getting it wrong can result in underpayment penalties or overpaying without realising it.

This guide explains how National Insurance works from the employer's perspective, what you need to calculate and report, and how to stay compliant.

## What is National Insurance?

National Insurance is a tax on earnings that funds state benefits including the State Pension, statutory sick pay, and maternity allowance. Both employers and employees pay National Insurance, but the rates and thresholds are different for each.

There are several classes of National Insurance, but as an employer running payroll, you primarily deal with:

- **Class 1 (Employee)**: Deducted from the employee's pay
- **Class 1 (Employer)**: An additional cost borne by the employer, calculated on top of the employee's gross pay
- **Class 1A**: Paid by employers on benefits in kind (such as company cars)
- **Class 1B**: Paid by employers under a PAYE Settlement Agreement

Most payroll processing focuses on Class 1 contributions for both employees and employers.

## How employee NICs work

Employee National Insurance is deducted from pay using a threshold system. Employees do not pay NI on earnings below the Primary Threshold. Once earnings exceed that threshold, contributions are charged at a percentage rate.

The key thresholds for employees are:

- **Lower Earnings Limit (LEL)**: Earnings below this level do not qualify for NI benefits. No contributions are due, but the employee builds a qualifying year for State Pension purposes.
- **Primary Threshold (PT)**: The point at which employee NI contributions begin.
- **Upper Earnings Limit (UEL)**: Earnings above this level are charged at a reduced rate.

Between the Primary Threshold and the Upper Earnings Limit, the standard employee NI rate applies. Above the UEL, a lower rate applies. The exact rates change with each tax year, so always check the current year's figures.

## How employer NICs work

Employer National Insurance is an additional cost on top of the employee's salary. It is not deducted from the employee's pay — it is an expense for the business.

The key threshold for employers is the **Secondary Threshold (ST)**. Employer NICs are charged on all earnings above this threshold at the employer's rate. Unlike employee NICs, there is no upper limit — employer contributions apply to all earnings above the secondary threshold.

This means employer NI costs increase linearly with salary. For high earners, employer NICs can represent a significant additional cost beyond the headline salary figure.

## NI categories

Each employee is assigned a National Insurance category letter, which determines the rate of contributions for both the employee and the employer. The most common categories are:

- **A**: Standard rate for most employees aged 21 to State Pension age
- **B**: Married women and widows who elected to pay reduced rate (very rare for new employees)
- **C**: Employees over State Pension age (no employee NI, but employer NI still applies)
- **H**: Apprentices under 25 (reduced employer rate)
- **M**: Employees under 21 (reduced employer rate)
- **V**: Veterans in first year of civilian employment (zero employer rate up to a threshold)

The category affects the rate at which NICs are calculated. Make sure each employee is assigned the correct category — using the wrong one means you are either over- or under-deducting.

## Calculating NICs: a worked example

Suppose you have an employee on category A earning £3,000 per month. Using illustrative thresholds and rates:

- **Primary Threshold**: £1,048 per month
- **Employee NI rate**: 8% on earnings between PT and UEL
- **Upper Earnings Limit**: £4,189 per month
- **Secondary Threshold**: £758 per month
- **Employer NI rate**: 13.8% on earnings above ST

**Employee NI**: (£3,000 − £1,048) × 8% = £156.16

**Employer NI**: (£3,000 − £758) × 13.8% = £309.40

The employee has £156.16 deducted from their pay. The employer pays an additional £309.40 on top of the gross salary. The total NI cost to the business for this employee is £309.40 per month.

Note: These are illustrative figures. Always use the current tax year's thresholds and rates, which HMRC publishes before the start of each tax year.

## Employment Allowance

The Employment Allowance allows eligible employers to reduce their employer NI liability by a set annual amount. As of recent tax years, this has been set at £5,000.

Not all employers qualify. You cannot claim the Employment Allowance if:

- You are a sole director company with no other employees
- Your employer NI liability in the previous tax year exceeded a specified threshold
- You are a public body or carry out functions of a public nature

If you do qualify, the allowance is applied against your employer NI bill each month until it is used up. For small employers, this can significantly reduce or even eliminate employer NI for the first few months of the tax year.

Claim the Employment Allowance through your payroll software or your Real Time Information (RTI) submission. You need to claim it each tax year — it does not carry forward automatically.

## Reporting NICs through RTI

Employer and employee NICs are reported to HMRC through the Real Time Information system as part of your regular payroll submissions. Each time you run payroll, your Full Payment Submission (FPS) includes:

- Each employee's NI category
- Gross pay for NI purposes
- Employee NI deducted
- Employer NI due

At the end of each tax month, you also submit an Employer Payment Summary (EPS) if you need to report adjustments such as Employment Allowance claims, statutory payment recoveries, or CIS deductions.

The NICs you report through RTI must match the amounts you pay to HMRC. Discrepancies trigger investigations and potential penalties.

## Paying NICs to HMRC

Employer and employee NICs are paid to HMRC alongside PAYE income tax. The combined amount is due by the 22nd of the month following the tax month (or the 19th if paying by post).

For most small employers, this means a single monthly payment covering all PAYE and NIC liabilities. HMRC provides a payment reference that links your payment to your employer account.

Late payment attracts interest and penalties. Setting up a direct debit or automated payment through your [payroll software](/payroll) ensures you never miss a deadline.

## Directors' National Insurance

Directors are a special case. Unlike regular employees, directors' NI is calculated on an annual basis rather than per pay period. This means:

- In months where a director takes no salary, no NI is calculated
- If the director takes their full annual salary in one month, NI is calculated on the annual earnings at that point
- The annual calculation prevents directors from overpaying NI by spreading earnings unevenly across the year

Some employers opt to calculate directors' NI on a cumulative period-by-period basis instead, which is also permitted. The choice should be made at the start of the tax year and applied consistently.

## Benefits in kind and Class 1A

If you provide employees with benefits in kind — company cars, private medical insurance, interest-free loans above a threshold — you will owe Class 1A National Insurance on the value of those benefits.

Class 1A is calculated at the employer's NI rate on the cash equivalent value of the benefit. It is reported via the P11D form (or through payrolling benefits in kind) and paid annually by the July deadline following the end of the tax year.

## Common NI mistakes

- **Wrong NI category**: Assigning category A to an employee under 21 (should be M) means you pay more employer NI than necessary.
- **Forgetting to claim Employment Allowance**: Free money left on the table.
- **Incorrect director calculations**: Using period-by-period when annual is more appropriate, or vice versa.
- **Not accounting for employer NI in budgets**: When calculating the true cost of an employee, add 13.8% on top of the gross salary for employer NI. A £30,000 salary costs closer to £34,000 once employer NI is included.
- **Misreporting on RTI**: Errors in FPS submissions create discrepancies that HMRC will query.
