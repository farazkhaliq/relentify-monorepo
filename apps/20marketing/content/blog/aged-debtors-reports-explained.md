If your business invoices clients, you almost certainly have money sitting in accounts receivable — invoices that have been sent but not yet paid. The question is: how much, and how old?

An aged debtors report (also called an accounts receivable aging report) answers both questions. It is one of the most useful reports in your accounting toolkit, and reviewing it regularly is one of the simplest ways to improve your cash flow.

## What is an aged debtors report?

An aged debtors report lists all your outstanding invoices, grouped by how long they have been unpaid. It typically uses aging buckets:

- **Current** — Not yet due
- **1-30 days overdue**
- **31-60 days overdue**
- **61-90 days overdue**
- **90+ days overdue**

Each customer appears as a row, with their outstanding amounts distributed across the aging buckets. The totals at the bottom show your overall receivables position.

### Example report

| Customer | Current | 1-30 | 31-60 | 61-90 | 90+ | Total |
|----------|---------|------|-------|-------|-----|-------|
| ABC Ltd | 2,000 | 1,500 | — | — | — | 3,500 |
| XYZ Corp | — | 3,000 | 2,000 | — | — | 5,000 |
| Smith & Co | — | — | — | 1,000 | 500 | 1,500 |
| **Totals** | **2,000** | **4,500** | **2,000** | **1,000** | **500** | **10,000** |

At a glance, you can see that:
- You are owed 10,000 in total
- 2,000 is not yet due (healthy)
- 4,500 is 1-30 days overdue (needs attention)
- 3,500 is more than 30 days overdue (needs urgent attention)
- Smith & Co has an invoice over 90 days old (potential bad debt)

## Why the aged debtors report matters

### Cash flow management

Your bank balance tells you how much cash you have right now. The aged debtors report tells you how much cash is coming — and how quickly. If a large portion of your receivables is in the older buckets, your cash flow is at risk because that money may arrive late or not at all.

### Early warning system

The report highlights problems before they become crises. A client who is consistently in the 31-60 day bucket is developing a pattern of late payment. You can address it now rather than waiting until they are 90 days overdue.

### Collection prioritisation

When you have limited time for chasing payments, the aged debtors report tells you where to focus. Large amounts in older buckets should be chased first — they represent the highest risk and the biggest cash flow impact.

### Bad debt identification

Invoices in the 90+ day bucket are candidates for bad debt write-off. The longer an invoice goes unpaid, the less likely it is to be collected. Reviewing this bucket regularly helps you identify and deal with bad debts promptly.

### Client relationship management

The report can reveal patterns in client payment behaviour. Some clients always pay within terms. Others always take an extra week. Knowing this helps you set appropriate terms for each client and plan your cash flow accordingly.

## How to use the report effectively

### Review it weekly

For businesses with significant receivables, weekly review keeps you on top of the numbers. At minimum, review it monthly.

### Focus on the aging trend

A single snapshot is useful, but the trend is more important. Is the total receivables balance growing? Is the proportion in older buckets increasing? These trends signal worsening collection performance.

### Set collection triggers

Define what happens at each aging threshold:

- **Current** — No action needed
- **1-30 days overdue** — Send an automated reminder
- **31-60 days overdue** — Personal follow-up (phone call or personalised email)
- **61-90 days overdue** — Escalate to a senior contact or formal notice
- **90+ days overdue** — Consider debt recovery or write-off

### Track your DSO

Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) is a metric derived from your aged debtors report. It measures the average number of days it takes to collect payment:

**DSO = (Total accounts receivable / Total credit sales) x Number of days**

A lower DSO means faster collection. Track it monthly and aim to reduce it over time.

### Compare with payment terms

If your standard terms are Net 30 but your average collection period is 45 days, clients are consistently paying 15 days late. This disconnect between terms and reality needs to be addressed — either by enforcing terms more strictly or by adjusting your expectations (and your cash flow forecasts).

## Common patterns and what they mean

### Concentration risk

If one or two clients represent a large proportion of your total receivables, you have concentration risk. If that client pays late or defaults, the impact on your cash flow is significant. Diversifying your client base reduces this risk.

### Seasonal patterns

Some businesses see receivables spike at certain times of year — after a busy season, over holiday periods, or at the end of financial quarters. Understanding these patterns helps you plan.

### Chronic late payers

If the same clients appear in the older buckets month after month, they either have cash flow problems of their own or they have learned that you do not enforce your terms. Address both with a combination of firm communication and adjusted terms.

### Sudden aging

If a previously reliable client suddenly appears in the 60+ day bucket, something has changed. It might be an invoice dispute they have not mentioned, an internal process change, or a cash flow problem. Reach out to find out.

## Improving your aged debtors position

### Invoice promptly and accurately

Errors on invoices cause delays. Missing purchase order numbers, wrong addresses, or incorrect amounts all give clients a reason not to pay. Get the invoice right first time.

### Offer convenient payment methods

The easier it is to pay, the faster it happens. Online payment links, card payments, and direct debit options all reduce friction.

### Automate reminders

Automated payment reminders sent before and after the due date consistently improve collection rates. Accounting software like [Relentify](/accounting) can send these automatically, removing the manual effort of chasing.

### Require deposits for new clients

Until a client has established a payment track record with you, requiring a deposit or using shorter payment terms reduces your risk.

### Tighten terms for late payers

If a client consistently pays late on Net 30, change their terms to Net 14 or even require payment upfront. Your terms should reflect the reality of the relationship.

### Consider factoring

Invoice factoring (selling your receivables to a third party at a discount) converts your aged debtors into immediate cash. It costs money, but if cash flow is critical, it can be worthwhile.

## The aged debtors report and your accountant

Share your aged debtors report with your accountant at least quarterly. They can advise on:

- Bad debt provisions for your accounts
- Tax implications of writing off bad debts
- Whether your collection processes are adequate
- Benchmarking your DSO against industry norms

The aged debtors report is not just an accounting document — it is a management tool. Used properly, it keeps your cash flowing, your clients accountable, and your business financially healthy. Ignore it, and you are flying blind on one of the most important aspects of your finances.
