Houses in Multiple Occupation present unique challenges for property inventories. Unlike a standard rental where one tenant or household is responsible for the entire property, an HMO has multiple tenants, each with their own room and shared access to communal areas. This creates complications around who is responsible for what, how to document shared spaces, and how to handle deposit deductions when communal areas are damaged.

Getting the inventory right is essential. Without clear documentation of each room and every communal space, sorting out responsibility at the end of a tenancy becomes a matter of guesswork.

## What makes HMO inventories different

### Multiple tenants, overlapping responsibility

In a standard let, the tenant or tenants are jointly responsible for the entire property. In an HMO, each tenant has exclusive use of their own room and shared use of communal areas. This creates a split in responsibility:

- **Individual rooms**: the occupying tenant is solely responsible
- **Communal areas**: all tenants share responsibility, which makes attribution of damage more difficult

### Rolling tenancies

HMO tenancies often start and end at different times. One tenant may check out while four others remain. This means inventories need to be conducted for individual rooms without disrupting the other tenants, and communal areas need to be assessed in the context of multiple occupancies.

### Higher turnover

Many HMOs, particularly those catering to students or young professionals, experience higher turnover than standard lets. This means more check-ins, more check-outs, and more frequent inventory inspections.

### Furnished rooms

Most HMO rooms are furnished (bed, desk, wardrobe, chest of drawers as a minimum), adding to the inventory workload for each room.

## Structuring the HMO inventory

The most practical approach is to treat the HMO as two linked inventories:

1. **Individual room inventories** — one for each lettable room
2. **Communal area inventory** — covering all shared spaces

### Individual room inventories

Each room should be documented independently, as if it were a self-contained unit. The inventory should include:

**Room identification**: Room number or name (e.g., "Room 3" or "First Floor Front Left"), clearly linked to the tenancy agreement.

**Room condition**: Standard room-by-room assessment — walls, ceiling, floor, door, window, light fittings, switches, sockets, radiator.

**Furniture and contents**: Every item provided by the landlord — bed frame, mattress, desk, chair, wardrobe, drawers, shelving, bedside table, lamp. Each item described with material, colour, and condition.

**En-suite bathroom** (if applicable): Full bathroom inventory as you would for any property — bath or shower, toilet, sink, tiles, mirrors, extractor fan.

**Photography**: Every element photographed, including close-ups of any pre-existing damage.

When a tenant checks out, only their room inventory is used for comparison. This keeps the assessment focused and fair — the tenant is only judged against conditions in their own space.

### Communal area inventory

The communal areas should be inventoried as a single, comprehensive document that covers all shared spaces:

**Kitchen**: This is usually the most detailed section. Document:
- Units, worktops, splashback, flooring — condition
- All appliances — fridge, freezer, oven, hob, microwave, washing machine, dishwasher (if shared)
- Shared kitchen contents — crockery, cutlery, pots, pans, utensils (noting quantities)
- Individual allocated storage (if applicable) — shelves, cupboards, fridge compartments

**Living room** (if provided): Furniture, soft furnishings, television, condition of all elements.

**Bathrooms** (shared): Full bathroom inventory for each shared bathroom.

**Hallways and stairs**: Flooring, walls, banisters, light fittings, smoke alarms.

**External areas**: Garden, bins, bike storage, drying areas.

**Utility areas**: Boiler location, fuse box, meters.

## Handling the communal area challenge

The most difficult aspect of HMO inventories is attributing responsibility for communal area damage. When five tenants share a kitchen and the hob has burn marks at the end of one tenant's tenancy, who is responsible?

### Approaches to communal area deductions

**Pro-rata deductions**: Some landlords and agents divide communal area deductions equally among all tenants. This is the simplest approach but can feel unfair — the tenant who never uses the kitchen pays the same as the one who caused the damage.

**Evidence-based attribution**: If there is clear evidence that a specific tenant caused the damage — for example, a mid-tenancy inspection identified the damage after a particular tenant moved in — the deduction can be attributed to that individual. This requires good documentation.

**Communal area deposit**: Some HMO operators take a separate communal area deposit from each tenant, used specifically for shared space deductions. This ring-fences the communal costs from individual room deposits.

**Cleaning charges**: Professional cleaning of communal areas between tenancies is often charged pro-rata to outgoing tenants. Including a cleaning clause in the tenancy agreement and documenting the cleanliness standard at check-in makes this more defensible.

### Mid-tenancy inspections are critical

For HMOs, regular mid-tenancy inspections of communal areas are even more important than in standard lets. They create a timeline of the property's condition that helps attribute responsibility when tenants leave at different times.

If the kitchen was in good condition at the six-month inspection and a tenant leaves at month eight with the kitchen in poor condition, the inspection report narrows the window and strengthens the case for deduction.

## Practical tips for HMO inventories

### Number your rooms clearly

Each room should have a clear, consistent identifier that matches the tenancy agreement, the inventory, and any door signage. "Room 3" is unambiguous. "The big room on the left at the top of the stairs" is not.

### Create a master template

Build a standard room template that covers a typical HMO bedroom (and en-suite if applicable). Customise it for each room's specific features, but start from the same base. This ensures consistency and speeds up the process.

### Inventory each room at its own check-in and check-out

Do not try to inventory all rooms at once unless all tenants are starting simultaneously. Each room should be inventoried when its occupant checks in and checked out when they leave.

### Update the communal inventory periodically

At each individual check-in or check-out, also update the communal area inventory. This creates regular snapshots that track changes over time. If you inspect the communal areas every time a tenant changes, you build a comprehensive timeline.

### Photograph shared appliances inside and out

Shared appliances — especially ovens, fridges, and washing machines — are common sources of disputes in HMOs. Photograph them thoroughly at every inspection, including interiors.

### Record allocated storage

If tenants are allocated specific cupboards, fridge shelves, or storage areas, note these allocations in the inventory. This helps attribute condition issues to specific tenants.

## Managing HMO inventory at scale

If you manage multiple HMOs with high tenant turnover, the volume of inventory work can be significant. A six-room HMO with quarterly inspections and average one-year tenancies generates roughly 18 inspections per year (6 check-ins, 6 check-outs, 6 mid-tenancy) plus communal area updates.

Digital inventory tools become essential at this scale. The alternative — managing all of this with paper forms, separate photo files, and word-processed reports — is unworkable at scale and prone to errors that cost money in deposit disputes.

## The bottom line

HMO inventories are more complex than standard lets, but the principles are the same: document everything, photograph everything, and maintain a clear record of condition at every transition point. The additional complexity of shared spaces and overlapping tenancies makes thorough documentation even more important, not less.

Get the process right, and your HMO inventories will protect both you and your tenants through every tenancy change.
