The flat rate VAT scheme is one of HMRC's simplified accounting options designed to make life easier for small businesses. Instead of calculating VAT on every individual transaction, you pay a fixed percentage of your gross turnover to HMRC.

It sounds straightforward, and for many businesses it is. But the flat rate scheme is not always the cheapest option, and understanding how it works in detail is essential before you sign up.

## How the flat rate scheme works

Under the standard VAT scheme, you calculate VAT in two parts:

1. **Output VAT** — The VAT you charge your customers (typically 20%)
2. **Input VAT** — The VAT you pay on your business purchases

You pay HMRC the difference: output VAT minus input VAT.

Under the flat rate scheme, you ignore this calculation entirely. Instead, you apply a fixed percentage to your gross turnover (including VAT) and pay that amount to HMRC. You still charge your customers the standard 20% VAT on your invoices, but you do not reclaim VAT on most of your purchases.

The flat rate percentage depends on your business type. HMRC publishes a table of rates for different industries, ranging from 4% to 16.5%.

## The flat rate percentages

Here are some common examples:

| Business type | Flat rate % |
|---------------|------------|
| Accountancy or bookkeeping | 14.5% |
| Advertising | 11% |
| Computer and IT consultancy | 14.5% |
| Hairdressing or beauty | 13% |
| Management consultancy | 14% |
| Photography | 11% |
| Pub or bar | 6.5% |
| Restaurants or cafes | 12.5% |
| Retail (food) | 4% |
| Retail (non-food) | 7.5% |
| Social work | 11% |
| Transport or storage | 10% |

If your business does not fit neatly into one category, HMRC has guidance on which category to use. If you perform multiple types of work, you use the rate for the activity that generates the most turnover.

### The first-year discount

New VAT-registered businesses get a 1% discount on their flat rate percentage for the first year. So if your rate is 14.5%, you would pay 13.5% in year one.

## Who can join?

To use the flat rate scheme, you must:

- Be VAT-registered
- Have a VAT taxable turnover of £150,000 or less (excluding VAT) in the next 12 months
- Not already be using the margin scheme or capital goods scheme

You must leave the scheme if your total business income (including VAT and non-VAT income) exceeds £230,000 in any 12-month period.

## The limited cost trader rule

In 2017, HMRC introduced the "limited cost trader" category. If your expenditure on goods (not services) is less than either:

- 2% of your VAT-inclusive turnover, or
- £1,000 per year (if your costs of goods are more than 2%)

...then you are classified as a limited cost trader and must use a flat rate of 16.5%, regardless of your industry.

This rule was introduced to prevent businesses with very low costs (like many consultancies and service businesses) from benefiting too much from the flat rate scheme. Before this rule, some businesses were paying significantly less VAT under the flat rate than they would under the standard scheme.

The limited cost trader rule effectively makes the flat rate scheme less attractive for pure service businesses with minimal goods purchases.

### What counts as "goods"?

For the limited cost trader calculation, goods include:

- Physical products you sell
- Materials and components
- Office supplies and stationery
- Fuel for business vehicles

Goods do not include:

- Services of any kind
- Capital assets (equipment, machinery worth over £2,000)
- Food or drink for yourself or your staff
- Vehicle costs other than fuel

## Is the flat rate scheme worth it?

### When the flat rate scheme saves money

The scheme tends to be beneficial when:

- **Your expenses are low** and your flat rate percentage is significantly below 20%. For example, a pub paying 6.5% on turnover is paying much less than 20% of the value-added portion.
- **You sell to consumers** who cannot reclaim VAT anyway, so the VAT you charge is a genuine cost to them and the difference between what you charge and what you pay HMRC is your benefit.
- **Simplicity has value to you** — The time saved on VAT accounting may be worth more than any marginal cost difference.

### When the flat rate scheme costs more

The scheme tends to be more expensive when:

- **You are a limited cost trader** — At 16.5%, you are paying nearly as much as you would under the standard scheme, without the ability to reclaim input VAT.
- **You have significant VATable expenses** — Under the standard scheme, you would reclaim VAT on all your purchases. Under the flat rate, you cannot (with the exception of capital goods over £2,000 including VAT).
- **Your profit margins are thin** — Businesses with tight margins and high costs generally do better under the standard scheme.

### Running the numbers

The only way to know for certain is to calculate both ways:

**Standard scheme calculation:**
- VAT charged on sales: £120,000 x 20% = £24,000
- VAT paid on purchases: £40,000 x 20% = £8,000
- Net VAT payable: £24,000 - £8,000 = £16,000

**Flat rate calculation (14.5%):**
- Gross turnover including VAT: £144,000
- Flat rate payment: £144,000 x 14.5% = £20,880

In this example, the standard scheme is cheaper by £4,880. But if the business had very low expenses:

**Flat rate with low expenses:**
- Gross turnover including VAT: £144,000
- Flat rate payment: £144,000 x 14.5% = £20,880
- Standard scheme: £24,000 - £1,000 = £23,000

Now the flat rate saves £2,120.

## The simplicity advantage

Even when the flat rate scheme costs slightly more than the standard scheme, many businesses choose it for the simplicity:

- No need to track VAT on every purchase
- Simpler VAT returns
- Less time spent on bookkeeping
- Fewer errors

If the difference is a few hundred pounds per year, the time you save might make it worthwhile. Modern accounting software like [Relentify](/accounting) supports both standard and flat rate VAT schemes, making the administration easier either way. But if simplicity is your primary concern, the flat rate scheme reduces the number of things you need to get right.

## How to join and leave

### Joining

You can apply to join the flat rate scheme:

- When you register for VAT (include it on your registration form)
- At any time after registration (apply through your VAT online account or by post)

HMRC will confirm your flat rate percentage based on your business type.

### Leaving

You can leave the flat rate scheme at any time by writing to HMRC. You must leave if:

- Your total business income exceeds £230,000 in any 12-month period
- You are no longer eligible for any other reason

When you leave, you move to the standard VAT scheme from the start of the next VAT period.

## Practical tips

### Review annually

Run the numbers each year to check whether the flat rate scheme is still the best option for you. Your expenses may change, your turnover may grow, or your business type may shift.

### Track goods separately

Even under the flat rate scheme, you need to track your expenditure on goods to determine whether the limited cost trader rule applies. If you are close to the 2% threshold, a few additional purchases could move you out of the 16.5% category.

### Claim capital goods separately

Under the flat rate scheme, you can still reclaim VAT on individual capital purchases of £2,000 or more including VAT. This is a valuable exception that many businesses overlook.

### Get the category right

Using the wrong flat rate category can lead to under or overpayment. If your business activities change, check whether your category is still correct.

The flat rate scheme is a useful tool for the right businesses. But it is not a universal time-saver, and the limited cost trader rule has reduced its appeal for many service businesses. Run the numbers, understand the rules, and make an informed choice.
