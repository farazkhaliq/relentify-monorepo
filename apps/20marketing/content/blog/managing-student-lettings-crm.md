Student lettings occupy a unique niche in the property market. The tenancy cycle is seasonal and predictable — most tenancies run from September to June, with a concentrated period of marketing and sign-ups in the preceding months. Properties are often shared, meaning multiple tenants on a single agreement or multiple individual agreements for a single property. Guarantors are almost always required. And turnover is guaranteed — by definition, students move on.

For letting agencies that specialise in or include student properties in their portfolio, these characteristics create specific operational challenges. A CRM configured for student lettings helps manage the volume, the timing, and the complexity in a way that generic processes cannot.

## The seasonal cycle

The student lettings calendar is front-loaded. Most properties need to be marketed and let months before the tenancy starts. In many markets, students begin searching for next year's accommodation before the current academic year is half over.

This creates a compressed marketing window. If your properties are not listed and viewable early enough, you miss the wave of demand. If your referencing and contract process is slow, you lose applicants to faster competitors.

A CRM supports this by automating the annual cycle. As the current academic year progresses, the system can trigger marketing preparation tasks — professional photography, listing creation, pricing review — at predetermined dates. When the previous year's tenancy ends, the re-marketing process is already underway, minimising void periods during the summer.

## Group tenancy management

Student properties are frequently let to groups — four students sharing a house, six students in an HMO. Managing group tenancies is more complex than managing individual lets because each decision involves multiple people.

Your CRM should handle group tenancies natively. This means linking multiple tenants to a single property and tenancy, tracking each tenant's referencing and documentation individually, and recording communications with both the group and individual members.

When one member of a group drops out — a common occurrence — the CRM should make it easy to update the tenancy, process a replacement tenant's referencing, and adjust the agreement without starting from scratch.

## Guarantor management

Almost every student tenancy requires a guarantor — typically a parent or guardian who agrees to cover the tenant's obligations if they default. Managing guarantors adds a layer of administration that does not exist in most residential lettings.

Each guarantor needs to be referenced, their guarantee needs to be signed, and their contact details need to be stored against the relevant tenant record. If rent goes into arrears, the guarantor is the first point of contact after the tenant.

Your CRM should store guarantor records linked to the tenant they are guaranteeing. When rent is overdue, the automated arrears workflow should include a step for contacting the guarantor — because in student lettings, the guarantor is often more responsive than the student.

## High-volume processing

A student lettings agency might process fifty or a hundred new tenancies in a single month. Each one requires marketing, viewings, applications, referencing, guarantor processing, contracts, deposit protection, and check-in.

At this volume, any manual step that takes five minutes becomes a full day of work when repeated across all tenancies. Automation is not optional — it is essential.

Automated workflows for student tenancy setup should include every step from application to move-in, with tasks assigned to the appropriate team members and deadlines calculated from the tenancy start date. Batch operations — sending referencing requests to twenty applicants at once, generating thirty tenancy agreements in a single run — save hours compared to processing each tenancy individually.

## HMO compliance

Many student properties are Houses in Multiple Occupation, which carry additional regulatory requirements. HMO licensing, fire safety compliance, room size regulations, and maximum occupancy limits all need to be monitored.

Your CRM should flag HMO properties and track the specific compliance requirements that apply to them. Licence expiry dates, fire safety inspection records, and room measurements should be recorded and monitored with the same rigour as standard compliance certificates.

For agencies managing a mix of student HMOs and standard residential properties, the CRM should differentiate between the two and apply the appropriate compliance checks to each.

## Summer void management

The academic calendar creates a predictable void period over the summer months. While the property is empty, maintenance work, deep cleaning, and property inspections need to be scheduled.

A CRM can automate the summer void process — generating tasks for end-of-tenancy inspections, scheduling contractors for maintenance and cleaning, and triggering the marketing process for the upcoming academic year.

For landlords, the summer void is an expected cost of student lettings. Your CRM should track the length and cost of each void period, providing landlords with accurate data for their financial planning.

## Deposit management at scale

Student tenancies involve a high volume of deposit transactions. Deposits need to be collected, protected within the required timeframe, and returned at the end of the tenancy — minus any deductions for damage or cleaning.

At scale, managing deposits manually is error-prone. A CRM that tracks deposit amounts, protection dates, and scheme references for each tenancy ensures that every deposit is handled correctly. Automated reminders ensure that deposits are protected within the required timeframe and that end-of-tenancy deposit returns are processed promptly.

## Communication with students

Students communicate differently from other tenant demographics. They are more likely to use messaging apps than email, more responsive to text messages than phone calls, and more comfortable with self-service portals than face-to-face interactions.

Your CRM should support the communication channels that students actually use. An online portal where students can report maintenance issues, view their tenancy details, and communicate with the agency reduces inbound calls and meets students' expectations for digital-first service.

## Repeat landlord management

Student property landlords are often highly engaged — they understand the market, they know the cycle, and they have clear expectations about yield, void periods, and property condition. Managing their expectations requires data.

Your CRM should provide each landlord with clear performance data for their properties — rent achieved, void period duration, maintenance costs, and tenant feedback. This data-driven approach builds confidence and supports the long-term relationship.

Many student property landlords own multiple properties. Your CRM should make it easy to manage portfolio landlords — showing all their properties in a single view, with consolidated financial summaries and compliance status across the portfolio.

## The efficiency imperative

Student lettings is a volume business. Margins per property are often tight, and profitability depends on managing a large number of tenancies efficiently. Every manual step that can be automated, every process that can be streamlined, and every error that can be prevented has a direct impact on the bottom line.

The agencies that succeed in student lettings are the ones that turn the seasonal, high-volume nature of the market from a challenge into a competitive advantage — through systems, automation, and relentless process improvement.
