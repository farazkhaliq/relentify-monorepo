Most businesses can tell you their total payroll cost. Far fewer can tell you what each project actually cost in labour. This gap between aggregate data and project-level data is where profitability hides — or leaks away unnoticed.

If you run a service business, a professional practice, a trades company, or any operation where staff time is allocated to specific projects or clients, understanding cost per project is not optional. It determines your pricing, informs your resource allocation, and reveals which work is worth pursuing and which is quietly destroying your margins.

This guide explains how to use timesheet data to calculate accurate staff costs per project and what to do with that information once you have it.

## Why project costing matters

### Pricing accuracy

If you do not know what past projects cost, your pricing for future projects is guesswork. You might win work because your prices are too low (and lose money delivering it) or lose work because your prices are too high (based on inflated assumptions). Historical cost data brings your estimates into line with reality.

### Profitability analysis

Revenue minus cost equals profit. If you know the revenue from a project but not the cost, you cannot calculate the profit. A project that billed well might have been barely profitable — or loss-making — once labour costs are properly allocated.

### Resource allocation

When you know which projects consume the most labour hours, you can allocate resources more effectively. You might decide to automate part of a high-cost process, assign less experienced (and less expensive) staff to routine tasks, or decline low-margin work that disproportionately consumes senior time.

### Client negotiations

When a client asks for a discount or disputes a fee, project cost data gives you a factual basis for the conversation. You can demonstrate what the work actually cost to deliver, rather than defending a price that feels arbitrary.

## The calculation

The basic formula for staff cost per project is straightforward:

**Staff cost = Total hours worked on the project x Fully loaded hourly rate for each worker**

The challenge is in the details.

### Step 1: Capture hours by project

Your timesheet system must allow workers to log time against specific projects or clients. This might be:

- A project code selected at clock-in
- A time allocation entered after the fact (e.g., "Today I spent four hours on Project A and four on Project B")
- Automatic allocation based on site (for businesses where each client site is a separate project)

The method depends on your business model, but the principle is the same: every hour must be attributed to a project.

Tools like [Relentify](/timesheets) allow workers to clock in against specific sites or projects, creating a direct link between time worked and the project it was worked on.

### Step 2: Determine the fully loaded hourly rate

The cost of an hour of labour is not just the worker's wage. The fully loaded rate includes all employment costs:

- **Gross pay**: The worker's hourly wage or salary divided by working hours
- **Employer taxes and contributions**: Social security, national insurance, payroll taxes
- **Pension contributions**: Employer's pension match
- **Benefits**: Health insurance, company car, phone allowance, etc.
- **Holiday pay**: The cost of paid leave spread across working hours
- **Sick pay**: The expected cost of paid sick leave
- **Training**: Investment in professional development
- **Overheads allocation** (optional): A share of office costs, management, and administration

A common rule of thumb is that the fully loaded cost is 1.3 to 1.5 times the gross pay, depending on the jurisdiction and benefit structure. For a worker earning the equivalent of $25 per hour, the fully loaded rate might be $33 to $38.

Calculate this rate for each worker or worker category (if workers at the same grade have similar costs).

### Step 3: Multiply and aggregate

For each project, multiply each worker's hours by their fully loaded rate, then sum the results:

| Worker | Hours on project | Loaded rate | Cost |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|------|
| Alice | 40 | $35/hr | $1,400 |
| Bob | 25 | $28/hr | $700 |
| Carol | 10 | $45/hr | $450 |
| **Total** | **75** | | **$2,550** |

This gives you the labour cost of the project. Compare it to the revenue earned (or the budget allocated) to understand profitability.

### Step 4: Add non-labour costs

For a complete project cost, add direct non-labour costs:

- Materials and supplies
- Subcontractor fees
- Travel and expenses
- Software or licensing costs specific to the project
- Equipment hire

Labour is usually the largest component, but a full picture requires all direct costs.

## Using the data

### Compare estimate to actual

For every project, compare the estimated labour cost (from your quote or proposal) to the actual labour cost (from timesheet data). If actuals consistently exceed estimates, your pricing is too low or your estimates are too optimistic. If actuals are consistently below estimates, you may be over-pricing (or your team is more efficient than you assumed).

### Identify margin by project type

Group projects by type (e.g., new installations versus maintenance, large clients versus small) and compare average margins. You may find that certain project types are consistently more profitable than others. This informs business development — pursue the profitable work, renegotiate or decline the loss-makers.

### Analyse labour mix

The blend of senior and junior staff on a project significantly affects cost. A project staffed primarily with senior workers will cost more per hour than one using a mix of grades. If quality requirements allow, shifting some tasks to less expensive staff improves margins without reducing output.

### Track efficiency over time

As your team gains experience with a particular type of project, efficiency should improve. Timesheet data lets you verify this: is the average labour cost per similar project declining over time? If not, investigate why.

### Inform client billing

If you bill on a time-and-materials basis, timesheet data feeds directly into invoices. If you bill on a fixed-fee basis, the data tells you whether the fixed fee is viable. A fixed-fee project that consistently costs more to deliver than it generates in revenue needs to be repriced or restructured.

## Common challenges

### Workers who do not log time by project

If workers are not disciplined about allocating time to projects, the data is useless. Make project-level time recording mandatory, not optional. Choose a system that makes it easy — a dropdown menu at clock-in, a tag added to the timesheet, or automatic allocation based on site.

### Shared time

Some hours cannot be cleanly attributed to a single project — team meetings, training, administrative tasks, travel between sites. Decide how to handle this: allocate it proportionally across active projects, charge it to a general overhead code, or exclude it from project costing entirely. Whatever you choose, be consistent.

### Incomplete data

If some workers use the system religiously and others do not, your project costs will be understated for projects worked on by the non-compliant workers. Enforce consistent use across the team.

### Changing scope

Projects rarely go exactly to plan. Scope changes, rework, and client-requested additions all increase labour hours. Track these separately if possible (using a different project code for change orders), so you can distinguish between planned costs and scope-related overruns.

## Building the habit

Project costing is not a one-time exercise. Its value compounds over time as you build a database of actual costs that informs future estimates, pricing decisions, and resource planning.

The habit starts with consistent time recording against projects. Once that data is flowing, the analysis follows naturally — compare actual to estimate, analyse margins, identify patterns, and adjust. Each project costed accurately makes the next project's estimate more reliable.

For businesses that sell time — directly or indirectly — this is not a back-office activity. It is the core mechanism for understanding what the business produces, what it costs to produce, and what it should charge.
