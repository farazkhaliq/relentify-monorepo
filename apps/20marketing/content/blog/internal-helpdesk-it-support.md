Every organisation, regardless of size, has IT support needs. Laptops fail, software needs configuring, passwords get forgotten, printers refuse to cooperate, and new starters need their accounts set up. How you handle these requests makes a real difference to employee productivity and satisfaction.

Many companies start with an informal approach. Employees walk over to the IT person's desk, send a Slack message, or fire off an email. This works when you have ten employees and one IT person. It does not scale. As your organisation grows, the informal approach leads to lost requests, uneven response times, duplicated effort, and frustrated employees who feel their issues are being ignored.

An internal helpdesk brings structure to IT support without making it bureaucratic. Done well, it makes life easier for both the people requesting help and the people providing it.

## Why You Need a Formal IT Helpdesk

The case for an internal helpdesk is straightforward. Without one, you have no visibility into how many requests your IT team handles, how long issues take to resolve, what the most common problems are, or whether your team is meeting reasonable service expectations.

With a helpdesk, every request is logged, assigned, tracked, and resolved in a documented process. This creates accountability, ensures nothing falls through the cracks, and generates data that helps you improve your IT operations over time.

Employees benefit too. Instead of wondering whether anyone received their message about a broken laptop, they can see the status of their ticket, any updates from the IT team, and an estimated resolution time. Transparency reduces frustration even when the fix takes time.

## Choosing the Right Helpdesk Platform

Your internal helpdesk does not need to be a separate, specialised tool. A [multi-channel helpdesk platform](/connect) that handles both internal and external support needs can serve both purposes, reducing the number of tools your organisation manages.

Key features to look for in an internal helpdesk include ticket management with categories and priorities, assignment rules that route tickets to the right team member, SLA tracking to ensure timely responses, a knowledge base for self-service, and reporting dashboards.

Integration with your existing tools matters. If your organisation uses email, Slack, or Microsoft Teams for communication, your helpdesk should accept tickets from these channels so employees can report issues through the tools they already use.

## Designing Your Ticket Categories

A well-designed category structure helps both employees and IT staff. Employees can quickly identify the right category for their issue, and IT staff can prioritise and route tickets efficiently.

Common categories for an internal IT helpdesk include hardware issues, software installation and configuration, network and connectivity problems, account access and passwords, email issues, printer problems, new starter setup, leaver account deactivation, and general enquiries.

Keep categories broad enough to be useful without being overwhelming. Ten to fifteen categories is usually sufficient. If employees cannot decide which category to choose, they will either pick randomly or avoid the system entirely.

## Setting Service Level Agreements

SLAs define how quickly your IT team commits to responding to and resolving different types of issues. Not all IT requests are equally urgent, and your SLAs should reflect this.

A practical SLA structure might include three tiers. Critical issues — such as a system-wide outage or a security breach — require a response within fifteen minutes and resolution within four hours. High-priority issues — such as an employee unable to work due to a hardware failure — require a response within one hour and resolution within one business day. Standard issues — such as a software installation request or a non-urgent configuration change — require a response within four hours and resolution within three business days.

Be realistic with your SLAs. Promising response times you cannot consistently deliver erodes trust faster than having slightly longer but reliable commitments.

## Building a Self-Service Knowledge Base

A significant percentage of IT support requests are for common, well-documented issues. Password resets, VPN setup, printer installation, software update instructions, and Wi-Fi connection guides are examples of issues that can be resolved without any IT staff involvement.

Build a knowledge base of articles that address these common issues with clear, step-by-step instructions. Include screenshots where they add clarity. Write for a non-technical audience — your knowledge base users are employees who need their problem solved, not IT professionals.

Promote your knowledge base prominently. When employees submit a ticket, suggest relevant articles before the ticket is created. Over time, a well-maintained knowledge base can deflect thirty to fifty percent of tickets, freeing your IT team to focus on complex issues that genuinely need their expertise.

## Automating Common Workflows

Certain IT processes follow predictable patterns that can be automated. New starter onboarding is a prime example. When a new employee joins, they need accounts created, software installed, hardware allocated, and access permissions configured. This can be managed through a standardised workflow that triggers automatically when HR confirms a new start date.

Similarly, when an employee leaves, a leaver workflow can ensure their accounts are deactivated, their devices are collected, and their access is revoked promptly. Automating these workflows reduces the risk of security gaps and ensures consistency.

Password reset requests, software approval workflows, and hardware replacement processes are other candidates for automation.

## Ticket Routing and Assignment

How tickets are assigned affects both resolution speed and team workload balance. Several approaches work well for internal IT helpdesks.

Round-robin assignment distributes tickets evenly across your team. This ensures a fair workload distribution but does not account for individual expertise.

Skills-based routing assigns tickets to the team member with the most relevant expertise. Hardware issues go to your hardware specialist, network problems go to your network engineer, and software queries go to your application support person.

For smaller teams, manual assignment by a team lead may be sufficient. The lead reviews incoming tickets, assesses priority, and assigns them to the appropriate person.

Whatever approach you choose, ensure it is transparent. Employees should be able to see who is handling their ticket, and IT staff should have clear visibility of their queue.

## Escalation Procedures

Not every ticket can be resolved at the first level. Some issues require specialist knowledge, vendor involvement, or management approval. Clear escalation procedures ensure that complex issues are handled efficiently.

Define when and how tickets should be escalated, who has authority to escalate, and what information needs to be included when a ticket is passed to a higher level. Automatic escalation rules can trigger when a ticket breaches its SLA, ensuring that overdue issues get additional attention.

## Measuring Performance

Your helpdesk generates valuable data about your IT operations. Track metrics that help you understand and improve your service.

Ticket volume trends show whether your IT demand is growing and help with capacity planning. Average response and resolution times indicate whether you are meeting your SLAs. First-contact resolution rate measures how often issues are resolved without escalation. Customer satisfaction scores from post-resolution surveys capture the employee experience.

The most common ticket categories reveal where your biggest IT challenges lie. If twenty percent of your tickets are password resets, investing in a self-service password reset tool could dramatically reduce your workload.

## Communication and Transparency

Keep employees informed about the status of their tickets. Automated notifications when a ticket is received, assigned, updated, and resolved set expectations and reduce follow-up enquiries.

For major incidents that affect multiple employees, proactive communication is essential. A status page or broadcast message that acknowledges the issue and provides regular updates prevents a flood of duplicate tickets and reassures affected employees that the problem is being addressed.

## Getting Employee Buy-In

The best helpdesk system in the world is useless if employees bypass it and continue walking to the IT desk or sending direct messages. Getting buy-in requires a combination of making the system easy to use and consistently routing requests through it.

Make ticket submission as frictionless as possible. If employees can submit a ticket by sending an email, typing a message in Slack, or filling in a brief form, they are more likely to use the system.

When employees approach IT staff directly, politely ask them to log a ticket. Explain that the system exists to ensure their issue is tracked and resolved, not to create bureaucracy. Once employees see that the helpdesk provides faster, more reliable support than informal channels, adoption follows naturally.

## Starting Small and Scaling

You do not need to implement every feature on day one. Start with basic ticket logging, assignment, and status tracking. Add your knowledge base once you have identified the most common issues. Implement SLAs and automation as your processes mature.

A [helpdesk platform](/connect) that grows with your needs allows you to start simple and add complexity as your organisation and IT team evolve. The goal is always the same: ensure every IT issue is captured, tracked, and resolved efficiently, keeping your employees productive and your IT team focused.
