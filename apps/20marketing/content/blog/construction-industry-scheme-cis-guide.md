The Construction Industry Scheme (CIS) is a set of rules from HMRC that governs how contractors in the construction industry pay subcontractors. Under CIS, contractors must deduct money from subcontractors' payments and pass it to HMRC. These deductions count as advance payments towards the subcontractor's tax and National Insurance.

If you work in construction — whether as a contractor who hires subcontractors or as a subcontractor who works for contractors — CIS directly affects how you get paid and how you handle your tax.

## Who does CIS apply to?

### Contractors

Under CIS, a "contractor" is not just a builder. You are classed as a contractor if you:

- Pay subcontractors for construction work, and your average annual spending on construction operations exceeds £3 million, OR
- Carry out construction work as part of your main business activity

This includes construction companies, property developers, housing associations, and even some businesses outside the construction industry that spend significant amounts on construction work.

### Subcontractors

A subcontractor is anyone who does construction work for a contractor. This includes:

- Self-employed individuals
- Partnerships
- Limited companies

The key is the relationship: if you are paid by a contractor to carry out construction work, you are a subcontractor under CIS, regardless of your business structure.

### What counts as construction work?

CIS covers most construction operations, including:

- Building and civil engineering work
- Alterations, repairs, extensions, and demolition
- Installing heating, lighting, power, and water systems
- Painting and decorating
- Cleaning (as part of construction)
- Landscaping (if part of a construction contract)

It does not cover:

- Architecture and surveying (professional services)
- Scaffold hire (without erection)
- Carpet fitting
- Delivery of materials
- Manufacturing components off-site

## CIS deduction rates

When a contractor pays a subcontractor, they must make a deduction and send it to HMRC. The rate depends on the subcontractor's registration status:

| Status | Deduction rate |
|--------|---------------|
| Registered for CIS | 20% |
| Not registered for CIS | 30% |
| Gross payment status | 0% |

### Gross payment status

Subcontractors who meet certain criteria can register for gross payment status, meaning no deductions are made. To qualify, you must:

- Be registered with HMRC for CIS
- Have a good tax compliance record (filed returns and paid tax on time)
- Meet a minimum turnover threshold (£30,000 for individuals, £30,000 per partner, or £30,000 per director)

Gross payment status is reviewed annually by HMRC. If your compliance record deteriorates, it can be removed.

## How CIS works in practice

### For contractors

**Step 1: Register as a contractor**
Register with HMRC as a CIS contractor before you take on your first subcontractor.

**Step 2: Verify the subcontractor**
Before paying any subcontractor for the first time, you must verify them with HMRC. This tells you their deduction rate (20%, 30%, or gross). You can verify online through HMRC's system.

**Step 3: Make the deduction**
When you pay the subcontractor, deduct the appropriate percentage from the labour element of the payment. CIS deductions apply to labour costs only — materials are not subject to deduction (but the subcontractor must provide evidence of material costs).

**Step 4: Provide a deduction statement**
Give the subcontractor a written statement showing the gross amount, the deduction made, and the net amount paid. This is their record for their own tax return.

**Step 5: Submit monthly returns**
File a monthly CIS return with HMRC by the 19th of each month, reporting all payments and deductions made in the previous tax month.

**Step 6: Pay HMRC**
Pay the deductions to HMRC by the 19th of each month (22nd if paying electronically).

### For subcontractors

**Step 1: Register for CIS**
Register with HMRC as a subcontractor. Without registration, you will have 30% deducted instead of 20%. If you qualify, apply for gross payment status.

**Step 2: Invoice the contractor**
Invoice for your work, separating labour and materials. Materials should be clearly identified and evidenced so the contractor can exclude them from the CIS deduction.

**Step 3: Receive payment with deduction**
The contractor pays you the net amount after deducting CIS. You receive a deduction statement showing the amount deducted.

**Step 4: Claim the deductions**
The deductions are advance payments of your tax. When you file your tax return (self-assessment for sole traders, corporation tax for companies), the CIS deductions are offset against your tax liability. If deductions exceed your tax liability, you can claim a refund.

## CIS and your accounts

### Recording CIS as a contractor

In your accounts, the gross payment to the subcontractor is recorded as an expense. The CIS deduction is recorded as a liability (money you owe to HMRC). When you pay HMRC, the liability is cleared.

Accounting software like [Relentify](/accounting) can track CIS deductions automatically, calculate the correct amounts, and generate the monthly returns you need to file with HMRC.

### Recording CIS as a subcontractor

Record the gross amount as your income (not just the net amount received). The CIS deduction is an asset (a prepayment of tax). When you file your tax return and offset the deductions, the asset is cleared.

It is important to record the gross amount as income. If you only record the net amount, you will understate your income and then not claim the full deduction — resulting in overpaying tax.

## Common CIS mistakes

### Not verifying subcontractors

Contractors must verify every subcontractor with HMRC before making the first payment. Failing to verify means you should deduct 30%, and if you deduct less without verification, you may be liable for the difference.

### Deducting from materials

CIS deductions only apply to the labour element. If a subcontractor's invoice includes 800 for labour and 200 for materials, the CIS deduction applies to the 800, not the full 1,000. But the materials must be properly evidenced.

### Late monthly returns

Monthly CIS returns are due by the 19th of each month. Late filing results in automatic penalties: £100 for each month late, per 50 subcontractors.

### Subcontractors not claiming deductions

Some subcontractors forget to offset their CIS deductions against their tax bill, effectively paying the tax twice. Always include CIS deductions on your tax return.

### Not separating labour and materials on invoices

Subcontractors should clearly separate labour and materials on their invoices. If everything is lumped together, the contractor may deduct CIS on the full amount, resulting in a higher deduction than necessary.

## CIS for limited companies

CIS applies to limited companies as well as sole traders and partnerships. If your company acts as a subcontractor, the contractor must still make CIS deductions (unless you have gross payment status). These deductions are offset against your company's corporation tax or PAYE liability.

If your company acts as a contractor, you must register, verify, deduct, and report just like any other contractor.

## Deemed contractors

Some organisations outside the construction industry are classed as "deemed contractors" because they spend heavily on construction work. This includes:

- Government departments
- Local authorities
- Housing associations
- Large businesses spending over £3 million on construction in a rolling 3-year period

If you are a subcontractor working for these organisations, CIS applies even though they are not construction companies.

## Penalties for non-compliance

HMRC takes CIS compliance seriously. Penalties include:

- **Late returns** — £100 per month late, per 50 subcontractors
- **Incorrect returns** — Penalties for inaccurate information
- **Late payment** — Interest on late payment of CIS deductions
- **Failure to register** — Penalties for contractors and subcontractors who fail to register

The best way to avoid penalties is to register correctly, verify subcontractors before paying them, file returns on time, and pay deductions promptly. Good accounting software and a reliable accountant make compliance straightforward.

CIS adds administrative overhead to construction businesses, but it is a legal requirement. Set up your processes correctly from the start, automate what you can, and treat the monthly return as a routine task rather than a last-minute scramble.
