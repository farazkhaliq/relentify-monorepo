The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) establishes the rules for minimum wage, overtime pay, and record keeping in the United States. At the center of FLSA compliance is the distinction between exempt and non-exempt employees — a classification that determines whether an employee is entitled to overtime pay.

Getting this classification wrong is one of the most expensive employment law mistakes a US business can make. Misclassifying a non-exempt employee as exempt can result in back pay, liquidated damages, and attorney's fees. This guide explains the rules clearly so you can classify your employees correctly.

## The basic rule

Under the FLSA, non-exempt employees must be paid overtime at a rate of one and one-half times their regular rate of pay for all hours worked over 40 in a workweek.

Exempt employees are not entitled to overtime pay, regardless of how many hours they work. They receive a fixed salary for the job, not hourly compensation.

**The default is non-exempt.** Every employee is presumed to be non-exempt (and therefore entitled to overtime) unless they meet specific criteria for an exemption. The burden of proving an exemption applies falls on the employer.

## The three tests for exemption

To classify an employee as exempt from overtime under the most common FLSA exemptions, the employee must meet all three of the following tests:

### 1. Salary basis test

The employee must be paid on a salary basis, meaning they receive a predetermined, fixed amount of compensation each pay period that is not subject to reduction based on the quality or quantity of work performed.

**Key points:**
- The salary must be paid regardless of whether the employee works a partial week (with limited exceptions)
- Deductions from salary for partial-day absences generally violate the salary basis test
- Permissible deductions include full-day absences for personal reasons, full-day absences for sickness (if the employer has a bona fide sick leave plan), and certain disciplinary suspensions

### 2. Salary level test

The employee must earn at least the minimum salary threshold set by the Department of Labor. This threshold is updated periodically, so check the current DOL guidance for the most recent figure.

The salary level test applies to the standard exemptions (executive, administrative, professional). The threshold has been increased several times in recent years and may continue to be adjusted.

**Note:** Highly compensated employees earning above a higher annual threshold may qualify for exemption under a more relaxed duties test, provided they customarily and regularly perform at least one of the duties of an exempt executive, administrative, or professional employee.

### 3. Duties test

The employee's primary duties must meet the criteria for one of the recognized exemptions. This is typically the most complex and contested part of the analysis.

## The major exemption categories

### Executive exemption

An employee qualifies for the executive exemption if their primary duty is managing the enterprise or a recognized department, they customarily and regularly direct the work of two or more full-time employees (or the equivalent), and they have the authority to hire or fire (or their recommendations on hiring and firing carry particular weight).

**Common roles:** Department managers, store managers, team leaders with genuine management authority.

**Common misclassification:** Employees with "manager" in their title who primarily perform the same work as their subordinates. Title alone does not determine exemption — actual duties do.

### Administrative exemption

An employee qualifies for the administrative exemption if their primary duty is office or non-manual work directly related to the management or general business operations of the employer or the employer's customers, and their duties include the exercise of discretion and independent judgment with respect to matters of significance.

**Common roles:** HR managers, finance professionals, marketing managers, compliance officers, executive assistants with significant decision-making authority.

**Key concept — "directly related to management or general business operations":** This includes areas like finance, accounting, budgeting, quality control, purchasing, advertising, marketing, research, legal, regulatory compliance, and similar functions. It does not include production work or day-to-day tasks that are part of the employer's core product or service delivery.

**Common misclassification:** Administrative assistants and coordinators whose work is routine and clerical rather than involving genuine independent judgment on significant matters.

### Professional exemption

This category covers two types of professionals:

**Learned professionals:** Employees whose primary duty requires advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning, customarily acquired through prolonged specialized intellectual instruction. The knowledge must be intellectual in character and not merely routine.

**Creative professionals:** Employees whose primary duty requires invention, imagination, originality, or talent in a recognized field of artistic or creative endeavor.

**Common roles:** Doctors, lawyers, engineers, architects, scientists, teachers, writers, composers.

**Common misclassification:** Technicians or para-professionals who apply established techniques rather than exercising advanced knowledge or creative judgment.

### Computer employee exemption

Employees working as computer systems analysts, computer programmers, software engineers, or similar roles may be exempt if their primary duties consist of:
- Systems analysis
- Design, development, documentation, analysis, creation, testing, or modification of computer programs
- Design, documentation, testing, creation, or modification of machine operating systems
- A combination of these duties

This exemption has both a salary threshold and an alternative hourly rate threshold.

**Common misclassification:** Help desk technicians, IT support staff, and hardware technicians whose work is primarily troubleshooting and maintenance rather than systems analysis or software development.

### Outside sales exemption

The outside sales exemption applies to employees whose primary duty is making sales or obtaining orders or contracts, and who customarily and regularly work away from the employer's place of business. This exemption does not have a salary level requirement.

## How to classify your employees

### Step 1: List every position

Create a list of every distinct role in your organization.

### Step 2: Apply the salary tests

Does the employee earn at least the required salary threshold on a salary basis? If no, they are non-exempt regardless of their duties.

### Step 3: Analyze actual duties

Look at what the employee actually does on a day-to-day basis, not their job title or job description. The duties test is based on primary duties — meaning the most important duty or the one that consumes the most time.

### Step 4: Document your analysis

For each position classified as exempt, document the basis for the classification — which exemption applies, how the duties test is satisfied, and the salary level. This documentation is your evidence if the classification is ever challenged.

## Record keeping requirements

Employers must maintain accurate records for non-exempt employees, including:
- Hours worked each workday and total hours worked each workweek
- Basis on which wages are paid (hourly rate, salary, piece rate, etc.)
- Total daily or weekly straight-time earnings
- Total overtime compensation for each workweek
- Total wages paid each pay period
- Dates of payment and pay period covered

## Common overtime pitfalls

### Automatic deductions for lunch breaks

If non-exempt employees are required to remain on-call or available during lunch, that time may be compensable. Automatically deducting 30 or 60 minutes for lunch without confirming employees actually took an uninterrupted break can create unpaid overtime liability.

### Off-the-clock work

Emails checked after hours, work done before clocking in, and calls taken during breaks are all potentially compensable time for non-exempt employees. If you know or should know that an employee is working, the time must be recorded and paid.

### Rounding practices

Some employers round time to the nearest quarter hour. This is permissible only if the rounding practice does not consistently favor the employer. Over time, rounding must be neutral.

### Averaging hours over multiple weeks

Under the FLSA, overtime is calculated on a weekly basis. You cannot average hours over a two-week pay period to avoid overtime. If an employee works 50 hours one week and 30 hours the next, they are owed 10 hours of overtime for the first week — even though the average is 40.

### State law differences

Many states have overtime rules that are more protective than the FLSA. Some require daily overtime (overtime for hours worked over eight in a single day), have lower salary thresholds for exemption, or provide additional protections. You must comply with both federal and state law, applying whichever is more favorable to the employee.

## Responding to misclassification

If you discover that an employee has been misclassified as exempt, take corrective action promptly:

1. Reclassify the employee as non-exempt going forward
2. Implement time tracking for the employee
3. Calculate any back overtime owed based on hours actually worked
4. Consult with an employment attorney about your liability and the best approach to remediation
5. Review all exempt classifications to identify other potential misclassifications

Proactive correction demonstrates good faith, which can be relevant in reducing penalties if a claim is filed.

## The bottom line

Exempt vs. non-exempt classification is not about job titles, seniority, or how employees prefer to be paid. It is a legal determination based on salary and duties that must be evaluated carefully for every position. When in doubt, classify as non-exempt. The cost of paying overtime when due is always less than the cost of defending a misclassification claim after the fact.
