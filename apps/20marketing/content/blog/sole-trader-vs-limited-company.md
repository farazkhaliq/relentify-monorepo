One of the first decisions any new business owner faces is how to structure their business. The two most common options are trading as a sole trader or forming a limited company. Each has distinct advantages and drawbacks, and the right choice depends on your circumstances, your appetite for admin, and your plans for growth.

This guide breaks down the key differences so you can make an informed decision.

## What is a sole trader?

A sole trader is the simplest form of business structure. You and the business are legally the same entity. You keep all the profits, but you are also personally responsible for all the debts.

Setting up as a sole trader is straightforward. You register with the tax authority, start trading, and file a self-assessment tax return each year. There is minimal paperwork, no requirement to file public accounts, and you have complete control over every decision.

## What is a limited company?

A limited company is a separate legal entity from its owner. It has its own identity, can enter into contracts in its own name, and — critically — limits the liability of its shareholders to the amount they have invested.

Forming a limited company involves registering with the companies authority, filing annual accounts, and maintaining certain statutory records. There is more administrative overhead than a sole trader, but there are also significant financial and practical benefits.

## Tax differences

This is often the deciding factor for many business owners.

**Sole traders** pay income tax on all business profits, plus national insurance contributions (or the equivalent social security contributions in other jurisdictions). As profits increase, so does your tax rate — and at higher levels, the marginal rate can be substantial.

**Limited companies** pay corporation tax on profits, which is typically lower than the higher rates of income tax. As a director, you can pay yourself a combination of salary and dividends. Dividends are taxed at lower rates than salary, which means you can often take more money home for the same gross profit.

However, the tax advantage is not automatic. For very low profits, the cost and complexity of running a limited company may outweigh the tax savings. As a rough guide, many accountants suggest that the tax benefits of incorporating become meaningful once your profits consistently exceed a certain threshold — often in the range of 30,000 to 50,000 in your local currency.

## Liability protection

As a sole trader, there is no legal distinction between you and your business. If your business incurs debts it cannot pay, creditors can pursue your personal assets — your home, your savings, your car.

A limited company provides a layer of protection. If the company fails, your liability is generally limited to the value of your shares and any personal guarantees you have given. Your personal assets are protected.

This protection is not absolute. Directors who act fraudulently, negligently, or in breach of their duties can still be held personally liable. And many lenders require personal guarantees from directors of small companies, which reduces the practical benefit of limited liability.

Still, for most business owners, the separation of personal and business liability is a significant comfort.

## Administrative requirements

**Sole traders** have minimal admin obligations. You need to keep records of your income and expenses, file an annual tax return, and register for VAT if your turnover exceeds the threshold. That is essentially it.

**Limited companies** have more extensive requirements:

- Annual accounts must be filed with the companies authority
- A corporation tax return must be filed separately
- A confirmation statement must be submitted each year
- If you pay yourself a salary, you need to run payroll
- Statutory records must be maintained (register of directors, shareholders, etc.)

The additional admin is manageable with good systems in place. Modern accounting software handles most of the heavy lifting — from payroll calculations to generating the reports your accountant needs. ## Professional perception

Limited companies are generally perceived as more established and professional. Some clients — particularly larger organisations and government bodies — prefer or even require their suppliers to be limited companies.

If you are a freelancer working primarily with individuals or small businesses, this may not matter. But if your target market includes corporates or public sector organisations, being a limited company can open doors that might otherwise remain closed.

## Privacy considerations

Sole traders have relatively little public disclosure. Your business name and VAT number may be visible, but your financial details remain private.

Limited company accounts are filed publicly. Anyone can look up your company on the register and see your annual turnover, profit, assets, and liabilities. Directors' names and registered office addresses are also publicly available.

If privacy is important to you, this is worth considering. You can mitigate some of this by using a virtual registered office address and filing abbreviated or micro-entity accounts where permitted.

## Flexibility and growth

A limited company structure makes it easier to:

- **Bring in investors or partners** by issuing shares
- **Transfer ownership** of the business
- **Set up employee share schemes** to attract talent
- **Apply for certain grants and funding** that are only available to incorporated businesses

If you plan to grow the business beyond yourself, a limited company gives you more options.

## Which should you choose?

**Choose sole trader if:**

- You are just starting out and want to keep things simple
- Your expected profits are modest (below the threshold where incorporation saves tax)
- You are testing a business idea and do not want to commit to the admin overhead
- You work in a low-risk industry where personal liability is minimal

**Choose limited company if:**

- Your profits are above the threshold where corporation tax savings become meaningful
- You want to protect your personal assets from business debts
- You work with clients who expect or require suppliers to be incorporated
- You plan to grow the business, take on partners, or seek investment
- You want more flexibility in how you pay yourself (salary plus dividends)

## Can you switch later?

Yes. Many business owners start as sole traders and incorporate later when their business reaches the point where the benefits outweigh the costs. The transition involves registering a new company, transferring your business assets, and informing your clients and suppliers of the change.

It is not a complicated process, but it does require some planning — particularly around transferring contracts and ensuring continuity of your banking and insurance arrangements.

## The cost comparison

**Sole trader costs:**
- Registration: Free in most jurisdictions
- Annual tax return filing: Can be done yourself or through an accountant (typical cost varies by complexity)
- Accounting software: From free to modest monthly fees

**Limited company costs:**
- Registration: A small fee (varies by jurisdiction)
- Annual accounts preparation and filing: Typically requires an accountant (cost varies)
- Corporation tax return: Usually handled by your accountant
- Accounting software: Depends on the platform and plan

The additional cost of running a limited company is real, but for businesses with healthy profits, the tax savings usually more than offset the extra expense. And with modern all-in-one platforms that include accounting, invoicing, payroll, and tax reporting in a single subscription, the per-tool cost is lower than it has ever been.

## Making the decision

There is no universally right answer. The best structure depends on your specific circumstances — your income level, your industry, your growth plans, and your tolerance for paperwork.

If you are unsure, speak to an accountant. A good accountant can model the tax implications of each structure based on your actual numbers and help you make a decision grounded in facts rather than assumptions.

What matters most is that you make the decision deliberately rather than by default. Too many business owners remain sole traders long past the point where incorporation would save them money, simply because they never got around to making the switch. Equally, some incorporate too early and spend money on admin that their modest turnover does not yet justify.

Take the time to understand the trade-offs, get professional advice if you need it, and choose the structure that best serves your business today — with an eye on where you want to be tomorrow.
