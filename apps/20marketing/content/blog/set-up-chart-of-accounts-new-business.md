Every accounting system, from a simple spreadsheet to enterprise software, is built on a chart of accounts. It is the backbone of your financial records — the categorisation system that determines how every transaction is classified and reported.

For a new business, setting up your chart of accounts correctly from the start saves enormous headaches later. Get it right and your bookkeeping flows naturally. Get it wrong and you will spend hours reclassifying transactions and trying to make sense of inaccurate reports.

## What is a chart of accounts?

A chart of accounts (COA) is a complete list of every account your business uses to record financial transactions. Think of it as a filing system for money. Every time money comes in or goes out, it gets assigned to an account in your COA.

Each account falls into one of five main categories:

1. **Assets** — What your business owns (bank accounts, equipment, money owed to you)
2. **Liabilities** — What your business owes (loans, credit cards, taxes payable)
3. **Equity** — The owner's stake in the business (capital, retained earnings)
4. **Revenue** — Money earned from business activities (sales, service income)
5. **Expenses** — Costs of running the business (rent, utilities, salaries, supplies)

These five categories are universal. Whether you run a consultancy, a shop, or a construction firm, your chart of accounts will contain accounts in all five categories.

## Why does it matter?

Your chart of accounts directly affects:

- **Your profit and loss statement** — Revenue and expense accounts feed into this
- **Your balance sheet** — Asset, liability, and equity accounts feed into this
- **Tax reporting** — Your expense categories need to align with what you can claim
- **Business decisions** — If your accounts are too vague, you cannot see where money is going

A well-structured COA gives you clarity. A poorly structured one gives you confusion.

## The numbering system

Most accounting systems use a numbering convention to organise accounts:

| Range | Category | Examples |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 1000-1999 | Assets | Bank accounts, accounts receivable, equipment |
| 2000-2999 | Liabilities | Accounts payable, loans, tax payable |
| 3000-3999 | Equity | Owner's capital, retained earnings |
| 4000-4999 | Revenue | Sales, service income, interest received |
| 5000-5999 | Cost of goods sold | Materials, direct labour |
| 6000-6999 | Operating expenses | Rent, utilities, insurance |
| 7000-7999 | Other expenses | Depreciation, bank fees |

The exact numbering can vary, but the principle is consistent: lower numbers for the balance sheet (assets, liabilities, equity) and higher numbers for the income statement (revenue, expenses).

Leaving gaps in your numbering is deliberate. If your bank accounts start at 1000, you might use 1000 for your main account, 1010 for a savings account, and 1020 for a petty cash account. The gaps allow you to add new accounts later without disrupting the structure.

## Setting up your chart of accounts: step by step

### Step 1: Start with a template

You do not need to build your chart of accounts from scratch. Most accounting software comes with a default template based on your business type and location. These templates include the standard accounts most businesses need.

Modern accounting platforms like [Relentify](/accounting) provide sensible defaults that you can customise. Starting with a template and adjusting it is far more efficient than building from zero.

### Step 2: Customise for your business

The default template will cover the basics, but every business has unique needs. Consider:

- **What products or services do you sell?** If you have multiple revenue streams, create separate revenue accounts for each (e.g., "Consulting Income" and "Training Income" rather than just "Sales")
- **What are your major expense categories?** Break expenses into categories that are meaningful for your business
- **Do you need to track cost of goods sold?** If you sell physical products, you need COGS accounts separate from operating expenses
- **What assets do you own?** Equipment, vehicles, property — each needs its own account

### Step 3: Keep it simple but specific

This is the most important balance to strike. Your chart of accounts should be:

- **Specific enough** to give you useful information
- **Simple enough** that you (or your bookkeeper) will actually use it correctly

A common mistake is creating too many accounts. If you have 15 different expense categories for office supplies, stationery, printer ink, paper, pens, and envelopes, you are over-engineering it. A single "Office Supplies" account is usually sufficient.

On the other hand, lumping everything into "General Expenses" tells you nothing. You cannot manage what you cannot measure.

A good rule of thumb: if you would make a different business decision based on the breakdown, it deserves its own account. If not, combine it.

### Step 4: Align with tax requirements

Your expense accounts should align with the categories your tax authority expects. This makes tax filing dramatically easier because your accounting reports map directly to your tax return.

For example, if your tax system has a specific deduction for vehicle expenses, having a dedicated "Vehicle Expenses" account means you can pull that figure instantly at tax time rather than sifting through a generic "Travel" account.

### Step 5: Set up sub-accounts where needed

Sub-accounts (sometimes called child accounts) let you add detail without cluttering the main view. For example:

- **Travel** (parent)
  - Mileage (sub-account)
  - Rail fares (sub-account)
  - Hotels (sub-account)
  - Flights (sub-account)

Your profit and loss report shows the "Travel" total, but you can drill down into the sub-accounts when you need more detail. This keeps your high-level reports clean while retaining granularity.

## A sample chart of accounts for a small service business

Here is a practical starting point:

### Assets (1000-1999)
- 1000 — Business Current Account
- 1010 — Business Savings Account
- 1020 — Petty Cash
- 1100 — Accounts Receivable
- 1200 — Prepaid Expenses
- 1500 — Computer Equipment
- 1510 — Office Furniture
- 1600 — Accumulated Depreciation

### Liabilities (2000-2999)
- 2000 — Accounts Payable
- 2100 — Credit Card
- 2200 — Tax Payable
- 2300 — Loan

### Equity (3000-3999)
- 3000 — Owner's Capital
- 3100 — Owner's Drawings
- 3200 — Retained Earnings

### Revenue (4000-4999)
- 4000 — Service Income
- 4100 — Interest Received
- 4200 — Other Income

### Expenses (5000-6999)
- 5000 — Cost of Sales
- 6000 — Rent
- 6010 — Utilities
- 6020 — Insurance
- 6030 — Office Supplies
- 6040 — Phone and Internet
- 6050 — Travel
- 6060 — Marketing and Advertising
- 6070 — Professional Fees (accountant, legal)
- 6080 — Subscriptions and Software
- 6090 — Bank Charges
- 6100 — Depreciation
- 6110 — Training and Development
- 6120 — Repairs and Maintenance

This gives you roughly 30 accounts, which is a sensible size for a small service business. You can always add more as your business grows.

## Common mistakes to avoid

### Too many accounts

If your chart of accounts has hundreds of entries, it becomes unmanageable. Every transaction requires a decision about which of 200 accounts to use, and inconsistency creeps in. Start small and add accounts only when you genuinely need the detail.

### Too few accounts

On the flip side, a chart of accounts with just "Income" and "Expenses" tells you nothing useful. You need enough granularity to understand your business.

### Inconsistent naming

Keep your naming conventions consistent. If you use "Phone and Internet" as one account, do not create a separate "Telecommunications" account later. Merge them or pick one name and stick with it.

### Not reviewing periodically

Your chart of accounts should evolve with your business. Review it at least once a year. Deactivate accounts you no longer use, add new ones for new activities, and consolidate accounts that are too granular.

### Mixing personal and business transactions

This is a bookkeeping issue rather than a COA issue, but it is worth mentioning. If personal expenses end up in your business accounts, your financial reports are meaningless. Keep business and personal finances separate.

## When to get help

If you are unsure about how to structure your chart of accounts, a brief consultation with an accountant can be invaluable. They can advise on:

- Which accounts you need for your specific industry
- How to align with tax reporting requirements
- Whether your structure supports the reports you need

An hour with an accountant at the start can save dozens of hours of reclassification later.

## The bottom line

Your chart of accounts is not glamorous, but it is foundational. A well-structured COA makes every other aspect of your bookkeeping easier — from day-to-day data entry to year-end reporting to tax filing.

Take the time to set it up properly when you start your business. Use a sensible template, customise it for your needs, and keep it simple enough to use consistently. Your future self will thank you.
