Overtime is one of those costs that creeps up on businesses. A few extra hours here, a weekend shift there — individually small, but collectively significant. Without proper tracking, overtime becomes invisible until it appears on the payroll report, at which point it is too late to manage.

This guide explains how to set up overtime tracking in your timesheet system, how overtime calculations typically work, and how to keep overtime under control without stifling the flexibility your business needs.

## Why overtime tracking matters

Overtime is expensive. In most jurisdictions and employment contracts, overtime hours are paid at a premium — typically 1.25 to 2 times the standard hourly rate. Even where overtime is paid at the standard rate, it still represents labour cost above what was budgeted.

Beyond the direct cost, uncontrolled overtime creates secondary problems:

- **Compliance risk**: Working time regulations set maximum weekly hours. Exceeding them exposes the business to legal action and penalties.
- **Worker fatigue**: Consistent overtime is a leading indicator of burnout, reduced productivity, and increased error rates.
- **Budget overruns**: If project costs or client fees are based on standard-hour estimates, untracked overtime erodes margins silently.
- **Unfair distribution**: Without visibility, overtime tends to concentrate on the most willing or available workers rather than being distributed equitably.

## How overtime calculations work

The specifics vary by jurisdiction and contract, but overtime calculations generally follow one of these patterns:

### Daily overtime

Overtime is triggered when a worker exceeds a daily hour threshold. For example, any hours worked beyond eight in a single day are paid at the overtime rate. This is common in industries with standard shift lengths.

### Weekly overtime

Overtime is triggered when total weekly hours exceed a threshold — often 40 or 48 hours. Daily hours are irrelevant; what matters is the weekly total. This is common where workers have variable daily schedules.

### Combined daily and weekly

Some contracts or regulations apply both: daily overtime for shifts exceeding a set length, and weekly overtime for total hours exceeding the weekly limit. The higher rate applies where both are triggered.

### Tiered rates

Some overtime structures have multiple tiers. For example:
- Hours 1–40: Standard rate
- Hours 41–48: 1.25x rate (time and a quarter)
- Hours 49+: 1.5x rate (time and a half)

### Weekend and holiday premiums

Some contracts specify enhanced rates for weekends, public holidays, or night shifts. These may stack with overtime rates or replace them, depending on the contract terms.

## Setting up overtime in your timesheet system

### Define your rules

Before configuring anything, document your overtime rules clearly:

- What triggers overtime? (daily threshold, weekly threshold, or both)
- What rates apply? (flat premium, tiered rates, weekend/holiday rates)
- Do different worker types have different rules? (full-time versus part-time, different contracts)
- Is overtime pre-approved or retrospectively identified?

### Configure thresholds

Set up your timesheet system with the relevant thresholds. For most systems, this means specifying:

- The standard daily hours (e.g., 8 hours)
- The standard weekly hours (e.g., 40 hours)
- The overtime rate or multiplier

The system should then automatically flag hours that exceed these thresholds and apply the correct rate when generating reports or exports.

Platforms like [Relentify](/timesheets) allow you to configure overtime rules per site or per worker group, so different contracts or locations can have different thresholds without creating separate systems.

### Set up alerts

Configure the system to send alerts when workers approach overtime thresholds. A notification to the manager when a worker reaches 36 of their 40 standard hours gives the manager time to adjust the remaining schedule and avoid unplanned overtime.

### Require approval for overtime

Make overtime a conscious decision rather than an afterthought. The simplest approach is to require manager approval before any overtime is worked. In practice, this might mean:

- The system flags any shift that will push a worker into overtime
- The manager receives a notification and either approves or adjusts the schedule
- Approved overtime is tagged in the timesheet so it can be tracked and reported separately

For businesses where overtime is routine (seasonal peaks, project deadlines), a blanket approval for a defined period may be more practical than per-shift approvals.

## Tracking overtime effectively

### Real-time visibility

The most important capability is real-time visibility into overtime across the business. Managers should be able to see, at any point during the week:

- Which workers have reached or exceeded their standard hours
- Which workers are approaching the threshold
- The total overtime hours and cost for the current period

This dashboard view turns overtime from a payroll surprise into a manageable variable.

### Historical reporting

Beyond real-time tracking, you need reports that show overtime trends over time:

- **Overtime by worker**: Which individuals are consistently working overtime? Is it voluntary, necessary, or a sign of understaffing?
- **Overtime by site**: Which locations generate the most overtime? This helps identify scheduling problems or demand mismatches.
- **Overtime by period**: Is overtime seasonal, cyclical, or trending upward? This informs staffing and budget decisions.
- **Overtime cost**: What is the total financial impact of overtime in a given period? How does it compare to the budget?

### Separating overtime from standard hours in payroll

When exporting timesheet data for payroll, overtime hours should be clearly separated from standard hours so that the correct rates can be applied. Many payroll systems require separate line items for standard and overtime pay. Your timesheet export should accommodate this.

## Controlling overtime costs

Tracking overtime is the first step. Controlling it requires a combination of scheduling discipline, staffing decisions, and cultural change.

### Schedule proactively

Most overtime results from poor scheduling — shifts that are understaffed, schedules that do not account for demand peaks, or a failure to plan for absences. Reviewing historical timesheet data and adjusting schedules based on actual demand patterns is the most effective way to reduce avoidable overtime.

### Address root causes

If the same workers or sites generate overtime week after week, the issue is structural, not incidental. Common root causes include:

- **Understaffing**: The headcount is simply too low for the workload. Hiring an additional worker may cost less than paying ongoing overtime premiums.
- **Skill gaps**: Only a few workers can perform certain tasks, so they end up working extra hours while others cannot help.
- **Poor handovers**: Shifts run over because workers need extra time to hand over to the next team. Improving the handover process can eliminate this overtime.
- **Client scope creep**: The work required at a client site has expanded beyond the original contract, but staffing levels have not been adjusted.

### Set an overtime budget

Allocate a specific overtime budget for each period and track spending against it. When the budget is exhausted, any further overtime requires senior management approval. This creates a natural constraint that encourages managers to find alternatives.

### Cross-train workers

The more workers who can perform any given task, the more scheduling flexibility you have. Cross-training reduces dependence on specific individuals and makes it easier to cover gaps without resorting to overtime.

## Legal considerations

Working time regulations in most jurisdictions cap weekly hours — often at 48 hours averaged over a reference period (typically 17 weeks). Workers may opt out of this limit in some countries, but the employer still has a duty of care.

Additionally, some jurisdictions require overtime to be compensated at premium rates by law, not just by contract. Check your local regulations and ensure your timesheet system is configured to track and report on working time limits as well as overtime pay.

Accurate overtime records are essential evidence in any dispute. If a worker claims they were required to work excessive hours, your timesheet records should be able to confirm or refute the claim definitively.

## Summary

Overtime is a useful tool when managed deliberately — it provides flexibility to handle peaks, deadlines, and unexpected demand. It becomes a problem when it is invisible, unmanaged, and habitual.

A timesheet system that tracks overtime automatically, alerts managers proactively, and reports trends clearly transforms overtime from a hidden cost into a visible, controllable variable. That visibility is the foundation of effective workforce cost management.
