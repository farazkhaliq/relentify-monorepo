Every manager will eventually face a disputed timesheet. A worker claims they worked hours that the system does not show. A manager believes a timesheet overstates the hours actually worked. The clock-in time does not match the schedule, and both parties have a different explanation.

These disputes are uncomfortable but manageable — provided you have a clear process, good records, and a commitment to fairness. Handle them well, and you reinforce trust. Handle them poorly, and you risk grievances, legal claims, and a team that loses faith in the system.

This guide provides a practical framework for resolving timesheet disputes as a manager.

## Why disputes happen

Understanding the common causes helps you resolve disputes more effectively and prevent future ones.

### System errors

Technology is not infallible. A mobile app might not register a clock-out. GPS might drift and place the clock-in outside the geofence. The system might crash during a busy period, losing data. These are genuine errors that require correction, not investigation.

### Honest mistakes

Workers forget to clock in, clock out at the wrong time, or record the wrong site. These are not fraudulent — they are the natural consequence of busy, distracted people interacting with a system that requires precise actions at specific moments.

### Misunderstandings

A worker believes that their commute counts as working time. A manager assumes that early arrival means early clock-in. Expectations about what "counts" as working time can differ, and these differences surface as disputes.

### Genuine disagreements

In some cases, the worker and manager simply disagree about what happened. The worker says they stayed until 6pm; the manager believes they left at 5:30pm. Without objective evidence, these disputes become a matter of credibility.

### Deliberate manipulation

Occasionally, a timesheet is deliberately inflated or falsified. This is the least common cause but the most serious, and it requires a different response than the others.

## A step-by-step resolution process

### Step 1: Gather the evidence

Before forming any conclusion, collect all available data:

- **The disputed timesheet entry** (times, dates, location)
- **GPS data** (if available) showing where the worker was at clock-in and clock-out
- **System logs** showing any edits or anomalies
- **The worker's account** of what happened
- **The manager's or supervisor's account** (if they were present)
- **Colleague observations** (where appropriate and relevant)
- **Schedule data** showing what was planned

The quality of your evidence determines how quickly and fairly the dispute can be resolved. This is where a digital timesheet system with GPS verification and audit logs proves its value — it provides objective data that neither party can retroactively alter.

### Step 2: Speak to the worker

Give the worker an opportunity to explain the discrepancy. Approach the conversation as an inquiry, not an accusation:

- "I noticed your timesheet shows you clocked in at 7:00 but your shift starts at 8:00. Can you help me understand?"
- "The GPS data shows your clock-out was from a different location than the site. Can you explain?"
- "You have recorded eight hours but the shift was scheduled for six. Was there additional work that needed to be done?"

Listen to the response. Most disputes have a straightforward explanation.

### Step 3: Assess the situation

Based on the evidence and the worker's explanation, categorise the dispute:

**System error:** The evidence supports a technical problem. Correct the record and move on. If the error recurs, investigate the technical cause.

**Honest mistake:** The worker forgot to clock in/out or made an error. Correct the record based on the best available information. No disciplinary action needed, but remind the worker of the process.

**Misunderstanding:** The worker genuinely believed the time was recordable (e.g., travel time, preparation time). Clarify the policy, correct the record if necessary, and document the clarification.

**Genuine disagreement:** The evidence is inconclusive. Make a reasonable judgment, document the decision and the reasoning, and inform the worker.

**Deliberate manipulation:** The evidence strongly suggests intentional falsification. This is a disciplinary matter and should be handled through your formal disciplinary process, not informally.

### Step 4: Correct the record

If the timesheet needs to be amended, make the correction in the system with a clear note explaining what was changed, why, and who authorised the change. This audit trail is important — it demonstrates that corrections are made transparently, not secretly.

### Step 5: Communicate the outcome

Tell the worker what the corrected record shows and why. If you are overriding their submission, explain the evidence that supports the correction. If you are accepting their submission, confirm that. Either way, the worker should not be left wondering what was decided.

### Step 6: Prevent recurrence

After resolving the dispute, consider whether a systemic change would prevent similar disputes in future:

- Do workers need clearer instructions about what counts as working time?
- Does the clock-in process need simplification to reduce forgotten entries?
- Are GPS geofences too tight, causing legitimate clock-ins to be flagged?
- Does the approval process need strengthening to catch issues before they become disputes?

## Tips for handling disputes well

### Act promptly

Address disputes as soon as they are identified — ideally within the same pay period. Delays allow memories to fade, evidence to become less clear, and resentment to build.

### Be consistent

Apply the same standards to every worker. If one worker's late clock-in is excused, every similar case should be treated the same way. Inconsistency undermines the process and invites claims of favouritism.

### Document everything

Keep a written record of every dispute, the evidence reviewed, the worker's explanation, and the outcome. This documentation protects both the business and the worker if the dispute escalates.

### Separate the investigation from the disciplinary process

If you suspect deliberate fraud, do not combine the fact-finding conversation with a disciplinary meeting. Gather the evidence first, then follow your formal disciplinary procedure if the evidence warrants it.

### Use objective data

GPS coordinates, system timestamps, and audit logs are more reliable than memories and assumptions. Build your resolution on objective data wherever possible. If your [timesheet system](/timesheets) provides this data, use it.

### Keep it proportionate

A five-minute discrepancy does not warrant the same response as a three-hour fabrication. Match the seriousness of your response to the seriousness of the issue.

## Preventing disputes in the first place

The best dispute is one that never happens. You can reduce the frequency of disputes by:

- **Making clock-in and clock-out easy** — the simpler the process, the fewer mistakes
- **Sending reminders** — automatic prompts for workers who have not clocked in by their scheduled start time
- **Defining working time clearly** — publish a clear policy on what counts (and what does not)
- **Reviewing timesheets promptly** — catch anomalies during the approval step, before they become pay disputes
- **Training managers** — ensure approvers understand what they are looking for and how to query entries constructively

## Summary

Timesheet disputes are a normal part of workforce management. They do not need to be adversarial. With a clear process — gather evidence, listen, assess, correct, communicate, prevent — most disputes can be resolved quickly and fairly.

The foundation is good data. A timesheet system that records exact times, verifies locations, and maintains an audit trail provides the objective evidence that turns disputes into straightforward corrections. Without that data, every dispute becomes a credibility contest — and nobody wins those.
