The terms "time tracking" and "timesheet software" are often used interchangeably, but they describe different tools with different purposes. Choosing the wrong one — or not understanding the distinction — can lead to a system that does not quite fit your needs.

This guide explains the differences, where they overlap, and how to decide which approach is right for your business.

## Time tracking software

Time tracking software is designed to measure how time is spent on tasks, projects, or activities. It is primarily used by knowledge workers, freelancers, consultants, and creative professionals who need to understand where their hours go.

**Core features:**
- Start/stop timers for individual tasks
- Project and client categorisation
- Activity logs and productivity reports
- Integration with project management tools
- Often includes idle detection or automatic tracking

**Primary purpose:** Understanding how time is allocated across tasks and projects, supporting billing, and improving productivity.

**Typical users:** Software developers, designers, consultants, lawyers, accountants, marketing agencies, freelancers.

**Example use case:** A graphic designer starts a timer when they begin working on a logo for Client A, stops it when they switch to a website mockup for Client B, and starts a new timer for internal admin. At the end of the day, they have a breakdown of hours by client and task.

## Timesheet software

Timesheet software is designed to record working hours — when employees start and finish work, how long they work, and where they work. It is primarily used by businesses that employ hourly workers, manage shift-based teams, or need to track attendance across multiple locations.

**Core features:**
- Clock-in and clock-out (often mobile)
- GPS verification of location
- Break tracking
- Shift scheduling
- Manager approval workflows
- Payroll export
- Compliance monitoring (overtime, working time limits)

**Primary purpose:** Recording and verifying employee working hours for payroll, compliance, and workforce management.

**Typical users:** Construction companies, cleaning firms, care agencies, security companies, hospitality businesses, staffing agencies, retail, manufacturing.

**Example use case:** A cleaner arrives at a client site at 6am, clocks in on their phone (GPS verifies the location), works until 9am with a 15-minute break, clocks out, and moves to the next site. The manager approves the timesheet, and the hours feed into payroll and client billing.

## Key differences

| Dimension | Time tracking | Timesheet software |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Focus | Tasks and projects | Shifts and attendance |
| Input method | Start/stop timers, manual entry | Clock-in/clock-out |
| Location | Usually not relevant | Often GPS-verified |
| Approval | Often self-managed | Manager-approved |
| Output | Productivity reports, billing | Payroll, compliance records |
| Break tracking | Rarely a focus | Core feature |
| Scheduling | Not included | Often included |
| Worker type | Knowledge workers, freelancers | Hourly, shift-based, field workers |

## Where they overlap

The overlap occurs in businesses where both time allocation and attendance matter. For example:

- **Professional services firms** need to track which client each consultant's hours are billed to (time tracking) and also ensure consultants are not exceeding working time limits (timesheets).
- **Agencies** need to bill clients for worker hours at specific sites (time tracking by project) and also manage attendance, breaks, and payroll (timesheets).
- **Multi-site businesses** need to know how hours are distributed across projects (time tracking) and also verify that workers were on site (timesheets with GPS).

In these cases, the business may need a tool that combines both capabilities or may use two tools that complement each other.

## Which do you need?

### You probably need time tracking if:

- Your workers are salaried professionals
- You bill clients by the hour
- You need to understand time allocation across projects or tasks
- Productivity analysis is more important than attendance verification
- Your workers are office-based or remote, working at a computer

### You probably need timesheet software if:

- Your workers are paid by the hour
- You need to verify attendance at physical locations
- You manage shift-based or field-based workers
- Payroll accuracy and compliance are your primary concerns
- GPS verification is important for your business
- You need manager approval before hours reach payroll

### You might need both if:

- You have a mixed workforce (salaried professionals and hourly workers)
- You bill clients for staff time and also need to track attendance
- You manage both project-based work and shift-based work
- Your business combines knowledge work with field operations

## The practical choice

For most small businesses with hourly or shift-based workers, timesheet software is the right choice. It handles the fundamental requirements — clock-in, clock-out, breaks, approval, payroll export, compliance — and provides the workforce management features that shift-based businesses depend on.

Platforms like [Relentify](/timesheets) are built around this model: mobile clock-in with GPS verification, break tracking, manager approval, and clean data export for payroll. If your primary need is to know when your workers are working, where they are, and how to pay them accurately, this is the category to focus on.

For businesses that primarily need to track how time is allocated across projects and clients — typically knowledge-work and professional services — time tracking software is the better fit.

And for businesses that need both, look for a platform that either combines the two or integrates cleanly with a complementary tool. The worst outcome is two disconnected systems that require manual reconciliation.

## Avoid the common mistake

The most common mistake is choosing a tool based on the wrong category. A construction company that chooses a task-based time tracker designed for software developers will find that it lacks GPS verification, break tracking, and shift management. A marketing agency that chooses workforce timesheet software will find that it lacks project-level time allocation and task timers.

Start with your actual needs — not the features that sound impressive, but the specific problems you need to solve. Then match those needs to the right category of tool. The distinction between time tracking and timesheet software may seem academic, but it determines whether the tool you choose will actually work for your business.
