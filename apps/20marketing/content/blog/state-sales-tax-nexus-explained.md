If you sell products or services across state lines in the United States, one of the most important compliance questions you face is: in which states must I collect sales tax? The answer depends on whether you have "nexus" — a sufficient connection to a state that triggers the obligation to collect and remit its sales tax.

Before 2018, nexus generally required a physical presence. Since the Supreme Court's Wayfair decision, states can also establish nexus based purely on your economic activity within their borders. This change dramatically expanded sales tax obligations for online sellers and remote service providers.

## What is nexus?

Nexus is the legal term for a sufficient connection between a business and a state that gives the state authority to require the business to collect and remit its sales tax. Without nexus, a state cannot compel you to act as its tax collector.

There are two main types of nexus:

### Physical nexus

Physical nexus exists when your business has a tangible presence in a state. This includes:

- **Office, store, or warehouse** — Any physical business location in the state
- **Employees** — Staff who live or work in the state, including remote employees
- **Inventory** — Products stored in the state, including at third-party fulfillment centers
- **Delivery vehicles** — Company-owned vehicles operating in the state
- **Property** — Business equipment or other property located in the state
- **Temporary presence** — Attending trade shows, pop-up shops, or temporary selling events (rules vary by state)
- **Affiliate nexus** — Related entities or independent representatives operating in the state on your behalf

### Economic nexus

Since the Wayfair decision, most states with a sales tax have enacted economic nexus laws. These establish nexus based on your sales volume or number of transactions in the state, regardless of physical presence.

The most common thresholds are:

- **$100,000 in sales** to customers in the state during a calendar year, OR
- **200 transactions** with customers in the state during a calendar year

However, thresholds vary significantly by state:

- Some states use only a dollar threshold (no transaction count)
- Some set the threshold at $250,000 or $500,000
- Some measure on a rolling 12-month basis rather than calendar year
- Some include only taxable sales; others include all sales

You must check each state's specific rules to determine your obligations.

## How to determine your nexus exposure

### Step 1: Identify physical presence

List every state where you have:

- A physical location (including home offices of remote employees)
- Inventory stored (check your fulfillment arrangements)
- Employees or representatives
- Any other physical connection

You have physical nexus in each of these states and must collect their sales tax (if the state has one and your products or services are taxable).

### Step 2: Track sales by state

For states without physical nexus, track your sales revenue and transaction count by state. Compare these against each state's economic nexus threshold.

Your [accounting software](/accounting) should track customer locations and generate reports showing sales by state. Review this data at least quarterly to identify states where you are approaching or have exceeded nexus thresholds.

### Step 3: Monitor changes

State nexus laws change. New legislation, revised thresholds, and court decisions can alter your obligations. Stay informed through professional advisors, industry associations, or sales tax compliance services.

## What to do when nexus is triggered

### Register for a sales tax permit

Once you establish nexus in a state, you must register for a sales tax permit before collecting tax. Apply through the state's department of revenue website. You will need:

- Your business legal name and trade name
- Federal EIN
- Business structure and ownership details
- Estimated sales volume in the state
- Start date for collecting tax

### Determine taxability

Not everything you sell may be taxable in every state. Determine whether your specific products or services are taxable, exempt, or taxed at a reduced rate in each nexus state.

### Configure tax collection

Set up your sales systems to charge the correct sales tax rate for each customer based on their location (for destination-based states) or your location (for origin-based states).

### Begin collecting

Start collecting sales tax from the effective date specified by the state. Do not collect retroactively on past sales unless specifically directed to do so.

### File and remit

File sales tax returns and remit collected tax according to the state's schedule. Filing frequency is usually assigned by the state based on your expected sales volume — monthly, quarterly, or annually.

## Marketplace facilitator laws

If you sell through online marketplaces like Amazon, Etsy, eBay, or Walmart Marketplace, the marketplace may be responsible for collecting and remitting sales tax on your behalf. Most states have enacted marketplace facilitator laws that shift this obligation to the marketplace operator.

However, you should:

- Understand which of your sales channels are covered by marketplace facilitator laws
- Ensure you are not double-collecting tax on marketplace sales
- Still track marketplace sales for nexus threshold calculations (rules vary)
- Continue collecting on direct sales (through your own website) where you have nexus

## Common nexus pitfalls

### Amazon FBA and inventory nexus

If you use Amazon's Fulfilled by Amazon (FBA) service, Amazon distributes your inventory across its fulfillment centers in multiple states. Having inventory in a state creates physical nexus in that state. Many sellers are unaware that using FBA creates nexus in dozens of states simultaneously.

### Remote employees

Hiring a remote employee in a new state creates physical nexus. Before hiring, consider the sales tax implications — you may need to register and begin collecting in that state.

### Trade shows and events

Attending trade shows and selling at events can create temporary physical nexus. Some states have specific rules about how much activity triggers nexus from trade show participation.

### Affiliate programs

If you have affiliates (individuals or businesses who refer customers in exchange for a commission) in certain states, their presence may create affiliate nexus for your business.

## Voluntary disclosure agreements

If you discover that you should have been collecting sales tax in a state but were not, a voluntary disclosure agreement (VDA) is usually the best path forward.

A VDA allows you to:

- Come into compliance with the state proactively
- Typically limits the look-back period (often 3-4 years instead of the full statute of limitations)
- Usually reduces or eliminates penalties
- Establishes a clean starting point for future compliance

VDAs are far preferable to being discovered during an audit, which typically results in the full look-back period and maximum penalties. Consider using a multistate tax professional to manage the VDA process.

## Managing multi-state compliance

### Use automation

With nexus in multiple states, manual sales tax compliance is impractical. Sales tax automation services:

- Determine the correct rate for each transaction based on customer location
- Apply product-specific taxability rules
- File returns across all registered states
- Remit payments on your behalf

### Monitor thresholds continuously

Do not wait until year-end to check nexus thresholds. Monitor monthly so you can register proactively when you approach a threshold.

### Keep organized records

For each nexus state, maintain:

- Sales tax permit and registration details
- Filing schedule and deadlines
- Copies of filed returns
- Payment records
- Exemption certificates received from customers

## Stay proactive

Sales tax nexus is not a set-and-forget compliance item. As your business grows, enters new markets, hires remote workers, or changes fulfillment arrangements, your nexus exposure changes too. Regular review of your state-by-state presence and sales volumes is essential for staying compliant and avoiding unpleasant surprises.
