Every chat conversation is a data point. But without a system for organising those data points, they remain an unstructured pile of transcripts that is difficult to analyse, impossible to report on, and useless for strategic decision-making.

Chat tags solve this by attaching labels to conversations that describe their topic, outcome, or any other characteristic you want to track. With a consistent tagging system, you can answer questions that would otherwise require reading hundreds of transcripts manually. What are visitors asking about most? Which topics generate the lowest satisfaction? What percentage of conversations are pre-sales versus support?

## What are chat tags?

Chat tags are short labels attached to conversations by agents, automated rules, or AI. They describe something about the conversation that you want to track, such as its topic, the visitor's intent, the outcome, or a specific product or service discussed.

A single conversation can have multiple tags. A conversation about upgrading a subscription that results in a successful upsell might be tagged with "billing," "upgrade," and "sale." This multi-tag approach allows you to analyse conversations across multiple dimensions.

## Building a tagging taxonomy

### Start with broad categories

Define five to ten top-level categories that cover the main types of conversations your team handles. Common starting categories include pricing, billing, product features, technical support, account management, onboarding, and feedback.

These broad categories should be mutually exclusive where possible. A conversation should clearly fit into one primary category. If you find conversations that straddle two categories, your categories may be too narrow or too overlapping.

### Add specificity where needed

Within each broad category, you may need more specific tags to capture detail. Under "technical support," you might have tags for "login issues," "integration errors," "performance," and "data export." Under "billing," you might have "payment failed," "refund request," "invoice query," and "plan change."

The level of specificity depends on your reporting needs. Start broad and add detail only when you find yourself needing finer-grained data.

### Keep it manageable

A tagging system with fifty options is a tagging system that nobody uses consistently. Agents facing a long list of tags will either pick the first one that seems close or skip tagging entirely. Aim for a total of fifteen to twenty-five tags across all categories.

If you need more granularity, consider a two-level system where agents select a primary category and an optional sub-tag. This keeps the initial choice simple while allowing for detail when relevant.

### Use clear, consistent naming

Tag names should be self-explanatory. "Pricing query" is clear. "PQ" is not. "Refund request" is specific. "Money" is vague. Avoid abbreviations, jargon, and tags that sound similar to each other.

Document your tags with brief descriptions so that every agent understands exactly when each tag should be used. "Technical support: login issues" means the visitor could not log in to their account. "Technical support: performance" means the visitor reported slow loading or timeouts.

## When to tag conversations

### At conversation close

The most common approach is for agents to tag conversations when they close them. The conversation is complete, the topic is clear, and the agent can select the appropriate tags based on the full context.

Most platforms present the tagging interface as part of the conversation closing workflow, making it a natural step rather than an afterthought.

### During the conversation

For longer or more complex conversations, agents might apply tags during the interaction rather than waiting until the end. This is useful when the conversation spans multiple topics or when tags are used for routing or priority purposes.

### Automatically

Some platforms support automatic tagging based on keywords, page context, or AI analysis. A conversation that mentions "invoice" and "payment" might be automatically tagged with "billing." A conversation initiated on the pricing page might be tagged with "pre-sales."

Automatic tagging reduces the burden on agents and improves consistency, but it should be reviewed periodically to ensure accuracy. Keyword-based tagging can misfire when words are used in unexpected contexts.

## Using tags for reporting

### Volume by topic

The most fundamental report is conversation volume by tag over time. This shows you what visitors are asking about most and how the distribution changes. A sudden increase in "login issues" might indicate a system problem. A steady rise in "pricing" conversations might indicate growing market interest.

### Satisfaction by topic

Cross-referencing tags with satisfaction scores reveals which topics generate the best and worst visitor experiences. If "refund request" conversations consistently produce low CSAT scores, investigate whether the issue is the policy, the process, or the communication.

### Resolution time by topic

Some topics take longer to resolve than others. Tracking resolution time by tag helps you set realistic expectations and identify opportunities for improvement. If "integration errors" take three times longer to resolve than "account management" queries, your team might need better troubleshooting tools or documentation for integrations.

### Agent performance by topic

Tags allow you to see how individual agents perform across different conversation types. An agent might excel at pre-sales conversations but struggle with technical support. This granularity helps you assign agents to conversations that match their strengths and target training where it is most needed.

## Using tags for automation

### Routing

Tags applied early in a conversation can trigger automatic routing. If a pre-chat form selection or an AI analysis tags a conversation as "billing," the platform can route it directly to the billing team without manual intervention.

### Prioritisation

Tags can determine priority. A conversation tagged as "cancellation" might be prioritised over one tagged as "general enquiry" because it has a higher business impact. Your platform can use tags to move higher-priority conversations to the front of the queue.

### Follow-up workflows

Tags can trigger post-conversation actions. A conversation tagged as "sale" might trigger a follow-up email with next steps. One tagged as "complaint" might trigger a satisfaction survey. One tagged as "feature request" might add the request to your product feedback log.

## Common tagging mistakes

### Too many tags

A system with dozens of tags is burdensome and inconsistent. Agents will not remember all the options, and the data quality will suffer. Keep your taxonomy lean.

### Inconsistent usage

If agents interpret tags differently, your reporting is unreliable. "Billing" might mean invoicing to one agent and payment issues to another. Clear documentation and periodic calibration sessions, where the team reviews sample conversations and agrees on the correct tags, maintain consistency.

### Not reviewing the taxonomy

Business priorities change, product lines evolve, and new types of questions emerge. Review your tagging taxonomy quarterly. Retire tags that are rarely used. Add tags for new topics that have become frequent. Merge tags that overlap.

### Treating tags as optional

If tagging is optional, it will not happen consistently. Make tagging a required step in the conversation closing workflow. This does not mean it needs to be onerous. A single required primary category tag, with optional sub-tags, balances data quality with agent convenience.

## Getting started

If your platform supports tagging, and most modern platforms including [Relentify Chat](/chat) do, start with a simple taxonomy of ten to fifteen tags covering your main conversation topics. Train your team on when to use each tag. Run the system for a month, then review the data.

You will likely find that a few tags account for the majority of conversations, that some tags are rarely used and can be consolidated, and that there are gaps where common topics do not have a corresponding tag. Refine the taxonomy based on these findings and continue iterating.

The effort of building and maintaining a tagging system pays dividends in reporting, routing, and strategic insight. Every tagged conversation becomes a searchable, analysable, actionable piece of data that helps you understand your visitors better and serve them more effectively.
