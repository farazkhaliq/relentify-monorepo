A job description is often the first interaction a potential employee has with your business. It sets expectations, filters candidates, and communicates your company culture — all in a few hundred words. Yet most job descriptions are written hastily, filled with jargon, and fail to answer the questions candidates actually care about.

This guide shows you how to write job descriptions that attract the right people.

## Why job descriptions matter

A good job description does three things:

1. **Attracts qualified candidates**: People who can do the job well read the description and think "this is for me."
2. **Filters unsuitable applicants**: Clear requirements help people self-select out if they are not a match.
3. **Sets expectations**: The new hire knows what the role involves, reducing the risk of early disillusionment.

A bad job description does the opposite — it repels good candidates, attracts poor ones, and creates misaligned expectations that lead to early turnover.

## The structure of a good job description

### Job title
Keep it clear and searchable. Use standard industry titles that candidates will actually search for. "Marketing Manager" works. "Marketing Ninja" does not.

Avoid internal jargon. If your company calls the role "Growth Hacker Level II," the rest of the world calls it "Digital Marketing Manager." Use the term people will search for.

### Opening paragraph
Two to three sentences explaining the role, the team, and why it matters. This is your hook — make it compelling. Answer the question: "Why would someone want this job?"

Focus on impact, not bureaucracy. "You will lead our product marketing, shaping how 10,000 customers discover and adopt our tools" is better than "This role reports to the Senior Vice President of Marketing and is responsible for product marketing activities."

### Responsibilities
List the core responsibilities — what the person will actually spend their time doing. Aim for five to eight bullet points, ordered by importance and time allocation.

Be specific. "Manage social media" is vague. "Plan and publish 15-20 social media posts per week across LinkedIn, Twitter, and Instagram, tracking engagement and adjusting strategy monthly" tells the candidate exactly what the job involves.

Start each bullet point with an action verb: manage, build, create, analyse, lead, design, implement, coordinate.

### Requirements
Split into "must have" and "nice to have." Be honest about which is which.

Must-have requirements should be genuine dealbreakers — qualifications, skills, or experience without which the person cannot do the job. If you list something as required but would actually consider someone without it, it belongs in "nice to have."

Over-inflating requirements discourages good candidates from applying. Research consistently shows that some demographic groups are more likely to self-select out when they do not meet every listed requirement, even if they could do the job well.

### Salary and benefits
Include the salary range. This is increasingly expected and, in some jurisdictions, legally required. Listing salary saves everyone time — candidates who are outside the range will not apply, and you will not waste interviews on people you cannot afford.

Beyond salary, list the benefits that genuinely differentiate your offer:
- Pension or retirement contributions
- Holiday allowance
- Flexible or remote working arrangements
- Health insurance or wellness benefits
- Professional development budget
- Equipment or home office allowance

### About the company
A brief paragraph about who you are, what you do, and what it is like to work there. Keep it authentic — candidates can spot corporate boilerplate a mile away.

### How to apply
Clear instructions on what to submit (CV, cover letter, portfolio) and where to send it. Include a closing date if applicable.

## Language and tone

### Be direct
Write in plain language. Avoid corporate jargon, buzzwords, and acronyms that candidates outside your organisation will not understand.

"We are looking for someone who can manage our social media presence and grow our audience" is better than "We seek a dynamic self-starter who can leverage synergies across our digital ecosystem."

### Be inclusive
Review your language for unintentional bias. Some words and phrases can discourage certain groups from applying:

- "Rockstar," "ninja," "guru" — informal and exclusionary
- "Young and dynamic team" — age discrimination
- "Must be a native speaker" — specify the actual language proficiency needed instead
- "Competitive salary" — this often means "we are not going to tell you"

Use gender-neutral language throughout. Tools exist to check job descriptions for biased language — consider using one.

### Be honest
If the job involves tight deadlines, difficult clients, or long hours during peak periods, say so. Candidates who accept the offer knowing the challenges are far more likely to stay than those who feel misled.

## Common mistakes

### Listing too many requirements
A job description with 20 required qualifications signals either that you do not know what the role needs, or that you have unrealistic expectations. Keep the must-have list to five or six genuinely essential items.

### Being vague about the role
"Other duties as assigned" tells the candidate nothing. If the role genuinely involves varied tasks, describe the range. But do not use vague language to hide the fact that you have not thought through the role properly.

### Forgetting about the candidate's perspective
Most job descriptions focus entirely on what the employer wants. The best ones also address what the candidate gets: career development, interesting work, impact, flexibility, and culture.

### Copying and pasting
Using the same template for every role produces generic descriptions that fail to attract the right people. Tailor each description to the specific role, team, and context.

### Not including salary
This deserves repeating. Not listing salary wastes everyone's time and signals that you either do not know what the role is worth or do not want to pay fairly. Candidates increasingly skip adverts without salary information.

## Job descriptions for small businesses

Small businesses have an advantage in recruitment: they can offer things that large companies cannot. Use your job description to highlight:

- **Direct impact**: "You will be one of six people shaping the direction of this company" resonates more than a title at a 10,000-person corporation.
- **Breadth of experience**: Small business roles are often broader, giving employees exposure to multiple areas.
- **Flexibility**: If you offer genuinely flexible working, say so prominently.
- **Growth potential**: If the role has a clear growth path, describe it.

Small businesses can also be more personal. Instead of corporate language, write like a real person talking to another real person.

## Using the job description after hiring

A good job description is not just a recruitment tool. It becomes the foundation for:

- **Performance reviews**: The responsibilities listed in the job description are the starting point for evaluating whether the employee is meeting expectations.
- **Compensation benchmarking**: When you review salaries, the job description defines the scope of the role.
- **Training and development**: Gaps between the employee's current skills and the requirements listed in the description identify development needs.

Keep job descriptions up to date. When a role evolves, update the description so it remains accurate for future recruitment and performance management.

## The payoff

A well-written job description takes an hour or two to produce. A bad hire costs months of salary, training time, and team disruption. Investing time in getting the description right is one of the highest-return activities in the entire hiring process.

Write clearly, be specific about requirements, include salary, and focus on what the candidate cares about as much as what you need. The right people will find you.
