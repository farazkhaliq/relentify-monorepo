The profit and loss statement — also called the P&L, income statement, or statement of earnings — is one of the three core financial reports every business produces. It tells you whether your business made money or lost money over a specific period.

Despite its importance, many business owners glance at the bottom line (profit or loss) and ignore everything else. That is a missed opportunity. The P&L contains a wealth of information about how your business is performing, where your money is going, and what you can do to improve.

You do not need an accounting qualification to read a P&L. You just need to understand the structure.

## What the P&L tells you

A profit and loss statement answers one fundamental question: did you make more money than you spent during this period?

It covers a specific timeframe — usually a month, quarter, or year — and shows:

- How much revenue your business generated
- What it cost to deliver your products or services
- What it cost to run the business
- Whether there is profit left over (and how much)

Unlike the balance sheet, which shows a snapshot of your financial position at a single point in time, the P&L shows flow — income flowing in and expenses flowing out over the period.

## The structure of a P&L

Every P&L follows the same basic structure, from top to bottom:

### Revenue (also called sales, turnover, or income)

This is the total money earned from your core business activities before any costs are deducted. If you are a consultancy, it is your consulting fees. If you are a shop, it is your sales.

Revenue should only include income from your normal business operations. One-off items like selling an old vehicle or receiving an insurance payout are typically shown separately.

### Cost of goods sold (COGS) or cost of sales

These are the direct costs of delivering your products or services. For a product business, this includes materials, manufacturing, and shipping. For a service business, it might include subcontractor costs or direct labour.

The key distinction is "direct" — these costs are directly tied to delivering what you sell. They go up when you sell more and down when you sell less.

### Gross profit

**Revenue minus cost of goods sold equals gross profit.**

Gross profit tells you how much money you have left after covering the direct costs of what you sell. This is the pool of money available to cover your overhead and generate net profit.

The **gross profit margin** (gross profit divided by revenue, expressed as a percentage) is one of the most important metrics in your business. It tells you how efficiently you are delivering your products or services.

### Operating expenses (also called overheads)

These are the costs of running your business that are not directly tied to individual sales. Common categories include:

- Rent and utilities
- Salaries and wages (for staff not directly involved in production)
- Marketing and advertising
- Insurance
- Professional fees (accountant, legal)
- Software and subscriptions
- Office supplies
- Depreciation
- Travel and entertainment

### Operating profit (also called EBIT — earnings before interest and tax)

**Gross profit minus operating expenses equals operating profit.**

Operating profit shows how much your business earns from its core operations, before financing costs and tax. This is the best measure of your business's operational performance because it strips out factors like how the business is financed (debt vs equity) and tax treatment.

### Interest and financing costs

If your business has loans, overdrafts, or other debt, the interest payments appear here. This is separate from operating expenses because it relates to how the business is funded, not how it operates.

### Profit before tax

**Operating profit minus interest equals profit before tax.**

This is your total earnings before the government takes its share.

### Tax

Corporation tax, income tax (for sole traders), or whatever tax applies to your business profits.

### Net profit (the bottom line)

**Profit before tax minus tax equals net profit.**

This is what is left after everything. It is the true earnings of the business for the period. Net profit can be distributed to owners (as dividends or drawings) or retained in the business to fund growth.

## Reading a P&L: what to look for

### Revenue trends

Is revenue growing, shrinking, or flat? Compare the current period with previous periods to spot trends. Seasonal businesses will naturally fluctuate, but the year-on-year trend should be moving in the right direction.

### Gross margin

Is your gross profit margin stable? A declining margin might indicate rising costs, pricing pressure, or a shift in your sales mix towards lower-margin products or services.

A healthy gross margin depends on your industry. Software businesses might see 80%+ margins. Retail might be 30-50%. Construction might be 15-25%. Compare with industry benchmarks, not arbitrary targets.

### Expense ratios

Look at each major expense category as a percentage of revenue. If rent was 10% of revenue last year and is now 15%, something has changed — either rent went up or revenue went down. Express expenses as percentages to make comparisons meaningful across different revenue levels.

### Operating leverage

As your revenue grows, do your profits grow faster? If revenue increases by 20% but profit increases by 40%, you have positive operating leverage — your fixed costs are being spread across more revenue. This is a sign of a scalable business.

### Unusual items

Look for anything unexpected. A spike in a particular expense category, a one-off gain or loss, or a significant change from the previous period. These need explanation.

## Common P&L mistakes

### Confusing profit with cash

Your P&L might show a healthy profit, but that does not mean you have cash in the bank. Under accrual accounting, revenue is recorded when invoiced, not when paid. You could show a profit while waiting for clients to pay you.

### Ignoring gross margin

Many business owners jump straight to the bottom line. But net profit is influenced by many things (tax, depreciation methods, one-off items). Gross margin is a purer measure of how efficiently you are running your core business.

### Comparing periods without context

Comparing January with December is meaningless for many businesses because of seasonal variation. Compare with the same period last year, or use rolling averages.

### Not producing one

Some small businesses do not produce a P&L at all, relying instead on their bank balance as a measure of success. This is like flying blind. You might be profitable on paper but heading towards a cash flow crisis, or vice versa.

## Using the P&L for decision-making

### Pricing decisions

If your gross margin is declining, you need to either reduce your direct costs or increase your prices. The P&L shows you the impact of pricing changes over time.

### Cost control

Which expenses are growing fastest? Are they growing in line with revenue (which is normal) or faster than revenue (which erodes profit)? The P&L highlights where to focus cost-cutting efforts.

### Investment decisions

Can you afford to hire someone new, invest in marketing, or buy equipment? The P&L shows your current earnings capacity and how much room you have for additional costs.

### Forecasting

Use historical P&L data to project future performance. If your revenue has grown 15% year-on-year for the last three years, what does that mean for next year's expected profit?

## Getting your P&L right

The accuracy of your P&L depends entirely on the accuracy of your bookkeeping. Every transaction needs to be recorded in the right category at the right time.

Modern accounting software like [Relentify](/accounting) generates P&L reports automatically from your transaction data. As long as your bookkeeping is up to date and transactions are categorised correctly, your P&L will be accurate and available at the click of a button.

Review your P&L at least monthly. Do not wait until year end to find out how your business performed — by then it is too late to make changes. Monthly reviews let you spot problems early and take corrective action while there is still time.

The profit and loss statement is not just a document for your accountant. It is a management tool that tells you whether your business is working. Learn to read it, review it regularly, and use it to make better decisions.
