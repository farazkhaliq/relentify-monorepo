Payment terms are one of those things that seem simple on the surface but have an outsized impact on your business. The terms you put on your invoices determine when you get paid, how your cash flow works, and how your client relationships function.

Yet many business owners choose their payment terms almost arbitrarily — copying what a competitor does or defaulting to whatever their accounting software suggests. Understanding what each term means, and when to use it, gives you much more control over your finances.

## What are invoice payment terms?

Payment terms are the conditions under which you expect to be paid for goods or services. They specify when payment is due, whether any discounts apply for early payment, and what happens if payment is late.

Payment terms appear on every invoice you send and form part of the contractual agreement between you and your customer.

## Common payment terms explained

### Due on Receipt

**What it means:** Payment is expected as soon as the client receives the invoice.

**When to use it:** New clients you have not worked with before, one-off projects, small transactions, or situations where you need immediate cash flow. It is also standard for retail and point-of-sale transactions.

**Considerations:** While "due on receipt" technically means immediate payment, in practice most clients will take a few days to process it. It is the most aggressive payment term and may not be appropriate for larger clients with established procurement processes.

### Net 7

**What it means:** Payment is due within 7 days of the invoice date.

**When to use it:** Short-term projects, smaller invoices, or clients you want to establish quick payment habits with. Common in trades and services where work is completed quickly.

**Considerations:** Seven days is tight. Make sure you send the invoice promptly — if you invoice three days after completing the work, the client only has four days to pay.

### Net 14

**What it means:** Payment is due within 14 days of the invoice date.

**When to use it:** A good middle ground for small to medium businesses. Common in professional services, freelancing, and small business-to-business transactions.

**Considerations:** Two weeks is enough time for most clients to receive, review, and process an invoice without being so long that it hurts your cash flow.

### Net 30

**What it means:** Payment is due within 30 days of the invoice date.

**When to use it:** The most common payment term in business-to-business transactions. Standard in many industries and expected by larger companies.

**Considerations:** Net 30 is effectively the default in many sectors. While it is reasonable, 30 days can feel like a long time when you have bills to pay. If your clients consistently take the full 30 days (or longer), it can create cash flow pressure.

### Net 60 and Net 90

**What it means:** Payment is due within 60 or 90 days of the invoice date.

**When to use it:** Large corporate clients and government contracts often impose these terms. They are rarely a choice — they are a condition of doing business with certain organisations.

**Considerations:** Extended terms can be challenging for small businesses. If you agree to Net 90, you are essentially providing three months of interest-free credit to your client. Make sure your cash flow can absorb this, or factor it into your pricing.

### End of Month (EOM)

**What it means:** Payment is due by the end of the month in which the invoice is received.

**When to use it:** Common in industries where billing cycles align with calendar months. Often combined with a net period — for example, "Net 30 EOM" means payment is due 30 days after the end of the month in which the invoice was issued.

**Considerations:** The actual payment window depends on when in the month you invoice. An invoice sent on the 1st gives the client nearly two months under "Net 30 EOM," while one sent on the 28th gives roughly 32 days.

### 2/10 Net 30 (Early payment discounts)

**What it means:** The client gets a 2% discount if they pay within 10 days; otherwise, the full amount is due within 30 days.

**When to use it:** When you want to incentivise early payment. The discount is small enough to be affordable but large enough to motivate faster payment.

**Considerations:** A 2% discount for paying 20 days early is actually a significant annualised return for the client (roughly 36%). This makes it attractive for financially savvy clients with available cash. However, it does reduce your revenue slightly, so factor this into your pricing.

Other common variations include 1/10 Net 30 (1% discount for paying within 10 days) and 3/10 Net 60.

### 50% Upfront, 50% on Completion

**What it means:** Half the payment is due before work begins, with the remainder due on completion.

**When to use it:** Projects with significant upfront costs, new client relationships, or high-value engagements where you want to share the financial risk.

**Considerations:** This is a milestone-based approach rather than a fixed-term approach. It protects you from doing all the work and then chasing payment, while also giving the client confidence that you are committed to completing the project.

### Milestone Payments

**What it means:** Payment is split across defined project milestones (e.g., 30% on signing, 30% at midpoint, 40% on completion).

**When to use it:** Long-term projects, construction, software development, or any engagement where work spans months.

**Considerations:** Define milestones clearly in your contract. Vague milestones lead to disputes about when payment is triggered.

## How to choose the right payment terms

### Consider your cash flow

If your business has tight cash flow, shorter terms (Net 7 or Net 14) help keep money coming in. If you have a comfortable cash buffer, you can afford to offer Net 30 or beyond.

### Consider your industry norms

Every industry has its expectations. Freelancers and small agencies often use Net 14 or Net 30. Construction typically uses milestone payments. Enterprise sales may require Net 60 or Net 90.

Going against industry norms is possible but may create friction. A freelance designer demanding "due on receipt" from a large corporation will likely be met with "we pay on Net 60."

### Consider the client relationship

New clients warrant more cautious terms. Established clients with a good payment history can earn more flexible terms. This is a form of trust, and it should be earned.

### Consider the project size

Small invoices (under a few hundred) are often due on receipt or Net 7. Larger invoices may justify longer terms or milestone structures.

## How to communicate payment terms effectively

### On your invoices

Your payment terms should be prominently displayed on every invoice. Include:

- The specific due date (not just "Net 30" — calculate the actual date)
- Your accepted payment methods
- Your bank details or payment link
- Any early payment discount terms
- Any late payment consequences

Modern invoicing tools like [Relentify](/accounting) let you set default payment terms that are automatically applied to every invoice, with the due date calculated for you. This removes the manual work and ensures consistency.

### In your contracts

Payment terms should be agreed before you start work, not introduced on the first invoice. Include them in your proposal, quote, or contract so there are no surprises.

### With clarity

Avoid ambiguity. "Payment due within 30 days" is clearer than "Net 30" for clients who are not familiar with the terminology. Use both if needed.

## What to do when terms are not met

Late payment is a reality of business. When it happens:

1. **Send a polite reminder** on the due date or the day after
2. **Follow up by phone** if the reminder is ignored after a few days
3. **Send a formal overdue notice** at 14 days past due
4. **Consider charging late payment interest** if your terms allow it
5. **Escalate to a collections process** for significantly overdue invoices

The key is to have a system. Do not let overdue invoices drift because you feel awkward chasing them. Automated payment reminders — which most modern accounting software can send — take the personal discomfort out of the process.

## Adjusting terms over time

Your payment terms are not set in stone. As your business evolves, review them:

- If cash flow is consistently tight, shorten your terms
- If you are losing clients to competitors with more flexible terms, consider extending them
- If a specific client repeatedly pays late on Net 30, tighten their terms to Net 14
- If a long-standing client always pays promptly, reward them with flexibility

Payment terms are a business tool. Use them strategically, communicate them clearly, and enforce them consistently.
