Running a cleaning company means managing a workforce that is rarely in one place. Your cleaners are spread across client sites — offices, schools, medical facilities, retail spaces — often working early mornings, late evenings, or weekends when nobody else is around. There is no supervisor watching the clock, no shared break room, and no central office where timesheets land on a desk.

This makes time tracking simultaneously more difficult and more important. Difficult because there is no natural checkpoint where attendance is observed. Important because every hour on a timesheet drives two financial transactions: a payment to the cleaner and an invoice to the client. Get the hours wrong, and both relationships suffer.

This guide explains how cleaning companies can build a reliable time tracking process that works across dozens of client sites and a dispersed workforce.

## The specific challenges for cleaning companies

### Dispersed, unsupervised work

Cleaners typically work alone or in small teams at client premises. The client may not be present during cleaning hours. This means there is no independent verification of attendance unless the timesheet system provides it.

### Early and late shifts

Much commercial cleaning happens outside standard business hours — before the office opens or after it closes. Supervisors and managers are unlikely to be available at these times, which means the clock-in process must be self-service and reliable.

### Multiple sites per day

Some cleaners visit several sites in a single day. A cleaner might do a two-hour office clean from 6am to 8am, a three-hour medical centre clean from 9am to noon, and a two-hour school clean from 3pm to 5pm. Each visit needs to be recorded separately for billing accuracy.

### High staff turnover

The cleaning industry has notoriously high turnover. New starters need to be onboarded to the timesheet system quickly, and leavers need to be removed promptly to prevent ghost entries.

### Tight margins

Cleaning contracts operate on thin margins. A few unbilled hours per week across a portfolio of clients can represent the difference between profit and loss. Accurate time tracking is not a convenience — it is a survival mechanism.

## Setting up time tracking for a cleaning business

### Step 1: Map your client sites

Create a record for every client site in your system, including:

- Client name and contract reference
- Site address
- Contracted hours and schedule (e.g., Monday to Friday, 6am to 8am)
- The cleaner or team assigned to the site
- The billing rate

This site register is the backbone of your time tracking and invoicing process.

### Step 2: Set up GPS-verified clock-in

Require cleaners to clock in and out at each site using a mobile app with GPS verification. When a cleaner clocks in, the system records the time and their location, confirming they are at the correct client site.

For sites where GPS signal is weak (underground car parks, interior rooms), consider widening the geofence radius or using an alternative check-in method such as an NFC tag or QR code placed at the site.

Platforms like [Relentify](/timesheets) support GPS-verified check-ins per site, giving you a defensible record of attendance at every client location.

### Step 3: Track hours per site

Because each client site is billed separately, time must be recorded per site, not just per day. When a cleaner visits three sites in one day, the system should produce three separate time entries — one for each site — with the correct client and billing rate attached.

### Step 4: Implement approval

Even with GPS verification, someone should review timesheets before they feed into payroll and billing. This might be a team leader, a supervisor, or an office administrator. The reviewer checks for:

- Hours that significantly exceed the contracted schedule
- Missed clock-ins or clock-outs
- Shifts at the wrong site (a cleaner covering for a colleague at a different location)
- Missing break records

### Step 5: Connect to billing

Once timesheets are approved, the data should flow into your invoicing process. For each client, pull the approved hours for the billing period, apply the contracted rate, and generate the invoice. The timesheet data provides both the invoice amount and the supporting evidence if the client requests detail.

## Common scenarios and how to handle them

### Cover and cross-site working

When a cleaner covers for a colleague at a different site, the system should record the substitute cleaner's hours at the cover site. This ensures the correct client is billed and the covering cleaner is paid for their actual hours. If the system is GPS-verified, this happens automatically — the cleaner clocks in at whatever site they are working at, and the system matches the location.

### Extra work and one-off cleans

Clients sometimes request additional cleaning — a deep clean before an event, post-renovation cleaning, or extra sanitisation during illness outbreaks. These should be recorded as separate entries, ideally tagged as "extra" or "ad hoc," so they can be billed at the appropriate rate (which may differ from the standard contract rate).

### Cleaners without smartphones

While most cleaners have smartphones, some may not. Options include:

- A shared tablet or phone left at the site (in a secure location)
- An NFC tag at the site entrance that can be tapped with a basic phone
- A call-in system where the cleaner dials a number and enters a PIN

Whatever the method, the goal is the same: a verified, timestamped record of arrival and departure.

### Client access to records

Some clients want to verify attendance independently. Consider providing clients with a view-only portal or a regular attendance report. This builds trust and pre-empts billing disputes.

## Using timesheet data for business management

### Profitability by client

With accurate time data, you can calculate the true cost of servicing each client. Some contracts that appear profitable may be marginal once you account for actual hours (including overtime and travel). Others may be more profitable than expected. This analysis informs contract renewals and pricing decisions.

### Labour cost per square metre

For commercial cleaning contracts, cost per square metre is a useful benchmark. Timesheet data provides the labour hours component of this calculation, allowing you to compare efficiency across sites and identify opportunities for improvement.

### Overtime and schedule adherence

Are cleaners consistently exceeding their scheduled hours at certain sites? This might indicate that the contracted hours are insufficient for the scope of work, that the cleaner is working inefficiently, or that the client has added requirements without adjusting the contract.

### Staff utilisation

How much of each cleaner's available time is spent on billable work versus travel, admin, or downtime? Timesheet data, combined with scheduling data, reveals utilisation rates that inform hiring decisions and route planning.

## The billing connection

For a cleaning company, the link between timesheets and invoices is direct and critical. Every invoice should be traceable to specific timesheet entries, and every timesheet entry should be verifiable.

When a client queries an invoice — "We were billed for 120 hours this month, but the contract is for 100" — you need to be able to show exactly which hours were worked, on which dates, by which cleaners, and with what verification. GPS-verified timesheets make this conversation straightforward.

Conversely, accurate timesheets prevent under-billing. If a cleaner consistently works twenty minutes beyond the contracted time to finish the job properly, that accumulated overtime represents revenue the company is entitled to — either by billing the client for the actual hours or by renegotiating the contract to reflect the real workload.

## Summary

For cleaning companies, timesheet management is the operational thread that connects workforce management, payroll, client billing, and profitability analysis. A system that captures accurate, verified hours per client site — and makes that data accessible for approval, billing, and reporting — is not a luxury. It is a fundamental requirement for running a profitable, compliant, and well-managed cleaning business.
