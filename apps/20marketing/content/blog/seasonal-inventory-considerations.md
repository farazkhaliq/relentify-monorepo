Property conditions change with the seasons. A garden that looks immaculate in July may be dormant and bare in January. A heating system that cannot be tested in summer is critical to check in winter. Condensation that is invisible in warm weather may be covering windows in cold months.

These seasonal factors affect what you see during an inventory inspection and how you should document it. Being aware of seasonal considerations helps you produce more accurate, more useful reports regardless of when the inspection takes place.

## Summer inspections

### What you can assess well in summer

**Gardens and external areas** are at their most visible in summer. Lawns are growing, borders are planted, hedges are full, and the overall condition of the garden is easy to assess. Summer is the best time to document garden condition in detail, because the tenant's maintenance (or lack of it) is most apparent.

**Exterior paintwork and rendering** are easier to assess in good light. Summer provides long daylight hours and better visibility for photographing building exteriors.

**Outdoor furniture and features** — decking, patios, garden furniture, barbecues, sheds — are all visible and accessible in summer. Document their condition thoroughly when the weather allows full access.

**Windows and doors** can be tested for operation — do they open and close smoothly? Are the locks working? Summer allows you to open everything without concerns about heat loss.

### What to watch for in summer

**Sun damage**: Prolonged sunlight can fade carpets, curtains, blinds, and wall coverings. If you are conducting a check-out in summer, look for fading near south-facing windows and compare against the check-in record.

**Insect damage**: Warmer months bring insects. Look for signs of moth damage in carpets (particularly under furniture), wasp nests in lofts or eaves, and any other pest activity.

**Ventilation issues**: Summer heat can reveal ventilation problems — properties that get excessively hot may have insufficient ventilation, which can also indicate winter condensation risks.

### What you cannot easily assess in summer

**Heating systems**: In warm weather, the heating has been off for months. You can turn on the boiler and check it fires, but you cannot assess whether radiators heat evenly throughout the property or whether the heating system maintains comfortable temperatures. Note that the boiler was tested briefly but that a full heating assessment was not possible due to the season.

**Condensation and damp**: Condensation is primarily a cold-weather issue. A summer inspection may not reveal condensation patterns that appear in winter. Look for historical signs — water marks on window sills, mould in corners, peeling paint near windows — but note that a summer inspection cannot fully assess winter condensation.

**Draught proofing**: You cannot feel draughts around windows and doors in summer. Any assessment of weather sealing is limited.

## Winter inspections

### What you can assess well in winter

**Heating system performance**: This is the primary advantage of a winter inspection. You can test the boiler, check that every radiator heats properly, assess whether the property reaches a comfortable temperature, and note any cold spots or heating issues.

**Condensation and damp**: Winter reveals the property's true condensation behaviour. Check all windows for condensation, especially in bathrooms, kitchens, and bedrooms. Look for mould on walls, ceilings, and window frames. Document any damp patches or musty odours.

**Draught proofing**: Cold weather makes draughts obvious. Check around windows, doors, letter boxes, and any other openings for cold air infiltration.

**Gutters and drainage**: Winter rain tests the property's drainage. Look for overflowing gutters, blocked downpipes, and any signs of water ingress. Standing water in gardens or on pathways is also more apparent.

**Roof condition**: After autumn storms, check for missing or displaced roof tiles (visible from ground level), damaged ridge tiles, and any signs of water ingress in loft spaces.

### What to watch for in winter

**Burst pipes and water damage**: In properties that have been empty over winter, check for signs of frozen or burst pipes. Inspect under sinks, around exposed pipework, and in unheated areas like garages and lofts.

**Seasonal damp**: Distinguish between condensation (caused by lifestyle and ventilation) and structural damp (caused by building defects). Both may be more visible in winter, but they have different causes and different responsibilities.

**Heating adequacy**: If the property does not heat effectively, this is a landlord responsibility, not a tenant issue. Document any concerns about heating performance.

### What you cannot easily assess in winter

**Garden condition**: Gardens are dormant in winter. Lawns may be muddy or bare. Deciduous plants have no leaves. It is very difficult to assess the true condition of a garden in winter.

If you are conducting a check-in in winter for a property with a garden, note the seasonal limitations in your report: "Garden inspected in January — lawn dormant, borders bare. Full garden condition cannot be fully assessed until spring growing season."

If you are conducting a check-out in winter and the check-in was in summer, the comparison is inherently limited. A summer check-in showing a lush garden against a winter check-out showing bare earth does not indicate neglect — it indicates seasonal change. Exercise reasonable judgement and note the seasonal context.

**Exterior paint and rendering**: Short daylight hours and overcast skies make exterior inspection more difficult. Photograph external areas with the best available light.

**Outdoor features**: Garden furniture may be stored. Decking may be slippery or covered in debris. Assess what you can and note any limitations.

## Documenting seasonal factors

### Always note the season and weather

Your report should include the date and a brief note about conditions at the time of inspection:

- "Inspection conducted 15 January, overcast, temperature approximately 3 degrees. Heating tested and operational."
- "Inspection conducted 22 July, sunny, temperature approximately 24 degrees. Garden in active growing season."

This context helps anyone reviewing the report understand the conditions under which observations were made.

### Flag seasonal limitations

If you cannot fully assess something due to the season, say so. Do not ignore it or pretend you assessed it when you could not:

- "Heating system: Boiler fired successfully, hot water confirmed. Full radiator assessment not possible in current warm conditions."
- "Garden: Inspected during winter dormancy. Lawn bare, borders empty. Condition cannot be fully compared to summer check-in."

### Take extra photos where relevant

If the season reveals conditions that might not be visible at other times of year, take additional photographs:

- Winter condensation on windows
- Summer sun fading on carpets
- Autumn leaf accumulation in gutters
- Spring evidence of damp that dried out in summer

## Seasonal check-in and check-out mismatches

One of the most common seasonal challenges is when the check-in happens in one season and the check-out in another. A summer check-in and winter check-out (or vice versa) creates comparison difficulties for seasonal elements.

### How to handle it

**Gardens**: If the check-in was in summer (garden at its best) and the check-out is in winter (garden dormant), do not penalise the tenant for seasonal changes. Compare the structural elements — fences, paths, sheds — and note that the growing condition of plants cannot be fairly compared across seasons.

**Heating**: If the check-in was in summer (heating not fully tested) and the check-out is in winter (heating in daily use), any heating issues at check-out may or may not be the tenant's responsibility. A boiler failure is likely an age or maintenance issue. A blocked radiator is more likely a tenant issue if it was documented as working at check-in.

**Condensation**: If condensation is visible at a winter check-out but there was no check-in record of it (because the check-in was in summer), the tenant should not automatically be held responsible. Condensation is often a property ventilation issue, not a tenant behaviour issue.

### The solution: mid-tenancy inspections

Mid-tenancy inspections help bridge seasonal gaps. If a check-in happens in summer, a mid-tenancy inspection in winter can document the property's condition in cold weather. If a check-in happens in winter, a summer mid-tenancy inspection can document the garden in growing season.

This additional data point makes the final check-out comparison fairer and more informed.

## The bottom line

Seasonal awareness makes you a better inventory professional. Knowing what to look for in each season, documenting seasonal limitations honestly, and using mid-tenancy inspections to fill seasonal gaps all contribute to fairer, more accurate inventory reports.

The property does not change with the seasons — but what you can see and assess does. Account for it, document it, and produce reports that reflect reality regardless of the calendar.
