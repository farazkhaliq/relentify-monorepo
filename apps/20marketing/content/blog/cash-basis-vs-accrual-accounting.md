One of the earliest decisions you make when setting up your business accounting is whether to use cash basis or accrual accounting. It might seem like a technical detail, but this choice affects how your income and expenses are recorded, when you pay tax, and how accurately your financial reports reflect reality.

Most small businesses start with cash basis because it is simpler. But as your business grows, understanding both methods — and knowing when to switch — is essential.

## The fundamental difference

The difference comes down to timing: when do you record income and expenses?

**Cash basis:** You record income when you receive the money and expenses when you pay them. If you invoice a client in March but they pay in April, the income is recorded in April.

**Accrual basis:** You record income when you earn it and expenses when you incur them, regardless of when money changes hands. That same invoice would be recorded as income in March, even if payment arrives in April.

This timing difference can have significant effects on your reported profit, your tax bill, and your understanding of your business finances.

## Cash basis accounting

### How it works

Under cash basis, transactions are recorded based on cash flow:

- **Income** is recorded when money hits your bank account
- **Expenses** are recorded when money leaves your bank account

If you invoice a client for 5,000 in December and they pay in January, that 5,000 appears as January income, not December.

### Advantages

**Simplicity** — Cash basis is straightforward. You look at what came in and what went out. There are no accruals, prepayments, or accounts receivable to track.

**Cash flow clarity** — Your profit figure closely reflects your actual cash position. If your books say you made a profit, you should have cash in the bank to show for it.

**Tax timing** — You only pay tax on money you have actually received. If a client is slow to pay, you are not taxed on income you have not yet collected.

**Lower bookkeeping cost** — Fewer adjustments and simpler records mean less time (or lower fees) for bookkeeping.

### Disadvantages

**Distorted profitability** — Cash basis can misrepresent your actual business performance. A month where you collect several large payments looks incredibly profitable, even if the work was done months ago. A month where clients are slow to pay looks terrible, even if you did plenty of work.

**No accounts receivable or payable** — You cannot see at a glance how much you are owed or how much you owe. This makes it harder to plan and manage cash flow proactively.

**Limited usefulness for investors and lenders** — Banks and investors typically want to see accrual-based financial statements. Cash basis accounts may not meet their requirements.

**Potential for manipulation** — It is possible to shift income between tax years by timing when you invoice or collect payments. While not illegal, it can attract scrutiny.

## Accrual basis accounting

### How it works

Under accrual basis, transactions are recorded when the economic event occurs:

- **Income** is recorded when you issue an invoice or earn the revenue, regardless of payment timing
- **Expenses** are recorded when you receive goods or services, regardless of when you pay

### Advantages

**Accurate profitability** — Accrual accounting matches income with the expenses incurred to generate it. This gives you a much truer picture of your business performance in any given period.

**Better decision-making** — When your reports accurately reflect what happened in each period, you can make better strategic decisions. You can see trends, spot problems, and plan with confidence.

**Accounts receivable and payable** — You have a clear view of what clients owe you and what you owe suppliers. This is essential for managing cash flow and credit.

**Required for larger businesses** — Most countries require businesses above a certain size to use accrual accounting. Switching early avoids a painful transition later.

**Standard for external reporting** — Banks, investors, and potential buyers expect accrual-based financial statements. If you ever seek funding or want to sell your business, you will need them.

### Disadvantages

**More complex** — Accrual accounting requires understanding concepts like prepayments, accruals, depreciation, and provisions. The bookkeeping is more involved.

**Tax on unpaid income** — You may owe tax on income you have invoiced but not yet received. If a client pays late (or not at all), you have a tax bill for money you do not have.

**Higher bookkeeping cost** — More complex records require more time and expertise, which often means higher accounting fees.

## A practical comparison

Imagine a freelance designer with the following transactions in March:

- Invoiced Client A: 3,000 (not yet paid)
- Received payment from Client B for February work: 2,500
- Purchased new software: 500 (paid immediately)
- Received electricity bill: 200 (not yet paid)

**Cash basis March report:**
- Income: 2,500 (only the payment received)
- Expenses: 500 (only the payment made)
- Profit: 2,000

**Accrual basis March report:**
- Income: 3,000 (the invoice issued this month)
- Expenses: 700 (software + electricity bill)
- Profit: 2,300

Neither is "wrong" — they are just measuring different things. Cash basis measures cash movement. Accrual basis measures economic activity.

## Eligibility and requirements

### Who can use cash basis?

The rules vary by jurisdiction. In general:

- **Sole traders and small partnerships** can usually choose cash basis up to a certain revenue threshold
- **Limited companies** are typically required to use accrual accounting
- **Larger businesses** above the cash basis threshold must use accrual accounting

Check the specific rules in your jurisdiction, as thresholds and eligibility criteria vary.

### When must you use accrual?

You typically need to switch to accrual accounting when:

- Your business incorporates as a limited company
- Your revenue exceeds the cash basis threshold
- You seek external investment or bank financing
- You need to prepare statutory accounts

## How to choose

### Cash basis is usually better when:

- You are a sole trader or small partnership
- Your transactions are straightforward (no complex inventory, long-term contracts, or significant receivables)
- You want to minimise bookkeeping complexity
- Your cash flow is tight and you want to align tax payments with actual receipts
- You do not need financial statements for external parties

### Accrual basis is usually better when:

- You need an accurate picture of business performance
- You have significant accounts receivable or payable
- You carry inventory
- You are a limited company (often required)
- You plan to seek investment or loans
- Your business is growing and you want to make informed decisions
- You have long-term contracts where revenue recognition matters

### The transition

If you start with cash basis and later switch to accrual, you will need to make transitional adjustments. These adjustments bring in any income that was invoiced but not yet received, and any expenses that were incurred but not yet paid. This can result in a one-off increase in taxable profit in the transition year, so plan for it.

Modern accounting software like [Relentify](/accounting) supports both methods and can help manage the transition. Some platforms even let you run reports on both bases simultaneously so you can compare before committing to a switch.

## Hybrid approaches

In practice, many small businesses use a hybrid approach:

- **Cash basis for tax** — Report income and expenses on a cash basis for tax purposes
- **Accrual-based management reports** — Use accrual figures internally for decision-making

This gives you the tax simplicity of cash basis with the accuracy of accrual for managing your business. It requires maintaining both sets of figures, which is more work but can be worthwhile.

## The bottom line

There is no universally correct answer. Cash basis is simpler and aligns tax with cash flow. Accrual basis is more accurate and better for decision-making. Your choice should depend on your business size, complexity, legal requirements, and what you need from your financial information.

If you are a sole trader with straightforward finances, cash basis is a sensible starting point. As your business grows in complexity, be prepared to transition to accrual. And whatever method you choose, keep your records consistent — switching back and forth creates confusion and makes your historical data less useful.
