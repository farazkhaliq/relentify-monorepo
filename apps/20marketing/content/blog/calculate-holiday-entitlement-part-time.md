Calculating holiday entitlement for full-time employees who work a standard five-day week is straightforward. But the moment you introduce part-time workers, shift patterns, zero-hours contracts, or employees who start mid-year, things get more complicated. Getting it wrong can lead to underpaying employees — which is a legal risk — or overpaying, which is a cost you did not plan for.

This guide explains how to calculate holiday entitlement correctly for every type of worker.

## The statutory minimum

Most jurisdictions set a minimum annual holiday entitlement for employees. This is usually expressed in working days or weeks per year. For example, many countries require a minimum of four weeks of paid annual leave, with some adding additional public holidays on top.

The key principle is that part-time workers are entitled to the same proportion of holiday as full-time workers. A part-time employee working three days a week gets three-fifths of the full-time entitlement — not three days total, but three-fifths of whatever the full-time figure is.

This principle of pro-rata entitlement applies regardless of the employment pattern.

## Calculating for regular part-time workers

If your part-time employee works a fixed number of days each week, the calculation is simple:

**Holiday entitlement = Full-time entitlement × (Part-time days per week ÷ Full-time days per week)**

For example, if your full-time entitlement is 28 days (including public holidays) based on a five-day week:

- An employee working 3 days per week: 28 × (3 ÷ 5) = 16.8 days
- An employee working 2.5 days per week: 28 × (2.5 ÷ 5) = 14 days
- An employee working 4 days per week: 28 × (4 ÷ 5) = 22.4 days

You can round up to the nearest half-day or whole day if your policy allows — you must never round down below the statutory minimum.

## Calculating in hours rather than days

For employees who work fewer hours per day than a full-time worker (for example, working five mornings a week), calculating in days can be misleading. A "day" of holiday for a part-time worker who does four hours means they only use four hours, not eight.

To avoid confusion, many employers calculate holiday in hours:

**Holiday entitlement (hours) = Full-time entitlement (hours) × (Part-time hours per week ÷ Full-time hours per week)**

If full-time is 37.5 hours per week with 28 days (210 hours) of holiday:

- An employee working 20 hours per week: 210 × (20 ÷ 37.5) = 112 hours
- An employee working 15 hours per week: 210 × (15 ÷ 37.5) = 84 hours

Tracking holiday in hours is more accurate and fairer, especially when employees work different shift lengths on different days.

## Shift workers and irregular patterns

Employees who work rotating shifts, alternating weeks, or irregular schedules present a bigger challenge. Their working days change from week to week, making a simple pro-rata calculation difficult.

### The average method

The most common approach for irregular workers is to calculate based on their average working pattern over a reference period. Look at the employee's hours over the previous 12 to 52 weeks (depending on your jurisdiction's rules) and calculate an average.

**Steps:**
1. Count the total hours worked in the reference period
2. Divide by the number of weeks in the reference period to get average weekly hours
3. Apply the same formula: entitlement hours = full-time entitlement hours × (average weekly hours ÷ full-time weekly hours)

For a shift worker who averaged 30 hours per week over a 52-week reference period, with a full-time baseline of 40 hours and 224 hours of holiday:

224 × (30 ÷ 40) = 168 hours of holiday per year

### Accrual method

An alternative for irregular workers is to accrue holiday for each hour worked. Under the statutory minimum of 5.6 weeks, the accrual rate is:

**5.6 weeks ÷ 46.4 working weeks per year = 12.07% of hours worked**

(46.4 = 52 weeks minus 5.6 weeks of holiday)

So for every hour worked, the employee accrues 0.1207 hours of holiday. An employee who works 100 hours in a month accrues 12.07 hours of holiday that month.

This method is particularly useful for zero-hours contracts and casual workers, where there is no guaranteed weekly pattern to base a pro-rata calculation on.

## Zero-hours and casual workers

Workers on zero-hours contracts or casual arrangements still accrue holiday entitlement. They are entitled to the same 5.6 weeks of paid annual leave as any other worker — the challenge is calculating what that means in practice.

The accrual method described above works well:

- Track all hours worked
- Apply the 12.07% accrual rate
- Allow the worker to book holiday from their accrued balance

Some employers use "rolled-up holiday pay," where an additional percentage is added to the worker's hourly rate to cover holiday pay. This means the worker receives their holiday pay with each payment rather than taking paid time off separately. However, this approach is not recommended in all jurisdictions and may not satisfy legal requirements — check your local rules before adopting it.

## Employees who start or leave mid-year

When an employee starts partway through the holiday year, their entitlement is pro-rated based on the number of complete months (or weeks) remaining in the year.

**Mid-year entitlement = Annual entitlement × (Remaining complete months ÷ 12)**

An employee who starts on 1 July with a holiday year running January to December:

28 days × (6 ÷ 12) = 14 days for the remainder of the year

For leavers, the same principle applies in reverse. Calculate the entitlement for the portion of the year worked:

**Leaver entitlement = Annual entitlement × (Complete months worked ÷ 12)**

If the employee has taken more holiday than they have accrued, you can deduct the excess from their final pay — but only if their contract allows this. If they have not taken their full accrued entitlement, you must pay them for the unused balance in their final pay.

## Public holidays and part-time workers

In many jurisdictions, public holidays are included in the statutory minimum rather than being additional to it. This can create confusion for part-time workers.

If your business closes on public holidays, a part-time worker who does not normally work on those days should not lose out. They should receive the pro-rated number of public holiday hours, which they can use on days that suit them.

For example, if there are eight public holidays in the year and a part-time worker works three days per week, they are entitled to:

8 × (3 ÷ 5) = 4.8 days of public holiday equivalent

If the public holidays fall on days they would not normally work, they still get 4.8 days to use on other days.

## Holiday pay: what rate to pay

Holiday pay should reflect what the employee would normally earn. For workers with regular hours and consistent pay, this is straightforward — pay them their normal rate.

For employees with variable pay (overtime, commission, regular bonuses, or irregular hours), holiday pay should be calculated based on their average earnings over a reference period. This ensures they are not financially penalised for taking time off.

The reference period varies by jurisdiction but is commonly 12 to 52 weeks. Use the employee's average weekly pay over that period as the basis for holiday pay.

## Practical tips for managing holiday correctly

- **Track in hours, not days**: This is more accurate for part-time and irregular workers.
- **Use an accrual system**: Especially for variable-hours workers. Track accrued, taken, and remaining balances continuously.
- **Review entitlements annually**: If an employee's hours change during the year, recalculate their entitlement.
- **Communicate clearly**: Make sure every employee knows their holiday entitlement and how to book time off.
- **Plan for public holidays**: Ensure part-time workers are not disadvantaged by the pattern of public holidays.

## Getting it right matters

Holiday entitlement errors are among the most common payroll disputes. Underpaying holiday — particularly for part-time and variable-hours workers — can result in legal claims, back-pay liabilities, and damage to your reputation as an employer.

Take the time to set up your calculations correctly, choose a method that works for your workforce, and automate wherever possible. Your employees have earned their time off — make sure they get every day they are entitled to.
