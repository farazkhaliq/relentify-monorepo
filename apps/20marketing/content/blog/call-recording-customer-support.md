Phone calls are the richest support channel. They capture tone, emotion, urgency, and nuance that text-based channels cannot convey. But they are also the most ephemeral. When a call ends, the only record is whatever the agent managed to type in their notes — which is inevitably a simplified, incomplete version of what actually happened.

Call recording changes this by capturing every word of every interaction. The recording becomes a permanent, searchable record that can be used for quality assurance, agent training, dispute resolution, and compliance.

For support teams that handle any significant volume of phone calls, recording is not optional. It is a fundamental operational tool.

## Why record calls

### Quality assurance

Recorded calls allow managers and QA specialists to review actual interactions, not summaries. They can assess how agents greet callers, handle difficult situations, explain policies, and close conversations. This level of detail is impossible to get from ticket notes alone.

### Agent training

New agents learn faster when they can listen to real calls from experienced colleagues. Recordings of well-handled calls serve as training material. Recordings of poorly-handled calls (reviewed constructively) highlight specific areas for improvement.

### Dispute resolution

When a customer claims they were promised something that differs from your records, the call recording provides an objective account of what was said. This protects both the customer and the business from misunderstandings.

### Compliance

In regulated industries, call recording is often a legal requirement. Financial services, healthcare, and insurance businesses may be required to record and retain customer interactions for specific periods.

### Performance measurement

Call recordings, combined with analytics like talk time, hold time, and resolution outcome, give managers a complete picture of agent performance. Metrics tell you what happened. Recordings tell you how it happened.

## Legal requirements for call recording

Call recording is regulated differently in different jurisdictions. Before implementing recording, you must understand the laws that apply to your business and your customers.

### Consent requirements

**One-party consent:** In some jurisdictions, only one party to the call needs to consent to recording. Since your business is one party, you can record without explicitly asking the customer. However, it is still best practice to inform them.

**Two-party (all-party) consent:** In other jurisdictions, all parties must consent to recording. You must inform the caller that the call is being recorded and obtain their consent before proceeding.

**The safest approach** is to always inform callers that the call may be recorded. A brief IVR message — "This call may be recorded for training and quality purposes" — satisfies most legal requirements and sets expectations.

### Data retention

Recorded calls are personal data. Data protection regulations (like GDPR in Europe or CCPA in California) require you to:

- Store recordings securely
- Retain them only as long as necessary
- Provide access to recordings if the customer requests it
- Delete recordings when the retention period expires or the customer requests deletion

Define a retention policy (typically 30 to 90 days for quality assurance, longer for compliance) and ensure your system automatically deletes recordings after the retention period.

### Employee notification

In many jurisdictions, you must inform agents that their calls are being recorded. This is typically covered in the employment contract or company policy, but verify the requirements in your location.

## Setting up call recording

### Choose your recording method

**Automatic recording:** Every call is recorded by default. This is the simplest approach and ensures no call is missed. Agents do not need to remember to start recording.

**On-demand recording:** Agents manually start and stop recording during a call. This gives agents control but introduces the risk of missed recordings.

**Selective recording:** Rules determine which calls are recorded based on criteria like customer tier, call type, or random sampling for QA.

For most support teams, automatic recording is the recommended approach. It eliminates human error and provides a complete record.

### Integrate with your helpdesk

Call recordings should be linked to tickets in your helpdesk. When an agent handles a phone call, the recording is automatically attached to the corresponding ticket. This allows anyone reviewing the ticket later to listen to the call alongside reading the text-based conversation.

The integration should also capture metadata: call duration, hold time, IVR path selected, and the agent who handled the call.

### Storage and security

Call recordings generate significant data volume. A typical support call produces a 1 to 5 MB audio file. At scale, this adds up quickly.

- Use cloud storage with automatic lifecycle management to move older recordings to cheaper storage tiers
- Encrypt recordings at rest and in transit
- Restrict access to recordings based on role — agents might access their own recordings, managers access their team's, and QA specialists access all recordings
- Implement audit logging so you know who accessed which recordings and when

## Using recordings for quality assurance

### Build a QA scorecard

Define the criteria you will evaluate on recorded calls:

- **Greeting** — Did the agent introduce themselves and the company?
- **Active listening** — Did the agent let the customer explain without interrupting?
- **Empathy** — Did the agent acknowledge the customer's frustration or situation?
- **Accuracy** — Was the information provided correct?
- **Resolution** — Was the issue resolved effectively?
- **Closing** — Did the agent confirm the resolution and ask if there was anything else?
- **Compliance** — Were required disclosures and verification steps followed?

Score each criterion on a simple scale (meets expectations, needs improvement, exceeds expectations) and track scores over time.

### Sample strategically

You do not need to review every call. A representative sample provides sufficient data for QA purposes. Common sampling strategies:

- **Random sampling** — Review a random selection of calls per agent per week
- **Targeted sampling** — Review calls flagged by specific criteria (long duration, low CSAT score, customer escalation)
- **New agent sampling** — Review a higher percentage of calls from agents in their first 90 days

### Provide constructive feedback

When reviewing calls with agents, focus on specific behaviours rather than general impressions. "In the second minute, when the customer mentioned the overcharge, you could have acknowledged their frustration before jumping to the solution" is more useful than "You need to be more empathetic."

Play specific segments of the recording during feedback sessions so the agent can hear exactly what you are referring to.

## AI-enhanced call recording

Modern call recording platforms increasingly incorporate AI to extract additional value from recordings.

### Automatic transcription

AI converts recordings to searchable text transcripts. This allows you to search across thousands of calls for specific keywords, phrases, or topics without listening to each one.

### Sentiment analysis

AI analyses the tone and language of both the caller and the agent throughout the call, identifying moments of frustration, satisfaction, or confusion. This helps QA teams focus their review on the most critical moments.

### Keyword spotting

Automatically flag calls that mention specific terms: competitor names, cancellation, legal action, or compliance-related phrases. This surfaces high-risk interactions for immediate review.

### Call summarisation

AI generates a summary of the call, capturing the key issue, actions taken, and outcome. This provides a quick reference without listening to the full recording.

## Common challenges

### Storage costs

High-volume teams generate terabytes of recordings. Implement automatic archival and deletion policies to manage costs. Recordings older than your retention period should be automatically deleted.

### Agent resistance

Some agents feel uncomfortable being recorded. Address this transparently: explain the purpose (quality improvement, not surveillance), share how recordings will be used, and involve agents in the QA process so it feels collaborative rather than punitive.

### Recording quality

Poor audio quality makes recordings useless. Test your recording system regularly, ensure adequate bandwidth for VoIP calls, and address any quality issues promptly.

## Getting started

1. Verify the legal requirements for call recording in your jurisdiction
2. Configure automatic recording on your phone system
3. Integrate recordings with your helpdesk so they attach to tickets
4. Define a retention policy and configure automatic deletion
5. Build a QA scorecard and begin regular reviews
6. Set access controls and audit logging

Call recording is one of those capabilities that, once implemented, you wonder how you ever managed without. The insights it provides into customer interactions, agent performance, and operational quality are impossible to replicate through any other means.
