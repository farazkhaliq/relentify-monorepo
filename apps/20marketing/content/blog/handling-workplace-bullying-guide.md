# Handling Workplace Bullying: A Practical Guide for Employers

Workplace bullying is more common than most employers realise or want to acknowledge. It takes many forms — from overt aggression and intimidation to subtle exclusion and undermining that can be difficult to identify but equally damaging. Left unchecked, bullying destroys individual wellbeing, poisons team dynamics, increases absenteeism and turnover, and exposes the business to legal liability.

As an employer, you have a duty to prevent bullying, act when it occurs, and create an environment where it cannot take root. This guide provides practical steps for handling workplace bullying effectively.

## Recognising Workplace Bullying

Bullying is persistent behaviour that intimidates, degrades, offends, or humiliates an individual. A single incident of rudeness, while unacceptable, is not usually classified as bullying. The pattern of behaviour — repeated and targeted — is what distinguishes bullying from ordinary workplace friction.

### Common Forms

**Overt bullying** is the most recognisable: shouting, threatening, public humiliation, aggressive criticism, or physical intimidation. These behaviours are clearly unacceptable in any workplace.

**Covert bullying** is harder to detect but equally harmful: consistently excluding someone from meetings or social events, spreading rumours, withholding information needed to do their job, taking credit for their work, setting unrealistic deadlines designed to cause failure, or subtly undermining their confidence through persistent negative comments disguised as jokes or "feedback."

**Cyber bullying** occurs through digital channels: hostile emails, exclusion from group chats, sharing embarrassing information online, or monitoring someone's activity to an unreasonable degree.

**Managerial bullying** is particularly damaging because the bully has positional power. It can look like excessive micromanagement, unfair allocation of undesirable tasks, blocking promotion or development opportunities, or using disciplinary processes as a weapon.

### Warning Signs

Even if nobody reports bullying directly, watch for these indicators:

- An employee's performance or attendance suddenly deteriorates
- Someone who was previously engaged and confident becomes withdrawn
- Team dynamics visibly change — people are tense, guarded, or avoid certain individuals
- Turnover increases in a specific team or under a specific manager
- Informal complaints or "off the record" comments from team members
- Increased use of sick leave, particularly in one team

## Creating an Anti-Bullying Policy

A clear policy is your foundation. Without one, handling complaints is inconsistent and legally vulnerable.

### Policy Elements

**Definition:** Define what constitutes bullying in your workplace, with examples. Include overt, covert, and cyber bullying. Distinguish between bullying and legitimate management actions (performance management, reasonable instructions, fair criticism of work).

**Scope:** The policy applies to all employees, contractors, and visitors. It covers behaviour in the workplace, at work events, online in work contexts, and anywhere that behaviour affects working relationships.

**Reporting:** Explain how employees can report bullying — to their manager, to HR, to a named individual, or through an anonymous reporting mechanism. Provide multiple channels so that victims are not forced to report to the person bullying them.

**Investigation:** Outline the investigation process — who investigates, how complaints are handled, what the timeline is, and how confidentiality is maintained.

**Consequences:** State clearly that bullying will result in disciplinary action, up to and including dismissal. This is not about being punitive — it is about making the standard clear.

**Support:** Describe the support available to anyone involved — counselling, mediation, Employee Assistance Programme, occupational health.

**No retaliation:** State explicitly that retaliation against someone who reports bullying will be treated as a disciplinary matter. Fear of retaliation is the single biggest barrier to reporting.

### Communication

A policy in a drawer helps nobody. Ensure every employee has read and acknowledged the policy. Include it in induction for new starters. Discuss it in team meetings. Make it visible and accessible.

## Handling a Bullying Complaint

When an employee comes to you with a bullying complaint, your response in the first few minutes sets the tone for everything that follows.

### Initial Response

**Listen.** Give the employee your full attention. Do not interrupt, minimise, or suggest that they might be overreacting. Even if the behaviour they describe does not sound like bullying to you, their experience is real to them.

**Thank them.** Acknowledge that coming forward takes courage, especially if the complaint is about someone more senior.

**Ask what they want.** Some employees want a formal investigation. Others want informal intervention. Some simply want to be heard and have their experience validated. Understanding their preference helps you choose the right approach.

**Explain next steps.** Tell them what will happen next, who will be involved, and how confidentiality will be handled. Do not make promises you cannot keep.

**Document everything.** Make detailed notes of the complaint, the date, the witnesses mentioned, and the employee's desired outcome.

### Informal Resolution

Not every complaint requires a formal investigation. If the employee prefers an informal approach and the behaviour is relatively low-level, options include:

- The manager having a private conversation with the accused about expectations
- Mediation between the parties (only if both are willing and the power dynamic allows it)
- Coaching for the accused on their interpersonal behaviour
- Separating the parties (different shifts, different projects, different office areas)

Informal resolution can be faster and less stressful for everyone. However, document the complaint and the action taken, even if the process is informal. If the behaviour continues, you need a record.

### Formal Investigation

For serious complaints, repeated behaviour, or situations where informal resolution has not worked, a formal investigation is necessary.

**Appoint an investigator** who is impartial — not the accused's manager, not a close friend of either party. In small businesses, this may need to be an external person.

**Gather evidence:** Interview the complainant in detail, interview the accused (giving them the right to be accompanied), interview witnesses, and review any documentary evidence (emails, messages, records).

**Maintain confidentiality** to the greatest extent possible. Both parties should be informed that the investigation is confidential and that breaching confidentiality could itself be a disciplinary matter.

**Be timely.** Investigations that drag on for months cause additional stress and damage. Set a target of completing the investigation within two to four weeks unless complexity requires more time.

**Reach a conclusion.** Based on the evidence, determine on the balance of probabilities whether bullying occurred. Document your reasoning clearly.

**Take action.** If bullying is substantiated, the consequences should be proportionate but meaningful — a warning, mandatory training, transfer, or dismissal depending on severity. If not substantiated, explain the conclusion to the complainant with care and sensitivity.

## Supporting Everyone Involved

### The Complainant

Regardless of the investigation outcome, the person who reported bullying needs ongoing support:

- Check in regularly on their wellbeing
- Offer counselling or EAP services
- Monitor for any retaliation
- Be willing to make workplace adjustments (different reporting line, changed seating, flexible working)

### The Accused

Being accused of bullying is stressful, even if the accusation is substantiated. The accused deserves:

- Fair process and the right to respond to allegations
- Confidentiality during the investigation
- Support (counselling, representation) during the process
- Clear communication about the outcome and any required behaviour changes
- An opportunity to learn and improve if the behaviour is confirmed

### The Team

Bullying rarely happens in a vacuum. The wider team is usually affected, even if they are not directly involved. Address the team dynamic:

- Without breaching confidentiality, acknowledge that an issue has been addressed
- Reiterate workplace behaviour standards
- Offer team support if relationships have been damaged
- Monitor for lingering tension or sides forming

## Prevention

The best approach to bullying is preventing it from happening in the first place.

**Hire for values.** In recruitment, assess interpersonal skills and values alongside technical competence. Someone who is brilliant at their job but toxic to colleagues is a net negative.

**Train managers.** Most managerial bullying stems from poor management skills rather than malicious intent. Train managers in communication, feedback, conflict resolution, and emotional intelligence.

**Build a speak-up culture.** Regularly encourage employees to raise concerns early. Demonstrate through action that concerns are taken seriously and addressed.

**Monitor absence and turnover.** Use your [HR and workforce data](https://relentify.com/features/timesheets) to spot teams with unusual absence or turnover patterns — these can be early indicators of a bullying problem.

**Model the behaviour you expect.** If senior leaders communicate respectfully, accept feedback gracefully, and treat everyone with dignity, this standard cascades through the organisation.

Workplace bullying is a serious issue that demands a serious response. With clear policies, prompt and fair investigation processes, genuine support for all parties, and a culture that does not tolerate intimidation, you can create a workplace where everyone feels safe, respected, and able to do their best work.
