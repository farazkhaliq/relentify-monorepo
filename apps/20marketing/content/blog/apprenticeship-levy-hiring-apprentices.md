Apprenticeships have evolved significantly in recent years. They are no longer limited to traditional trades — apprenticeship programmes now cover everything from digital marketing and software development to accounting and project management. For small businesses, hiring apprentices can be a cost-effective way to develop the skills you need while benefiting from government funding.

This guide explains how the Apprenticeship Levy works and how small businesses can hire apprentices.

## What is the Apprenticeship Levy?

The Apprenticeship Levy is a tax on larger employers to fund apprenticeship training. It applies to employers with an annual pay bill above a specified threshold (currently £3 million).

### If your pay bill exceeds the threshold

You pay 0.5% of your total pay bill, minus a levy allowance. The levy is paid through PAYE alongside your normal tax and NI payments.

The money goes into a digital apprenticeship service account, which you can use to fund apprenticeship training for your employees. Unused funds expire after 24 months.

### If your pay bill is below the threshold

Most small businesses do not pay the levy. However, you can still hire apprentices and access significant government funding for training costs.

Non-levy-paying employers typically co-invest — the government funds the majority of the training cost (usually 95%), and the employer pays the remaining 5%. For small employers (fewer than 50 employees), the government may fund 100% of the training costs for apprentices aged 16-18 or 19-24 with certain characteristics.

## How apprenticeships work

An apprenticeship is a job that combines practical on-the-job training with off-the-job learning. The apprentice is employed by you, earns a wage, and works towards a recognised qualification.

### Key features

- **Minimum duration**: At least 12 months
- **Off-the-job training**: At least 20% of the apprentice's working time must be spent on training
- **Training provider**: Training is delivered by an approved provider (a college, university, or specialist training company)
- **Assessment**: Apprentices are assessed at the end of the programme through an independent end-point assessment
- **Qualification**: The apprenticeship results in a nationally recognised qualification at a specified level

### Apprenticeship levels

| Level | Equivalent to |
|-------|--------------|
| Level 2 (Intermediate) | GCSEs |
| Level 3 (Advanced) | A-levels |
| Level 4-5 (Higher) | Foundation degree |
| Level 6-7 (Degree) | Bachelor's or Master's degree |

There are hundreds of apprenticeship standards available across dozens of industries and job roles.

## Hiring an apprentice: the process

### Step 1: Identify the role

Think about what skills your business needs. Apprenticeships work best when there is a genuine role that the apprentice will fill and develop in over time. Browse the available apprenticeship standards to find one that matches the role.

### Step 2: Find a training provider

Choose an approved training provider who will deliver the off-the-job training element. Consider:
- Do they offer the apprenticeship standard you need?
- What is their reputation and success rate?
- How flexible is their delivery (classroom, online, blended)?
- What support do they provide to employers?

### Step 3: Recruit the apprentice

You can recruit an apprentice through:
- The government's apprenticeship recruitment service
- Direct applications through your own recruitment channels
- Via the training provider, who may have a pool of candidates
- Converting an existing employee into an apprentice (upskilling)

Apprentices must be at least 16 years old. There is no upper age limit — you can offer apprenticeships to employees of any age who have not already achieved a qualification at the same or higher level in the same subject.

### Step 4: Set up the apprenticeship

Once you have recruited the apprentice and selected a training provider:

- Sign an apprenticeship agreement with the apprentice (a legal requirement)
- Agree a training plan with the training provider
- Register the apprenticeship on the digital apprenticeship service
- Set up the apprentice on your [payroll](/payroll) system

### Step 5: Support the apprentice

During the apprenticeship:
- Provide meaningful work that develops the skills covered by the standard
- Allow time for off-the-job training (minimum 20% of working hours)
- Assign a mentor or supervisor
- Conduct regular progress reviews
- Provide feedback and support

## Costs and funding

### Training costs
The cost of training varies by apprenticeship standard and level. The government publishes funding bands that set the maximum it will contribute.

For non-levy employers:
- The government pays 95% of the training cost (up to the funding band maximum)
- You pay the remaining 5%
- For employers with fewer than 50 employees hiring apprentices aged 16-18, the government pays 100%

### Wages
You pay the apprentice a wage. The minimum wage for apprentices is set by the government and is lower than the standard minimum wage. However, many employers pay above the apprentice minimum to attract better candidates.

The apprentice's wages are paid through your normal [payroll](/payroll) and are subject to tax and NI in the usual way.

### Additional incentives
The government periodically offers additional incentive payments for hiring apprentices — for example, a cash payment for hiring a young apprentice or someone with a disability. Check the current incentives available.

## Benefits of hiring apprentices

### Developing skills your business needs
Apprenticeships let you train someone in exactly the skills your business requires, rather than hoping to find someone with the perfect CV.

### Cost-effective recruitment
With government funding covering most or all of the training costs, apprenticeships are one of the most affordable ways to bring new talent into your business.

### Fresh perspective
Apprentices bring enthusiasm and a willingness to learn. They often challenge the status quo and bring new ideas.

### Loyalty
Employees who train with you from the start often develop strong loyalty to the business. Retention rates for apprentices are high.

### Upskilling existing staff
Apprenticeships are not just for new hires. You can use them to develop existing employees, giving them recognised qualifications and new skills.

## Common concerns

### "We are too small for apprentices"
There is no minimum business size. Businesses with as few as one or two employees successfully hire apprentices. The key is having a genuine role with real work and someone to provide supervision and support.

### "We cannot afford to pay someone while they train"
Remember that the apprentice is working for you while they train — they are contributing to the business from day one. The training is largely or fully funded by the government. The net cost is often less than hiring a fully qualified person at full salary.

### "The off-the-job training will disrupt operations"
Plan for the 20% off-the-job requirement from the start. Many training providers offer flexible delivery — one day a week, block release, or online learning — that can be scheduled around business needs.

### "What if the apprentice is not good enough?"
Apprentices are employees and subject to the same expectations as any other employee. If performance is not adequate after proper support and feedback, you manage it through your normal performance procedures. The probation period applies to apprentices just as it does to other employees.

## Processing apprentice payroll

Apprentices are processed through payroll like any other employee, with a few considerations:

- Apply the correct minimum wage (apprentice rate or standard rate, depending on age and year of apprenticeship)
- Use the correct NI category — apprentices under 25 may use category H, which has a reduced employer NI rate
- Deduct tax, pension contributions, and other deductions as normal
- Track and report via RTI as usual

## The bottom line

Apprenticeships are an underused resource for small businesses. The government funds most of the training, the apprentice contributes to your business from day one, and you develop exactly the skills you need. If you have a role that could benefit from a motivated person learning on the job, an apprenticeship is worth serious consideration.
