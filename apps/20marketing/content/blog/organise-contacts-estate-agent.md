An estate agency's contact database is one of its most valuable assets. Every landlord, tenant, applicant, contractor, solicitor, and referral partner represents a relationship that has been built over time — and each one has the potential to generate future business.

Yet in many agencies, contacts are scattered across email accounts, phone address books, spreadsheets, sticky notes, and the memories of individual staff members. When a team member leaves, their contacts often leave with them. When a landlord calls and the usual contact is on holiday, nobody knows the full history.

Organising your contacts is not glamorous work. But it is foundational. Everything else — pipeline management, marketing, compliance tracking, relationship management — depends on having a clean, structured, and accessible contact database.

## The problem with unstructured contacts

Most agencies do not have a contact problem — they have a contact organisation problem. The contacts exist. They are just spread across too many places, stored inconsistently, and maintained by nobody in particular.

The consequences are predictable. Two agents independently add the same landlord to their phone, each with slightly different details. A contractor's updated phone number is saved on one person's device but not in the shared system. An applicant who enquired six months ago is buried in an email archive, and when a property matching their requirements becomes available, nobody thinks to contact them.

These are not technology problems. They are organisation problems. And they are solved by establishing a clear structure for how contacts are stored, categorised, and maintained.

## Building a contact taxonomy

The first step is defining the types of contacts your agency works with and creating a consistent way to categorise them. For most estate agencies, the primary categories are as follows.

**Landlords.** Property owners who use your agency for management or lettings services. Each landlord record should link to their properties, tenancies, and financial history.

**Tenants.** Current tenants in properties you manage. Each tenant record should link to their tenancy agreement, the property they occupy, and their communication history.

**Applicants.** Prospective tenants who have enquired about properties but have not yet secured a tenancy. Applicants may be active (currently looking) or dormant (enquired in the past, may look again).

**Contractors.** Plumbers, electricians, cleaners, decorators, locksmiths, and other tradespeople you work with. Each contractor record should include their trade, contact details, availability, and any notes on their reliability and quality.

**Professional contacts.** Solicitors, mortgage brokers, inventory clerks, referencing companies, and other professionals you work alongside. These relationships often generate referrals in both directions.

**Suppliers.** Companies that provide services to your agency — software providers, insurance companies, portal subscriptions, and so on.

Each contact should be assigned to one or more categories. A person who is both a landlord and a tenant, for example, should appear in both contexts without requiring duplicate records.

## Essential data fields

Consistent data entry is the difference between a useful database and a cluttered one. For each contact, define the minimum required fields and ensure that every team member populates them.

For all contacts, the basics include full name, primary phone number, primary email address, category, and the date the contact was created.

For landlords, add their property portfolio, management agreement type, fee structure, preferred communication method, and any compliance notes.

For tenants, add their tenancy start and end dates, rent amount, deposit details, and emergency contact information.

For applicants, add their requirements — property type, preferred location, budget, desired move-in date, and the source of their enquiry.

For contractors, add their trade or specialty, hourly or job rate, insurance and certification details, and your agency's rating of their work.

The key is consistency. If one agent records phone numbers with country codes and another does not, searching becomes unreliable. If one agent uses "John Smith" and another uses "Smith, John," duplicates will proliferate. Establish conventions and enforce them.

## Deduplication and data hygiene

Even with good conventions, duplicates will creep in over time. People change phone numbers, email addresses, and names. The same person might enquire through different channels — the website, a portal, and a phone call — and be entered three times.

Regular deduplication is essential. Most CRM systems offer tools to identify potential duplicates based on matching names, email addresses, or phone numbers. Schedule a monthly review to merge duplicates and update stale records.

Data hygiene also means removing contacts that are no longer relevant. A tenant who moved out three years ago and has not been in touch since does not need to be in your active database — although you may want to archive rather than delete, in case they return.

## Tags and custom fields

Categories alone are often not enough to capture the nuances of your contacts. Tags and custom fields add an additional layer of organisation.

Tags are freeform labels that you can apply to any contact. A landlord might be tagged as "overseas investor," "portfolio landlord," "HMO," or "considering sale." An applicant might be tagged as "pet owner," "guarantor required," or "immediate mover."

Custom fields let you capture data that is specific to your agency's workflow. Perhaps you track how each landlord was acquired — referral, portal, networking event — to understand which channels are most effective. Perhaps you track each contractor's response time to help you choose the right person for urgent jobs.

The value of tags and custom fields emerges over time, as you accumulate enough data to spot patterns and make better decisions.

## Linking contacts to properties and tenancies

In a property context, contacts rarely exist in isolation. A landlord is connected to their properties. A tenant is connected to their tenancy. A contractor is connected to the jobs they have completed.

Your contact system should reflect these relationships. When you open a landlord's record, you should see every property they own, every active and historical tenancy, and every communication you have had with them. When you open a property record, you should see the landlord, the current tenant, upcoming compliance deadlines, and maintenance history.

These links turn a flat list of contacts into a navigable web of relationships — and that web is far more useful than any spreadsheet.

## Access and permissions

Not everyone in your agency needs access to every contact. The lettings team needs to see landlords, tenants, and applicants. The maintenance coordinator needs to see contractors. The accounts team needs to see financial contacts.

A good CRM lets you control who can see what, ensuring that sensitive information — landlord bank details, tenant personal data — is only accessible to those who need it. This is not just good practice; it is a requirement under data protection regulations.

## Making it a habit

The best contact management system in the world is useless if your team does not use it. Adoption requires two things: simplicity and accountability.

Simplicity means making it as easy as possible to add and update contacts. If it takes ten clicks to add a new contact, people will skip it. If it takes two, they will do it routinely.

Accountability means making contact management part of the job, not an optional extra. If a new landlord instruction comes in and the landlord is not added to the CRM within 24 hours, that is a process failure. If an agent conducts a viewing and does not log the feedback, that is a missed step.

Regular pipeline reviews — where the team walks through their active contacts and updates statuses — reinforce the habit and ensure data quality.
