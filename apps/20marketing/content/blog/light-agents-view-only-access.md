Your support team is not the only part of your business that needs visibility into customer issues. The sales team wants to know if a prospect has open support tickets before a renewal call. The product team wants to see what customers are complaining about. Engineering needs to investigate bug reports. Finance needs to verify refund requests. Management wants to monitor escalations.

But giving all of these people full agent access creates problems. They might accidentally reply to a customer. They clutter the queue by appearing as available agents. They consume expensive agent licences. And they have access to actions — closing tickets, changing priorities, reassigning — that they should not be performing.

Light agents (sometimes called collaborators, observers, or restricted agents) solve this by providing limited helpdesk access to people who need visibility without the ability to handle tickets directly.

## What light agents can do

The specific capabilities vary by helpdesk platform, but light agents typically have access to:

- **View tickets** — Read the full conversation history, including customer messages and agent replies
- **Add internal notes** — Contribute information, context, or instructions without the customer seeing the message
- **View customer profiles** — See the customer's contact information, ticket history, and account details
- **Search and filter** — Find specific tickets using the same search and filtering tools available to full agents

## What light agents cannot do

Light agents are intentionally restricted from actions that should only be performed by trained support staff:

- **Reply to customers** — They cannot send messages that the customer will see
- **Change ticket status** — They cannot open, close, or pending a ticket
- **Reassign tickets** — They cannot move tickets between agents or teams
- **Change priority or other fields** — They cannot modify ticket properties
- **Access macros or automation tools** — They interact with tickets in a read-only capacity (plus notes)
- **Appear in the agent queue** — They do not receive ticket assignments

## Who benefits from light agent access

### Sales team

Sales representatives need to know the support health of their accounts. Before a renewal conversation, checking whether the customer has a string of unresolved complaints is essential context. Light agent access lets sales view tickets associated with their accounts without interfering with the support workflow.

### Product team

Product managers who can read support tickets gain direct insight into customer pain points, feature requests, and product usability issues. Instead of relying on second-hand summaries from the support team, they can read the customer's words directly.

### Engineering team

When a bug is reported, engineers often need to see the original customer description, the error messages reported, and the troubleshooting steps already attempted. Light agent access lets them review the ticket and add internal notes (like "fix deployed in v4.3.2") without handling the customer communication.

### Finance team

Refund requests, billing disputes, and payment issues often need finance team verification. Light agents in finance can review the ticket, add a note confirming or denying the refund, and leave the customer communication to the support agent.

### Management

Executives and department heads who want to monitor support quality, escalations, or VIP customer issues can do so through light agent access without being in the operational flow.

### External partners

In some cases, external consultants, contractors, or partner companies need limited visibility into your support system. Light agent access provides this without granting full system access.

## The cost advantage

Most helpdesk platforms charge per agent seat. Full agent licences are typically the most expensive tier because they include all capabilities. Light agent licences are significantly cheaper — often 50 to 80 percent less than a full agent seat, and some platforms include a number of light agent seats for free.

This cost difference makes it practical to give helpdesk visibility to dozens of people across your organisation without a proportional increase in licensing costs.

For a company with 10 full agents and 30 people across other departments who occasionally need ticket visibility, the difference between 40 full licences and 10 full plus 30 light licences can be thousands of dollars per year.

## Setting up light agents

### Define who needs access

Start by listing the roles outside your support team that regularly need ticket visibility. For each role, document why they need access and what they need to do (view, add notes, or both).

### Configure permissions

Most helpdesks allow you to define what light agents can see and do:

- **Ticket visibility scope** — Can they see all tickets, or only tickets related to their department, team, or accounts?
- **Note permissions** — Can they add internal notes, or is their access strictly read-only?
- **Customer data access** — Can they view customer profiles and contact information?
- **Report access** — Can they view reports, or is that restricted to full agents and managers?

Set permissions conservatively. Start with the minimum access needed and expand only if there is a clear need.

### Provide guidance

Light agents are not support professionals. They may not understand your ticketing workflow, your tagging conventions, or the significance of different ticket statuses. Provide a brief orientation that covers:

- How to find relevant tickets (search, filters, views)
- How to add useful internal notes
- What not to do (and what to do if they accidentally take an action they should not have)
- Who to contact if they have questions about the system

### Monitor usage

Track how light agents are using the system. Are they logging in regularly? Are their internal notes useful to the support team? Is anyone requesting additional permissions they do not need?

## Internal notes from light agents

The internal note capability is what makes light agents genuinely useful rather than just passive observers. Here are examples of high-value internal notes from different departments:

**Sales:** "This customer is in renewal discussions. Please prioritise their ticket and keep me updated."

**Engineering:** "Confirmed this is a known bug tracked in issue #4521. Fix is scheduled for the next release on March 15."

**Finance:** "Refund of $240 approved. Please process and confirm with the customer."

**Product:** "This is the fifth request for bulk export this month. Adding to the Q2 roadmap discussion."

**Legal:** "This customer has referenced regulatory requirements. Please ensure our response is compliant with GDPR Article 17."

These notes enrich the ticket with cross-departmental context that the support agent would not have on their own.

## Common mistakes

### Giving too many people access

Not everyone who expresses curiosity about support tickets needs access. Each light agent is another person with visibility into customer data, which has privacy implications. Grant access based on demonstrated business need, not casual interest.

### Not scoping visibility

A light agent in the sales department should probably see tickets from their own accounts, not the entire ticket database. Use scoping rules to limit visibility to relevant tickets.

### Neglecting light agent onboarding

Dropping someone into a helpdesk without context leads to confusion and unhelpful notes. Take 15 minutes to orient new light agents on how the system works and what is expected of them.

### Treating internal notes as a chat room

Internal notes are part of the ticket record. They may be reviewed during audits, escalations, or quality reviews. Light agents should keep notes professional, relevant, and concise.

## The organisational benefit

Light agents break down the silos between support and the rest of the business. When sales can see support tickets, they anticipate customer concerns during renewals. When product can read feedback firsthand, they build features that address real pain points. When engineering can investigate bugs through the ticket, resolution is faster.

The support team benefits too. Instead of fielding constant internal requests for ticket updates ("Can you check on account X?"), they can point stakeholders to the helpdesk where they can check for themselves.

The result is a more connected organisation where customer insights flow freely, collaboration happens within the ticket record, and the support team is not the sole gatekeeper of customer information.
