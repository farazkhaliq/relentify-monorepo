Running payroll in one state is manageable. Running payroll across two, five, or twenty states introduces a layer of complexity that catches many growing businesses off guard. Each state has its own income tax rates, unemployment insurance rules, wage and hour laws, and reporting requirements. What is straightforward in your home state may work entirely differently in the next one.

Whether you are opening a second office, hiring remote workers in other states, or sending employees to work on projects across state lines, multi-state payroll requires systematic processes and careful attention to detail. This guide explains what you need to know.

## When multi-state payroll applies

You have multi-state payroll obligations when you have employees physically working in more than one state. Common scenarios include:

**Remote employees.** If you hire an employee who works from their home in a different state, you generally have payroll tax obligations in that employee's state of residence and work.

**Multiple office locations.** If you operate offices, stores, or facilities in more than one state, you have obligations in each state where employees work.

**Traveling employees.** Employees who regularly travel to other states for work — sales representatives, consultants, construction workers — may trigger payroll obligations in those states.

**Temporary projects.** Sending employees to work on a project in another state, even temporarily, can create payroll tax obligations depending on the duration and the state's rules.

## State income tax withholding

### The general rule

Employers must withhold state income tax based on where the employee performs work. If an employee works entirely in one state, you withhold that state's income tax. If an employee works in multiple states, you may need to withhold income tax for each state based on the wages earned in that state.

### States with no income tax

Nine states currently impose no state income tax on wages: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (limited to interest and dividends), South Dakota, Tennessee (limited to interest and dividends), Texas, Washington, and Wyoming.

Employees working in these states have no state income tax withholding — though they may still have other state payroll obligations.

### Reciprocal agreements

Some pairs of neighboring states have reciprocal agreements that simplify withholding for employees who live in one state and work in another. Under a reciprocal agreement, the employee is only subject to withholding in their state of residence, not their state of work.

**Example:** If State A and State B have a reciprocal agreement, and an employee lives in State A but commutes to work in State B, the employer withholds State A income tax only.

The employee must typically file an exemption form with the employer to claim reciprocity. Without it, the employer must withhold for the work state.

### States without reciprocal agreements

When no reciprocal agreement exists, the employee may owe income tax to both the state where they work and the state where they live. The work state typically has the primary claim to tax. The residence state usually provides a credit for taxes paid to the work state, reducing or eliminating double taxation for the employee.

**Employer obligation:** Withhold income tax for the work state. The residence state withholding depends on whether the residence state also requires withholding — many do.

## State unemployment insurance (SUI)

Each state administers its own unemployment insurance program with its own:

- **Tax rates** — Based on your experience rating in that state
- **Wage bases** — The maximum amount of each employee's wages subject to SUI tax
- **Filing requirements** — Quarterly wage reports to each state's unemployment agency
- **Registration requirements** — You must register as an employer in each state

### Which state gets the SUI tax?

The general rules for determining which state receives the SUI tax for an employee are:

1. **Localization:** The state where the employee's work is localized — where they perform most of their services
2. **Base of operations:** If work is not localized in any state, the state from which the employee's work is directed or controlled
3. **Place of direction and control:** The state from which the employer directs the work
4. **State of residence:** If none of the above applies, the employee's state of residence

For most employees who work in a single state, the answer is straightforward. For traveling employees or remote workers, the analysis requires more attention.

## Registration requirements

When you have employees in a new state, you typically need to:

1. **Register for state income tax withholding** with the state's department of revenue or taxation
2. **Register for state unemployment insurance** with the state's labor or workforce agency
3. **Obtain any local or municipal tax registrations** required by the specific city or county
4. **Register for workers' compensation** in the new state (requirements vary by state)
5. **Comply with new-hire reporting** requirements in the state

Each registration creates ongoing filing and payment obligations. Missing a registration is a common mistake for businesses expanding into new states — and the penalties for non-registration can be significant.

## Compliance challenges

### Differing pay frequency requirements

States set their own rules for how often employees must be paid. Some require weekly or biweekly payment, while others allow monthly or semi-monthly. If you have employees in multiple states, your payroll schedule must comply with the most frequent requirement — or you need to run different pay schedules for different states.

### Varying wage and hour laws

Beyond minimum wage (covered in our separate guide), states differ on:

- **Overtime rules** — Some states require daily overtime (over 8 hours in a day), not just weekly
- **Meal and rest break requirements** — Some states mandate specific break durations and timing
- **Final paycheck deadlines** — States set different deadlines for when terminated employees must receive their last paycheck, ranging from immediately to the next regular payday
- **Pay stub requirements** — The information that must appear on a pay stub varies by state

### Benefit mandates

States increasingly mandate specific employee benefits:

- **Paid sick leave** — A growing number of states require employers to provide paid sick leave
- **Paid family and medical leave** — Several states have enacted mandatory paid leave programs funded through payroll deductions
- **State disability insurance** — A handful of states require short-term disability contributions
- **Retirement plan mandates** — Some states require employers without a retirement plan to enroll employees in a state-run retirement savings program

Each mandate creates its own registration, contribution, and reporting requirements.

## Managing multi-state payroll effectively

### Centralize your payroll processing

Attempting to manage payroll state by state using manual processes is a recipe for errors. Centralize your payroll through a single system that handles multi-state calculations, withholding, and filing.

Modern payroll platforms handle most of the complexity automatically — calculating the correct withholding rates, applying state-specific rules, and generating the required filings for each jurisdiction. An integrated platform like Relentify that combines payroll with accounting and HR ensures that multi-state payroll data flows seamlessly into your financial records and compliance reporting.

### Maintain a state-by-state compliance checklist

For each state where you have employees, maintain a record of:

- Your registration numbers (withholding account, unemployment account)
- Applicable tax rates and wage bases
- Filing frequencies and deadlines
- Wage and hour requirements (minimum wage, overtime, breaks)
- Benefit mandates (sick leave, family leave, disability)
- New-hire reporting requirements

Update this checklist at least annually, as rates and rules change frequently.

### Track employee work locations

For remote workers and traveling employees, maintain records of where each employee works. This is critical for determining the correct withholding and SUI obligations.

For employees who work in multiple states, track the days or hours worked in each state. Some states have threshold rules — an employee may not trigger withholding obligations until they have worked in the state for a specified number of days.

### Plan for nexus implications

Having employees in a state can create nexus — a tax presence that triggers obligations beyond payroll tax. This may include state corporate income tax, sales tax collection obligations, and business registration requirements. Consult with a tax professional when expanding your workforce into a new state.

### Budget for compliance costs

Multi-state payroll increases your administrative burden. Budget for:

- Payroll software capable of multi-state processing
- State registration fees
- Professional tax preparation for multi-state filings
- Potential legal or accounting advice for complex situations

## Common multi-state payroll mistakes

**Not registering in a new state.** Hiring a remote employee in a state where you have not previously had workers requires registration. Operating without registration leads to penalties and back-tax liability.

**Applying the wrong state's rules.** Using your home state's tax rate, minimum wage, or overtime rules for employees in other states is a compliance violation. Each employee must be governed by the laws of their work location.

**Ignoring local taxes.** Many cities and counties impose additional taxes that are easy to overlook. If your employee works in a city with a local income tax, you must withhold and remit it.

**Failing to update for rate changes.** State tax rates, unemployment rates, and minimum wages change regularly. Using outdated rates throughout the year creates errors that must be corrected.

**Not tracking remote worker locations.** If a remote employee moves to a different state, your payroll obligations change. Establish a policy requiring employees to notify you of any change in work location.

## Getting started

If you are expanding into your first additional state:

1. Identify all the states where your employees will work
2. Register with each state's tax and unemployment agencies
3. Determine the correct withholding rates and rules for each state
4. Ensure your payroll system supports multi-state processing
5. Establish a process for tracking employee work locations
6. Set up a compliance calendar with filing deadlines for every jurisdiction
7. Consult with a payroll professional or employment attorney for complex situations

Multi-state payroll is one of the more complex aspects of running a growing US business, but it is manageable with the right systems and processes. The key is to treat each state as a separate compliance environment, automate as much as possible, and stay current on the rules that affect your employees.
