An employment contract is one of the most important documents in the relationship between employer and employee. It sets the terms of employment, protects both parties, and provides a reference point when questions or disputes arise. Yet many small businesses either use inadequate templates, copy contracts from the internet without understanding them, or skip written contracts altogether.

This guide explains what belongs in an employment contract and why each element matters.

## Why you need a written contract

In most jurisdictions, employers are legally required to provide certain terms in writing within a specific timeframe — often on or before the employee's first day of work. Even where the legal requirement is less strict, a written contract is essential for practical reasons:

- **Clarity**: Both parties know what is expected.
- **Protection**: The contract protects the employer against claims and the employee against unfair treatment.
- **Dispute resolution**: When disagreements arise, the contract is the first point of reference.
- **Compliance**: Employment law requires certain terms to be provided in writing.

A verbal agreement is technically binding, but it is almost impossible to prove what was agreed. Always put employment terms in writing.

## Essential terms to include

### Job title and description
State the job title and a brief description of the role. This does not need to reproduce the full job description, but it should give enough detail to define the scope of the role.

Include a clause allowing reasonable changes to duties over time. Businesses evolve, and roles evolve with them. A clause like "The company may reasonably require you to perform other duties consistent with your role and experience" provides flexibility.

### Start date and continuous employment
The date employment begins. If the employee has continuous service from a previous employer (for example, under a business transfer), state the date continuous employment began.

### Place of work
Where the employee will normally work. If the role involves travel or multiple locations, state this. If you offer hybrid or remote working, specify the arrangement and whether it can be changed.

### Working hours
The normal hours of work per week or per day. State whether overtime may be required and whether it is paid or unpaid.

If you need flexibility in hours — for example, to cover busy periods — include a clause requiring reasonable flexibility with appropriate notice.

### Salary and payment
The gross salary or hourly rate, the pay frequency (weekly, monthly), and the payment method (bank transfer). Include any regular additional payments such as shift premiums or allowances.

State when salary reviews take place, but do not guarantee increases. A clause like "Your salary will be reviewed annually, but there is no obligation to award an increase" protects you from implied commitments.

### Holiday entitlement
The number of days or hours of paid annual leave, including or excluding public holidays. State the holiday year (January to December, April to March, etc.) and any rules about carrying over unused holiday.

Include a clause about what happens to holiday when the employee leaves — specifically, whether you can deduct overpaid holiday from final pay. Without this clause, recovering overpaid holiday can be difficult.

### Pension
Details of the workplace pension scheme, including who contributes and at what rates. If the employee will be auto-enrolled, state this.

### Notice periods
How much notice each party must give to end the employment. Notice periods often vary by length of service — for example, one week for the first two years, then one additional week per year of service up to a maximum.

State whether the employer can make a payment in lieu of notice (PILON). If the contract includes a PILON clause, notice pay is treated as earnings for tax purposes. Without the clause, the tax treatment may differ.

### Sickness absence
Your policy on sick pay — whether you offer occupational sick pay above the statutory minimum, and if so, the rates and duration. Also cover the notification requirements for reporting sickness.

### Disciplinary and grievance procedures
Reference your disciplinary and grievance procedures. You can either include them in the contract or state where the employee can find them (for example, in the employee handbook).

### Confidentiality
A clause requiring the employee to keep business information confidential during and after their employment. This protects trade secrets, client lists, financial information, and other sensitive data.

### Restrictive covenants (if applicable)
If the role involves access to clients, suppliers, or sensitive information, consider including restrictive covenants such as:

- **Non-compete**: Preventing the employee from joining a competitor for a specified period after leaving.
- **Non-solicitation**: Preventing them from approaching your clients or employees for a specified period.
- **Non-dealing**: Preventing them from working with your clients even if the client initiates contact.

Restrictive covenants must be reasonable in scope and duration to be enforceable. An overly broad covenant — such as "you may not work in the same industry anywhere in the world for five years" — will not hold up. Consult a legal adviser when drafting these.

## Additional clauses to consider

### Probation period
A period (typically three to six months) during which either party can end the employment with shorter notice. This gives you a chance to assess the new hire's suitability without the full notice obligations.

### Intellectual property
If the employee will create anything in the course of their work — software, designs, written content, inventions — a clause assigning intellectual property rights to the company is essential.

### Data protection
A clause confirming how you will process the employee's personal data, and their rights under data protection law. This can reference your privacy policy for full details.

### Deductions from pay
A clause authorising specific deductions from pay, such as overpaid holiday, outstanding loans, or unreturned company property. Without written authorisation, deductions from pay can be challenged.

### Company property
A clause requiring the return of all company property (laptop, phone, keys, documents) when employment ends.

## Common mistakes

### Using a generic template without adaptation
Internet templates are a starting point, not a finished product. Every contract should be tailored to the specific role, your business, and the local employment law that applies. A contract designed for one jurisdiction may include terms that are unenforceable or insufficient in another.

### Including unenforceable terms
Terms that are overly restrictive, unreasonable, or contrary to employment law are unenforceable. Including them can undermine the credibility of the entire contract.

### Forgetting to update contracts
When you change an employee's role, salary, working hours, or other terms, the contract should be updated. Changes to contractual terms generally require the employee's agreement.

### Not issuing contracts on time
Many jurisdictions require written terms to be provided on or before the first day of employment. Issuing the contract weeks or months later creates a compliance gap and makes it harder to establish the agreed terms.

### Omitting the holiday deduction clause
This is one of the most commonly forgotten clauses and one of the most frequently needed. Without it, you may not be able to recover overpaid holiday from an employee who leaves having taken more than their accrued entitlement.

## Getting professional help

For your first few contracts, or whenever you hire for a new type of role, consider getting professional advice. An employment law solicitor or HR consultant can:

- Draft a contract template tailored to your business
- Advise on restrictive covenants and their enforceability
- Ensure compliance with local employment law
- Review and update contracts when legislation changes

The cost of professional advice on a contract template is modest compared to the cost of a dispute arising from a poorly drafted contract.

## Keeping contracts accessible

Once contracts are signed, store them securely and make sure both parties have a copy. Digital storage is fine — many businesses now sign and store contracts electronically.

When you need to reference a contract — during a disciplinary process, a leaver calculation, or a salary review — you need to find it quickly. ## The bottom line

An employment contract is not a formality — it is a foundational document that defines the working relationship. Take the time to get it right, keep it up to date, and make sure every employee has one. It protects your business, sets clear expectations, and provides a fair framework for both parties.
