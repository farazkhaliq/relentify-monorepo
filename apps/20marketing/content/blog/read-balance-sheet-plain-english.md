If the profit and loss statement tells you how your business performed over a period, the balance sheet tells you what your business is worth at a specific point in time. It is a snapshot of your financial position — what you own, what you owe, and what is left over for the owners.

Many business owners skip the balance sheet entirely, focusing only on the P&L. That is a mistake. The balance sheet reveals things the P&L cannot: how much cash you actually have, how much clients owe you, how much debt you are carrying, and whether your business is financially healthy.

## The fundamental equation

Every balance sheet is built on one equation:

**Assets = Liabilities + Equity**

This equation always balances — hence the name "balance sheet." If it does not balance, something is wrong with the bookkeeping.

In plain terms:
- **Assets** = Everything your business owns or is owed
- **Liabilities** = Everything your business owes to others
- **Equity** = What is left for the owners after paying off all debts

Think of it this way: if you sold everything your business owns (assets) and used the money to pay off everything it owes (liabilities), the amount remaining would be the equity — the owner's stake in the business.

## Assets: what your business owns

Assets are listed on the balance sheet in order of liquidity — how quickly they can be converted to cash.

### Current assets

These are assets that will be used up or converted to cash within the next 12 months:

- **Cash and bank balances** — Money in your accounts right now
- **Accounts receivable** — Money clients owe you for invoices you have sent
- **Inventory** — Stock you are holding for sale
- **Prepaid expenses** — Bills you have paid in advance (e.g., annual insurance paid upfront)

### Non-current assets (also called fixed assets)

These are longer-term assets that your business uses over multiple years:

- **Property** — Buildings or land the business owns
- **Equipment and machinery** — Tools, computers, vehicles
- **Intangible assets** — Intellectual property, patents, goodwill
- **Long-term investments** — Investments not intended for sale within 12 months

Non-current assets are usually shown at their original cost minus accumulated depreciation. A computer bought for 2,000 three years ago might be shown at 500 on the balance sheet, reflecting its reduced value.

## Liabilities: what your business owes

Like assets, liabilities are split into current (due within 12 months) and non-current (due after 12 months).

### Current liabilities

- **Accounts payable** — Money you owe suppliers for goods or services received
- **Tax payable** — Tax you owe but have not yet paid
- **Short-term loans** — Loans or credit card balances due within 12 months
- **Accrued expenses** — Costs you have incurred but not yet been billed for
- **Deferred revenue** — Money you have received for work you have not yet completed

### Non-current liabilities

- **Long-term loans** — Business loans, mortgages, or finance agreements extending beyond 12 months
- **Lease obligations** — Long-term lease commitments

## Equity: the owner's stake

Equity represents the owners' interest in the business after all liabilities are paid. It includes:

- **Share capital / owner's capital** — Money invested in the business by the owners
- **Retained earnings** — Accumulated profits that have not been distributed as dividends or drawings
- **Current year profit/loss** — The net profit from the current period (which links to the P&L)
- **Drawings / dividends** — Money taken out of the business by owners (reduces equity)

For a sole trader, equity is simply capital plus retained earnings minus drawings. For a limited company, it includes share capital and reserves.

## What the balance sheet tells you

### Liquidity: can you pay your bills?

The **current ratio** (current assets divided by current liabilities) tells you whether you have enough short-term assets to cover your short-term obligations.

- A ratio above 1 means you have more current assets than current liabilities — generally healthy
- A ratio below 1 means you might struggle to pay bills as they fall due
- A ratio significantly above 2 might mean you have cash sitting idle that could be put to better use

### Solvency: are you financially stable?

The **debt-to-equity ratio** (total liabilities divided by total equity) shows how much of your business is funded by debt versus owner investment.

- A lower ratio means less debt and more owner funding — generally safer
- A higher ratio means more debt — which increases risk but can also increase returns

### Working capital: the engine of your business

**Working capital = Current assets minus current liabilities**

This is the money available to fund day-to-day operations. Positive working capital means you have a buffer. Negative working capital means you are relying on future income to pay current obligations.

### Asset efficiency

How effectively are you using your assets to generate revenue? Compare your total assets with your revenue (from the P&L). If assets are growing faster than revenue, you may be over-investing or under-utilising resources.

## Reading a real balance sheet

Here is a simplified example:

```
ASSETS
  Current Assets
    Cash and bank               15,000
    Accounts receivable         22,000
    Prepaid expenses             3,000
  Total Current Assets          40,000

  Non-Current Assets
    Equipment                   25,000
    Less: Depreciation         (10,000)
  Total Non-Current Assets      15,000

TOTAL ASSETS                    55,000

LIABILITIES
  Current Liabilities
    Accounts payable             8,000
    Tax payable                  4,000
    Credit card                  2,000
  Total Current Liabilities     14,000

  Non-Current Liabilities
    Business loan               10,000
  Total Non-Current Liabilities 10,000

TOTAL LIABILITIES               24,000

EQUITY
  Owner's capital                5,000
  Retained earnings             26,000
TOTAL EQUITY                    31,000

TOTAL LIABILITIES + EQUITY      55,000
```

From this, we can quickly see:

- **Current ratio**: 40,000 / 14,000 = 2.86 — healthy liquidity
- **Working capital**: 40,000 - 14,000 = 26,000 — comfortable buffer
- **Debt-to-equity**: 24,000 / 31,000 = 0.77 — moderate leverage
- **Cash position**: 15,000 — decent but 22,000 is tied up in receivables

The biggest concern here might be the 22,000 in accounts receivable. How much of that is overdue? An aged debtors report would give more detail.

## Common balance sheet issues

### Accounts receivable growing faster than revenue

If your receivables are increasing but your sales are not, your clients are taking longer to pay. This ties up cash and increases risk.

### Negative equity

If liabilities exceed assets, your business has negative equity — it owes more than it owns. This is a serious warning sign, though not uncommon for new businesses that have invested heavily and not yet recouped their investment.

### Hidden liabilities

Not everything appears on the balance sheet. Contingent liabilities (potential obligations from lawsuits, guarantees, or disputes) might not show up but could become real costs.

### Asset values that do not reflect reality

Balance sheet values are based on historical cost minus depreciation, not market value. Your property might be worth far more (or less) than the balance sheet shows. Equipment might be fully depreciated but still in use.

## How often should you review it?

At minimum, review your balance sheet:

- **Monthly** alongside your P&L to get the full picture
- **Before making major decisions** like taking on debt, hiring, or making large purchases
- **When applying for financing** — lenders will scrutinise your balance sheet
- **At year end** for tax and compliance purposes

## The balance sheet and the P&L work together

Neither report tells the full story alone:

- A profitable business (per the P&L) can be insolvent if its balance sheet shows insufficient assets to cover liabilities
- A business with a strong balance sheet might be unprofitable, living off reserves while the P&L deteriorates
- Cash on the balance sheet is different from profit on the P&L — you can be profitable and cash-poor, or cash-rich and unprofitable

Review both reports together, regularly, and you will have a comprehensive understanding of your business's financial health.
