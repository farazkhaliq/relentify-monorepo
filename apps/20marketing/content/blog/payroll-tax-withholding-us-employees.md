When you hire your first employee in the United States, you become responsible for withholding and remitting payroll taxes. This is not optional. Federal and state governments require employers to withhold income tax and employment taxes from employee wages and remit them on a regular schedule.

Getting payroll tax withholding wrong — whether by calculating incorrectly, missing deposits, or filing late — can result in trust fund recovery penalties, which are among the most aggressively enforced penalties in the US tax system. The IRS can hold business owners personally liable for unpaid employment taxes, even if the business itself fails.

This guide walks you through setting up payroll tax withholding correctly from the start.

## What you must withhold

### Federal income tax

You must withhold federal income tax from each employee's wages based on:

- The employee's Form W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate)
- Their filing status (single, married, head of household)
- Any additional withholding they request
- IRS withholding tables or the percentage method

The amount withheld depends on the employee's earnings and W-4 elections. Higher earners and those claiming fewer allowances have more tax withheld.

### Social Security tax (employee share)

Withhold 6.2 percent of each employee's wages up to the annual Social Security wage base (this amount is adjusted annually). Once an employee's earnings exceed the wage base for the year, stop withholding Social Security tax on additional earnings.

### Medicare tax (employee share)

Withhold 1.45 percent of all wages — there is no wage base limit for Medicare.

For employees earning above $200,000 in a calendar year, withhold an additional 0.9 percent Medicare tax on wages exceeding that threshold.

### State and local income tax

Most states impose their own income tax, and some cities and counties do as well. You must withhold state (and local, where applicable) income tax based on each state's rules, rates, and withholding tables.

The requirements vary by state:

- Nine states have no state income tax (Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming)
- Other states have varying rates, brackets, and withholding methods
- Some states use the federal W-4; others have their own state withholding forms

## What you must pay as the employer

In addition to amounts withheld from employee wages, you owe employer-paid taxes:

### Employer Social Security tax

You pay 6.2 percent of each employee's wages up to the Social Security wage base — matching the employee's share.

### Employer Medicare tax

You pay 1.45 percent of all wages — matching the employee's share. (The additional 0.9 percent Medicare tax is employee-only; employers do not match it.)

### Federal unemployment tax (FUTA)

You pay FUTA tax at 6.0 percent on the first $7,000 of each employee's wages per year. However, if you pay state unemployment tax on time, you receive a credit of up to 5.4 percent, reducing the effective FUTA rate to 0.6 percent.

### State unemployment tax (SUTA)

Each state has its own unemployment insurance program with varying rates. Your rate is typically based on your industry, your experience (claims history), and the state's rate schedule. New employers usually receive a standard new employer rate.

## Setting up the process

### Step 1: Get an Employer Identification Number (EIN)

If you do not already have one, apply for an EIN from the IRS. This is your business's tax identification number for employment tax purposes. Apply online at irs.gov — it is free and immediate.

### Step 2: Register with your state

Register as an employer with your state's tax agency and unemployment insurance agency. This typically involves:

- Setting up a state withholding account
- Registering for state unemployment insurance
- Obtaining your state employer account numbers and rates

### Step 3: Collect employee forms

For each new employee, collect:

- **Form W-4** — Federal income tax withholding elections
- **State withholding form** — If your state requires a separate form
- **Form I-9** — Employment eligibility verification (immigration, not tax, but required at hiring)

### Step 4: Determine your deposit schedule

The IRS assigns payroll tax deposit schedules based on your total tax liability:

**Monthly depositor:** If your total employment tax liability (withheld income tax + employee and employer Social Security and Medicare) during the look-back period was $50,000 or less, you deposit monthly. Deposits are due by the 15th of the following month.

**Semi-weekly depositor:** If your total liability exceeded $50,000 during the look-back period, you deposit semi-weekly:

- For wages paid Wednesday through Friday: deposit by the following Wednesday
- For wages paid Saturday through Tuesday: deposit by the following Friday

**Next-day deposit:** If you accumulate $100,000 or more in tax liability on any day, you must deposit the next business day.

New employers are typically monthly depositors until their liability grows.

### Step 5: Set up your payroll system

Choose how you will process payroll:

- **Payroll software** — Handles calculations, generates pay stubs, and files returns
- **Payroll service** — A third-party provider manages the entire process
- **Manual processing** — Possible but error-prone and not recommended

Whichever method you choose, integrate it with your [accounting software](/accounting) so payroll expenses are automatically recorded in your financial records.

## Running payroll

### Each pay period

For every pay period, you must:

1. Calculate gross pay (salary, hourly wages, overtime, bonuses, commissions)
2. Calculate federal income tax withholding (based on W-4 and IRS tables)
3. Calculate Social Security withholding (6.2 percent up to wage base)
4. Calculate Medicare withholding (1.45 percent, plus 0.9 percent additional above $200,000)
5. Calculate state and local tax withholding
6. Subtract all withholdings to determine net pay
7. Calculate employer's matching Social Security and Medicare
8. Record the payroll in your accounting system

### Deposit withheld taxes

Deposit employee withholdings plus employer Social Security and Medicare according to your deposit schedule. Deposits must be made electronically through EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System).

Late deposits incur penalties:

- 2 percent if 1-5 days late
- 5 percent if 6-15 days late
- 10 percent if more than 15 days late
- 15 percent if not deposited within 10 days of IRS notice

## Filing requirements

### Quarterly: Form 941

File Form 941 (Employer's Quarterly Federal Tax Return) by the end of the month following each quarter:

- Q1 (January-March): due April 30
- Q2 (April-June): due July 31
- Q3 (July-September): due October 31
- Q4 (October-December): due January 31

Form 941 reports total wages, tips, and compensation paid, plus income tax withheld and Social Security and Medicare taxes.

### Annual: Form 940

File Form 940 (Employer's Annual Federal Unemployment Tax Return) by January 31 for the prior year. This reports your FUTA tax liability and any deposits made.

### Annual: Form W-2

By January 31, provide each employee with a W-2 showing their total wages and tax withheld for the year. File copies with the Social Security Administration.

### State filings

File state withholding returns and unemployment insurance reports according to each state's schedule — typically quarterly.

## Common mistakes

### Missing deposit deadlines

Payroll tax deposits have strict deadlines with escalating penalties. Set up calendar reminders or use a payroll service that handles deposits automatically.

### Incorrect withholding calculations

Using outdated withholding tables, failing to apply W-4 elections correctly, or miscalculating wages subject to Social Security results in incorrect withholding. Verify your calculations or use reliable payroll software.

### Not depositing the employer share

Remember that you owe matching Social Security and Medicare taxes in addition to amounts withheld from employees. These employer taxes are deposited together with the withheld amounts.

### Misclassifying workers

Treating employees as independent contractors to avoid payroll obligations is one of the most costly compliance errors. See our guide on W-2 vs 1099 classification for details.

### Commingling trust fund taxes

Taxes withheld from employees are held in trust for the government. Using these funds for business expenses — even temporarily — is a serious compliance violation.
