Millions of people now work from home, whether running their own business, freelancing, or working remotely for an employer. What many do not realise is that a portion of their household costs can be claimed as a legitimate business expense, reducing their tax bill.

The rules vary depending on your situation — whether you are self-employed, a company director, or an employee — and the amounts involved can make a meaningful difference over the course of a year. This guide explains what you can claim, how to calculate it, and how to keep the records your tax authority expects.

## Who can claim working from home expenses?

### Self-employed and sole traders

If you work from home regularly as part of your self-employment, you can claim a proportion of your household running costs as a business expense. This applies whether you work from home full-time or split your time between home and other locations.

### Company directors working from home

Directors of limited companies can claim home office expenses through the business, either as a reimbursement from the company or as an allowable expense against the company's profits.

### Employees working from home

Employees who are required to work from home (not just choosing to) may be able to claim tax relief on additional household costs. The rules here are more restrictive than for self-employed workers, and the amounts are typically smaller.

### Who cannot claim

You generally cannot claim if you choose to work from home for convenience but could work from a provided workplace. The requirement is that you must work from home, not simply prefer to.

## What costs can you claim?

The costs you can claim fall into two categories: fixed costs and running costs.

### Fixed costs (proportional)

These are costs you pay regardless of whether you work from home:

- **Rent or mortgage interest** — You can claim a proportion based on the space used for work and the time spent working. Note that claiming mortgage interest for a room used exclusively for business can have capital gains tax implications when you sell the property.
- **Council tax or local property taxes** — A proportion based on the business use of your home.
- **Home insurance** — A proportion of the premium attributable to business use.

### Running costs (proportional or actual)

These are costs that increase because you work from home:

- **Heating and electricity** — The additional energy used to heat and light your workspace during working hours.
- **Water rates** — If you use water for business purposes, a small proportion may be claimable.
- **Internet and phone** — The business proportion of your broadband and telephone costs. If you use your internet primarily for work, a significant proportion can be claimed.
- **Cleaning** — If you clean your workspace as part of business use, a proportion of cleaning costs.

### Costs you cannot claim

- **Mortgage capital repayments** — Only the interest portion is potentially claimable, not the capital repayment.
- **Food and drink** — General food consumed while working from home is a personal expense, not a business one.
- **Clothing** — Unless it is specialist protective equipment or a required uniform.
- **General household maintenance** — Repairs and maintenance to your home that are not directly related to your workspace.

## Methods for calculating your claim

### The simplified method (flat rate)

Many tax authorities offer a simplified flat-rate allowance that avoids the need for detailed calculations. You claim a fixed amount per month based on the hours you work from home.

For example, in the UK, HMRC allows self-employed individuals to claim a flat rate based on hours worked:

- 25 to 50 hours per month: a set monthly amount
- 51 to 100 hours per month: a higher monthly amount
- 101 or more hours per month: the maximum monthly amount

The advantage of the flat rate is simplicity — no need to calculate proportions or keep utility bills. The disadvantage is that the actual cost of your home office may be significantly higher than the flat rate, particularly if you work from home full-time.

### The actual costs method (proportional)

This method involves calculating the actual costs of running your home and then claiming a proportion based on business use.

**Step 1: Calculate your total household running costs.** Add up rent or mortgage interest, utilities, council tax, insurance, and other eligible costs for the year.

**Step 2: Determine the business proportion.** There are several approaches:

- **Room-based proportion:** If you use one room out of five as a dedicated office, you could claim one-fifth of eligible costs.
- **Area-based proportion:** Divide the floor area of your workspace by the total floor area of your home.
- **Time-based adjustment:** If you use a room for business during working hours but for personal use in the evenings and weekends, adjust the proportion to reflect the time spent on business use.

Most people combine room-based and time-based approaches. If your office is one-fifth of your home and you use it for business eight hours a day, five days a week, the proportion would be one-fifth multiplied by the fraction of the week you use it for work.

**Step 3: Apply the proportion to each eligible cost.**

### Which method should you choose?

Calculate both and compare. If your home office costs are modest and you work from home occasionally, the flat rate may be simpler and sufficient. If you work from home full-time and have significant household costs, the actual costs method will almost certainly produce a larger claim.

## Record keeping requirements

Regardless of which method you use, you need to keep records that support your claim:

**For the flat rate method:**
- A log of hours worked from home each month
- Evidence that you regularly work from home (contracts, correspondence)

**For the actual costs method:**
- Utility bills and receipts for all claimed costs
- Your calculation showing the business proportion
- A description of the space used for business
- Evidence of hours worked from home

Keep these records for the period required by your tax authority — typically five to seven years after the tax year in question.

## Equipment and furniture

In addition to running costs, you can typically claim the cost of equipment and furniture purchased specifically for your home office:

- **Desk and chair** — If bought for business use.
- **Computer and peripherals** — Monitors, keyboards, printers, etc.
- **Office supplies** — Stationery, printer cartridges, storage solutions.
- **Software and subscriptions** — Business software licences.

If an item is used partly for business and partly for personal use, you can claim the business proportion. A computer used fifty per cent for work and fifty per cent for personal use would be half-claimable.

For higher-value items, you may need to claim the cost over several years through capital allowances or depreciation rather than as an immediate expense.

## Common mistakes to avoid

### Claiming a dedicated room exclusively for business

While having a dedicated office maximises your claim for running costs, claiming that a room is used exclusively for business can create a capital gains tax liability when you sell your home. In many jurisdictions, the portion of your home used exclusively for business loses its main residence tax exemption.

The safer approach is to ensure the room has some personal use — even a bookshelf with personal books or occasional family use — so it remains part of your residence for tax purposes.

### Not claiming at all

Many home workers, particularly employees, simply do not bother claiming. Even small amounts add up over time. If you are entitled to relief, claim it.

### Over-claiming

Claiming disproportionate amounts invites scrutiny. Your claim should be reasonable and defensible. If you work from a corner of your living room, claiming fifty per cent of your household costs is not credible.

### Inconsistent record keeping

If you are audited, you need to produce records that support your claim. Keeping receipts in a shoebox or relying on memory is not sufficient. Digitise your records and keep them organised by tax year.

## Making the most of your home office claim

The key to maximising your working from home tax relief is consistency:

1. Choose the calculation method that produces the best result for your situation.
2. Track your expenses throughout the year rather than estimating at tax time.
3. Keep digital copies of all relevant bills and receipts.
4. Review your claim annually — if your circumstances change, your claim should too.
5. Consider getting professional advice if your situation is complex.

Working from home tax relief will not transform your finances, but it is money you are entitled to that reduces your tax bill legitimately. The effort required to claim it is minimal once you have the right systems in place.
