Late payment is one of the most frustrating aspects of running a business. You have done the work, delivered the results, and now you are waiting for money that was supposed to arrive weeks ago. Meanwhile, you have your own bills to pay.

The temptation is to fire off an angry email or pick up the phone and demand payment. But in many cases, the client you are chasing is someone you want to work with again. Burning that bridge over a late payment — even when you are completely in the right — can cost you far more in future revenue than the overdue invoice is worth.

The good news is that you can be firm about payment without being aggressive. Here is how.

## Why invoices are paid late

Before you chase, it helps to understand why invoices go unpaid. In most cases, the reason is not malicious:

- **They forgot** — Your invoice is sitting in an inbox with hundreds of other emails
- **Internal processes** — The person who received your invoice is not the person who approves payments
- **Cash flow issues** — They are waiting on their own clients to pay them
- **Disputed amounts** — They disagree with something on the invoice but have not told you
- **Lost invoice** — It went to spam, was filed incorrectly, or was never received

Understanding the reason helps you tailor your approach. A forgotten invoice needs a gentle reminder. A disputed amount needs a conversation.

## The chasing timeline

Having a structured process removes the emotional decision-making from collections. Here is a timeline that works for most businesses:

### Before the due date: the pre-reminder

Send a brief, friendly reminder 3-5 days before the invoice is due. This is not chasing — it is a courtesy.

> "Hi [Name], just a quick note that invoice #1234 for [amount] is due on [date]. If you need a copy of the invoice or have any questions, please let me know."

This serves two purposes: it nudges the client to process the payment, and it catches any issues (wrong address, missing PO number) before the invoice is actually overdue.

### Day 1 past due: the first follow-up

On the day after the due date, send another polite message:

> "Hi [Name], I hope you are well. I wanted to follow up on invoice #1234 for [amount], which was due yesterday. I have attached a copy for your reference. Please let me know if there is anything you need from my end to process the payment."

Keep the tone neutral and helpful. Assume good intent — they probably just forgot.

### Day 7 past due: the firm reminder

If you have heard nothing after a week, escalate slightly in tone:

> "Hi [Name], I am following up on invoice #1234 for [amount], which is now 7 days overdue. I understand that things can slip through the cracks, but I would appreciate an update on when I can expect payment. If there is an issue with the invoice, I am happy to discuss it."

### Day 14 past due: the phone call

Two weeks overdue warrants a phone call. Emails are easy to ignore; phone calls are not.

Keep the call professional and focused:
- Reference the specific invoice and amount
- Ask if there is an issue you can help resolve
- Agree on a specific payment date
- Follow up the call with an email confirming what was discussed

### Day 21 past due: the formal notice

If the phone call did not resolve things, send a formal overdue notice:

> "Dear [Name], this is a formal notice that invoice #1234 for [amount], issued on [date], is now 21 days overdue. Please arrange payment within 7 days. If payment is not received by [date], we may need to [charge interest / pause further work / refer to collections]."

### Day 30+ past due: escalation

At this point, you need to make a judgement call:

- **Is the client responsive but struggling?** — Offer a payment plan
- **Is the client ignoring you entirely?** — Consider formal debt recovery
- **Is the amount small?** — Weigh the cost of chasing against the amount owed
- **Is this a client you want to keep?** — Adjust your approach accordingly

## Techniques that work

### Make it easy to pay

The easier you make it to pay, the faster you get paid. Include multiple payment options on your invoice: bank transfer, credit card, online payment link. The fewer steps between "I should pay this" and "I have paid this," the better.

Accounting platforms like [Relentify](/accounting) let you include a direct payment link on every invoice, so clients can pay with a single click rather than manually setting up a bank transfer.

### Automate your reminders

Sending manual follow-up emails takes time and willpower. Most accounting software can send automatic payment reminders at intervals you define. Set them up once and they run in the background, chasing consistently without any effort from you.

Automated reminders also remove the personal awkwardness. It is easier to chase payment when it is "the system" sending the reminder rather than you personally.

### Always be specific

Vague messages get vague responses. Always include:
- The invoice number
- The exact amount
- The original due date
- A copy of the invoice (or a link to it)

The client should be able to find and pay the invoice without any additional effort.

### Address disputes immediately

If a client raises a query about an invoice, respond promptly. Every day you take to resolve a dispute is a day the payment is delayed. Even if the dispute is minor, treat it as urgent.

If the dispute is about part of the invoice, suggest paying the undisputed portion while the rest is resolved.

### Document everything

Keep a record of every communication about overdue invoices. Emails are self-documenting. For phone calls, send a follow-up email summarising what was discussed and agreed. This protects you if things escalate.

## What not to do

### Do not get emotional

It is natural to feel frustrated when you are not being paid. But angry emails, passive-aggressive messages, or threatening tones almost always make things worse. Stay professional, even when you do not feel like it.

### Do not stop working without warning

If you are mid-project and want to pause work until an overdue invoice is paid, communicate this clearly and give the client a chance to resolve it first. Abruptly stopping work can trigger disputes that are worse than the late payment.

### Do not accept vague promises

"I will sort it out next week" is not a commitment. Ask for a specific date: "Can you confirm that payment will be made by [specific date]?" Then follow up on that date if it is not paid.

### Do not wait too long to start chasing

The longer an invoice is overdue, the harder it is to collect. Start your process on day one, not day thirty.

## Preventing late payments in the first place

The best collection strategy is prevention:

- **Agree payment terms upfront** in writing before starting work
- **Invoice promptly** as soon as the work is completed
- **Send pre-reminders** before the due date
- **Offer early payment incentives** if cash flow is a priority
- **Require deposits** for new clients or large projects
- **Run credit checks** on clients placing large orders
- **Set up automatic reminders** so follow-ups happen without manual effort

## When to write it off

Sometimes, despite your best efforts, an invoice will not be paid. Knowing when to stop chasing is important:

- The cost of collection exceeds the invoice amount
- The client has gone out of business
- The relationship damage from continued chasing outweighs the amount owed
- You have exhausted all reasonable options

Write off the bad debt in your accounts and learn from the experience. Adjust your terms, deposits, or client vetting process to reduce the risk next time.

Late payments are an unavoidable part of business, but they do not have to dominate your time or damage your relationships. A structured, professional approach to chasing gets the money in while keeping the door open for future work.
