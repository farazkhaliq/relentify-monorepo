Every estate agency receives complaints. A tenant is unhappy with a maintenance response time. A landlord disputes a fee. An applicant feels they were treated unfairly. These situations are uncomfortable, but they are an unavoidable part of managing relationships between people with competing interests.

What separates a professional agency from a struggling one is not the absence of complaints — it is how those complaints are handled. A complaint that is acknowledged quickly, investigated thoroughly, and resolved fairly can actually strengthen a client relationship. A complaint that is ignored, minimised, or handled inconsistently causes lasting damage.

A CRM provides the structure to handle complaints systematically — ensuring that every issue is logged, tracked through to resolution, and used as an opportunity to improve.

## Why complaints need a system

In many agencies, complaints are handled informally. The agent who receives the complaint tries to resolve it personally. If they succeed, the matter is closed. If they do not, it escalates — but the escalation is informal too, relying on verbal handoffs and good intentions.

This approach fails for several reasons. There is no record of the complaint, so if the situation resurfaces later — or if the client contacts a different team member — nobody knows what happened. There is no timeline, so it is impossible to know whether the response was timely. There is no pattern analysis, so recurring issues go unnoticed.

A complaint management system within your CRM solves all of these problems by creating a structured, auditable record of every complaint from receipt to resolution.

## Logging complaints

The first step is to ensure that every complaint is logged — not just the formal ones, but any expression of dissatisfaction that warrants attention. A tenant who calls to say they are "not happy" about something is raising a complaint, even if they do not use that word.

Each complaint record should capture the following.

**Who is complaining.** The name and role of the complainant (tenant, landlord, applicant), linked to their contact record in the CRM.

**What the complaint is about.** A clear description of the issue, in the complainant's own words where possible.

**When it was received.** The date and time the complaint was first raised, and the channel it came through (phone, email, in person, online).

**Which property or tenancy it relates to.** Linking the complaint to the relevant property record provides context and helps identify patterns.

**How urgent it is.** A prioritisation based on the severity of the issue and the potential consequences of a delayed response.

## The resolution workflow

Once a complaint is logged, it should move through a defined workflow with clear steps, timelines, and accountabilities.

### Acknowledgement

Acknowledge the complaint within 24 hours. The acknowledgement does not need to contain a resolution — it simply confirms that the complaint has been received and is being taken seriously. Include a reference number, the name of the person handling the complaint, and an expected timeline for the next update.

This acknowledgement is important psychologically. A complainant who knows their issue has been received and assigned feels heard. A complainant who hears nothing assumes they are being ignored.

### Investigation

Investigate the complaint thoroughly. This means gathering relevant information — communication logs, maintenance records, tenancy documents, colleague accounts — and understanding what happened from all perspectives.

Your CRM should provide the evidence you need. If the complaint is about a slow maintenance response, the maintenance log will show when the request was received, when it was assigned, and when the work was completed. If the complaint is about a miscommunication, the contact history will show what was said, by whom, and when.

### Response

Respond to the complainant with a clear explanation of what you found, what you are doing about it (if anything), and why. If the complaint is justified, acknowledge the failing and explain the steps you are taking to prevent it from happening again. If the complaint is not justified, explain the reasons respectfully and provide supporting evidence.

The response should be documented in the CRM against the complaint record, creating a complete audit trail.

### Resolution and follow-up

If remedial action is needed — an apology, a service credit, a process change — carry it out and confirm with the complainant that the matter is resolved to their satisfaction. If they are not satisfied, the complaint may need to be escalated to a more senior level.

A follow-up check after a few days — "Are you satisfied with how this was handled?" — demonstrates that you care about the outcome, not just the process.

## Escalation procedures

Not every complaint can be resolved at the first level. Some require management involvement. Others may involve external parties — ombudsman services, regulatory bodies, or legal advisors.

Your CRM should support a tiered escalation structure. If a complaint is not resolved within a defined timeframe — say, five working days — it automatically escalates to a manager. If it remains unresolved after ten days, it escalates to a director.

Each escalation should be visible in the complaint record, ensuring that senior staff can see the full history when they take over.

## Using complaints to improve

Individual complaints are problems to be solved. Patterns in complaints are opportunities to improve. If multiple tenants are complaining about maintenance response times, the issue is not individual — it is systemic. If landlords are consistently disputing fees, the fee communication process needs review.

Your CRM's reporting capabilities should allow you to analyse complaints by category, property, team member, and time period. This analysis reveals the recurring issues that, if addressed, would reduce complaint volumes and improve client satisfaction.

Regular complaint reviews — monthly or quarterly — where the team examines trends and discusses improvements, create a continuous improvement culture. The goal is not to eliminate complaints entirely (that is unrealistic) but to reduce preventable complaints and handle unavoidable ones as well as possible.

## Protecting the agency

Complaints that are poorly handled can escalate into formal disputes, ombudsman referrals, or legal action. The audit trail created by your CRM is your best protection.

When a complaint is investigated by an external body, the first thing they will ask for is evidence of how the complaint was handled. A CRM that records every step — the initial complaint, the acknowledgement, the investigation, the response, and the resolution — provides exactly this evidence.

Without a system, reconstructing the timeline relies on memory, scattered emails, and incomplete notes. With a system, the complete record is available at the click of a button.

## Team training

Complaints handling is a skill that can be taught. Ensure that your team knows how to receive a complaint calmly, log it accurately, investigate it fairly, and respond appropriately.

Role-playing complaint scenarios in team training sessions builds confidence and consistency. When the whole team approaches complaints with the same professionalism and process, the client experience is uniform regardless of who handles the initial contact.
