If you use your personal vehicle for business, you can claim mileage expenses to reduce your tax bill. It is one of the most common business deductions, but also one of the most frequently claimed incorrectly.

Getting mileage right means understanding what qualifies, what the rates are, and what records you need to keep. Get it wrong and you could face penalties from HMRC or miss out on legitimate claims.

## Approved mileage rates

HMRC publishes Approved Mileage Allowance Payment (AMAP) rates that determine how much you can claim per business mile:

### Cars and vans

| Miles per tax year | Rate per mile |
|-------------------|---------------|
| First 10,000 miles | 45p |
| Over 10,000 miles | 25p |

### Motorcycles

| Miles per tax year | Rate per mile |
|-------------------|---------------|
| All miles | 24p |

### Bicycles

| Miles per tax year | Rate per mile |
|-------------------|---------------|
| All miles | 20p |

These rates are set by HMRC and apply regardless of your vehicle's actual running costs. Whether you drive a small hatchback or a large SUV, the rate is the same.

The rates are designed to cover fuel, insurance, road tax, maintenance, depreciation, and all other costs associated with using the vehicle. You cannot claim these individual costs separately if you use the mileage rates.

### Passenger payments

If you carry a passenger who is also travelling for business purposes, you can claim an additional 5p per mile for each passenger. This is separate from the main mileage rate.

## What counts as a business journey?

This is where many people make mistakes. Not every journey in your car is a business journey, even if you are thinking about work.

### Qualifying business journeys

- Travelling to a client's premises
- Visiting a supplier
- Going to a meeting at a location that is not your regular workplace
- Travelling between different work locations
- Going to the bank to pay in business takings
- Collecting supplies or materials
- Travelling to a training course related to your business

### Journeys that do NOT qualify

- **Commuting** — Your regular journey between home and your normal workplace does not count. This is private mileage, even though you need to make the journey for work.
- **Personal errands** — Stopping at the supermarket on the way back from a client does not make the supermarket trip a business journey.
- **Journeys with no business purpose** — The journey must have a genuine business reason.

### The home office exception

If you work from home and your home is your principal place of business (i.e., you do not have a separate office), then journeys from home to clients, suppliers, or other business locations do qualify as business mileage. This is because your "commute" is zero — your workplace is your home.

This is a significant distinction. If you rent an office and drive there every day, that is commuting (not claimable). If you work from home and drive to a client, that is a business journey (claimable).

## Mileage rates vs actual costs

You have two options for claiming vehicle costs:

### Option 1: Mileage rates (simplified expenses)

Use HMRC's approved rates (45p/25p per mile). This is simpler because you only need to track your business miles, not your actual vehicle costs.

**Advantages:**
- Simple record-keeping — just log your miles
- No need to track fuel, insurance, servicing, etc.
- Often results in a higher claim than actual costs for lower-mileage drivers

**Disadvantages:**
- Cannot claim actual vehicle costs separately
- The 25p rate for miles over 10,000 is quite low
- If your actual costs are high (expensive vehicle, lots of miles), you might claim less

### Option 2: Actual costs

Track all your actual vehicle costs (fuel, insurance, road tax, servicing, repairs, depreciation) and claim the business proportion.

The business proportion is calculated as: (business miles / total miles) x total costs.

**Advantages:**
- Can result in a higher claim for high-mileage drivers or expensive vehicles
- More accurate reflection of actual costs

**Disadvantages:**
- Much more record-keeping required
- Must track every cost and every journey (business and personal)
- Once you choose actual costs for a vehicle, you cannot switch back to mileage rates for that vehicle

### Which should you choose?

For most small business owners and sole traders, the mileage rate is the better option. It is simpler, requires less record-keeping, and often results in a reasonable claim.

If you drive a significant number of business miles (over 10,000) or have a particularly expensive vehicle, it is worth calculating both methods to see which gives you the higher deduction.

## Record-keeping requirements

HMRC requires you to maintain a mileage log that records:

- **Date** of each journey
- **From and to** — Start and end locations
- **Purpose** — The business reason for the journey
- **Distance** — Miles driven

You do not need to submit this log with your tax return, but you must be able to produce it if HMRC enquires.

### Methods of tracking

**Mileage book** — A physical notebook kept in your car. Simple but easy to forget.

**Spreadsheet** — A digital log updated daily or weekly. More organised but still manual.

**Mileage tracking app** — Uses your phone's GPS to automatically track journeys. You classify each journey as business or personal. This is the most accurate and least effort-intensive method.

**Accounting software** — Some accounting platforms like [Relentify](/accounting) include mileage tracking features that let you log journeys and automatically calculate the claim amount at the correct HMRC rate.

### Tips for good record-keeping

- **Log journeys at the time** — Do not try to reconstruct your mileage log at year end from memory
- **Be specific about the purpose** — "Client meeting with ABC Ltd to review Q2 results" is better than "Meeting"
- **Round down, not up** — If in doubt about the exact distance, claim less rather than more
- **Keep supporting evidence** — Meeting confirmations, client emails, and diary entries corroborate your mileage log

## Mileage for employees

If you are an employer and your employees use their own vehicles for business, you should reimburse them at up to the AMAP rates. If you pay less than the AMAP rate, the employee can claim the difference as tax relief.

For example, if an employee drives 5,000 business miles and you reimburse them at 30p per mile, they receive 1,500 from you but could have claimed 2,250 at the 45p AMAP rate. The employee can claim tax relief on the 750 difference.

If you pay more than the AMAP rate, the excess is treated as a taxable benefit and must be reported.

## Electric and hybrid vehicles

The AMAP rates apply equally to electric, hybrid, and conventional vehicles. There is no separate rate for electric vehicles. If you use an electric car for business, you claim 45p per mile for the first 10,000 miles and 25p thereafter, the same as any other car.

This can make electric vehicles particularly tax-efficient for business use, since the actual running cost per mile is typically much lower than the AMAP rate.

## Company cars

If you have a company car, different rules apply. You cannot use the AMAP rates for a company car. Instead, you receive an Advisory Fuel Rate (AFR) for business mileage, which varies by engine size and fuel type. Check the latest HMRC guidance for current AFR rates.

## Common mistakes

### Claiming commuting as business mileage

Your regular journey to and from your workplace is not business mileage. This is the most common error and can result in penalties if HMRC investigates.

### Not keeping a mileage log

Claiming mileage without records to back it up is risky. If HMRC asks for evidence and you cannot provide it, the claim may be disallowed.

### Mixing mileage rates and actual costs

You must choose one method per vehicle and stick with it. You cannot claim the mileage rate for some journeys and actual costs for others.

### Claiming more than the AMAP rate

If you are a sole trader using the simplified expenses method, you claim the AMAP rate. You cannot claim more, even if your actual costs are higher (unless you switch to the actual cost method).

### Forgetting about the 10,000-mile threshold

The rate drops from 45p to 25p after 10,000 business miles. If you drive more than 10,000 business miles, make sure you are applying the correct rate.

Mileage expenses are a straightforward deduction, but they require discipline in record-keeping. Start logging your journeys from day one, use a consistent method, and review your records periodically to ensure they are complete and accurate. The tax savings add up quickly, especially for businesses that rely on driving.
