One of the biggest advantages of running a limited company is flexibility in how you pay yourself. Unlike sole traders who are taxed on all their profits, company directors can choose a combination of salary and dividends — and the split you choose has a significant impact on your total tax bill.

Getting this right is one of the simplest and most effective tax planning strategies available to small company directors. Getting it wrong means paying more tax than you need to.

## How salary and dividends are taxed differently

### Salary

A salary is a business expense for the company and employment income for you. It is taxed through PAYE (Pay As You Earn) and attracts:

- **Income tax** — At your marginal rate (20%, 40%, or 45%)
- **Employee National Insurance** — Currently 8% on earnings between the primary threshold and upper earnings limit, 2% above that
- **Employer National Insurance** — 13.8% on earnings above the secondary threshold (paid by the company)

The employer's NI is a cost to the company that does not benefit you directly. It is often described as "the tax on tax" — the company pays tax on the act of paying you.

However, salary (including employer's NI) is deductible against corporation tax. So while you are paying NI, you are reducing the company's corporation tax bill.

### Dividends

Dividends are paid from the company's post-tax profits. They are not a business expense and cannot be deducted from corporation tax. The company has already paid corporation tax on the profits before distributing them.

For you personally, dividends are taxed at:

- **Dividend allowance** — The first £1,000 (current threshold, check latest guidance) is tax-free
- **Basic rate** — 8.75% on dividends within the basic rate band
- **Higher rate** — 33.75% on dividends within the higher rate band
- **Additional rate** — 39.35% on dividends above the additional rate threshold

Dividends do not attract National Insurance — neither employee NI nor employer NI. This is the key advantage.

## Why the combination matters

The tax efficiency of salary versus dividends depends on the total tax picture — not just personal tax, but corporation tax and National Insurance as well.

### The low salary, high dividend strategy

The most common approach for small company directors is:

1. Take a salary at or just above the NI threshold — high enough to qualify for state pension credits, but low enough to avoid significant NI contributions
2. Take the rest of your income as dividends

This works because:
- The salary is deductible against corporation tax
- The salary level is below the NI threshold, so no NI is triggered (or very little)
- Dividends are taxed at lower rates than salary
- Dividends do not attract NI

### Example calculation

Let us say your company has profits of 60,000 and you want to extract 50,000.

**Option A: All salary (50,000)**
- Corporation tax saved: 50,000 x 25% = 12,500 (salary is deductible)
- But: employee NI on roughly 37,430 (above the threshold) at 8% = ~2,994
- Employer NI on roughly 40,790 (above secondary threshold) at 13.8% = ~5,629
- Income tax: complex calculation but approximately 7,486 (after personal allowance)
- Total tax burden: approximately 16,109 (personal) plus 5,629 (employer NI) = ~21,738

**Option B: Salary of 12,570 + dividends of 37,430**
- Salary set at personal allowance level — no income tax, no employee NI
- Employer NI on ~3,360 (above secondary threshold) at 13.8% = ~464
- Corporation tax on remaining profit (approximately 46,966 after salary + employer NI): 25% x 46,966 = ~11,742
- Dividend tax: first £1,000 tax-free. Remainder (36,430) at 8.75% = ~3,188
- Total tax burden: approximately 464 + 11,742 + 3,188 = ~15,394

The difference is substantial. Option B saves over 6,000 in tax. These are illustrative figures — actual savings depend on your specific circumstances, current rates, and thresholds.

## Important considerations

### State pension eligibility

To qualify for the state pension and certain benefits, you need to earn at least the Lower Earnings Limit (currently £6,396 per year) in salary. If your salary is below this level, you may not receive a qualifying year for state pension purposes.

Many advisors recommend setting the salary at or just above this threshold to maintain pension eligibility while minimising NI.

### Mortgage applications

Mortgage lenders look at your personal income when assessing affordability. If your salary is very low (even if your dividend income is high), some lenders may not count dividends as income, or may require additional documentation.

If you plan to apply for a mortgage, discuss the optimal salary level with both your accountant and your mortgage advisor.

### Retained profits

You can only pay dividends from retained profits. If your company does not have sufficient retained profits (accumulated after-tax earnings that have not been distributed), you cannot legally pay a dividend. Dividends paid in excess of available profits are unlawful distributions.

### Dividend documentation

Every dividend payment should be supported by:
- Board minutes recording the decision to pay the dividend
- A dividend voucher showing the amount, date, and shareholder details

This is a legal requirement for limited companies and protects you in the event of an HMRC enquiry.

### IR35 and off-payroll working

If you provide services to clients through your limited company and the relationship resembles employment, IR35 rules may apply. Under IR35, the income is treated as salary for tax purposes, eliminating the dividend advantage. Make sure your working arrangements are genuinely those of a self-employed contractor.

## The optimal salary level

The "optimal" salary level changes with each tax year as thresholds and rates are updated. Common strategies include:

- **At the Lower Earnings Limit** — Preserves state pension eligibility, no NI triggered
- **At the NI primary threshold** — Just below where employee NI kicks in
- **At the personal allowance** — Uses the full tax-free allowance, but may trigger some NI
- **Higher levels** — Sometimes beneficial if the company is in the marginal rate band (profits between 50,000 and 250,000), where the corporation tax saving from a higher salary outweighs the NI cost

The right level for you depends on your company's profits, your other income, your personal tax position, and the current tax rates. This is an area where professional advice pays for itself.

## Employer's NI: the hidden cost

Employer's NI at 13.8% is easy to overlook because it does not appear on your payslip. But it is a real cost to the company and reduces the amount available for dividends.

When comparing salary vs dividends, always include employer's NI in the calculation. A salary of 50,000 actually costs the company around 55,600 once employer's NI is added.

## Keeping good records

Whatever split you choose, maintain clear records:

- Monthly payslips for your salary (processed through PAYE)
- Board minutes authorising each dividend
- Dividend vouchers for each payment
- A running record of retained profits available for distribution

Good accounting software like [Relentify](/accounting) tracks your company's financial position so you always know whether sufficient retained profits exist to support a dividend payment.

## Review annually

The optimal salary/dividend split is not a "set and forget" decision. Review it at least once a year with your accountant, ideally at the start of each tax year when new thresholds and rates take effect.

Changes in corporation tax rates, NI thresholds, dividend tax rates, and personal allowances can all shift the optimal strategy. What was best last year may not be best this year.

The dividend versus salary question is one of the most impactful tax planning decisions for small company directors. Take the time to get it right, and review it regularly.
