A busy inventory clerk can handle three to five inspections per day, sometimes more. Across a week, that is 15 to 25 individual appointments, each at a different property, for different clients, with different requirements. Managing this workload efficiently — without missed appointments, rushed inspections, or wasted travel time — requires a system.

This guide covers practical strategies for scheduling, routing, time management, and workflow organisation that help inventory clerks handle a full diary without sacrificing quality.

## Scheduling fundamentals

### Book realistic time slots

The biggest scheduling mistake is underestimating how long inspections take. A common pattern:

- **Studio/1-bed check-in**: 45 to 75 minutes on-site
- **2-bed check-in**: 60 to 90 minutes
- **3-bed check-in**: 90 to 120 minutes
- **4-bed+ check-in**: 120 to 180 minutes
- **Check-outs**: Typically 75 to 85 percent of the check-in time
- **Mid-tenancy inspections**: 30 to 60 minutes

These are on-site times. Add travel between properties and buffer time for overruns. A 90-minute inspection with 30 minutes of travel and a 15-minute buffer uses more than two hours of your day.

### Build in buffer time

Inspections overrun. Properties are larger than described. Tenants arrive late. Keys do not work. If every minute of your day is committed, one delay cascades through every subsequent appointment.

Build 15 to 30 minutes of buffer between each inspection. This absorbs minor delays without disrupting the rest of your schedule.

### Group by geography

Plan your daily route to minimise travel time. If you have five inspections in a day, arrange them geographically so you are moving in a logical pattern across your area rather than criss-crossing back and forth.

This seems obvious but is often neglected when bookings come in from different clients at different times. Review your weekly schedule and rearrange where possible.

### Separate check-ins and check-outs

Some clerks prefer to do all check-ins on certain days and all check-outs on others. The workflows are slightly different — check-ins require building a report from scratch, while check-outs involve comparing against an existing report — and batching similar work can be more efficient.

This is not always possible (client scheduling requirements may override your preferences), but when you have flexibility, batching similar work types can improve your focus and speed.

## Client management

### Set clear booking expectations

When clients (agents or landlords) book inspections, be clear about:

- How much notice you need (24 to 48 hours minimum for standard bookings; more for busy periods)
- What information you need in advance (property address, type, furnished/unfurnished, tenant contact details, key collection details)
- Your standard turnaround time for report delivery
- Your cancellation policy

### Confirm bookings

Send a confirmation for every booking, ideally the day before. Include the property address, the time, and any access requirements. This reduces no-shows and ensures everyone is prepared.

### Communicate delays promptly

If you are running late, contact the next client or tenant immediately. A five-minute courtesy call is much better received than turning up 20 minutes late without warning.

### Track client preferences

Different clients have different preferences. Some agents want reports emailed to a specific address. Some landlords want a phone call after the inspection. Some tenants are only available at certain times. Keep a record of these preferences and follow them consistently.

## Time management on-site

### Follow your template systematically

Working through your template room by room, in the same order every time, is faster than approaching each property ad hoc. Muscle memory builds with practice — after hundreds of inspections, the template becomes second nature, and you move through it efficiently without missing anything.

### Photograph as you go

Take photos within your inspection workflow, not as a separate pass. When you assess the living room walls, photograph them immediately. When you note the oven condition, photograph it now. Going back to photograph areas you have already inspected wastes time and increases the risk of missing something.

### Do not get drawn into conversations

Tenants are sometimes present during inspections, and they may want to chat — about the property, the neighbourhood, their plans, or their complaints. Be polite but focused. Extended conversations eat into your schedule and do not improve the quality of the report.

### Know when to move on

Perfectionism is the enemy of productivity. There is a point of diminishing returns in how meticulously you describe each room. Your report needs to be thorough and accurate, but it does not need to be a novel. Develop a sense of when a room is adequately documented and move on.

## Workflow after the inspection

### Generate reports immediately

If you are using digital inventory software like [Relentify](/inventory), the report is largely complete when you leave the property. A quick review and any final notes, and it is ready to send. This should take 10 to 15 minutes, not hours.

If you are using tools that require post-visit report production, schedule this time into your day. Do not let reports pile up — a backlog of unfinished reports creates stress, delays delivery, and increases the risk of errors as details fade from memory.

### Deliver reports promptly

Aim to send reports to clients on the same day as the inspection, or at most the following morning. Prompt delivery impresses clients and keeps your workflow clean.

### File and organise immediately

Every report should be filed and stored as soon as it is completed. Use a consistent naming convention (property address, date, report type) and store reports in a system where you can retrieve them easily.

## Managing seasonal peaks

The inventory market has predictable busy periods:

- **End of academic year** (June/July): Student check-outs and new check-ins create a surge of work
- **Summer months** (July-September): High turnover across the market
- **January**: New year tenant moves
- **End of each month**: Tenancies often start and end on the 1st, creating a monthly peak in the last and first weeks of each month

### How to handle peak periods

- Accept bookings early — do not wait until the week before to fill your diary
- Consider extending your working hours temporarily during peaks
- If you have staff, schedule them to cover the maximum capacity
- Communicate lead times to clients so they book with sufficient notice
- Prioritise existing client relationships over one-off bookings

### Quiet periods

Use quieter periods for:

- Business development (contacting new agents, marketing)
- Training and professional development
- Updating your templates and processes
- Equipment maintenance
- Financial administration

## Tools for booking management

### Calendar systems

A shared or cloud-based calendar (Google Calendar, Outlook, or similar) is the minimum. Block out inspection times, travel time, and buffer periods. Colour-code by client or report type for easy visual reference.

### Booking management software

As your business grows, dedicated booking management features become valuable. Some inventory platforms include scheduling functionality. Others integrate with general scheduling tools.

Look for features like:
- Online booking for clients
- Automated confirmation and reminder emails
- Calendar synchronisation
- Travel time estimation
- Job status tracking (booked, completed, delivered)

### Route planning

For clerks covering a wide area, route planning tools (Google Maps route planner or dedicated routing apps) can optimise your daily travel, saving time and fuel.

## Scaling your booking capacity

When demand exceeds your personal capacity, you have options:

- **Hire additional clerks**: Expand your team to cover more inspections. This requires investment in training and quality management.
- **Subcontract to other clerks**: Partner with independent clerks for overflow work. Maintain quality by providing your template and standards.
- **Raise prices**: If demand consistently exceeds supply, your prices may be too low. Increasing prices reduces demand to a manageable level while increasing revenue per inspection.
- **Narrow your geographic area**: Reducing travel time increases the number of inspections you can fit into a day.

## The bottom line

Managing multiple bookings efficiently is what separates a successful inventory business from a stressed one. The fundamentals are simple: schedule realistically, route intelligently, work systematically on-site, deliver promptly, and communicate clearly with clients.

Build these habits into your daily practice, and you will handle a full diary with confidence rather than chaos.
