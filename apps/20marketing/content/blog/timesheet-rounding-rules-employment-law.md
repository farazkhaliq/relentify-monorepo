When a worker clocks in at 7:53 and their shift starts at 8:00, what time goes on the timesheet? What about a clock-out at 5:07 when the shift ends at 5:00? These seem like trivial questions, but they play out across every shift, every worker, and every pay period — and the accumulated effect of rounding decisions can be significant.

Timesheet rounding is the practice of adjusting recorded clock-in and clock-out times to the nearest defined increment — typically five, six, ten, or fifteen minutes. It simplifies payroll calculations and smooths out the natural variation in when people actually start and stop work. But rounding also creates risk. If the rounding consistently benefits the employer at the expense of the worker, it may violate minimum wage laws, employment contracts, or principles of fair pay.

This guide explains how timesheet rounding works, when it is legally acceptable, and how to implement it without creating liability.

## How rounding works

The most common rounding approach is the "nearest increment" method. Here is how it works with a fifteen-minute increment:

- Clock-in at 7:53 → rounds to 8:00 (the nearest quarter hour)
- Clock-in at 7:52 → rounds to 7:45 (the nearest quarter hour)
- Clock-out at 5:07 → rounds to 5:00
- Clock-out at 5:08 → rounds to 5:15

The critical principle is that the rounding should be **neutral over time** — sometimes it adds minutes to the worker's recorded hours, sometimes it subtracts them, and over a reasonable period, the effect balances out.

Other common increments include:

- **5-minute rounding**: Clock-in at 7:58 rounds to 8:00; clock-in at 7:57 rounds to 7:55
- **6-minute rounding**: Based on tenths of an hour, useful for billing in professional services
- **10-minute rounding**: Less common but used in some manual payroll systems

## When rounding is legally acceptable

The general legal position in most jurisdictions is that rounding is permissible provided it does not systematically disadvantage workers. The key tests are:

### Neutrality

The rounding method must be neutral — it should not consistently round in the employer's favour. If analysis of actual timesheet data shows that rounding reduces workers' paid hours more often than it increases them, the method is biased and potentially unlawful.

### Minimum wage compliance

Regardless of rounding, the worker must be paid at least the minimum wage for all hours actually worked. If rounding causes a worker's effective hourly rate to fall below the minimum wage threshold, the employer is in breach — even if the rounding policy itself is ostensibly neutral.

This is particularly relevant for workers who are paid close to the minimum wage. A few minutes of rounding per shift can push the effective rate below the legal floor.

### Contractual terms

If the employment contract specifies that workers will be paid for actual hours worked, rounding may breach the contract. Check your employment agreements before implementing a rounding policy.

### Collective agreements

In workplaces covered by a collective agreement or union contract, rounding may be addressed specifically. Ensure your rounding policy is consistent with any applicable agreement.

## The risks of rounding

### Systematic under-payment

The most serious risk is that rounding systematically reduces workers' paid hours. This typically happens when:

- Clock-in times are rounded up (to the start of the shift) but clock-out times are also rounded down (to the end of the shift)
- The rounding increment is large (fifteen minutes) relative to the actual variation in clock times
- Workers consistently arrive a few minutes early and leave a few minutes late, but are only paid for the scheduled hours

Over a year, these small deductions compound. A worker who loses seven minutes per shift to rounding loses approximately thirty hours of paid time per year — nearly a full working week.

### Minimum wage breaches

For a worker earning the minimum wage at a scheduled eight-hour shift, losing even ten minutes to rounding reduces their effective hourly rate below the minimum. If regulators or tribunals examine the records, this constitutes a breach regardless of the employer's intent.

### Worker dissatisfaction

Even where rounding is technically neutral, workers who see their clock-in of 7:52 rounded to 8:00 feel that they are losing eight minutes of paid time. Over time, this perception erodes trust and contributes to disengagement — particularly if the rounding never seems to work in their favour.

## Alternatives to rounding

### Exact time recording

The simplest alternative is to pay for exact times. Modern timesheet systems record clock-in and clock-out to the minute, so there is no technical need to round. Payroll software can handle non-round numbers, and the resulting pay is precisely fair.

This is the approach recommended by most employment law advisors and is the default in many modern [timesheet platforms](/timesheets). Workers are paid for the time they actually worked, with no rounding applied.

### Grace periods

Instead of rounding, some businesses use a grace period: any clock-in within a defined window (say, seven minutes before or after the scheduled start) is treated as on time. This avoids rounding calculations while accommodating the natural variation in arrival times.

The key difference from rounding is that a grace period does not change the recorded time — it simply defines what counts as "on time" for attendance purposes. Pay is still calculated from the actual clock-in time.

### Scheduled time payment

Some businesses pay workers for their scheduled hours regardless of the exact clock-in and clock-out times, provided the worker was present for the full shift. Under this approach, a worker scheduled for 8:00 to 5:00 is paid for nine hours (minus break) whether they clocked in at 7:55 or 8:03.

This simplifies payroll but requires a clear policy on what happens when a worker is significantly early or late — at what point does the scheduled time no longer apply?

## Implementing rounding properly

If you choose to use rounding, follow these principles:

### Use the smallest practical increment

Five-minute rounding carries less risk than fifteen-minute rounding because the potential deduction per shift is smaller. Use the smallest increment that your payroll system can handle.

### Apply rounding symmetrically

Round both clock-in and clock-out times using the same method. Do not round clock-ins up (towards the shift start) while rounding clock-outs down (towards the shift end) — this creates a systematic bias against the worker.

### Audit regularly

At least quarterly, analyse your timesheet data to confirm that rounding is neutral in practice. Sum the minutes gained and lost by rounding for each worker over the period. If there is a consistent net loss for workers, adjust your approach.

### Document the policy

Include your rounding policy in the employee handbook and ensure workers understand how it works. Transparency reduces disputes and demonstrates good faith.

### Monitor minimum wage impact

For workers paid at or near the minimum wage, run a separate check to ensure that rounding does not reduce their effective hourly rate below the legal threshold. If it does, pay the difference.

## Summary

Timesheet rounding is a legacy practice from an era when manual payroll calculations required round numbers. Modern technology has largely eliminated the need for it — digital timesheets record exact times, and payroll software handles non-round calculations effortlessly.

If you do round, do it carefully: small increments, symmetrical application, regular audits, and minimum wage monitoring. Better yet, consider paying for exact time. It is simpler, fairer, and eliminates the compliance risk entirely.

The goal of any timesheet system is accuracy. Rounding, by definition, introduces inaccuracy. In most cases, the simplest path to compliance and worker trust is to record and pay for the actual time worked.
