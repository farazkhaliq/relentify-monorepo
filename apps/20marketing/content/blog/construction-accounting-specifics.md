# Construction Accounting: Unique Challenges and How to Handle Them

Construction accounting operates in a world of its own. Long project timelines, complex cost structures, multiple subcontractors, retention clauses, and progress billing create financial management challenges that standard accounting approaches simply cannot handle. A retail business records a sale when a customer pays at the till. A construction business might not complete a project for two years, billing in stages against milestones that are themselves subject to variation and dispute.

If you run or manage the finances of a construction business, understanding these unique requirements is not optional — it is the difference between knowing whether you are profitable and hoping you are.

## Why Construction Is Different

Most businesses sell products or services in relatively short cycles. A transaction happens, revenue is recognised, and costs are matched. Construction projects can span months or years. A single project might involve hundreds of cost lines, dozens of subcontractors, and multiple billing milestones.

This creates several accounting challenges that are largely unique to the industry:

- Revenue must be recognised over time, not at a single point
- Costs must be tracked at the individual job level, not just overall
- Billing often happens before or after work is actually completed
- Retention money is withheld for extended periods
- Variations and claims can significantly change the final value of a contract

Standard accounting methods designed for simpler business models fall short. Construction businesses need specialised approaches to get an accurate picture of their financial position.

## Job Costing: The Foundation

Job costing is the practice of tracking all costs associated with each individual project. This includes direct costs (materials, labour, subcontractors, equipment hire) and an allocation of indirect costs (site supervision, insurance, head office overhead).

Without job costing, you might know that your business made a profit overall last year, but you would not know which projects were profitable and which were losing money. That information is critical for pricing future work, managing current projects, and making strategic decisions about which types of work to pursue.

Effective job costing requires:

**A detailed cost code structure** that categorises expenses consistently across projects. Common categories include preliminaries, groundworks, structural work, mechanical and electrical installations, finishes, and external works.

**Disciplined cost recording** where every purchase order, invoice, and timesheet is allocated to the correct job and cost code. This is where many construction businesses struggle — it is easy to fall behind, and catching up is difficult.

**Regular cost reviews** comparing actual costs to the budget for each project. Ideally, this happens weekly on active projects. Early identification of cost overruns gives you time to take corrective action.

## Revenue Recognition: Percentage of Completion

The percentage of completion method is the standard approach for recognising revenue on long-term construction contracts. Instead of waiting until a project is finished to record any revenue, you recognise revenue in proportion to the work completed.

If a project is estimated to be 40% complete, you recognise 40% of the total expected revenue (and the corresponding proportion of expected costs). As the project progresses, you update the percentage and recognise additional revenue accordingly.

The challenge is measuring the percentage of completion accurately. There are two common approaches:

**Cost-to-cost method:** The percentage of completion equals the costs incurred to date divided by the total estimated costs. This is the most common method because cost data is readily available. However, it can be misleading if costs are front-loaded (such as purchasing materials early in a project).

**Survey method:** An independent assessment of physical progress. A surveyor or project manager estimates the percentage of work completed based on site inspections. This is more accurate but more subjective and resource-intensive.

Whichever method you use, the key input is the total estimated cost at completion. If this estimate changes (and it almost always does as projects progress), the revenue recognised must be adjusted. This makes accurate and regularly updated cost forecasts essential.

## Progress Billing and Valuation

Construction contracts typically provide for interim payments based on the value of work completed. Each month (or at agreed milestones), the contractor submits a valuation or application for payment.

Progress billing and revenue recognition are related but separate concepts. You might bill more or less than the revenue you have recognised. The difference appears on the balance sheet:

- If you have billed more than the revenue recognised, the excess is a liability (sometimes called "billings in excess of costs" or "deferred income")
- If you have billed less than the revenue recognised, the shortfall is an asset (sometimes called "costs in excess of billings" or "accrued income")

Managing these timing differences is important for understanding your true financial position. A business that appears to have strong cash from progress billing might actually have recognised very little profit if much of that billing is ahead of actual work completion.

## Retention

Retention is a standard feature of construction contracts. The client withholds a percentage (typically 3-5%) of each progress payment as security against defects. Half is usually released at practical completion, and the remainder at the end of the defects liability period (often 12 months later).

From an accounting perspective, retention creates a timing challenge. You have earned the money and recognised it as revenue, but you will not receive the cash for months or even years. Retention receivables must be tracked carefully and reviewed for recoverability.

For cash flow purposes, retention can be significant. A business with several large projects might have hundreds of thousands in retention outstanding at any time. This money is effectively financing the client, not the contractor.

Include retention in your cash flow forecasts and factor it into your working capital management. Some businesses negotiate reduced retention percentages or provide retention bonds as an alternative to cash retention.

## Subcontractor Management

Most construction projects involve subcontractors, and managing their costs is a major accounting challenge. Each subcontractor submits their own applications for payment, which must be verified against their contract, the work they have actually completed, and any variations or claims.

From an accounting perspective, you need to:

- Record subcontractor liabilities when work is certified
- Track retention withheld from subcontractors
- Manage variation accounts and disputed amounts
- Process payments in accordance with contract terms and applicable legislation

Depending on your jurisdiction, there may also be specific tax obligations related to subcontractor payments. In the UK, the Construction Industry Scheme (CIS) requires contractors to deduct tax from subcontractor payments unless the subcontractor holds a gross payment certificate.

## Variations and Claims

Few construction projects are completed exactly as originally contracted. Variations — changes to the scope, specification, or timing of work — are routine. Each variation potentially changes both the contract value and the estimated cost at completion.

Accounting for variations requires judgement. If a variation has been instructed and valued, including it in revenue is straightforward. If a variation has been instructed but not yet valued, you need to estimate the likely value and decide whether it meets the criteria for revenue recognition.

Claims for additional time or money (due to delays, disruption, or other issues) are even more subjective. Until a claim is agreed or determined, including it in revenue is risky because the amount and even the entitlement may be uncertain.

The prudent approach is to recognise variation and claim revenue only when it is probable that the amount will be received and can be measured reliably. Document your reasoning and review it regularly as negotiations progress.

## Working Capital and Cash Flow

Construction businesses are notoriously cash-hungry. Long payment cycles, retention, upfront material purchases, and the gap between incurring costs and receiving payment create constant cash flow pressure.

Effective cash flow management in construction requires:

- **Accurate project cash flow forecasts** for each active project, showing when costs will be incurred and when payments are expected
- **Consolidated cash flow forecasting** that aggregates all project forecasts with head office income and expenditure
- **Prompt billing** — submit valuations on time every month and follow up rigorously on late payments
- **Payment monitoring** — track payment timelines against contractual terms and chase overdue amounts immediately
- **Working capital facilities** — maintain adequate credit facilities to cover timing gaps

Many profitable construction businesses fail not because they run out of work but because they run out of cash. Cash flow management is arguably the single most important financial discipline in construction.

## Getting It Right

Construction accounting is complex, but the principles are well established. Job costing gives you visibility into project performance. The percentage of completion method matches revenue to the work done. Progress billing and retention management keep cash flow on track. And disciplined variation accounting prevents nasty surprises at project completion.

The businesses that manage these processes well consistently outperform their competitors. They price work more accurately, manage projects more profitably, collect cash more efficiently, and make better strategic decisions.

If your current accounting processes are not giving you clear, timely, project-level financial information, it is time to invest in better systems and processes. The cost of not knowing your true financial position is always higher than the cost of finding out.
