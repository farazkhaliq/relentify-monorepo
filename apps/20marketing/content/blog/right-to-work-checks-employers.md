Every employer has a legal duty to check that their employees have the right to work in the country before they start employment. Getting this wrong can result in significant penalties, including fines and, in serious cases, criminal prosecution. Yet the process itself is straightforward once you understand the steps.

This guide explains how to conduct right to work checks properly and consistently.

## Why right to work checks matter

Right to work checks serve two purposes:

1. **Legal compliance**: Employers who hire someone without the legal right to work face civil penalties. In many jurisdictions, fines can be substantial — often tens of thousands per illegal worker.
2. **Statutory excuse**: Conducting the check correctly gives you a "statutory excuse" — a legal defence that protects you from penalties if it later turns out the employee did not have the right to work, provided you conducted the check in good faith.

The statutory excuse is the key concept. Even if an employee produces fraudulent documents, if you followed the correct checking process, you are protected. If you did not follow the process, you are liable regardless of what the employee told you.

## When to conduct checks

Right to work checks must be conducted **before employment begins**. This means before the employee's first day of work — not during their first week, not during their probation period, and not when you get around to it.

For some employees, you will also need to conduct **follow-up checks** at specified intervals. This applies to employees with time-limited permission to work — for example, those on a visa with an expiry date. You must conduct a follow-up check before the expiry date to confirm they still have the right to work.

## The three-step check

The right to work check follows a simple process:

### Step 1: Obtain original documents

Ask the employee to provide original documents from the acceptable documents list published by your government. You cannot accept photocopies or digital images — you need the originals.

Acceptable documents typically fall into two categories:

**List A: Unrestricted right to work** — Documents that show the person has permanent or unrestricted permission to work. These include:
- A passport showing citizenship of the country
- A permanent residence card or document
- A birth or adoption certificate combined with an official document showing the holder's permanent status

If the employee provides a List A document, a single check before employment is sufficient. No follow-up checks are needed.

**List B: Time-limited right to work** — Documents that show the person has temporary or restricted permission to work. These include:
- A passport with a visa or endorsement showing a time-limited right to work
- A residence permit with an expiry date
- A certificate of application with a positive verification from the relevant authority

If the employee provides a List B document, you must conduct follow-up checks before the permission expires.

### Step 2: Check the documents

Once you have the original documents, check them carefully:

- **Photographs**: Does the photo match the person in front of you?
- **Date of birth**: Is it consistent across documents?
- **Expiry dates**: Are the documents still valid?
- **Permission to work**: Does the visa or endorsement permit the type of work you are offering?
- **Restrictions**: Are there any restrictions on hours or type of work?
- **Authenticity**: Do the documents appear genuine? Look for signs of tampering, inconsistent fonts, or poor-quality reproduction.

If anything looks suspicious, do not simply reject the candidate — seek advice. Contact the relevant authority to verify the document if you have doubts.

### Step 3: Make and retain copies

Make a clear copy of every document provided:

- For passports: copy the page showing the photograph, nationality, date of birth, signature, date of expiry, and any visa endorsements.
- For other documents: copy the entire document, front and back.

Record the date the check was conducted and store the copies securely. You must keep these records for the duration of the employee's employment and for a specified period after they leave (typically two years).

## Online verification services

Many countries now offer online verification systems that allow employers to check an individual's right to work digitally. These systems are often linked to the employee's immigration status and can provide real-time confirmation.

If an online system is available in your jurisdiction, it may be the required method for certain types of checks (for example, for employees with digital-only immigration status).

To use online verification:
1. The employee provides you with a share code or reference number
2. You enter the code into the government's online system
3. The system confirms the person's right to work and any restrictions
4. You save or print the confirmation as your record

Online checks are increasingly replacing document-based checks for certain categories of worker. Check the current guidance to understand when online verification is required or optional.

## Conducting checks fairly

You must conduct right to work checks on **every** new employee, regardless of nationality, appearance, or any other characteristic. Checking only employees who "look foreign" or have non-local names is discriminatory and illegal.

Apply the same process to every new hire. This protects you from discrimination claims and ensures consistency.

If an employee cannot provide the required documents before their start date, do not allow them to start work until the check is complete. Making exceptions creates legal risk.

## Follow-up checks

For employees with time-limited permission to work, you need to conduct a follow-up check before their current permission expires. The follow-up check follows the same three-step process:

1. Obtain updated original documents
2. Check them thoroughly
3. Make and retain copies with the date of the follow-up check

If the employee's permission has been extended, record the new expiry date and schedule the next follow-up. If their permission has not been extended and they cannot demonstrate an ongoing right to work, you may need to end the employment.

Set up a system to track expiry dates and schedule follow-up checks. A spreadsheet works for small teams, but integrated [payroll](/payroll) and HR software is more reliable as you grow. ## Record keeping

Keep all right to work check records securely. Your records should include:

- Copies of all documents checked (passport pages, visa endorsements, residence permits)
- The date of each check
- The method used (document check, online verification, or both)
- Notes on any follow-up actions required

Records must be kept for the required retention period after the employee leaves. In the UK, this is two years. Other jurisdictions have different requirements — check your local rules.

Good record keeping is your evidence that you conducted the check. Without it, you cannot establish a statutory excuse even if you did check the documents at the time.

## Penalties for non-compliance

The penalties for employing someone without the right to work are severe:

- **Civil penalties**: Fines per illegal worker, which can be substantial. Repeat offences attract higher penalties.
- **Criminal prosecution**: In cases of knowing or reckless employment of illegal workers, criminal charges can be brought against the employer, with potential prison sentences.
- **Reputational damage**: Immigration enforcement actions are often publicised, which can damage your business reputation.

The statutory excuse is your defence. Conduct every check properly, keep your records, and you are protected.

## Practical tips

- **Check before the first day**: Never allow an employee to start without a completed check.
- **Use a checklist**: Create a standard checklist for right to work checks so nothing is missed.
- **Train your team**: Anyone involved in hiring should understand the process.
- **Be consistent**: Apply the same process to every new hire.
- **Track expiry dates**: For time-limited permissions, set reminders well before the expiry date.
- **Keep records secure**: Store copies in compliance with data protection requirements.
- **Seek advice when unsure**: If a document looks unfamiliar or suspicious, contact the relevant authority rather than guessing.

Right to work checks are a non-negotiable part of the hiring process. They take a few minutes per employee but protect your business from serious penalties. Build them into your onboarding process and treat them with the same importance as any other compliance obligation.
