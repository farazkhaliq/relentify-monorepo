If your business employs hourly workers, you need a reliable way to record when they start and finish each shift. This is not optional — it is a legal requirement in most jurisdictions, a prerequisite for accurate payroll, and the foundation of fair employment practices.

Clock-in clock-out systems have evolved significantly from the mechanical punch clocks of the twentieth century. Today, options range from wall-mounted terminals to mobile apps, biometric scanners to GPS-verified digital timesheets. This guide explains the different approaches, their pros and cons, and how to choose the right one for your business.

## What a clock-in clock-out system does

At its core, a clock-in clock-out system records four things:

1. **Who** clocked in or out
2. **When** they did it
3. **Where** they were (in some systems)
4. **How long** they worked, including breaks

This data feeds directly into payroll calculations, compliance records, and workforce reports. Without it, you are relying on memory, estimates, or paper records — all of which introduce errors and disputes.

## Types of clock-in systems

### Physical time clocks

The traditional approach: a device mounted on a wall near the entrance to the workplace. Workers either swipe a card, enter a PIN, or use a biometric scanner (fingerprint or face recognition) to register their arrival and departure.

**Pros:**
- Simple and familiar
- Workers cannot clock in from the wrong location
- No smartphone required

**Cons:**
- Requires hardware purchase and maintenance
- Only works at fixed locations — unsuitable for field-based or multi-site workers
- Vulnerable to buddy punching (one worker clocking in for another) unless biometric

### Spreadsheet-based tracking

Some businesses use shared spreadsheets where workers manually enter their start and end times. This is a step up from paper but shares many of the same weaknesses.

**Pros:**
- Free or very low cost
- Familiar to most workers
- Can be accessed from anywhere

**Cons:**
- Relies on self-reporting and honesty
- No real-time visibility for managers
- Error-prone — typos, formatting issues, forgotten entries
- Difficult to audit or search at scale

### Mobile timesheet apps

The modern standard for most small businesses. Workers download an app to their smartphone and clock in with a tap. The system records the time and, optionally, the GPS location. Managers review and approve timesheets from a web dashboard.

**Pros:**
- Works anywhere — office, field, home, or client site
- Real-time visibility for managers
- GPS verification available
- Automatic break tracking
- Data feeds directly into payroll and reporting

**Cons:**
- Requires workers to have a smartphone
- May require mobile data or Wi-Fi (though most apps work offline)
- Some workers may resist using personal devices for work purposes

Platforms like [Relentify](/timesheets) use this approach — a mobile-first system where workers clock in and out from their phone, with GPS verification and manager approval built in.

### Kiosk mode

A hybrid approach where a single tablet or device is set up at a workplace, and workers clock in using a shared terminal. Each worker selects their name and taps a button to register their arrival.

**Pros:**
- No personal device required
- Suitable for workplaces where smartphones are impractical (kitchens, warehouses, factories)
- Lower hardware cost than dedicated time clocks

**Cons:**
- Queues can form at shift changeover
- Vulnerable to buddy punching unless combined with PIN or biometric
- Only works at fixed locations

## Key features to look for

### Real-time dashboard

Managers should be able to see at a glance who is currently clocked in, who is on break, and who has not yet started their shift. This is especially valuable for businesses with multiple sites or shift patterns.

### GPS verification

For any business with workers operating away from a central office — construction, cleaning, care, security, maintenance — GPS verification is essential. It confirms that the worker was at the correct location when they clocked in, without requiring a physical terminal at every site.

### Break tracking

Working time regulations in most countries mandate minimum rest breaks after a certain number of consecutive hours. A good clock-in system should track breaks automatically or prompt workers to record them, ensuring compliance without relying on memory.

### Overtime alerts

If a worker is approaching their overtime threshold, the system should flag it. This gives managers the opportunity to adjust schedules before overtime costs accumulate — or to approve the extra hours knowingly rather than discovering them after the fact.

### Offline mode

Not every worksite has reliable internet. A clock-in system should allow workers to record their times even when offline, syncing the data automatically when connectivity returns.

### Payroll export

The whole point of tracking hours is to pay people accurately. The system should make it straightforward to export approved hours in a format compatible with your payroll software — whether that is a CSV file, a direct integration, or an API connection.

## Handling common challenges

### Workers forgetting to clock in

This happens, especially in the early days. The best systems send automatic reminders — a push notification at the expected start time, or an alert to the manager when a scheduled worker has not clocked in.

### Disputed clock-in times

Occasionally, a worker will dispute a recorded time — claiming they started earlier than the system shows, or that they forgot to clock out and worked later than recorded. Having GPS data and exact timestamps makes these disputes easier to resolve. Most systems also allow managers to manually adjust entries, with an audit trail that records who made the change and when.

### Workers without smartphones

For businesses where some workers do not have smartphones, kiosk mode or a shared tablet at the worksite is the practical solution. Alternatively, some systems allow clock-in via SMS or a simple phone call.

### Resistance to tracking

Some workers view clock-in systems with suspicion, especially if they are being introduced for the first time. Transparent communication is key: explain that the system protects their hours and ensures they are paid correctly. Most resistance fades quickly once workers see that the system is simple to use and works in their favour.

## Legal requirements

In most jurisdictions, employers are legally required to keep records of hours worked by their employees. The specific requirements vary:

- **Retention period**: Some countries require records to be kept for two years, others for six or more
- **Data points**: Some jurisdictions require start and end times, others also mandate break records and overtime hours
- **Accessibility**: Records must be producible within a reasonable timeframe if requested by an employee or labour authority

A digital clock-in system handles all of these requirements automatically. Records are stored securely, timestamped, and searchable — a significant advantage over paper or spreadsheet-based approaches.

## Choosing the right system for your business

The best system depends on your workforce:

- **Office-based teams**: A mobile app or desktop-based clock-in works well. GPS is optional.
- **Field-based workers** (construction, cleaning, care): A mobile app with GPS verification is the clear choice.
- **Retail and hospitality**: Kiosk mode on a shared tablet, combined with a mobile app for managers.
- **Mixed workforce**: A system that supports both mobile clock-in and kiosk mode gives you flexibility.

Regardless of the approach, the system should be simple enough that workers can use it without training, robust enough to handle edge cases (offline mode, disputed times, forgotten clock-outs), and connected enough to feed data into your payroll and reporting workflows.

## Getting started

The transition from no system — or a paper-based system — to a digital clock-in tool is one of the highest-value operational improvements a small business can make. It reduces payroll errors, strengthens compliance, and gives managers real-time visibility into their workforce.

Start simple. Get everyone clocking in and out consistently. Then build on that foundation with break tracking, overtime monitoring, shift scheduling, and reporting. The data you collect in the first month will already be more useful than anything a paper timesheet could provide.
