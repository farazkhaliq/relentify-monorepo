Minimum wage legislation exists in most countries, and compliance is not optional. Yet minimum wage violations are among the most common employment law breaches, and many of them are unintentional. Businesses that believe they are paying correctly discover — often during an audit or a worker complaint — that their actual pay practices fall short of the legal minimum.

The root cause is almost always the same: inaccurate or incomplete time records. If you do not know exactly how many hours a worker actually worked, you cannot know whether their effective hourly rate meets the minimum. This guide explains the connection between time records and minimum wage compliance and how to get both right.

## How minimum wage is calculated

The minimum wage is expressed as a rate per hour. Compliance is assessed by dividing the total pay in a pay period by the total hours worked in that period:

**Effective hourly rate = Total pay / Total hours worked**

If the effective rate is at or above the minimum wage, you are compliant. If it falls below, you are in breach — regardless of what the contract says the hourly rate is.

This is where the nuance lies. The contract might state an hourly rate above the minimum wage. But if the worker's actual hours exceed the hours they are paid for — due to unpaid preparation time, unreported overtime, or timesheet rounding — the effective rate drops. And if it drops below the minimum, the business is non-compliant.

## Common ways businesses accidentally breach the minimum wage

### Unpaid working time

Any time a worker is required to be at the workplace or available for work counts as working time. This includes:

- Time spent putting on uniforms or protective equipment before clocking in
- Security checks at the end of a shift
- Compulsory briefings or handover periods
- Time spent waiting for work to be assigned (even if no work is performed)
- Travel between work sites during the working day

If these activities happen before clock-in or after clock-out, the hours are effectively unpaid. The worker's recorded hours understate their actual working time, and the effective hourly rate is reduced accordingly.

### Timesheet rounding

As discussed in depth in our [guide to timesheet rounding](/blog/timesheet-rounding-rules-employment-law), rounding clock-in times to the nearest increment can reduce recorded hours. For workers paid at or near the minimum wage, even small reductions can push the effective rate below the legal threshold.

### Deductions from pay

Some employers make deductions from pay for uniforms, tools, training, or breakages. If these deductions reduce the worker's pay below the minimum wage for that pay period, the deduction is unlawful — even if the worker agreed to it.

### Unpaid overtime

Workers who regularly work beyond their scheduled hours without pay — whether by arriving early, staying late, or working through breaks — are effectively working for free during those extra minutes. If total pay divided by total actual hours (including the unpaid extra) falls below the minimum wage, there is a breach.

### Sleep-in shifts

Workers who are required to be on the premises overnight (common in care and residential settings) may or may not be entitled to the minimum wage for those hours, depending on the jurisdiction and the specific circumstances. The case law in this area is complex and has been subject to significant litigation.

## The role of time records

Accurate time records are the only way to verify minimum wage compliance. Without them, you cannot calculate the effective hourly rate, and you cannot defend against a complaint.

The records should capture:

- **Actual start and end times** (not scheduled times)
- **Break start and end times** (to calculate actual working hours)
- **Any additional working time** outside normal shifts (preparation, handover, travel between sites)
- **Total hours per pay period**
- **Total pay per pay period** (including any deductions)

From this data, you can calculate the effective hourly rate for every worker in every pay period and confirm that it meets or exceeds the minimum wage.

## Conducting a minimum wage audit

Even if you believe your pay practices are compliant, it is worth conducting a periodic audit. Here is how:

### Step 1: Identify workers most at risk

Focus first on workers whose pay is closest to the minimum wage, workers who regularly work unpaid overtime, and workers who are subject to deductions. These are the groups where a breach is most likely.

### Step 2: Compare actual hours to paid hours

For each worker, compare their actual working hours (from timesheets) to the hours used for payroll calculation. Are they the same? If not, why not? Common discrepancies include:

- Timesheet rounding reducing paid hours
- Breaks being deducted from pay even when workers did not take them
- Overtime hours not being recorded or paid

### Step 3: Calculate the effective hourly rate

For each pay period, divide total pay by total actual hours. If the result is below the minimum wage, you have a compliance issue.

### Step 4: Check for hidden working time

Are workers doing anything that counts as working time but is not being recorded? Briefings, uniform changes, equipment setup, security checks, travel between sites? If so, this time needs to be captured in the timesheet or accounted for in the pay calculation.

### Step 5: Review deductions

List all deductions applied to workers' pay and check whether any of them reduce pay below the minimum wage in any pay period. If they do, the deductions need to be restructured — spread over more pay periods, reduced, or eliminated.

## Building compliance into your timesheet process

The best way to ensure ongoing minimum wage compliance is to build it into your standard timesheet and payroll process:

### Record actual hours, not scheduled hours

Your timesheet system should capture when workers actually start and finish, not when they are scheduled to. This is the single most important change a business can make.

### Include all working time

Define what counts as working time in your business and ensure it is all captured. If workers need ten minutes to put on protective equipment before their shift, that ten minutes should be recorded.

### Automate the compliance check

Some payroll systems can calculate the effective hourly rate automatically and flag any worker whose rate falls below the minimum. If your system does not do this, run the calculation manually at least quarterly.

### Train managers

Managers who approve timesheets should understand the minimum wage implications. If they routinely approve timesheets that understate hours — either through rounding, omitted preparation time, or ignored overtime — they are inadvertently facilitating non-compliance.

### Keep records

Maintain time records for the period required by your jurisdiction — typically two to six years. These records are your defence against future complaints and your evidence of good practice during audits.

## The consequences of non-compliance

Minimum wage violations carry significant consequences:

- **Back pay**: The employer must pay the difference between what was paid and what should have been paid, for every affected worker, for every affected pay period
- **Penalties**: Many jurisdictions impose financial penalties in addition to back pay — sometimes a percentage of the underpayment, sometimes a fixed amount per worker
- **Public naming**: Some countries publish the names of employers found to have breached minimum wage law, creating reputational damage
- **Legal costs**: Defending against a minimum wage claim or investigation involves legal fees regardless of the outcome
- **Worker relations**: Workers who discover they have been underpaid — even unintentionally — lose trust in the employer

## Summary

Minimum wage compliance is not complicated in principle: pay workers at least the legal minimum for every hour they work. The challenge is in measuring those hours accurately. Every minute of unrecorded working time, every rounding deduction, and every unapproved deduction from pay pushes the effective rate closer to — or below — the legal floor.

Accurate time records are the foundation of compliance. A system that captures actual hours, includes all working time, and produces data that can be audited provides both ongoing protection and peace of mind. The cost of implementing such a system is trivial compared to the cost of getting it wrong.
