Zero-hours contracts (also known as casual contracts in some jurisdictions) allow employers to offer work without guaranteeing a minimum number of hours. Workers accept or decline work as it is offered. This arrangement provides flexibility for both parties, but it creates specific challenges for timesheet management, payroll, and compliance that fixed-hours contracts do not.

This guide explains how to manage zero-hours workers effectively using timesheet software, covering the practical, legal, and financial considerations.

## What makes zero-hours contracts different

### No guaranteed hours

The defining feature of a zero-hours contract is that the employer is not obligated to offer any minimum number of hours, and the worker is not obligated to accept. This means the workforce is inherently variable — the number of hours worked changes week to week based on demand and worker availability.

### Variable pay

Because hours vary, pay varies. Workers may receive significantly different amounts from one pay period to the next. Accurate timesheets are essential to ensure that each pay cheque reflects the actual hours worked — there is no fixed salary to fall back on.

### Accrued entitlements

Despite the lack of guaranteed hours, zero-hours workers are typically entitled to:

- Holiday pay (accrued based on hours worked)
- Statutory sick pay (where qualifying conditions are met)
- Minimum wage protections
- Working time protections (rest periods, maximum hours)
- Protection from unfair dismissal (after qualifying service)

Calculating these entitlements requires accurate, ongoing records of hours worked.

### Availability versus worked hours

Zero-hours workers may be available for work but not offered any. The distinction between "available" and "working" matters for compliance and occasionally for entitlement calculations. Your records should focus on actual hours worked, not hours available.

## Why timesheet software is essential for zero-hours workers

### Payroll accuracy

With no fixed salary, every payroll run depends entirely on timesheet data. An error in recorded hours translates directly to an error in pay. Given that zero-hours workers often have limited financial buffers, underpayment is especially damaging.

### Holiday pay calculation

Holiday pay for zero-hours workers is typically calculated as a percentage of pay earned over a reference period — often 12.07% (representing the statutory 5.6 weeks of leave divided by the remaining 46.4 working weeks). Accurate records of hours worked and pay earned are needed to calculate the correct holiday pay.

Some businesses roll up holiday pay into the hourly rate (paying a higher hourly rate that includes the holiday element). Others accrue holiday separately and pay it when leave is taken. Either approach requires accurate time records.

### Working time compliance

Zero-hours workers are covered by working time regulations. If a worker accepts shifts across multiple days and the total exceeds the weekly maximum, the employer has a compliance issue — even though no individual shift appeared problematic.

This is particularly relevant for workers who hold zero-hours contracts with multiple employers. While the employer can only control and monitor the hours worked for their own organisation, they should still be alert to signs that a worker is exceeding safe limits.

### Minimum wage verification

As with all workers, zero-hours employees must be paid at least the minimum wage for every hour worked. Because their hours vary, the minimum wage calculation should be checked for every pay period — not assumed based on the contracted rate.

## Setting up timesheet tracking for zero-hours workers

### Use the same system as your other workers

Zero-hours workers should use the same timesheet system as your permanent staff. This ensures consistency, simplifies administration, and provides a single data source for payroll and reporting.

Platforms like [Relentify](/timesheets) handle variable-hour workers within the same framework as fixed-hour employees — same clock-in process, same approval workflow, same reporting.

### Record every shift

Every shift worked by a zero-hours employee must be recorded, regardless of how short. A two-hour shift is just as important to track as an eight-hour one. The system should make it equally easy to clock in for a short shift as a long one.

### Track offered and declined shifts (optional)

While not legally required in most jurisdictions, tracking the shifts offered to each worker and whether they accepted or declined can be useful for:

- Demonstrating that work was genuinely offered (relevant if a worker claims they were effectively dismissed by not being offered hours)
- Understanding worker availability patterns
- Managing the worker pool effectively

### Configure variable schedules

Unlike fixed-hour workers who appear on the rota every week, zero-hours workers may work some weeks and not others. Your scheduling system should accommodate this variability — allowing you to offer shifts to a pool of available workers rather than assigning them in advance.

## Handling common scenarios

### Irregular work patterns

A zero-hours worker might work twenty hours one week and none the next. Your system should handle this without treating the zero-hour weeks as anomalies. Payroll should process correctly even when a worker has no hours in a given period.

### Multiple employers

Some zero-hours workers hold contracts with several employers. While you can only track hours worked for your organisation, be aware that the worker may be approaching working time limits when their total hours across all employers are considered. If a worker appears fatigued or mentions working elsewhere, it may be worth a conversation about safe working hours.

### Converting to permanent

If a zero-hours worker transitions to a permanent contract, their historical timesheet data is valuable for determining their typical working pattern, average hours, and entitlement calculations. Complete records make the transition smooth.

### Ending the arrangement

When a zero-hours arrangement ends, the employer owes any accrued but untaken holiday pay. This calculation requires accurate records of all hours worked and any leave already taken or paid. Without these records, the final payment becomes a negotiation rather than a calculation.

## Legal considerations

### Exclusivity clauses

In some jurisdictions, exclusivity clauses in zero-hours contracts (preventing the worker from working for other employers) are unlawful or unenforceable. Be aware of the rules in your jurisdiction.

### Regular work patterns

If a zero-hours worker has been working a regular pattern over an extended period (for example, consistently working 30 hours per week for six months), some jurisdictions may consider this to have established a regular contract — even if the written agreement says zero hours. Timesheet records demonstrate the actual pattern.

### National insurance and tax

In many countries, national insurance contributions or payroll taxes are based on earnings per pay period. Variable earnings from zero-hours work mean that the contributions vary too. Accurate timesheet data ensures correct calculations.

## Best practices

### Treat zero-hours workers fairly

The fact that hours are not guaranteed does not mean the workers should be treated as second-class. Ensure they have equal access to the timesheet system, receive the same communication about shifts and policies, and are included in training and development opportunities.

### Communicate clearly

Be transparent about how the arrangement works: how shifts are offered, how timesheets should be submitted, how pay is calculated, and how holiday entitlement accrues. Clear communication prevents misunderstandings that can escalate into grievances or claims.

### Monitor utilisation

If you consistently offer a zero-hours worker significant hours week after week, consider whether a fixed-hours contract would be more appropriate. It provides the worker with more security and may simplify your administration.

### Keep complete records

Maintain timesheet records for zero-hours workers for at least the legally required retention period. These records are your defence against claims of underpayment, non-compliance, and unfair treatment.

## Summary

Zero-hours contracts offer valuable flexibility, but they demand rigorous time tracking to ensure fair pay, accurate entitlement calculations, and legal compliance. The variability that makes these arrangements attractive is the same variability that makes record-keeping essential.

A timesheet system that handles variable hours, calculates holiday accrual accurately, and integrates with payroll is not optional for businesses that use zero-hours contracts — it is a prerequisite for managing them responsibly.
