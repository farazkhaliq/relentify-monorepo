At some point, every growing business hits the limit of managing HR in spreadsheets and filing cabinets. Employee records are scattered across multiple files, holiday tracking depends on a shared calendar that nobody updates, and every new starter means re-entering the same information in five different places.

HR software solves these problems by putting employee information, processes, and records in one system. But with dozens of options available, choosing the right one can be difficult. This guide helps you evaluate what you actually need.

## When do you need HR software?

There is no magic number, but most businesses start feeling the pain when they reach 10-15 employees. At that point:

- Tracking holiday and absence manually becomes error-prone
- Employee records are stored inconsistently
- Onboarding involves too many manual steps
- Compliance requirements (pension, right to work, contracts) become harder to track
- Managers spend increasing time on administrative tasks

If you recognise these problems, it is time to consider a system.

## Essential features for small businesses

Not every feature matters equally. Focus on the features that address your actual pain points:

### Employee records
A central database for all employee information: personal details, contact information, employment dates, job title, salary, emergency contacts, and documents. This is the foundation — everything else builds on it.

Good employee records should be:
- Easy to update
- Searchable
- Secure (with appropriate access controls)
- Able to store documents (contracts, certificates, letters)

### Holiday and absence management
Automated tracking of holiday bookings, approvals, balances, and accrual. The system should handle:
- Holiday requests and approvals (ideally with a self-service portal for employees)
- Automatic accrual calculations for part-time and mid-year starters
- Absence recording (sickness, appointments, other leave types)
- Visibility of team availability
- Reporting on absence patterns

This is often the feature that delivers the most immediate time saving.

### Document storage
The ability to store and associate documents with employee records: contracts, policies, certificates, disciplinary records, performance reviews. Good document management means you can find any employee document in seconds rather than searching through email or filing cabinets.

### Onboarding workflows
Checklists and workflows for new starters: collecting personal information, right to work checks, issuing contracts, setting up payroll, pension enrolment, and equipment provisioning. A good onboarding module ensures nothing gets missed.

### Reporting
Basic reporting on headcount, absence rates, turnover, and other workforce metrics. Reports should be easy to generate and customisable enough to answer the questions you actually ask.

## Important but not essential for day one

### Performance management
Tools for setting objectives, conducting reviews, and tracking performance over time. Useful for structured businesses, but many small teams manage performance informally with good results.

### Time and attendance
Tracking working hours, especially for shift workers or remote teams. This overlaps with [timesheet software](/timesheets) — if you already have a timekeeping solution, integration is more important than duplication.

### Recruitment
Applicant tracking, job posting, and candidate management. Valuable for businesses that hire frequently, but may be overkill if you recruit a few times a year.

### Payroll
Some HR platforms include payroll processing. Others integrate with standalone payroll software. Whether you want HR and [payroll](/payroll) in one system or two depends on your existing setup and preferences.

## What to evaluate

### Usability
The best features in the world are useless if nobody can figure out how to use them. Evaluate:
- How intuitive is the interface? Can a non-technical manager use it without training?
- Is there a mobile app or mobile-friendly interface?
- How easy is it for employees to access their own information (self-service)?

A system that requires extensive training or constant support from IT is a poor fit for a small business.

### Integration
Your HR system does not operate in isolation. Consider how it connects to:
- **Payroll**: Employee data changes (new starters, leavers, salary changes) should flow to payroll without re-entry
- **Accounting**: Employment costs should be reflected in your accounts
- **Timesheets**: Hours worked should feed into both HR (attendance tracking) and payroll (pay calculation)
- **Pension provider**: Contribution data should be exportable in the format your provider requires

The fewer places you need to enter the same information, the fewer errors you will make and the less time you will spend on administration.

### Scalability
Choose software that can grow with your business. A system designed for five employees may struggle at fifty. Consider:
- Pricing model: per user, per feature, flat rate?
- Feature tiers: will you need to upgrade as you grow?
- Data limits: are there caps on storage or records?

### Support
When something goes wrong — and it will — you need access to help. Evaluate:
- Is support included in the price?
- What channels are available (phone, email, chat)?
- What are the response times?
- Is there a knowledge base or help centre for self-service?

### Security and compliance
Employee data is sensitive. Check:
- Where is data stored? (Ideally in your region, in compliance with data protection laws)
- What encryption and access controls are in place?
- Is the provider compliant with relevant data protection regulations?
- Who owns the data if you switch providers?

### Pricing
HR software pricing varies enormously. Common models include:
- **Per employee per month**: The most common model for small businesses. Costs scale with headcount.
- **Flat monthly fee**: A fixed price regardless of employee count. Better for very small teams.
- **Tiered pricing**: Different feature sets at different price points.

Watch for hidden costs: setup fees, training charges, additional modules, and data migration fees. The headline price is rarely the total price.

## Avoiding common mistakes

### Buying features you do not need
A platform with 200 features is only useful if you use more than a handful. Start with the features that address your current problems and add complexity over time.

### Not involving end users
If managers will use the system to approve holidays and the HR person will manage records, involve them in the evaluation. A system that impresses the decision-maker but frustrates the daily users will not be adopted.

### Ignoring data migration
If you have existing employee records in spreadsheets, you need to get them into the new system. Evaluate how the software handles data import and whether the provider offers migration support.

### Choosing based on price alone
The cheapest option is rarely the best value. A slightly more expensive system that saves two hours per week of administrative time pays for itself quickly.

### Not testing before committing
Most HR software providers offer free trials or demo environments. Use them. Test with real scenarios: booking a holiday, adding a new starter, generating an absence report. The trial period is where you discover whether the software actually works for your business.

The most efficient approach for small businesses is a platform that combines HR, payroll, and workforce management in a single system. When employee records, holiday tracking, absence management, timesheets, and payroll all live in one place, data flows naturally and administrative overhead is minimised.

## Making your decision

1. **List your pain points**: What problems are you trying to solve?
2. **Define your must-haves**: Which features address those problems directly?
3. **Set a budget**: What can you realistically afford per month?
4. **Shortlist three to four options**: Based on features, price, and reviews
5. **Test each one**: Use free trials to evaluate usability and fit
6. **Check integrations**: Confirm the software connects to your existing tools
7. **Talk to references**: Ask the provider for customer references in similar businesses

The right HR software saves time, reduces errors, and gives you confidence that your people processes are handled properly. Choose carefully, start with what you need today, and grow from there.
