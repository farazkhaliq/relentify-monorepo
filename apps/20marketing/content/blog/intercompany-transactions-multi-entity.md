When a business operates through more than one legal entity, transactions between those entities are inevitable. One company might provide management services to another. A parent company might lend funds to a subsidiary. A shared services entity might pay bills on behalf of the group.

These intercompany transactions need careful accounting treatment. If they are not properly recorded and eliminated during consolidation, your group financial statements will overstate revenue, expenses, assets, and liabilities. The result is a misleading picture of how the business is actually performing.

This guide explains what intercompany transactions are, why they matter, and how to manage them effectively.

## What are intercompany transactions?

Intercompany transactions occur whenever two or more entities within the same corporate group exchange goods, services, or funds. Common examples include:

- **Management fees** — A parent company charges subsidiaries for administrative services
- **Intercompany loans** — One entity lends money to another within the group
- **Transfer of goods** — A manufacturing entity sells products to a distribution entity
- **Shared costs** — One entity pays rent or insurance that covers multiple group companies
- **Recharges** — One entity incurs an expense on behalf of another and invoices it back

Each of these creates a receivable in one entity and a payable in another. At the individual entity level, these are real transactions that need to be recorded accurately. But at the group level, they are internal movements that should not inflate the consolidated numbers.

## Why intercompany accounting matters

### Accurate consolidated reporting

The primary reason to manage intercompany transactions carefully is to produce accurate consolidated financial statements. When you combine the results of multiple entities, you need to eliminate intercompany balances and transactions so the group accounts reflect only external activity.

Without proper elimination, a 100,000 intercompany sale would appear as both revenue in one entity and a cost in another. The group would show 100,000 of revenue and 100,000 of costs that do not represent real economic activity with the outside world.

### Tax compliance

Tax authorities in most jurisdictions pay close attention to intercompany transactions, particularly transfer pricing. If one entity charges another an artificially high or low price, it can shift profits between jurisdictions and reduce the overall tax burden. This is closely scrutinised, and businesses need to ensure intercompany pricing reflects arm's length terms.

### Regulatory requirements

Depending on your jurisdiction and business size, you may be legally required to prepare consolidated accounts. Proper intercompany accounting is a prerequisite for meeting these requirements.

### Internal decision-making

If intercompany transactions distort individual entity results, management decisions based on those results will be flawed. You might think one entity is highly profitable when much of its revenue comes from internal sources, or that another is underperforming when it is bearing costs that benefit the wider group.

## Types of intercompany transactions

### Revenue and expense transactions

These are the most common type. One entity provides a service or product to another and charges for it. On consolidation, the revenue in the selling entity and the corresponding expense in the buying entity are both eliminated.

### Intercompany loans and interest

When one entity lends money to another, the loan creates an asset in the lending entity and a liability in the borrowing entity. Any interest charged creates income for the lender and an expense for the borrower. All of these must be eliminated on consolidation.

### Dividends

When a subsidiary pays a dividend to its parent, the parent records dividend income. On consolidation, this is eliminated because it represents an internal transfer of profits, not new income for the group.

### Asset transfers

If one entity sells a fixed asset to another within the group, any profit or loss on that sale is unrealised from a group perspective. The asset needs to appear in the consolidated accounts at its original carrying value, not the transfer price.

### Cost allocations and recharges

Shared costs like IT infrastructure, office space, or insurance are often paid by one entity and recharged to others. These recharges create intercompany balances that need elimination.

## The intercompany reconciliation process

### Step 1: Identify all intercompany transactions

The first step is ensuring every intercompany transaction is identified and recorded correctly in both entities. This requires a clear policy on how intercompany transactions are documented and coded.

Using a dedicated intercompany account code in your chart of accounts makes it much easier to identify these transactions during the reconciliation process.

### Step 2: Match and reconcile balances

At each reporting period, the intercompany receivable in one entity should match the intercompany payable in the other. In practice, timing differences, errors, and different accounting treatments often cause mismatches.

Common causes of discrepancies include:

- **Timing differences** — One entity records the transaction in March, the other in April
- **Currency differences** — Entities in different countries may record the same transaction at different exchange rates
- **Errors** — Wrong amounts, duplicate entries, or missing transactions
- **Unrecorded transactions** — One entity has recorded the transaction but the other has not

### Step 3: Resolve discrepancies

Each discrepancy needs to be investigated and resolved. This typically involves communication between the finance teams of the different entities. Having a regular reconciliation schedule — monthly is ideal — prevents discrepancies from accumulating and becoming difficult to untangle.

### Step 4: Eliminate on consolidation

Once balances are reconciled, the elimination entries are prepared. These remove intercompany revenue and expenses, receivables and payables, and any unrealised profit on internal transactions.

## Best practices for managing intercompany transactions

### Establish clear policies

Document your intercompany pricing methodology, approval processes, and recording procedures. Everyone involved should understand how intercompany transactions are to be handled.

### Use consistent account codes

Designate specific account codes for intercompany transactions across all entities. This makes identification and reconciliation far more efficient than hunting through general expense categories.

### Reconcile regularly

Monthly reconciliation is the gold standard. The longer you leave it, the harder it becomes to identify and resolve discrepancies. Quarterly or annual reconciliation often leads to last-minute panic and errors.

### Automate where possible

Modern [accounting software](/accounting) can automate much of the intercompany process. When one entity records an intercompany transaction, the corresponding entry can be automatically created in the other entity. This eliminates timing differences and reduces manual errors.

Platforms that support multi-entity accounting can also automate the elimination process during consolidation, saving significant time and reducing the risk of errors.

### Maintain arm's length pricing

Ensure intercompany pricing reflects what would be charged between unrelated parties. Document your pricing methodology and keep it consistent. This protects you from transfer pricing challenges by tax authorities.

### Keep an audit trail

Every intercompany transaction should be supported by documentation — invoices, agreements, or board resolutions. This is important for both internal control and external audit or tax examination.

## Common challenges and how to address them

### Volume of transactions

In larger groups, the sheer number of intercompany transactions can be overwhelming. The solution is automation and standardisation. Use accounting software that supports intercompany workflows, and reduce unnecessary intercompany activity where possible.

### Multiple currencies

When group entities operate in different currencies, exchange rate differences create reconciliation challenges. Agree on a consistent rate source and timing for converting intercompany transactions, and ensure both parties use the same rate.

### Different accounting policies

If group entities use different accounting treatments — for example, different depreciation methods or revenue recognition policies — intercompany transactions may be recorded differently in each entity. Standardising accounting policies across the group eliminates this problem.

### Staff turnover and knowledge gaps

Intercompany accounting is often understood by only a few people in each entity. Documenting processes thoroughly and cross-training staff ensures continuity when people move on.

## Getting it right from the start

If you are setting up a multi-entity structure, invest time in your intercompany accounting framework from day one. Establishing clear policies, consistent account codes, and regular reconciliation processes will save enormous amounts of time and frustration as the group grows.

Retrofitting intercompany controls onto an established group with years of inconsistent practices is far more difficult than building them in from the beginning.

Intercompany accounting is not glamorous, but getting it right is essential for any business operating through multiple entities. With the right processes and tools, it does not have to be painful.
