Real Time Information — commonly known as RTI — is the system through which UK employers report payroll data to HMRC. Before RTI was introduced, employers reported payroll information annually at the end of the tax year. RTI changed this to real-time reporting: you submit data to HMRC on or before every payday.

If you run payroll in the UK, understanding RTI is essential. This guide explains what it involves, what you need to submit, and how to stay compliant.

## What is RTI?

RTI is the method by which employers report PAYE (Pay As You Earn) information to HMRC in real time. Every time you pay an employee, you submit a report to HMRC containing details of the payment, tax deducted, National Insurance contributions, and other relevant information.

The system was introduced to give HMRC up-to-date information about employment income, tax, and NI. This helps HMRC:

- Collect the right amount of tax in real time
- Reduce tax code errors
- Detect fraud more quickly
- Administer tax credits and benefits more accurately

For employers, RTI replaced the old year-end return system. Instead of one large submission at the end of the tax year, you make smaller submissions throughout the year.

## What you need to submit

There are several types of RTI submission, but the two you will use most frequently are:

### Full Payment Submission (FPS)

An FPS must be submitted on or before every payday. It contains details of each employee's pay for the period, including:

- Employee's name, address, date of birth, and National Insurance number
- Tax code and NI category
- Gross pay for the period
- Income tax deducted
- Employee NI contributions
- Employer NI contributions
- Student loan deductions (if applicable)
- Pension contributions
- Statutory payments (SSP, SMP, SPP, ShPP)
- Whether the employee is a new starter or leaver
- Starter/leaver details if applicable

The FPS is the main submission and must be sent for every pay run. If you run weekly and monthly payrolls, you send an FPS for each one.

### Employer Payment Summary (EPS)

An EPS is submitted when you need to report information that is not included in the FPS, such as:

- Employment Allowance claim
- Recovery of statutory payments (SMP, SPP, etc.)
- CIS deductions (Construction Industry Scheme)
- Apprenticeship Levy
- Adjustments to previous submissions

The EPS is typically submitted monthly, by the 19th of the following tax month. You do not need to submit an EPS every month — only when there is something to report.

### Earlier Year Update (EYU)

If you need to correct information from a previous tax year, you submit an EYU. This is used when errors are discovered after the tax year has closed.

## When to submit

### FPS timing

The FPS must be submitted **on or before** the date you pay your employees. If you pay monthly on the 28th, the FPS must be sent by the 28th. If you pay weekly on Fridays, the FPS must be sent by each Friday.

In practice, most employers submit the FPS as part of their pay run processing. Your [payroll software](/payroll) generates the FPS and submits it electronically to HMRC when you finalise each pay run.

### Late FPS

If you submit an FPS late, HMRC may issue a penalty. Penalties are calculated per month and per employee, so a late FPS for a large workforce can result in a significant charge.

There are some exceptions — for example, if the FPS is your first submission of the tax year or if you are a new employer. But as a general rule, always submit on or before payday.

### No payment in a period

If you have no employees to pay in a particular tax month (for example, a seasonal business with no staff in winter), you can submit an EPS to tell HMRC that no payment was made. This prevents HMRC from expecting an FPS and potentially issuing penalties for a "missing" submission.

## How RTI submission works

The technical process is straightforward:

1. **Process your payroll**: Calculate pay, deductions, and employer contributions.
2. **Generate the FPS**: Your payroll software creates the submission based on your payroll data.
3. **Submit electronically**: The FPS is sent to HMRC via the Government Gateway. Most payroll software handles this automatically.
4. **Receive acknowledgement**: HMRC confirms receipt. If there are errors, you will receive an error message to correct.

The entire process happens within your payroll software. You do not need to log into an HMRC website or fill in manual forms — the software handles the formatting and transmission.

## Common RTI errors

### Employee data mismatches

HMRC validates the employee information in your FPS against their records. If there is a mismatch — for example, a different date of birth or National Insurance number — the submission may be rejected or flagged.

To avoid mismatches:
- Verify employee details when they join (using their P45, passport, or other official documents)
- Check NICs carefully (transposition errors are common)
- Keep records up to date when employees notify you of name changes

### Incorrect tax codes

Applying the wrong tax code results in incorrect tax calculations, which are reported through RTI. HMRC will eventually correct the code, but in the meantime, the employee may be over- or under-deducting.

When you receive a new tax code notification from HMRC, apply it promptly. Most payroll software receives these notifications electronically and applies them automatically.

### Missing submissions

Failing to submit an FPS for a pay period creates a gap in HMRC's records. They may assume you have not paid your employees and issue a penalty or investigation. If you process payroll but forget to submit (or the submission fails), correct it as quickly as possible.

### Year-end errors

At the end of the tax year (5 April), you need to send a final FPS with the "final submission for the year" indicator. You must also submit a final EPS if applicable. Missing these year-end steps can trigger penalties and delay the issue of P60s.

## RTI and new starters

When you hire a new employee, their first appearance on an FPS includes starter information:

- Starter declaration (whether they have a P45 from their previous employer)
- Student loan plan type (if applicable)
- Date of starting

If the employee provides a P45, you enter the information from it into your payroll system, and the FPS carries this data to HMRC. If they do not have a P45, they complete a starter declaration and you apply the appropriate tax code.

## RTI and leavers

When an employee leaves, their final FPS includes:

- Leaving date
- Reason for leaving (if applicable)
- Final pay and deduction figures

You must also issue the employee with a P45, which is generated from your payroll software based on the year-to-date data reported through RTI.

## Penalties for non-compliance

HMRC issues penalties for:

- **Late FPS submissions**: A fixed amount per month, per affected employee
- **Late payment of PAYE/NI**: Interest on late payments, plus penalties for persistent late payment
- **Inaccurate returns**: If errors in your RTI submissions result in too little tax being collected

Penalties can be significant, especially for businesses with larger workforces. The most effective way to avoid them is to submit on time, every time — which is why automated submission through payroll software is so important.

## Key takeaways

- RTI requires you to report payroll data to HMRC on or before every payday
- The FPS is your main submission, containing details of each employee's pay and deductions
- The EPS reports additional information like Employment Allowance claims and statutory payment recoveries
- Late or missing submissions attract penalties
- Payroll software handles RTI submission automatically — use it

RTI is not complicated once you understand the rhythm: process payroll, submit FPS, pay your employees. With the right software, the entire process happens as a natural part of your pay run, and you stay compliant without thinking about it.
