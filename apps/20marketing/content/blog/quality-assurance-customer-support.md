Metrics like first response time and resolution time tell you how fast your team works. They do not tell you how well. A ticket resolved in ten minutes means nothing if the answer was wrong, the tone was dismissive, or the customer left more confused than when they started.

Quality assurance (QA) in customer support is the practice of systematically reviewing interactions to ensure they meet your standards for accuracy, empathy, professionalism, and effectiveness. It is the mechanism that turns good intentions into consistent execution.

## Why QA matters

### Consistency across the team

Without QA, each agent develops their own style. Some are thorough, some are brief. Some are warm, some are clinical. Some follow policy precisely, some improvise. QA establishes a shared standard and identifies where individual agents diverge from it.

### Identifying training needs

QA reviews surface specific skill gaps that aggregate metrics cannot. An agent with good resolution times but poor QA scores on empathy needs coaching in a specific area. Without QA, that gap remains invisible until it shows up in customer complaints.

### Protecting the customer experience

Every interaction your team has with a customer shapes their perception of your business. QA ensures that the experience is reliably good, not dependent on which agent happens to handle the ticket.

### Continuous improvement

QA data reveals systemic patterns. If multiple agents struggle with the same type of question, the knowledge base needs updating. If tone scores are dropping across the team, the communication guidelines need revisiting. QA turns individual reviews into organisational insights.

## Building a QA scorecard

The scorecard is the foundation of your QA programme. It defines what "good" looks like and provides a consistent framework for evaluation.

### Choose your criteria

Select five to eight criteria that cover the most important aspects of a support interaction. Common criteria include:

**Accuracy** — Was the information provided correct? Did the agent follow the right process?

**Completeness** — Did the agent fully address the customer's question, or did they leave loose ends?

**Empathy** — Did the agent acknowledge the customer's situation and demonstrate understanding?

**Tone** — Was the communication style appropriate for the channel and the situation?

**Efficiency** — Did the agent resolve the issue without unnecessary back-and-forth?

**Policy compliance** — Did the agent follow company policies and procedures?

**Grammar and formatting** — Was the response well-written and easy to read?

**Resolution** — Was the issue actually resolved, or just closed?

### Define your scoring scale

Keep it simple. A three-point scale works well for most teams:

- **Meets expectations (2)** — The agent handled this criterion well
- **Needs improvement (1)** — There is a specific area where the agent could do better
- **Does not meet expectations (0)** — The agent missed this criterion significantly

Some teams use a five-point scale for more granularity, but three points is sufficient for most purposes and reduces ambiguity for reviewers.

### Weight the criteria

Not all criteria are equally important. Accuracy might be weighted at 30 percent because giving wrong information is more damaging than a minor tone issue. Empathy might be weighted at 20 percent. Grammar might be weighted at 5 percent.

The weights should reflect your team's priorities and the criteria that most directly affect customer satisfaction.

### Document with examples

For each criterion and score level, provide specific examples. What does "meets expectations" look like for empathy? What does "needs improvement" look like for accuracy? Concrete examples ensure that different reviewers apply the scorecard consistently.

## Who should do QA reviews

### Dedicated QA specialists

In larger teams, dedicated QA roles provide the most consistent and thorough reviews. QA specialists develop expertise in evaluation and can maintain calibration across the team.

### Team leads and managers

In smaller teams, managers or team leads conduct QA reviews as part of their regular responsibilities. This works well when the team is small enough for the manager to review a meaningful sample.

### Peer review

Agents review each other's work. Peer review has the advantage of being educational for the reviewer — evaluating someone else's work sharpens your own skills. It also reduces the perception of QA as a top-down surveillance activity.

### Self-review

Agents review their own interactions before formal QA. Self-review builds self-awareness and often surfaces issues that the agent can correct independently.

**Recommended approach:** Use a combination. Peer review for learning, manager review for accountability, and self-review for development.

## Sampling strategy

Reviewing every interaction is not practical for most teams. You need a sampling strategy that provides sufficient coverage without overwhelming your reviewers.

### Random sampling

Review a random selection of tickets per agent per week. This provides an unbiased view of typical performance. A common target is five to ten reviews per agent per month.

### Targeted sampling

Supplement random sampling with targeted reviews of specific interaction types:

- Tickets with low CSAT scores
- Tickets that were escalated
- Tickets from high-value customers
- Complex multi-touch tickets
- Tickets handled by new agents (higher sampling rate during onboarding)

### Volume considerations

The right number of reviews depends on your team size and resources. As a guideline:

- **Small teams (under 10 agents):** 5 reviews per agent per month
- **Medium teams (10 to 30 agents):** 4 reviews per agent per month
- **Large teams (30+ agents):** 3 reviews per agent per month, supplemented by AI-assisted review

## The review process

### Conduct reviews consistently

Set a regular cadence for reviews — weekly or biweekly. Consistency matters more than volume. Five reviews conducted reliably every week are more valuable than twenty reviews done sporadically.

### Provide timely feedback

Share QA results with agents within a week of the interaction being reviewed. Feedback on a three-month-old ticket is much less impactful than feedback on a conversation from last Tuesday.

### Make feedback specific and actionable

Generic feedback like "be more empathetic" is not useful. Specific feedback like "when the customer mentioned they had been waiting for a week, acknowledging that wait before jumping to the solution would have made the response feel more caring" gives the agent something concrete to work on.

### Separate coaching from scoring

QA scores should inform coaching conversations, but the coaching itself should be supportive and forward-looking. Focus on what the agent can do differently next time, not on penalising past performance.

### Calibrate regularly

If multiple people conduct reviews, hold regular calibration sessions where everyone reviews the same interaction and compares scores. Discuss disagreements and align on how to apply the scorecard. Without calibration, different reviewers will interpret criteria differently, and the data becomes unreliable.

## Using QA data

### Individual development

Each agent's QA scores reveal their strengths and development areas. Use this data to create personalised coaching plans. An agent who scores well on accuracy but struggles with tone needs different coaching than one who is warm and engaging but occasionally provides incorrect information.

### Team-level insights

Aggregate QA data across the team to identify patterns. If the entire team scores low on completeness, the issue is likely systemic — perhaps the knowledge base is incomplete or the team needs training on a specific product area.

### Correlation with other metrics

Compare QA scores against CSAT, first contact resolution, and escalation rates. Agents with high QA scores should generally have high CSAT scores. If they do not, either your QA criteria do not align with what customers value, or external factors (product issues, long wait times) are overshadowing agent quality.

### Trend analysis

Track QA scores over time. Are scores improving, declining, or flat? Improvements validate your coaching efforts. Declines signal emerging issues. Flat scores might mean your coaching is not landing or your targets need to be raised.

## Common QA mistakes

**Treating QA as punishment.** If agents fear QA reviews, they will be defensive rather than open to learning. Frame QA as a development tool, not a disciplinary mechanism.

**Reviewing only bad interactions.** If QA only examines complaints and escalations, it biases the data negatively. Random sampling provides a balanced view.

**Inconsistent scoring.** Without regular calibration, QA data is unreliable. Two reviewers scoring the same interaction differently undermines trust in the programme.

**Not acting on the data.** QA without follow-up action is a waste of everyone's time. Every review should lead to either positive reinforcement or a specific improvement action.

**Over-complicating the scorecard.** Fifteen criteria with ten scoring options each creates analysis paralysis. Keep it focused on what matters most.

## Getting started

1. Define five to eight QA criteria based on your team's priorities
2. Build a simple scorecard with a three-point scale
3. Train reviewers and calibrate on sample interactions
4. Begin with random sampling — five reviews per agent per month
5. Share feedback within one week of the reviewed interaction
6. Review aggregate data monthly to identify team-level trends
7. Iterate the scorecard based on what you learn

QA is not a project with an end date. It is an ongoing practice that improves your team continuously. Start simple, be consistent, and let the data guide your evolution.
