Workplace health and safety can feel like a bureaucratic burden for small businesses, but it does not need to be. At its core, health and safety is about preventing your employees from getting hurt at work. The legal requirements exist because workplace injuries and illnesses are preventable — and the consequences of ignoring them can be severe for both your people and your business.

This guide covers what small businesses actually need to do, without the jargon or unnecessary complexity.

## Your legal obligations

As an employer, you have a general duty to ensure the health, safety, and welfare of your employees so far as is reasonably practicable. This means you must:

- Provide a safe working environment
- Ensure equipment is safe and properly maintained
- Provide adequate training and information
- Assess and manage risks
- Provide appropriate welfare facilities (toilets, drinking water, rest areas)
- Consult with employees on health and safety matters

The key phrase is "so far as is reasonably practicable." You do not need to eliminate every conceivable risk — you need to take reasonable steps proportionate to the risk level and the size of your business.

## Risk assessments

A risk assessment is the foundation of workplace health and safety. It is a systematic process of identifying hazards in your workplace, evaluating the risks they pose, and deciding what controls to put in place.

### How to conduct a risk assessment

**Step 1: Identify the hazards**
Walk around your workplace and identify anything that could cause harm. Common hazards include:
- Slipping and tripping hazards (wet floors, cables, uneven surfaces)
- Working at height (ladders, raised platforms)
- Manual handling (lifting, carrying, pushing)
- Display screen equipment (desks, monitors, keyboards)
- Fire risks
- Electrical hazards
- Hazardous substances (cleaning chemicals, dust, fumes)
- Stress and mental health risks

**Step 2: Decide who might be harmed and how**
For each hazard, consider who is at risk: employees, visitors, contractors, members of the public. Think about how they could be harmed — a slip on a wet floor could cause a broken bone; prolonged screen use could cause eye strain.

**Step 3: Evaluate the risks and decide on controls**
For each risk, assess the likelihood and severity. Then decide what controls are needed:
- Can you eliminate the hazard entirely?
- If not, can you reduce the risk through engineering controls (guards, barriers)?
- If not, can you reduce exposure through administrative controls (procedures, training)?
- As a last resort, provide personal protective equipment

**Step 4: Record your findings**
Write down the hazards you identified, the risks they pose, and the controls you have put in place. If you have five or more employees, you are required to record your risk assessment in most jurisdictions. Even if you have fewer, keeping a written record is good practice.

**Step 5: Review and update**
Risk assessments are living documents. Review them whenever something changes — new equipment, new processes, a workplace incident, or at least annually.

### Keeping it proportionate

A small office-based business does not need the same level of risk assessment as a construction site. Your assessment should be proportionate to the actual risks in your workplace. A two-page document covering the main hazards in your office is perfectly adequate.

## Health and safety policy

If you employ five or more people, you typically need a written health and safety policy. Even below that threshold, having one is good practice.

Your policy should include:

### Statement of intent
A brief statement from the business owner or senior manager committing to health and safety. This sets the tone.

### Organisation
Who is responsible for what? In a small business, this might be simple — the owner is responsible for health and safety, with specific tasks delegated to named individuals.

### Arrangements
The practical arrangements for managing health and safety: how risk assessments are conducted, how incidents are reported, how training is provided, and how the policy is communicated.

Keep it concise and practical. A 50-page policy that nobody reads is less effective than a 5-page one that everyone understands.

## Training

Employees need to know how to work safely. Training does not need to be elaborate, but it does need to cover:

### Induction training
Every new employee should receive basic health and safety training on their first day:
- Fire exits and emergency procedures
- First aid arrangements
- How to report hazards and incidents
- Any specific risks related to their role

### Role-specific training
Depending on the role, additional training may be needed:
- Manual handling (for roles involving lifting)
- Display screen equipment assessment (for desk-based workers)
- Working at height (for roles involving ladders or raised areas)
- Use of specific equipment or machinery
- Handling hazardous substances

### Refresher training
Schedule periodic refresher training — annually for most topics. This keeps safety awareness current and covers any changes to procedures or risks.

### Record keeping
Record all training provided: who attended, what was covered, when it took place. This is your evidence of compliance. Store training records alongside employee records in your HR or [payroll](/payroll) system for easy access.

## Incident reporting

When an accident or near-miss occurs, you need a process for reporting and investigating it:

### Internal reporting
Every incident should be recorded, no matter how minor. A simple incident report form should capture:
- Date, time, and location
- Who was involved
- What happened
- Any injuries sustained
- What action was taken

### External reporting
Certain types of incidents must be reported to the relevant authority. In most jurisdictions, this includes:
- Deaths
- Specified serious injuries
- Dangerous occurrences (near-misses with the potential for serious harm)
- Occupational diseases
- Injuries that result in the employee being unable to work for a specified period

Know the reporting thresholds and timescales for your jurisdiction. Failure to report a reportable incident is an offence.

### Investigation
Investigate every incident to understand what went wrong and prevent recurrence. For minor incidents, a brief investigation by the line manager may suffice. For serious incidents, a more formal investigation is appropriate.

## Fire safety

Fire safety is a specific legal requirement:

- Conduct a fire risk assessment
- Ensure adequate fire detection and warning systems
- Provide appropriate firefighting equipment (extinguishers)
- Ensure escape routes are clear and exits are marked
- Train employees in fire evacuation procedures
- Conduct regular fire drills (at least annually)
- Appoint fire wardens or marshals

For a small office, fire safety does not need to be complex. But it does need to be taken seriously — fires in small premises can be deadly precisely because people assume "it will not happen here."

## First aid

You must provide adequate first aid provision for your workplace. At a minimum:

- A suitably stocked first aid kit
- A designated person responsible for first aid arrangements
- Information for employees about first aid arrangements

Depending on your workforce size and the nature of risks, you may need trained first aiders. A small, low-risk office may only need an appointed person and a first aid kit. A larger or higher-risk workplace will need trained first aiders.

## Mental health and wellbeing

Workplace health and safety increasingly includes mental health. Work-related stress is one of the most common causes of sickness absence, and employers have a duty to manage the risk.

Practical steps include:
- Assessing stress-related risks as part of your general risk assessment
- Managing workloads to prevent excessive pressure
- Providing support for employees experiencing difficulties
- Creating a culture where employees feel comfortable raising concerns
- Training managers to recognise signs of stress

## Enforcement and penalties

Health and safety enforcement varies by jurisdiction, but typical consequences of non-compliance include:

- **Improvement notices**: Requiring you to fix a specific issue within a deadline
- **Prohibition notices**: Immediately stopping a dangerous activity
- **Fines**: For breaches of health and safety law, often calculated based on the seriousness of the risk and the size of the business
- **Criminal prosecution**: For serious breaches, individuals (including directors) can face criminal charges and imprisonment

The most effective enforcement is prevention. A small business that takes reasonable steps to manage risks, trains its employees, and keeps records will rarely have problems with enforcement.

## Making it manageable

Health and safety for a small business is not about bureaucracy — it is about common sense applied systematically:

1. Do a risk assessment (and keep it updated)
2. Write a simple policy
3. Train your people
4. Report and investigate incidents
5. Keep records
