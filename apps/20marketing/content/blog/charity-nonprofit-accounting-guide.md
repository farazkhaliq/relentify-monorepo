# Charity and Nonprofit Accounting: A Complete Guide for Small Organisations

Running a charity or nonprofit brings unique financial challenges that standard business accounting simply does not address. Donors want to know their money is being used as intended. Regulators require specific reporting formats. And the organisation itself needs clear financial visibility to fulfil its mission effectively.

Whether you are a trustee, treasurer, or staff member responsible for finances, understanding charity accounting principles is essential. This guide covers the fundamentals that every small nonprofit needs to get right.

## Why Charity Accounting Is Different

Commercial businesses exist to generate profit for their owners. Charities exist to further a charitable purpose. This fundamental difference shapes every aspect of how money is recorded and reported.

The most significant difference is **fund accounting**. Instead of having a single pot of money, charities typically manage multiple funds — each with its own purpose and restrictions. A donation given specifically for building repairs cannot be spent on staff salaries, even if the organisation desperately needs to cover payroll. Fund accounting ensures this separation is maintained and visible.

Charities also face stricter transparency requirements. Donors, grant-makers, and the public have a legitimate interest in how charitable funds are used. Financial reports must demonstrate that money has been spent in accordance with the organisation's objectives and any donor restrictions.

Finally, the terminology differs. Charities do not generate revenue — they receive income. They do not make profits — they have surpluses (or deficits). They do not have owners — they have beneficiaries. These differences are not just semantic; they reflect a fundamentally different relationship with money.

## Understanding Fund Accounting

Fund accounting is the cornerstone of charity financial management. There are three main types of funds.

**Unrestricted funds** are the most flexible. The charity can use these for any purpose that supports its charitable objectives. General donations, proceeds from fundraising events, and trading income typically fall into this category.

**Restricted funds** come with conditions attached by the donor or grant-maker. The money must be used for a specific purpose — for example, funding a particular programme, buying specific equipment, or supporting beneficiaries in a defined area. The charity has a legal obligation to honour these restrictions.

**Endowment funds** are donated with the intention that the capital is retained permanently (permanent endowment) or for a set period (expendable endowment). Only the income generated by investing the endowment capital can be spent.

Every transaction must be allocated to the correct fund. When you receive a restricted donation, it goes into the restricted fund. When you spend money from that fund on the specified purpose, the expenditure is charged against the same fund. At any point, you should be able to report the balance and activity of each fund separately.

This is where many small charities struggle. Without proper systems, restricted and unrestricted funds can become muddled, leading to the risk of spending restricted money on the wrong things — a serious compliance issue.

## Income Recognition

Charities receive income from diverse sources: donations, grants, legacies, trading activities, investment income, and fees for services. Each type has specific recognition rules.

**Donations** are generally recognised when received, unless a future condition must be met before the charity is entitled to the money. A pledge to donate is not income until the money actually arrives (unless there is a legally enforceable commitment).

**Grants** can be trickier. Performance-related grants — where funding depends on delivering specific outcomes — should be recognised as the conditions are fulfilled, not when the cash is received. A grant to deliver training to 100 people should be recognised proportionally as trainees complete the programme.

**Legacies** are recognised when three conditions are met: the charity is aware of the legacy, it is probable the money will be received, and the amount can be reliably measured. This often means legacies are recognised when probate is granted and the estate's assets are sufficient to cover the bequest.

**Trading income** from charity shops, events, or services is recognised like commercial revenue — when the goods are delivered or services provided.

Getting income recognition right is important because it affects how your financial position appears to donors, regulators, and the public.

## Expenditure Categories

Charity expenditure is typically reported under three headings.

**Costs of raising funds** include everything spent on generating income: fundraising staff salaries, event costs, marketing materials, and donation processing fees. Donors and regulators scrutinise this figure closely. High fundraising costs relative to total income can raise questions about efficiency.

**Charitable activities** represent the core spending that directly furthers the charity's mission. For a food bank, this includes food purchases and distribution costs. For an education charity, it includes programme delivery and teaching materials.

**Support costs** are the overhead that keeps the organisation running but does not directly deliver charitable activities: office rent, utilities, IT systems, governance costs, and finance staff. Support costs are typically allocated across the other two categories on a reasonable basis.

Small charities sometimes try to minimise reported overhead by misclassifying support costs as charitable activities. This is misleading and, if detected by regulators or auditors, damages credibility.

## The Statement of Financial Activities (SOFA)

The SOFA is the charity equivalent of a profit and loss account. It shows all income and expenditure for the period, broken down by fund type (restricted and unrestricted).

A well-prepared SOFA tells the reader exactly where money came from, how it was spent, and what is left in each fund at year-end. It also shows any transfers between funds (which must be properly authorised and documented) and any gains or losses on investments.

For small charities below certain income thresholds, a simplified receipts and payments account may be acceptable instead of a full SOFA. Check the requirements in your jurisdiction, as the thresholds and rules vary.

## The Balance Sheet

A charity balance sheet shows what the organisation owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the resulting fund balances. The key difference from a commercial balance sheet is that the equity section is replaced by fund balances, broken down into unrestricted, restricted, and endowment funds.

This breakdown is critical. It shows at a glance how much of the charity's resources are committed to specific purposes and how much is available for general use. A charity might appear financially healthy with substantial total funds, but if most of those funds are restricted, it could still struggle to pay its day-to-day bills.

## Budgeting for Nonprofits

Budgeting in the charity sector requires a different mindset from commercial budgeting. Revenue is often uncertain and lumpy — large grants arriving periodically rather than steady monthly income. Expenditure commitments may be driven by project timelines rather than business cycles.

Build your budget around confirmed and probable income sources. Be realistic about the timing of grant applications and the likelihood of success. Include a contingency for income shortfalls.

On the expenditure side, budget by fund and by project. This ensures you are planning to spend restricted funds on their intended purpose and that unrestricted funds cover core operating costs.

Cash flow forecasting is particularly important for charities because grant payments often arrive in arrears or in instalments. You might have committed to a full year of programme delivery but only receive quarterly grant payments. Without careful cash management, you can run out of money mid-project.

## Internal Controls and Governance

Charities hold money in trust for the public benefit. This creates a heightened duty of care around financial controls.

At a minimum, every charity should have:

- **Segregation of duties**: No single person should control an entire financial process from start to finish. The person who authorises payments should not be the same person who processes them.
- **Authorisation limits**: Set clear thresholds for spending approval. Small purchases might need one signature; large commitments should require trustee approval.
- **Bank reconciliation**: Reconcile bank accounts monthly and have someone independent review the reconciliation.
- **Expense policies**: Clear rules about what can be claimed, by whom, and what documentation is required.
- **Regular financial reporting**: Trustees should receive financial reports at every meeting, showing actual versus budget by fund.

These controls protect the organisation, its beneficiaries, and its trustees. They also demonstrate to donors and regulators that the charity takes stewardship seriously.

## Using Technology Effectively

Many small charities still manage their finances on spreadsheets, but this approach becomes increasingly risky as the organisation grows. Spreadsheets do not enforce fund separation, they are prone to formula errors, and they make it difficult to produce the reports that trustees and regulators need.

Modern [accounting software](https://relentify.com/features/accounting) designed for or adaptable to charity needs can track funds separately, automate reporting, and provide real-time visibility into the financial position of each fund. Look for features like project or department tracking, which map well to fund accounting requirements.

Integration with bank feeds reduces manual data entry and the errors that come with it. Online access means trustees can review financial information between meetings, improving governance and decision-making.

## Reporting Obligations

Charities typically must file annual reports and accounts with a regulatory body. The specific requirements depend on your jurisdiction and the size of your organisation, but generally include:

- A trustees' annual report explaining the charity's activities, achievements, and financial position
- Financial statements (SOFA and balance sheet, or receipts and payments account)
- An independent examination or audit (above certain income thresholds)

Even if your charity is below the threshold for mandatory filing, preparing proper annual accounts is good practice. It builds donor confidence, supports grant applications, and protects trustees from personal liability.

## Building Donor Trust Through Transparency

Financial transparency is not just a regulatory requirement — it is a strategic asset. Charities that communicate their financial position clearly and honestly find it easier to attract donations, win grants, and recruit volunteers.

Publish your annual accounts prominently. Explain in plain language where the money came from and how it was spent. Show the impact of each pound or dollar. Address any challenges or setbacks honestly rather than hiding them.

When donors trust that their money is being well managed, they give more — and they give more consistently. That trust starts with getting the accounting right.

## Getting Started

If your charity's accounting needs improvement, start with these steps:

1. Review your fund structure and ensure every fund is properly classified
2. Implement or upgrade your accounting software to support fund tracking
3. Set up a chart of accounts that maps to your reporting requirements
4. Establish internal controls appropriate to your size
5. Create a budget for the coming year, broken down by fund
6. Schedule regular financial reviews with your trustees

Charity accounting may be more complex than standard business bookkeeping, but the principles are logical and the tools are accessible. Getting it right protects your organisation, respects your donors, and ultimately allows you to deliver more impact for the people and causes you serve.
