A mid-tenancy inspection is a property visit carried out during an active tenancy to check the condition of the property, identify maintenance issues, and ensure the tenant is meeting their obligations. It sits between the check-in and check-out, providing a snapshot of the property's condition while it is being lived in.

Some landlords and agents treat mid-tenancy inspections as a formality. Others skip them entirely. Both approaches are mistakes. A well-conducted mid-tenancy inspection can catch problems early, prevent small issues from becoming expensive ones, and provide valuable evidence if a dispute arises later.

## Why mid-tenancy inspections matter

### Catching maintenance issues early

Properties develop problems whether or not they are occupied. Leaks, damp, faulty appliances, and wear on fixtures all occur over time. A mid-tenancy inspection catches these issues while they are still manageable. A small damp patch identified and addressed during an inspection is far cheaper to fix than the extensive mould remediation it becomes if ignored for another year.

### Checking tenant compliance

Most tenancy agreements include obligations around property care, garden maintenance, and prohibited activities (such as smoking or keeping pets without permission). A mid-tenancy inspection provides an opportunity to verify that these obligations are being met — not as an adversarial exercise, but as part of good property management.

### Building evidence

The mid-tenancy report creates an additional data point between check-in and check-out. If damage is visible at the mid-tenancy inspection that was not present at check-in, it narrows the window for when the damage occurred. This can be useful in deposit disputes, especially for long tenancies where significant time passes between the start and end reports.

### Maintaining the relationship

Regular inspections demonstrate that the landlord or agent is actively managing the property. For tenants, this can be reassuring — it shows that maintenance requests will be taken seriously and that the property is being looked after. For landlords, it provides peace of mind that their investment is in good hands.

## Legal considerations

Before scheduling a mid-tenancy inspection, understand the legal requirements in your jurisdiction regarding access to the property:

- **Notice period**: In most places, landlords or agents must give the tenant reasonable written notice before an inspection — typically 24 to 48 hours, though this varies
- **Reasonable hours**: Inspections should be conducted at a reasonable time, usually during normal business hours unless the tenant agrees otherwise
- **Tenant consent**: While the tenancy agreement usually allows for inspections, the tenant's right to quiet enjoyment must be respected
- **Frequency**: Most jurisdictions do not specify a maximum frequency, but quarterly or six-monthly inspections are the accepted norm

Always refer to the tenancy agreement and local regulations for specific requirements.

## What to check during the inspection

### General property condition

Walk through every room and assess the general condition:

- Walls — any new marks, damage, or signs of damp
- Floors — condition of carpets, laminate, tiles
- Ceilings — stains (which may indicate leaks), cracks, or damage
- Windows — condition, condensation (which may indicate ventilation issues)
- Doors — working properly, no damage

### Safety equipment

Check that all safety equipment is present and operational:

- Smoke alarms — test each one
- Carbon monoxide detectors — test each one
- Fire extinguisher or blanket (if provided) — present and accessible
- Window restrictors (if applicable) — working

### Damp and ventilation

Damp is one of the most common and damaging issues in rental properties:

- Check for condensation on windows, particularly in bathrooms and kitchens
- Look for mould on walls, ceilings, window frames, and behind furniture
- Check extractor fans are working in kitchens and bathrooms
- Assess whether the tenant appears to be ventilating the property adequately

### Kitchen

- Appliances — working condition, cleanliness
- Under sink — check for leaks
- Worktops and units — condition
- Extractor fan — working

### Bathroom

- Check for leaks around the bath, shower, and toilet
- Assess sealant condition around bath and shower
- Check for mould or mildew
- Toilet — flushing correctly, no leaks at base
- Extractor fan — working

### Heating and hot water

- Check the boiler (if accessible) — pressure, error codes, any unusual sounds
- Test a radiator in at least one room
- Run the hot water tap briefly

### External areas

- Garden — maintained to a reasonable standard
- Gutters (visible from ground) — any obvious blockages or overflow
- Fences and gates — condition
- Bins — present, in correct location

### Tenant obligations

- Is the property being kept in a reasonable condition?
- Are there any signs of prohibited activities (smoking, unauthorised pets, subletting)?
- Is the tenant reporting maintenance issues?

## How to document the inspection

### Use the same format as your inventories

Consistency between your check-in inventory, mid-tenancy reports, and check-out report strengthens all three documents. Use the same room-by-room structure and the same descriptive vocabulary.

### Photograph everything notable

Take photos of:

- Any damage or deterioration since check-in
- Any maintenance issues that need attention
- Any areas of concern (damp, mould, cleanliness)
- The general condition of each room (for the record)

You do not need the same volume of photos as a check-in inventory, but anything notable should be captured.

### Note actions required

Your report should clearly distinguish between:

- **Landlord actions** — maintenance and repairs that are the landlord's responsibility (boiler service, fixing a leak, replacing a broken appliance)
- **Tenant actions** — things the tenant needs to address (garden maintenance, cleaning, ventilation)
- **Observations** — conditions noted for the record but not requiring immediate action

### Record the tenant's feedback

If the tenant is present during the inspection (which is common and often preferable), note any issues they raise. Maintenance requests, concerns about the property, or requests for improvements should all be recorded and followed up.

## Handling common issues found at inspection

### Damp and mould

If you find damp or mould, determine the likely cause:

- **Condensation** — often caused by inadequate ventilation. Advise the tenant on ventilation best practices (using extractor fans, opening windows, not drying clothes on radiators). Check that extractor fans are working.
- **Rising damp or penetrating damp** — a building issue that is the landlord's responsibility. Report it and arrange remediation.
- **Leak** — identify the source and arrange repair promptly.

### Garden neglect

If the tenancy agreement requires the tenant to maintain the garden and it is significantly overgrown, raise it with the tenant diplomatically. Provide a reasonable timeframe for them to address it, and follow up.

### Cleanliness concerns

A mid-tenancy inspection is not a cleaning inspection. Tenants have the right to live in the property, and some degree of everyday mess is expected. However, if the property is in a condition that risks damage (heavy grease build-up, mould from lack of cleaning, pest-attracting food waste), it is appropriate to raise it.

### Unauthorised alterations

If you notice alterations that were not agreed — shelving screwed into walls, rooms painted different colours, changes to fixtures — note them and discuss with the landlord. These may need to be addressed before or at the end of the tenancy.

### Tenant-reported issues

If the tenant raises maintenance requests during the inspection, record them and follow up promptly. A tenant who reports a problem and sees no response is less likely to report future issues, which can lead to greater damage over time.

## After the inspection

### Complete and distribute the report

Finalise the report within a day or two of the inspection. Send a copy to:

- The landlord
- The tenant
- Your own records

### Follow up on actions

Any maintenance issues identified should be addressed within a reasonable timeframe. Any tenant actions should be followed up to confirm they have been completed.

### Schedule the next inspection

Regular inspections work best when they are part of a routine. Quarterly or six-monthly inspections, scheduled in advance, set expectations and ensure nothing is left too long between visits.
