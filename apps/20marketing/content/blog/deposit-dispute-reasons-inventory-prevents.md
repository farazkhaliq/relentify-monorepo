Deposit disputes are stressful for everyone involved. They take time, create friction between landlords and tenants, and often result in outcomes that neither party is entirely happy with. But the encouraging reality is that most disputes fall into a small number of common categories — and a thorough inventory prevents the majority of them.

Understanding why disputes happen is the first step to avoiding them. This guide covers the most common reasons for deposit disputes and shows how good inventory practices address each one.

## The most common dispute categories

Deposit protection schemes publish data on the disputes they adjudicate. While the specific percentages vary by year and jurisdiction, the same categories consistently appear at the top of the list.

### 1. Cleaning

Cleaning is the single most common reason for deposit disputes. The tenant believes they left the property clean enough. The landlord believes it was not cleaned to the standard recorded at check-in. Without clear evidence of both the check-in standard and the check-out condition, the disagreement becomes subjective.

**How an inventory prevents it:**

A thorough check-in inventory records the cleaning standard in detail. If the property was professionally cleaned before the tenancy, the report states that. If the oven was clean inside and out, that is documented with a photograph. At check-out, the same areas are assessed against the same standard.

When the evidence shows a clean oven at check-in and a grease-covered oven at check-out, the dispute resolves itself. The photos tell the story.

The key is specificity. "Property clean throughout" is too vague. "Oven interior — clean, no grease or residue (see photo 34)" is evidence.

### 2. Damage to walls and decorations

Marks on walls, holes from picture hooks, scuffs and scrapes — these are among the most frequently disputed items. The question is always whether the damage goes beyond fair wear and tear.

**How an inventory prevents it:**

The check-in inventory should photograph every wall, noting any pre-existing marks, holes, or wear. This baseline prevents the landlord from attributing old damage to the current tenant. It also prevents the tenant from claiming that new damage was already there.

At check-out, the same walls are photographed from the same angles. New marks, holes, or damage are immediately visible in comparison. The adjudicator can see exactly what changed.

### 3. Damage to carpets and flooring

Stains, burns, tears, and excessive wear on carpets and flooring generate a significant proportion of disputes. Landlords often try to charge for full carpet replacement; tenants argue that wear was normal.

**How an inventory prevents it:**

The check-in report should note the type, colour, age (if known), and condition of all flooring. Existing stains, wear patterns, and any damage should be photographed in close-up and with a wide shot showing location.

At check-out, the same areas are inspected. If a new stain has appeared, the check-in photo shows it was not there before. If the landlord claims excessive wear, the check-in photo provides context for how much wear was already present.

Importantly, the age of the carpet matters for calculating fair deductions. A carpet that was eight years old at check-in has very little remaining life expectancy. Even significant wear during the tenancy may not justify a large deduction because the item was near the end of its useful life regardless.

### 4. Missing or damaged furniture and contents

For furnished properties, missing items and damaged furniture are common dispute categories. Tenants sometimes break or lose items and do not report it, or they inadvertently pack landlord-owned items when moving out.

**How an inventory prevents it:**

An itemised contents schedule with quantities, descriptions, and photographs makes it straightforward to verify what was there at check-in and what is there at check-out. If six plates were documented at check-in and only four are found at check-out, the inventory provides the evidence.

For damaged furniture, check-in photographs showing the item's condition serve the same function as wall and floor photos — they establish the baseline that the check-out is compared against.

### 5. Garden and external area neglect

When tenancy agreements require the tenant to maintain the garden, neglect can lead to deductions for garden restoration. Tenants may argue the garden was already in poor condition, or that the work required is excessive.

**How an inventory prevents it:**

Photographs of the garden at check-in — showing the lawn condition, borders, hedges, fences, and any features — establish what the tenant received. If the garden was well-maintained at check-in and overgrown at check-out, the photographic evidence supports the deduction.

If the garden was already in mediocre condition at check-in, that too is documented, preventing the landlord from expecting a higher standard at check-out than what was provided at check-in.

### 6. Appliance damage or malfunction

Broken or damaged appliances — ovens with non-functioning elements, washing machines that no longer drain, fridges with damaged shelves — are another common dispute category. The question is whether the appliance was working at check-in and whether the damage was caused by the tenant.

**How an inventory prevents it:**

The check-in inventory should confirm that each appliance was tested and working, noting any existing issues. At check-out, the same appliances are tested again. If the washing machine was confirmed working at check-in and is broken at check-out, the tenant's responsibility is clear (unless it is a mechanical failure from age, which is fair wear and tear).

### 7. Redecoration claims

Landlords sometimes claim the cost of redecorating the property, arguing that the tenant left the walls in a condition that requires repainting. Tenants argue that the need for repainting is normal after a multi-year tenancy.

**How an inventory prevents it:**

The check-in report should note the paint colour, condition, and age (if known). If walls were freshly painted at check-in, that is documented. At check-out, the condition is compared.

The depreciation framework is important here. Interior decoration typically has a lifespan of three to five years. If the walls were freshly painted at check-in and the tenancy lasted four years, the paint has reached the end of its expected life. The tenant should not be charged for repainting that would have been needed regardless.

## Why disputes happen despite inventories

Even properties with inventories sometimes end up in disputes. The common reasons are:

### Inventory quality is too low

A vague, incomplete, or poorly photographed inventory is only marginally better than no inventory at all. If the check-in report says "property in good condition" with no photos, the adjudicator has very little to work with.

### Check-in and check-out are not comparable

If the check-in was detailed and photographic but the check-out was rushed and sparse (or vice versa), direct comparison is difficult. Both reports need to be produced to the same standard.

### Fair wear and tear is not properly considered

Landlords who claim for normal deterioration undermine their credibility with adjudicators. If part of a claim is clearly fair wear and tear, the adjudicator may question the entire claim.

### The tenant was not given the inventory

If the tenant did not receive the check-in report and did not have the opportunity to review it, they can challenge its accuracy. Always provide a copy and allow a reasonable period for feedback.

### Costs are not evidenced

Even with a perfect inventory showing clear damage, the landlord needs to provide evidence of the cost of remediation — invoices, quotes, or receipts. A deduction without cost evidence is difficult to sustain.

## Building a dispute-proof inventory process

The pattern is clear: comprehensive documentation at check-in, comparable documentation at check-out, fair assessment of changes, and proper evidence of costs.
