Every solo business owner reaches a point where they cannot grow further alone. The work keeps coming, the hours keep stretching, and the quality of both the work and your life begins to suffer. Hiring your first employee is a significant step — it changes your costs, your responsibilities, and the nature of your business. Getting the timing right matters.

Hire too early and you burn through cash before the revenue is there to support it. Hire too late and you miss opportunities, exhaust yourself, and risk losing clients who need more than one person can deliver.

This guide helps you recognise the signs that you are ready, prepare for the financial and practical realities, and make a decision you will not regret.

## Signs your business is ready for its first hire

### You are consistently turning down work

If you regularly decline projects or tell prospective clients you cannot take them on, you are leaving money on the table. Occasional capacity constraints are normal, but if it is happening every week, the cost of not hiring is the revenue you are losing.

Calculate what those declined projects would have been worth over the past three to six months. If the total significantly exceeds the cost of employing someone, the financial case for hiring is clear.

### Your growth has plateaued despite demand

Your revenue has flatlined not because clients have stopped coming, but because you physically cannot do more work. You are at capacity, and no amount of efficiency improvement will create meaningful additional hours.

This is different from a demand problem. If clients are not coming, hiring will not help. But if the pipeline is full and you are the bottleneck, adding capacity is the logical next step.

### You are spending too much time on low-value tasks

If you spend half your week on administration, bookkeeping, email management, and other tasks that do not directly generate revenue, you have a delegation opportunity. Hiring someone to handle these tasks frees you to focus on the work that only you can do — and that work is typically worth significantly more per hour.

### Quality is slipping

When you are stretched too thin, deadlines slip, communication suffers, and the quality of your deliverables drops. This is dangerous because quality issues compound — one unhappy client leads to a lost referral, which leads to lost future revenue.

If you are noticing mistakes you would not normally make, missing follow-ups, or rushing work to meet deadlines, your workload has exceeded your capacity.

### Your health or wellbeing is suffering

Consistently working evenings and weekends, feeling anxious about your to-do list, or experiencing physical symptoms of stress are not badges of honour. They are signals that your business model has outgrown its current structure.

A business that depends on its owner working seventy hours a week is not sustainable. It is one illness or holiday away from a crisis.

## The financial reality check

Recognising the signs is one thing. Affording the hire is another. Before you commit, run the numbers carefully.

### Calculate the true cost of employment

The salary is only part of the cost. Depending on your jurisdiction, you will also need to budget for:

- Employer pension contributions
- National insurance or payroll taxes
- Employers' liability insurance
- Equipment and workspace
- Training and onboarding time
- Holiday pay and sick pay
- Recruitment costs

A common rule of thumb is that the total cost of an employee is 1.25 to 1.5 times their salary, though this varies by location and industry.

### Ensure you have a financial runway

Do not hire based on a single good month. You need confidence that the revenue to support a salary will continue. A good benchmark is having at least three to six months of the employee's total cost available in cash reserves, plus evidence that your revenue trend supports the ongoing expense.

Your accounting software should make it straightforward to review your revenue trends and cash flow forecasts. If you are using a platform like Relentify, you can pull reports showing monthly revenue patterns, outstanding invoices, and projected cash flow to support your decision.

### Consider the alternatives first

Before committing to a permanent employee, explore whether other arrangements might work:

**Freelancers and contractors** — Useful for project-based work or specialist skills. Higher hourly cost but no ongoing commitment, no employment overhead, and no management responsibility.

**Part-time employees** — If you need regular help but not full-time capacity, a part-time role reduces costs while providing the consistency that freelancers may not offer.

**Virtual assistants** — For administrative tasks, a virtual assistant can handle email, scheduling, data entry, and other support functions at a fraction of the cost of a full-time hire.

**Automation** — Some tasks that feel like they need a person can actually be handled by software. Automatic invoice reminders, bank reconciliation, email templates, and scheduling tools can free up significant time without adding headcount.

## What to consider before you hire

### Define the role clearly

Do not hire a generic helper. Define exactly what the person will do, what skills they need, and what success looks like in the role. This makes recruitment easier, onboarding faster, and performance evaluation clearer.

Start by listing every task you currently do. Divide them into tasks only you can do and tasks someone else could handle. The second list becomes the foundation of the job description.

### Get your systems in order first

Hiring someone into a business with no documented processes means they will either work inefficiently or constantly interrupt you for guidance. Before your new hire starts, document your key workflows:

- How do you handle client enquiries?
- What is your invoicing process?
- How are projects tracked and delivered?
- Where are important files stored?
- What tools and logins will they need?

This investment in documentation pays for itself many times over in faster onboarding and fewer questions.

### Understand your legal obligations

Employing someone creates legal responsibilities. The specifics vary by jurisdiction, but common requirements include:

- Written employment contract
- Minimum wage compliance
- Working time regulations
- Holiday entitlement
- Pension auto-enrolment
- Payroll registration and tax reporting
- Employers' liability insurance
- Right to work checks

If employment law is not your area of expertise, consider getting professional advice before making your first hire. The cost of a consultation is minimal compared to the cost of getting something wrong.

### Set up payroll before the start date

Running payroll for the first time involves registering with your tax authority, choosing payroll software or outsourcing to an accountant, and understanding your reporting obligations. Do not leave this until the last minute.

Many accounting platforms include payroll functionality, which simplifies the process by keeping everything in one system.

## How to make the hire

### Write a clear job description

A good job description includes the role title, key responsibilities, required skills and experience, working arrangements (full-time, part-time, remote, office), salary range, and benefits. Be honest about the nature of working in a small business — the right candidate will value the variety and autonomy.

### Look in the right places

For your first hire, personal networks and referrals often produce the best candidates. Someone recommended by a trusted contact is likely to be a better cultural fit than a stranger from a job board. That said, job boards, social media, and industry-specific communities are all worth exploring.

### Hire for attitude and adaptability

In a small business, the first employee needs to be comfortable with ambiguity, willing to take on varied tasks, and able to work independently. Specific technical skills can be taught; initiative and reliability are harder to develop.

### Start with a probationary period

A probationary period — typically three to six months — gives both you and the employee time to assess whether the arrangement is working. Set clear expectations at the start and have regular check-ins during the probation.

## After the hire: the first few weeks

### Invest in onboarding

The first two weeks set the tone for the entire employment relationship. Have a structured plan that covers your business, your clients, your tools, your processes, and your expectations. Do not throw someone into the deep end and hope they figure it out.

### Delegate gradually

Resist the temptation to hand over everything at once. Start with clearly defined tasks, provide feedback, and gradually increase responsibility as trust and competence develop.

### Adjust your own role

Hiring someone changes what you do every day. The tasks you used to handle are now someone else's responsibility. Use the freed-up time for the high-value work that grows your business — sales, strategy, client relationships, product development.

## The decision framework

If you answer yes to three or more of these questions, it is likely time to hire:

1. Are you consistently turning down work due to capacity?
2. Has your revenue plateaued despite strong demand?
3. Do low-value tasks consume more than thirty per cent of your week?
4. Is quality suffering because you are stretched too thin?
5. Do you have at least three months of employment costs in cash reserves?
6. Is your revenue stable or growing?

Hiring your first employee is a leap, but it is also one of the most impactful steps a small business can take. Done at the right time and with proper preparation, it transforms what your business can achieve.
