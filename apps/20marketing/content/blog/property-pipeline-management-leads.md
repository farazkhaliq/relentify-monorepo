Every letting agency generates leads — enquiries from prospective tenants, instructions from landlords, referrals from other professionals. The difference between agencies that convert these leads efficiently and those that let them quietly disappear often comes down to one thing: pipeline management.

A pipeline is simply a structured way of tracking where each lead is in your process — from first contact to completed outcome. Without one, leads exist in email inboxes, on sticky notes, and in people's memories. With one, every lead has a status, an owner, and a next action.

## Why leads get lost

In a busy agency, it is remarkably easy for leads to fall through the cracks. An applicant emails on Monday, the agent replies on Tuesday, the applicant responds on Wednesday, but by then the agent is handling five other enquiries and the email sits unread until Friday. By Friday, the applicant has found somewhere else.

This happens because most agencies do not have a system for tracking leads through their lifecycle. They have email, they have phone logs, and they have whatever each individual agent remembers. But there is no centralised view of all active leads, their current status, and what needs to happen next.

The cost of lost leads is difficult to measure precisely because you never know what you missed. But consider this: if your agency receives fifty enquiries per week and converts fifteen of them into tenancies, what would happen if you converted eighteen instead? Over a year, those three extra conversions per week — driven purely by better follow-up — could represent a significant increase in revenue.

## Defining your pipeline stages

The first step in building a property pipeline is defining the stages that a lead moves through. These will vary slightly between agencies, but a typical lettings pipeline might look like this.

**Enquiry received.** The applicant has made first contact — via your website, a portal, a phone call, or a walk-in. At this stage, you know very little about them. The goal is to qualify the lead and schedule a viewing.

**Viewing arranged.** The applicant has been matched to one or more properties and a viewing has been scheduled. The goal is to conduct the viewing and capture feedback.

**Viewing completed.** The viewing has happened. The applicant has either expressed interest or declined. If interested, the goal is to move towards an offer.

**Offer made.** The applicant has made an offer, which needs to be presented to the landlord. The goal is to secure acceptance and begin referencing.

**Referencing in progress.** The offer has been accepted and referencing has begun. The goal is to complete referencing satisfactorily and proceed to tenancy setup.

**Tenancy confirmed.** Referencing is complete, the tenancy agreement is signed, and the move-in date is set. The lead has been successfully converted.

Each stage has a clear entry point, a clear exit point, and a defined set of actions. This clarity is what separates pipeline management from chaos.

## Tracking leads in a CRM

A CRM provides the infrastructure to manage your pipeline. Each lead is a record that moves through the stages as actions are completed. At any point, you can see how many leads are at each stage, which ones are stalled, and which are progressing normally.

### The pipeline view

Most CRMs offer a visual pipeline view — a board with columns for each stage and cards for each lead. This gives you an immediate overview of your entire pipeline. If one stage is overloaded and the next is empty, you know there is a bottleneck. If a lead has been sitting at the same stage for a week without any activity, it needs attention.

### Lead details

Each lead record should capture the key information you need to move it forward. For a tenant applicant, this includes their contact details, their requirements (property type, location, budget, move-in date), their current status in the pipeline, any notes from viewings or conversations, and the next scheduled action.

For a landlord instruction, the details are different but the principle is the same: property address, expected rent, any conditions, the current stage in the process, and the next action.

### Activity logging

Every interaction with a lead should be logged against their record. This creates a complete history that anyone in the team can review. If a colleague is covering for you while you are on leave, they can pick up exactly where you left off without needing a briefing.

## Automating the follow-up

The biggest killer of lead conversion is slow or inconsistent follow-up. Studies across industries consistently show that the speed of initial response is one of the strongest predictors of conversion. In property, where applicants are often enquiring about multiple properties simultaneously, the first agent to respond often wins.

A CRM can automate several aspects of the follow-up process.

**Auto-acknowledgement.** When an enquiry comes in, an automated response confirms receipt and sets expectations about when they will hear back. This buys time without leaving the applicant wondering if anyone received their message.

**Reminders.** If a lead has not been contacted within a defined timeframe — say, two hours for new enquiries — the CRM can alert the assigned agent or escalate to a manager.

**Scheduled follow-ups.** After a viewing, the CRM can automatically schedule a follow-up call or email for the next day. After an offer is made, it can remind the agent to chase the landlord for a decision.

**Drip sequences.** For applicants who are not ready to move immediately but might be in a few weeks, automated email sequences can keep your agency top of mind without requiring manual effort.

## Measuring pipeline health

A pipeline is only useful if you measure it. The key metrics to track include the following.

**Conversion rate by stage.** What percentage of leads progress from one stage to the next? If you have a high enquiry-to-viewing conversion rate but a low viewing-to-offer rate, the problem is likely with the properties or how viewings are conducted, not with your marketing.

**Average time in stage.** How long does a lead typically spend at each stage? If referencing routinely takes three weeks when it should take one, that is a process issue worth investigating.

**Total pipeline value.** How many active leads do you have, and what is their estimated value? This gives you a forward-looking view of your revenue.

**Source analysis.** Which lead sources generate the highest conversion rates? If portal enquiries convert at five percent but referrals convert at thirty percent, that should influence how you allocate your marketing budget.

## Common pipeline pitfalls

### Overloaded pipelines

A pipeline full of leads that are not progressing is worse than an empty one. It creates a false sense of activity and makes it harder to focus on the leads that are genuinely viable. Regularly review your pipeline and archive leads that have gone cold.

### No ownership

Every lead should have a named owner — the person responsible for moving it forward. Unassigned leads are nobody's responsibility and therefore nobody's priority.

### Inconsistent data entry

A pipeline is only as good as the data in it. If agents are not logging interactions, updating statuses, or capturing key information, the pipeline becomes unreliable. Make data entry part of the workflow, not an afterthought.

### Ignoring the top of the funnel

It is tempting to focus all your energy on leads that are close to conversion. But the top of the funnel — new enquiries — is where your future revenue comes from. If you neglect new enquiries in favour of chasing existing leads, your pipeline will eventually dry up.

## Building your pipeline in practice

If your agency does not currently have a structured pipeline, start simple. Define four or five stages. Assign every active lead to a stage. Review the pipeline weekly as a team.

As you become more comfortable, add automation — follow-up reminders, auto-acknowledgements, status-triggered tasks. Layer in reporting so you can measure conversion rates and identify bottlenecks.

## The compounding effect

Pipeline management is one of those improvements that compounds over time. In the first week, it simply gives you a clearer view of your current leads. In the first month, it starts to improve follow-up speed and consistency. Over six months, it fundamentally changes your conversion rates.

The agencies that manage their pipelines well do not necessarily generate more leads than their competitors. They simply convert more of the leads they already have — and that difference, compounded over time, is enormous.
