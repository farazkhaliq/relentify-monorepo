Accountancy practices sell time. Whether billing by the hour, by the project, or on a fixed-fee basis, the underlying economics depend on how efficiently the team uses its hours. Without accurate time data, you cannot price engagements correctly, measure profitability, or manage workload — three capabilities that separate thriving practices from struggling ones.

Yet many accountancy firms track time poorly. Partners rely on self-reported estimates. Associates fill in timesheets retrospectively, guessing at allocations from memory. The data is inaccurate, incomplete, and of limited use for management decisions. This guide explains what accountancy practices specifically need from timesheet software and how to implement it effectively.

## Why accountancy practices need time tracking

### Client billing

For firms that bill by the hour, time data is the invoice. Every hour logged against a client becomes a line item. Inaccurate time recording means inaccurate billing — either under-billing (lost revenue) or over-billing (client dissatisfaction).

Even for firms using fixed-fee billing, time data is essential. Fixed fees should be based on realistic estimates of the time required, and those estimates should be informed by actual data from previous similar engagements.

### Engagement profitability

Not all clients are equally profitable. A client paying a high fee but consuming disproportionate partner time may be less profitable than a smaller client serviced efficiently by junior staff. Time data by engagement reveals the true cost of each client relationship.

### Resource management

Partners and managers need to know who is working on what, who has capacity, and where bottlenecks are forming. During busy periods — year-end, tax filing season — this visibility is critical for managing workload and meeting deadlines.

### Performance management

Time data contributes to performance evaluation. How productive is each team member? What is their utilisation rate? Are they spending time on the right work? These questions cannot be answered without reliable time records.

### Regulatory compliance

Some jurisdictions require accountancy practices to maintain records of work performed for audit purposes. Time records linked to specific engagements provide an audit trail that demonstrates the work done for each client.

## What to look for in practice-specific timesheet software

### Client and engagement structure

The system should allow time to be logged against a specific client and engagement (e.g., "Smith Ltd — Annual Accounts" or "Jones Corp — Tax Advisory"). This two-level structure — client and engagement — is fundamental to practice management reporting.

### Multiple billing rates

Different team members bill at different rates: partner, manager, senior, associate. The system should apply the correct rate automatically based on who logged the time. Some clients may have negotiated rates that differ from the standard schedule — the system should accommodate this.

### Task categorisation

Within an engagement, time should be allocable to tasks or phases (e.g., "Planning," "Fieldwork," "Review," "Finalisation"). This enables more detailed profitability analysis and helps estimate future engagements more accurately.

### Retrospective entry

Unlike shift-based workers who clock in and out, accountancy professionals typically enter time retrospectively — at the end of the day or week. The system should support this workflow with a simple daily or weekly entry form.

### Narrative support

Each time entry should include space for a brief narrative — a description of the work done. This supports billing (clients want to know what they are paying for), compliance (evidence of work performed), and knowledge management (understanding the scope of past engagements).

### Work-in-progress tracking

Unbilled time represents work-in-progress (WIP). The system should track WIP by client and engagement, allowing managers to see how much time has been invested but not yet billed. High WIP balances indicate billing delays or scope creep.

### Integration with accounting software

Ideally, time data should flow into the practice's own accounting software for invoicing and financial reporting. Since accountancy practices typically use professional-grade software, integration capabilities are important.

## Implementing timesheet software in a practice

### Start with the partners

If partners do not use the system, nobody will. Partners are often the most resistant to time tracking — they view their time as flexible and their contributions as difficult to categorise. But partner time is also the most expensive, and its allocation has the biggest impact on profitability. Lead from the top.

### Keep entries simple

The biggest barrier to timesheet compliance in accountancy is the perceived effort. If entering time requires navigating through multiple screens, selecting from long lists of codes, and writing detailed narratives for every entry, people will put it off — and then fill in estimated entries from memory, which defeats the purpose.

Design the entry process to be as fast as possible: default to the most recent client, use favourites, allow quick entry with minimal categorisation for initial capture, and add detail later if needed.

### Set expectations for timing

Encourage same-day entry. Time recorded at the end of the day is significantly more accurate than time reconstructed at the end of the week. Some firms enforce this with daily reminders; others set a deadline for the previous day's entries.

### Review the data

Time data is only valuable if it is used. Partners should review engagement profitability regularly — at least at the conclusion of each engagement and monthly for ongoing work. Utilisation reports should be reviewed weekly during busy periods.

### Use the data for pricing

Over time, your time records build a database of actual effort by engagement type. Use this data to price new engagements more accurately. If annual accounts for a business of a certain size consistently take 40 hours, your fixed fee should reflect that — not an optimistic estimate of 30.

## Common challenges

### Low compliance

Some team members resist time tracking or do it inconsistently. Address this through a combination of culture (partners leading by example), simplicity (easy entry interface), and accountability (linking time recording to performance reviews).

### Write-offs masking reality

When an engagement exceeds the budget, the excess time is often written off — removed from the invoice but still representing real effort. If write-offs are not tracked, they distort the picture of engagement profitability. Ensure your system records both billed and written-off time.

### Over-categorisation

Requiring too many categories, codes, and sub-codes makes time entry burdensome and reduces compliance. Start with the minimum useful categorisation (client, engagement, maybe task) and add complexity only when you have a specific reporting need.

### Inaccurate narratives

Vague narratives ("work on accounts," "emails") provide little value for billing or management. Set expectations for the level of detail required — enough to justify the billing to the client, but not so much that it becomes a writing exercise.

Timesheet data is one input into a broader practice management framework. Combined with billing data, client records, and workflow management, it provides a comprehensive view of practice performance.

## Summary

For accountancy practices, time data is operational intelligence. It drives pricing decisions, reveals client profitability, supports resource allocation, and provides the evidence base for performance management. The practice that tracks time well has a structural advantage over the one that does not — better pricing, better resource management, and clearer insight into what is working and what is not.

The key is implementation: start with partners, keep it simple, record same-day, and use the data. A timesheet system that sits unused is worthless; one that is embedded in daily practice transforms how the firm is managed.
