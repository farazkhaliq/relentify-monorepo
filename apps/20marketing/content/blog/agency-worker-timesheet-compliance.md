Agency workers occupy a unique position in the workforce. They are employed (or engaged) by a staffing agency but work on-site for a client business, often under the day-to-day direction of the client's managers. This triangular relationship — worker, agency, client — creates complications for timesheet management that do not exist with direct employees.

Who is responsible for recording hours? Who approves the timesheet? Who pays for overtime? And whose system is the worker supposed to use?

This guide addresses these questions and provides a practical framework for managing agency worker timesheets in a way that keeps all three parties informed and compliant.

## The triangular relationship

Understanding the responsibilities of each party is the starting point:

### The agency

The agency is typically the employer (or the party with the contractual relationship with the worker). They are responsible for:

- Paying the worker, based on the hours reported and approved
- Invoicing the client for those hours at the agreed rate
- Maintaining employment records, including time records
- Ensuring compliance with working time regulations

### The client

The client directs the worker's day-to-day activities. They are responsible for:

- Managing the worker's schedule and attendance
- Approving or verifying hours worked on their site
- Ensuring the worker is treated in accordance with applicable regulations (such as equal treatment provisions)
- Communicating any concerns about hours or performance to the agency

### The worker

The worker is responsible for:

- Accurately recording their hours
- Submitting timesheets on time
- Reporting any issues with their schedule or working conditions

The complication arises because the party paying the worker (the agency) is not the party supervising them (the client). This means timesheet data must flow between two organisations, and both need confidence in its accuracy.

## Why accurate timesheets are critical for agency work

### Pay depends on reported hours

Unlike salaried employees, agency workers are paid for the hours they work. Every inaccuracy in the timesheet — whether a missed hour, an incorrect date, or a rounding error — directly affects the worker's pay. This makes accuracy a fairness issue as well as a financial one.

### Invoicing depends on approved hours

The agency invoices the client based on hours worked. If the timesheet overstates hours, the client is overcharged. If it understates them, the agency loses revenue. Disputed invoices strain the commercial relationship and consume admin time.

### Compliance depends on complete records

Employment regulations require records of hours worked, regardless of whether the worker is a direct employee or an agency worker. If the agency cannot produce accurate time records for a worker, they are exposed to regulatory risk — as is the client, in some jurisdictions.

### Equal treatment provisions

In many countries, agency workers are entitled to the same basic working conditions as comparable direct employees after a qualifying period. This includes working time protections. Accurate time records are essential evidence that these protections are being met.

## Common timesheet approaches for agency workers

### Paper timesheets signed by the client

The traditional approach: the worker fills in a paper timesheet, the client supervisor signs it, and the worker submits the signed sheet to the agency. The agency uses it to calculate pay and generate the client invoice.

**Problems:** Slow, error-prone, and dependent on physical documents that can be lost or delayed. The agency receives the data days after the hours were worked, making real-time visibility impossible.

### Client system access

Some clients give agency workers access to their own time tracking system. The worker clocks in and out alongside direct employees, and the client shares the data with the agency.

**Problems:** The agency is dependent on the client for its own payroll and invoicing data. If the client is slow to share data, or if the data format is incompatible with the agency's systems, bottlenecks and errors follow.

### Agency-provided system

The agency provides its own timesheet system (typically a mobile app) that agency workers use regardless of which client they are working for. The client approves hours through the same system or via a separate confirmation process.

**Advantages:** The agency controls the data pipeline, ensuring consistency across all clients and workers. Real-time data is available for payroll, invoicing, and compliance.

This is the approach that scales best, and it is increasingly common among staffing agencies of all sizes. ## Building a compliant process

### Step 1: Define the timesheet process in the client contract

Before placing workers, agree with the client on how timesheets will be managed:

- Which system will be used (agency's, client's, or a combination)
- Who is responsible for approving hours
- How quickly approvals must happen (e.g., within 48 hours of the end of the working week)
- How disputes will be handled

Documenting this in the contract or service level agreement prevents confusion later.

### Step 2: Configure sites and workers

Set up each client site in your timesheet system. For each site, define:

- The client contact responsible for approving hours
- The standard shift pattern and break rules
- Any site-specific requirements (GPS verification, specific clock-in procedures)

Assign workers to sites so that their timesheets are automatically routed to the correct approver.

### Step 3: Train workers on the process

Agency workers often move between assignments and clients, which means they may encounter different timesheet processes at each placement. Brief every worker on how to use the system before their first shift. Keep the instructions simple: how to clock in, how to record breaks, how to submit their timesheet.

### Step 4: Implement dual approval

For maximum accuracy, consider a two-step approval process:

1. The client supervisor confirms the hours worked on site
2. The agency administrator reviews and gives final approval

This dual check catches errors that either party alone might miss. The client supervisor knows who was on site; the agency knows the contractual rates and working time limits.

### Step 5: Maintain records centrally

Regardless of how data is collected, the agency should maintain a centralised record of all hours worked by every agency worker. This record should be:

- Searchable by worker, client, site, and date range
- Retained for the legally required period (which varies by jurisdiction but is often at least two years)
- Available for audit by the worker, the client, or regulatory bodies

## Handling common issues

### Disputed hours

When the client's record of hours differs from the worker's submission, investigate promptly. Common causes include:

- Clock-in times that differ from scheduled start times (worker arrived early or late)
- Breaks that were taken but not recorded (or vice versa)
- Confusion about whether travel time is included

GPS-verified timesheets significantly reduce these disputes by providing objective evidence of when and where the worker clocked in.

### Late approvals

If the client is slow to approve timesheets, the agency's payroll and invoicing are delayed. Set clear expectations in the contract and follow up promptly. Many timesheet systems support automatic reminders to client approvers.

### Workers on multiple sites

Some agency workers split their time across multiple client sites in a single day. The timesheet system must capture time at each site separately, so that billing and records are accurate for each client.

### Working time compliance

The agency, not the client, is typically responsible for ensuring that agency workers do not exceed working time limits. This means the agency needs a consolidated view of hours across all assignments — not just hours at a single client site. A worker might be within limits at each individual site but exceeding the weekly maximum when hours from multiple sites are combined.

## The business case for getting it right

For staffing agencies, timesheet management is not a back-office task — it is a core business process. Accurate timesheets drive accurate pay, accurate invoicing, and accurate compliance records. Inaccurate timesheets drive disputes, delayed payments, margin erosion, and regulatory risk.

Investing in a robust timesheet process — supported by the right technology — is one of the highest-return operational improvements an agency can make. It reduces admin time, improves worker satisfaction, strengthens client relationships, and protects the business from compliance failures.

The triangular relationship between agency, client, and worker will always create complexity. But with the right process and tools, that complexity is manageable — and the data it produces becomes a competitive advantage rather than an administrative burden.
