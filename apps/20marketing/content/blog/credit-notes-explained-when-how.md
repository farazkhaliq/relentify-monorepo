A credit note is one of those accounting documents that everyone encounters but few fully understand. It is a formal record that reduces the amount a customer owes you — essentially the opposite of an invoice. While invoices tell a customer "you owe us this much," credit notes tell them "we owe you this much back."

Understanding when and how to issue credit notes keeps your accounts accurate, your tax reporting correct, and your client relationships healthy.

## What is a credit note?

A credit note (sometimes called a credit memo) is a document issued by a seller to a buyer that reduces the amount owed on a previous invoice. It is not a refund in itself — it is an accounting record that adjusts the balance between you and your customer.

When you issue a credit note, the customer's outstanding balance decreases. They might use the credit against a future invoice, or you might issue a refund based on the credit note.

## When to issue a credit note

### Goods returned

If a customer returns goods, a credit note records the reversal of the original sale. The credit note should reference the original invoice and describe what was returned.

### Invoicing errors

If you issued an invoice with the wrong amount, wrong items, or any other error, a credit note corrects it. Rather than deleting or modifying the original invoice (which creates audit trail problems), you issue a credit note to cancel the incorrect amount and then issue a new, correct invoice if needed.

### Partial refunds

If you agree to a price reduction after an invoice has been issued — perhaps due to a quality issue, a service that was not fully delivered, or a negotiated discount — a credit note records the adjustment.

### Cancelled orders

If a customer cancels an order after an invoice has been raised, a credit note reverses the sale.

### Overcharges

If you accidentally charged a customer more than the agreed price, a credit note corrects the overcharge.

### Contract adjustments

If the scope of work changes after invoicing and the price decreases, a credit note records the reduction.

## Why not just delete the invoice?

This is a common question. If the invoice was wrong, why not just delete it and start again?

The answer is audit trail integrity. In proper accounting, every document should be traceable. Deleting invoices creates gaps in your numbering sequence and makes it impossible to reconstruct what happened. Tax authorities expect a complete, sequential record of all invoices issued.

The correct process is:
1. Issue the credit note referencing the original invoice
2. If a new invoice is needed, issue it with a new number
3. Both the original invoice and the credit note remain in your records

This gives you a clear trail: "Invoice #100 was issued for 1,000. Credit note #CN-005 was issued for 1,000 against invoice #100 due to [reason]. Invoice #105 was issued for the corrected amount of 800."

## What should a credit note include?

A credit note should contain:

- **A unique credit note number** — Sequential, just like invoice numbers
- **The date of issue**
- **Your business details** — Name, address, registration numbers
- **The customer's details** — Name, address
- **Reference to the original invoice** — Invoice number and date
- **Description of the credit** — What is being credited and why
- **Line items** — Matching the format of the original invoice (quantity, description, unit price, total)
- **Tax information** — The tax amount being credited (important for tax reporting)
- **Total credit amount**

If you are registered for tax purposes (such as VAT), the credit note must show the tax adjustment. This is essential because the credit note reduces your tax liability.

## How credit notes affect your accounts

### Revenue

A credit note reduces your revenue for the period. If you issued 50,000 in invoices and 2,000 in credit notes, your net revenue is 48,000.

### Tax

Credit notes reduce your tax output. If you charged 20% tax on the original invoice and then issued a credit note, you also credit the tax portion. This reduces the tax you owe to the tax authority.

### Accounts receivable

The credit note reduces the customer's outstanding balance. If they owed you 5,000 and you issue a credit note for 1,000, they now owe 4,000.

### Customer statements

Credit notes appear on customer statements as negative entries, reducing the amount owed.

## Issuing credit notes in practice

### Using accounting software

Modern accounting platforms make credit note management straightforward. In [Relentify](/accounting), for example, you can create a credit note directly from the original invoice, automatically linking the two and adjusting the customer's balance. The tax calculation is handled automatically.

### Manual process

If you are handling credit notes manually:

1. Create the credit note document with all required information
2. Record the credit note in your accounts (debit revenue, credit accounts receivable)
3. Adjust the tax calculation
4. Apply the credit to the customer's account
5. Communicate with the customer about the credit
6. Issue a refund if applicable

### Communicating with the customer

Always send the credit note to the customer with a brief explanation. They need to know:

- That a credit has been applied to their account
- Why (returned goods, pricing adjustment, error correction)
- How the credit will be used (applied to next invoice, refund issued)

## Credit note vs refund

A credit note and a refund are not the same thing:

- **Credit note** — An accounting adjustment that reduces the customer's balance. The customer can use the credit against future invoices.
- **Refund** — A payment back to the customer. Money leaves your account and goes to theirs.

A credit note may lead to a refund, but not always. If the customer has ongoing work with you, they might prefer to have the credit applied to their next invoice rather than receiving a refund.

The typical flow is:

1. Issue credit note (adjusts the accounts)
2. Decide with the customer: apply to future invoice or refund?
3. If refunding, process the payment separately

## Voiding vs crediting

Some accounting software offers a "void" option for invoices, which is different from issuing a credit note:

- **Voiding** nullifies the entire invoice as if it never existed. Use this only for invoices that should never have been created (accidental duplicates, test invoices).
- **Credit notes** partially or fully reverse a legitimate invoice. Use this for returns, adjustments, and corrections.

Voiding is the nuclear option. Credit notes are the surgical one. In most cases, a credit note is the correct approach.

## Common mistakes

### Not referencing the original invoice

A credit note without a reference to the original invoice is confusing for both the customer and anyone reviewing your accounts. Always include the original invoice number.

### Forgetting the tax adjustment

If the original invoice included tax, the credit note must also adjust the tax. Missing this means you overpay tax on sales that have been reversed.

### Issuing credit notes for the wrong amount

Double-check the amount before issuing. A credit note for the wrong amount creates a second error on top of the first, requiring yet another adjustment.

### Not applying the credit

Issuing a credit note is only half the job. Make sure the credit is actually applied to the customer's account — either against a future invoice or as a refund. Unapplied credits sit as liabilities on your balance sheet.

### Using credit notes to disguise revenue manipulation

Credit notes should always have a legitimate business reason. Issuing them to artificially reduce revenue or defer income to a different period is improper accounting.

Credit notes are a routine part of business accounting. They keep your records accurate, your tax reporting correct, and your customer relationships transparent. The key is to issue them promptly, reference the original transaction clearly, and apply them to the customer's account without delay.
