No employer wants to deal with disciplinary issues, but they arise in every business eventually. Whether it is persistent lateness, poor performance, misconduct, or a serious breach of policy, having a fair and consistent process protects both the business and the employee.

This guide explains how to handle disciplinary procedures properly.

## Why process matters

A disciplinary procedure is not about punishment — it is about addressing unacceptable behaviour or performance and giving the employee an opportunity to improve. The process protects you in two ways:

1. **Fairness**: A proper procedure demonstrates that you acted reasonably, considered the evidence, and gave the employee a chance to respond.
2. **Legal protection**: If a dismissal is later challenged, a tribunal or court will examine whether you followed a fair procedure. Failing to do so can make even a justified dismissal legally unfair.

## The stages of a disciplinary procedure

### Stage 1: Identify the issue

Before taking any formal action, be clear about what the problem is:

- Is it **performance** (the employee cannot do the job to the required standard)?
- Is it **conduct** (the employee can do the job but is behaving unacceptably)?
- Is it **gross misconduct** (a serious breach that may warrant immediate dismissal)?

Performance issues and conduct issues may require different approaches. Performance typically involves support and improvement plans. Conduct involves addressing specific behaviours.

### Stage 2: Investigate

Before any disciplinary hearing, conduct a reasonable investigation to establish the facts. The investigation should:

- **Gather evidence**: Witness statements, documents, CCTV footage, system logs, or any other relevant material.
- **Interview witnesses**: Speak to anyone who observed or was affected by the alleged incident.
- **Interview the employee** (if appropriate): Give them an opportunity to explain what happened.
- **Be proportionate**: The investigation should be thorough enough to establish the facts but not disproportionately heavy-handed for the seriousness of the issue.

The investigator should be someone who will not be involved in the disciplinary hearing itself. This separation ensures the hearing is conducted by someone who approaches the evidence with fresh eyes.

### Stage 3: Decide whether formal action is needed

Based on the investigation, decide whether the issue warrants formal disciplinary action:

- **Informal action**: For minor first offences, an informal conversation or verbal warning may be sufficient.
- **Formal action**: If the issue is serious or there is a pattern, move to a formal disciplinary hearing.
- **No action**: If the investigation does not support the allegation, close the matter.

### Stage 4: Invite the employee to a disciplinary hearing

If formal action is appropriate, write to the employee with:

- A clear description of the allegation(s)
- A summary of the evidence (provide copies of relevant documents)
- The date, time, and location of the hearing
- Their right to be accompanied (by a colleague or trade union representative in most jurisdictions)
- The potential outcomes, including any sanctions that may apply

Give the employee reasonable notice — at least a few working days — to prepare for the hearing.

### Stage 5: Conduct the hearing

The hearing is a structured meeting where the employer presents the evidence and the employee responds. It should be conducted fairly:

- **Present the case**: Explain the allegations and the evidence gathered during the investigation.
- **Hear the employee's response**: Give them a full opportunity to explain, challenge the evidence, and present any mitigating circumstances.
- **Ask questions**: Probe where necessary to understand the full picture.
- **Take notes**: Record what is said by all parties.
- **Adjourn if needed**: If new evidence emerges or the employee needs time, adjourn and reconvene.

The person chairing the hearing should approach it with an open mind. The purpose is to hear both sides and make a fair decision, not to rubber-stamp a predetermined outcome.

### Stage 6: Make a decision

After the hearing, consider all the evidence and the employee's response. Possible outcomes include:

- **No action**: The allegation is not substantiated or the explanation is satisfactory.
- **Verbal warning**: A formal note that the behaviour is unacceptable, with an expectation of improvement.
- **Written warning**: A more serious record that improvement is required within a specified timeframe.
- **Final written warning**: The last step before dismissal. The employee is warned that further issues may result in termination.
- **Dismissal**: Employment is ended. This is usually reserved for gross misconduct or failure to improve after previous warnings.
- **Other sanctions**: Demotion, transfer, or loss of privileges, depending on the circumstances and your policies.

Communicate the decision in writing within a reasonable timeframe. The letter should explain the decision, the reasons, any actions required, and the right to appeal.

### Stage 7: Right of appeal

The employee should have the right to appeal any disciplinary decision. The appeal should be heard by a different person — ideally someone more senior than the original decision-maker.

The appeal can cover:

- The fairness of the procedure
- Whether the evidence supported the decision
- Whether the sanction was proportionate
- New evidence that was not available at the original hearing

Communicate the appeal outcome in writing. The appeal decision is usually final.

## Handling gross misconduct

Gross misconduct is behaviour so serious that it justifies immediate dismissal without notice (summary dismissal). Examples typically include:

- Theft or fraud
- Physical violence
- Serious breach of health and safety
- Serious insubordination
- Deliberate damage to property
- Being under the influence of drugs or alcohol at work

Even for gross misconduct, you should still follow a procedure: investigate, hold a hearing, and give the employee a chance to respond. The difference is that the outcome may be immediate dismissal rather than a warning.

Suspending the employee on full pay during the investigation is common in gross misconduct cases. This is not a punishment — it is a precaution while the facts are established.

## Documentation

Document every stage of the disciplinary process:

- Investigation notes and evidence
- The invitation to the hearing (copy of the letter)
- Notes from the hearing
- The decision letter
- Any appeal correspondence and outcome

These records should be kept securely and retained for the legally required period. If the matter is ever revisited — by the employee, a tribunal, or a future employer seeking a reference — your documentation is your evidence.

Store disciplinary records alongside the employee's other records in your [payroll](/payroll) and HR system. Platforms like Relentify keep employment documents in one place, making them easy to find when needed.

## Common mistakes

### Skipping the investigation
Going straight from allegation to hearing without investigating the facts is a fundamental procedural failure. Always investigate first.

### Not giving the employee a chance to respond
The hearing exists to hear both sides. If you do not genuinely listen to the employee's response, the process is not fair.

### Inconsistent treatment
If you dismiss one employee for lateness but only warn another for the same behaviour, you are vulnerable to claims of unfair treatment. Apply your policies consistently.

### Disproportionate sanctions
The sanction should fit the offence. A first verbal warning for persistent lateness is proportionate. Immediate dismissal for a first minor offence is not (unless it constitutes gross misconduct).

### No right of appeal
Failing to offer the right to appeal is a procedural failing that can invalidate the entire process.

### Emotional decision-making
Disciplinary decisions should be based on evidence and policy, not frustration or personality clashes. If you are personally involved in the issue, have someone else handle the process.

## Prevention is better than cure

The best disciplinary process is one you rarely need to use. Invest in:

- Clear policies that set expectations upfront
- Regular feedback and performance conversations
- Good onboarding that sets the right tone from day one
- An open culture where problems are discussed before they escalate

When issues do arise, deal with them early. An informal conversation at the first sign of a problem is almost always more effective than a formal hearing after months of frustration.

## The bottom line

Disciplinary procedures are a necessary part of managing people. Handled well, they resolve problems, protect the business, and treat employees fairly. Handled poorly, they create legal risk, damage morale, and waste everyone's time. Follow the process, document everything, and always act fairly.
