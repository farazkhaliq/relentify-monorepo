Choosing a business structure is one of the most consequential decisions an entrepreneur makes in the United States. While legal liability protection is often the primary motivation, the tax implications are equally significant — and often more complex to navigate.

The right structure can save you thousands in taxes annually. The wrong one can result in unnecessary tax burden, administrative overhead, or compliance complications. This guide explains the tax treatment of the most common business structures to help you make an informed decision.

## Sole proprietorship: the starting point

Before discussing entities, it is worth understanding the baseline. A sole proprietorship is the simplest structure — you and the business are legally the same. All business income is reported on Schedule C of your personal tax return.

**Tax treatment:**
- Business income is taxed as personal income at your individual tax rate
- Self-employment tax of 15.3 percent applies to net business income
- No separation between business and personal assets for liability purposes

Most freelancers and small business owners start here. As income grows, the self-employment tax burden often motivates a move to a different structure.

## Limited Liability Company (LLC)

### What it is

An LLC is a state-level legal entity that provides liability protection — separating your personal assets from business debts and obligations. It is the most popular business structure for small businesses because of its flexibility and simplicity.

### Default tax treatment

The IRS does not have a specific tax classification for LLCs. Instead, it taxes them based on the number of members:

**Single-member LLC:** Taxed as a sole proprietorship by default. All income flows to your personal return on Schedule C. You pay income tax and self-employment tax on net business income.

**Multi-member LLC:** Taxed as a partnership by default. The LLC files Form 1065 (informational return), and each member receives a Schedule K-1 reporting their share of income. Members pay income tax and self-employment tax on their share.

### Electing S-Corp or C-Corp taxation

The key feature of an LLC is flexibility. You can elect to have your LLC taxed as an S-Corp or C-Corp without changing the legal structure. This is done by filing:

- **Form 2553** — to elect S-Corp taxation
- **Form 8832** — to elect C-Corp taxation

This means you can maintain the legal simplicity of an LLC while choosing the tax treatment that best suits your situation.

## S-Corporation (S-Corp)

### What it is

An S-Corp is not a separate legal entity type — it is a tax election. Both corporations and LLCs can elect S-Corp tax treatment. The "S" refers to Subchapter S of the Internal Revenue Code.

### How S-Corp taxation works

An S-Corp is a pass-through entity. Income passes through to the shareholders' personal tax returns and is taxed at individual rates. The S-Corp itself does not pay federal income tax (though some states impose a minimum franchise tax).

The key tax advantage is how income is divided:

**Reasonable salary:** As an S-Corp owner-employee, you must pay yourself a "reasonable salary" for the services you perform. This salary is subject to payroll taxes (Social Security and Medicare — 15.3 percent combined, split between employer and employee).

**Distributions:** Income above your reasonable salary can be taken as distributions (also called dividends). Distributions are subject to income tax but NOT self-employment or payroll tax.

### The S-Corp tax savings example

Consider a business with $150,000 net profit:

**As a sole proprietorship:**
- Self-employment tax on $150,000: approximately $21,200
- Plus income tax on the full amount

**As an S-Corp with $80,000 salary:**
- Payroll tax on $80,000 salary: approximately $12,200
- Distribution of $70,000: no payroll tax
- Income tax on the full $150,000

**Tax savings:** Approximately $9,000 in payroll taxes alone.

### S-Corp requirements and limitations

S-Corps come with restrictions:

- **Shareholder limit:** Maximum 100 shareholders
- **Shareholder type:** Only US citizens and residents; no foreign shareholders, no corporate shareholders, no partnerships as shareholders
- **One class of stock:** All shares must have equal rights
- **Calendar year:** Must use a calendar year-end (with limited exceptions)
- **Reasonable salary requirement:** The IRS scrutinizes owner compensation. A salary that is unreasonably low (to minimize payroll taxes) can trigger reclassification of distributions as wages, plus penalties
- **Payroll administration:** You must run payroll, withhold taxes, and file payroll returns
- **State considerations:** Some states do not recognise S-Corp elections or impose additional taxes

### When S-Corp makes sense

S-Corp election typically makes sense when:

- Net business income exceeds approximately $50,000 - $60,000 per year
- The payroll tax savings exceed the additional administrative costs
- You can justify a reasonable salary that leaves meaningful distributions

Below a certain income level, the administrative burden and cost of running payroll outweigh the tax savings.

## C-Corporation (C-Corp)

### What it is

A C-Corp is a corporation taxed under Subchapter C of the Internal Revenue Code. It is a separate taxpaying entity — the corporation itself pays tax on its profits.

### How C-Corp taxation works

C-Corps face what is commonly called "double taxation":

1. **Corporate tax:** The corporation pays a flat 21 percent federal corporate income tax on its profits
2. **Dividend tax:** When profits are distributed to shareholders as dividends, shareholders pay tax again on those dividends at their individual rate (qualified dividends are taxed at preferential capital gains rates of 0, 15, or 20 percent)

### When C-Corp makes sense

Despite double taxation, C-Corps can be advantageous in specific situations:

**Reinvesting profits:** If the business retains most of its earnings for growth rather than distributing them, the 21 percent flat corporate rate may be lower than the owner's personal rate (which can reach 37 percent for high earners).

**Investor requirements:** Venture capital and institutional investors typically require C-Corp structure.

**Stock options:** C-Corps can offer incentive stock options (ISOs) and qualified small business stock (QSBS) treatment, which provide significant tax benefits.

**Foreign shareholders:** Unlike S-Corps, C-Corps can have foreign shareholders.

**Unlimited shareholders:** No limit on the number or type of shareholders.

**Multiple classes of stock:** C-Corps can issue preferred stock, common stock, and other classes.

**Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS):** Under Section 1202, shareholders who hold C-Corp stock for more than five years may exclude up to $10 million (or 10 times their basis) of capital gains from federal tax. This is one of the most powerful tax benefits in the code for qualifying businesses.

## Comparing the structures

| Feature | Sole prop / LLC (default) | S-Corp | C-Corp |
|---------|--------------------------|--------|--------|
| Entity-level tax | None | None | 21% corporate tax |
| Pass-through | Yes | Yes | No (double taxation) |
| Self-employment tax | On all net income | Only on salary | N/A (payroll on salary only) |
| Payroll required | No | Yes (for owner-employees) | Yes |
| Shareholder limits | None | 100 US individuals | None |
| Stock classes | N/A | One | Unlimited |
| Administrative complexity | Low | Medium | High |
| Best for | Early-stage, low income | Profitable small businesses | High-growth, investor-funded |

## Changing your structure

You can change your tax treatment as your business evolves:

- **Sole prop to LLC:** Form a state LLC (legal change only; tax treatment stays the same unless you elect otherwise)
- **LLC to S-Corp taxation:** File Form 2553 (tax change only; legal structure stays LLC)
- **S-Corp to C-Corp:** Revoke S-Corp election (but be cautious — there is a five-year waiting period to re-elect)
- **C-Corp to S-Corp:** File Form 2553 (subject to eligibility requirements and potential built-in gains tax)

Work with a tax professional before making any structural changes. The timing and method of transition can have significant tax consequences.

## Making the decision

### Factors to consider

- **Current and projected income level** — Higher income favours S-Corp for payroll tax savings
- **Growth plans** — Seeking investors? C-Corp may be necessary
- **Administrative tolerance** — S-Corp and C-Corp require more compliance than sole proprietorship
- **State tax implications** — Some states treat these structures very differently
- **Exit strategy** — How you plan to eventually sell or close the business affects the optimal structure
- **Number and type of owners** — S-Corp restrictions may rule it out

### Get professional advice

Business structure selection is one of the most impactful decisions you will make, and it has long-term consequences. A tax professional can model the specific tax impact of each structure based on your actual numbers and plans.

The cost of professional advice at this stage is trivial compared to the potential tax savings — or the cost of choosing the wrong structure and having to restructure later.
