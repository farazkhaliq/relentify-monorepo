Estate agents handle an enormous amount of personal data. Landlord bank details, tenant employment records, applicant references, identification documents, financial histories — the data flowing through a typical agency is both sensitive and voluminous.

Data protection regulations exist to ensure that this information is handled responsibly. For estate agents, compliance is not optional. Breaches can result in significant fines, reputational damage, and loss of client trust. Yet many agencies treat data protection as an afterthought — something to worry about only if something goes wrong.

This guide explains the core principles of data protection as they apply to estate agencies and provides practical guidance on how to implement them.

## The core principles

Data protection regulations around the world share common principles. While the specific legal framework varies by jurisdiction, the underlying requirements are broadly consistent.

**Lawfulness, fairness, and transparency.** You must have a legitimate reason for collecting personal data, you must use it fairly, and you must be transparent with people about how their data is used.

**Purpose limitation.** Data should only be collected for specified, explicit purposes. If you collect a tenant's employment details for referencing, you should not use those details for marketing without separate consent.

**Data minimisation.** Only collect the data you actually need. If you do not need a tenant's date of birth for your processes, do not collect it.

**Accuracy.** The data you hold should be accurate and kept up to date. Inaccurate data should be corrected or deleted.

**Storage limitation.** Do not keep personal data longer than necessary. If a tenant moved out three years ago and there is no ongoing reason to hold their data, it should be deleted or anonymised.

**Integrity and confidentiality.** Personal data must be protected against unauthorised access, loss, or damage. This means appropriate security measures — both technical and organisational.

**Accountability.** You must be able to demonstrate compliance. It is not enough to follow the rules; you must be able to prove that you follow them.

## What data estate agents typically hold

Understanding what data you hold is the first step towards managing it properly. A typical estate agency holds personal data about several groups.

**Landlords.** Names, addresses, contact details, bank account details, tax reference numbers, identification documents, property ownership records.

**Tenants.** Names, addresses, contact details, employment details, income information, identification documents, references, credit check results, tenancy agreements, deposit information.

**Applicants.** Names, contact details, property requirements, employment status, financial information submitted during the application process.

**Guarantors.** Names, contact details, employment details, financial information.

**Contractors.** Names, business details, contact details, insurance certificates, trade qualifications.

Some of this data is particularly sensitive — financial information, identification documents, and credit check results all require careful handling.

## Lawful basis for processing

You need a lawful basis for every type of personal data you process. The most commonly relied upon bases for estate agents are as follows.

**Contract.** Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract. When you manage a property on behalf of a landlord, you need to process their personal data to fulfil the management agreement. When you reference a tenant, you need their personal data to complete the tenancy application.

**Legal obligation.** Processing is necessary to comply with a legal requirement. Right to rent checks, anti-money laundering checks, and tax reporting all fall under this basis.

**Legitimate interest.** Processing is necessary for your legitimate business interests, provided these interests do not override the individual's rights. Marketing to existing clients, for example, may be justified under legitimate interest — but you must balance your interest against the individual's expectation of privacy.

**Consent.** The individual has given clear, informed consent for a specific purpose. This is typically required for marketing to people who are not existing clients.

For each type of data you hold and each purpose you use it for, you should document which lawful basis applies. This documentation is part of your accountability obligation.

## Privacy notices

You must inform people about how you use their data. This is done through privacy notices — clear, accessible documents that explain what data you collect, why you collect it, how you use it, who you share it with, how long you keep it, and what rights the individual has.

Most agencies need at least three privacy notices: one for landlords, one for tenants and applicants, and one for website visitors. These should be provided at the point of data collection — when a landlord signs a management agreement, when a tenant submits an application, or when a visitor fills in a contact form on your website.

Privacy notices should be written in plain language. Legal jargon does not demonstrate compliance; it demonstrates that you have copy-pasted a template without understanding it.

## Data security

Protecting personal data requires both technical and organisational measures.

**Technical measures** include encrypted storage, secure passwords, two-factor authentication, regular software updates, and secure backup procedures. If you use cloud-based software, ensure that the provider meets appropriate security standards.

**Organisational measures** include access controls (ensuring that only authorised staff can access sensitive data), training (ensuring that all staff understand their data protection responsibilities), and clear procedures for handling data breaches.

A CRM system provides a natural framework for data security. Rather than sensitive data being scattered across emails, spreadsheets, and paper files, it is centralised in a system with access controls, audit trails, and security features built in.

## Data retention

One of the most commonly overlooked aspects of data protection is retention — how long you keep personal data after it is no longer needed.

Many agencies keep data indefinitely, reasoning that it might be useful someday. This is not compliant. You should have a data retention policy that specifies how long each type of data is kept and what happens when the retention period expires.

For tenant data, a reasonable retention period might be six years after the end of the tenancy — aligning with the limitation period for most contractual claims. For applicant data — where no tenancy was entered into — a much shorter period is appropriate, perhaps six to twelve months.

Your CRM can support retention policies by flagging data that has exceeded its retention period and either deleting it automatically or prompting a review.

## Subject access requests

Individuals have the right to request a copy of the personal data you hold about them. This is known as a subject access request, and you must respond within a defined timeframe — typically one month.

The request can come from anyone whose data you hold — landlords, tenants, applicants, even contractors. You must provide a copy of the data in a commonly used format, along with information about how the data is used and who it has been shared with.

Having your data organised in a CRM makes responding to subject access requests significantly easier than searching through emails, spreadsheets, and paper files. You can locate all data associated with an individual quickly and export it in a structured format.

## Data breaches

A data breach is any event that results in the unauthorised access, loss, or destruction of personal data. This includes obvious events like a cyber attack, but also everyday mistakes — sending an email to the wrong recipient, losing a USB drive, or leaving a paper file on a train.

When a breach occurs, you must assess its severity. If the breach is likely to result in a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms, you must report it to the relevant supervisory authority within the required timeframe. In serious cases, you must also notify the affected individuals.

Having a breach response plan — documented and understood by all staff — ensures that you can act quickly and appropriately when something goes wrong.

## Practical steps for compliance

Data protection compliance is not a one-time project. It is an ongoing practice. Here are the most important steps for an estate agency.

**Audit your data.** Understand what personal data you hold, where it is stored, and how it is used. This is the foundation of everything else.

**Document your lawful bases.** For each type of data processing, document which lawful basis applies.

**Write clear privacy notices.** Ensure that everyone whose data you collect is informed about how it is used.

**Implement access controls.** Ensure that sensitive data is only accessible to those who need it.

**Train your team.** Data protection is everyone's responsibility, not just the compliance officer's.

**Review regularly.** Data protection is not static. Review your practices, update your policies, and adapt to changes in regulation.
