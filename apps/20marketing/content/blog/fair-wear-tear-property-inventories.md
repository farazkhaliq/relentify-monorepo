Fair wear and tear is one of the most disputed concepts in residential lettings. It is the dividing line between what a landlord can deduct from a deposit and what they cannot. Get it wrong in either direction, and someone loses out unfairly — either the landlord absorbs costs they should not have to, or the tenant pays for deterioration they are not responsible for.

For inventory clerks, letting agents, and landlords, understanding fair wear and tear is not optional. It is fundamental to producing reports that are fair, accurate, and defensible. This guide explains what fair wear and tear means in practice, how to assess it, and how to document it in your inventory reports.

## What fair wear and tear actually means

Fair wear and tear is the natural and reasonable deterioration that occurs through normal, everyday use of a property. It is the gradual decline in condition that happens simply because a home is being lived in.

The key word is "reasonable." The deterioration must be consistent with the type of property, the length of the tenancy, and the number of occupants. A family of four living in a house for three years will create more wear than a single person living in the same house for one year. Both scenarios involve fair wear and tear — but the degree is different.

Fair wear and tear is the landlord's responsibility. It cannot be deducted from a tenant's deposit. This is a consistent principle across most rental markets and deposit protection frameworks.

## Fair wear and tear vs damage

The distinction between fair wear and tear and damage is sometimes clear-cut and sometimes genuinely difficult. Here is a framework that helps:

### Fair wear and tear examples

- Slight fading of paint or wallpaper from sunlight
- Small scuffs on skirting boards from vacuuming
- Minor carpet wear in high-traffic areas (hallways, doorways, in front of sofas)
- Gradual dulling of a polished worktop from regular use
- Loose door handles or hinges from repeated use
- Tiny pin holes from picture hooks (typically one or two per wall)
- Slight discolouration of white goods over time
- Worn key in a lock
- Fading of curtains or blinds from sun exposure
- Minor thinning of carpet pile

### Damage examples (not fair wear and tear)

- Large holes in walls from shelving, heavy items, or impact
- Burns on carpets, worktops, or furniture
- Pet scratches on doors, floors, or furniture
- Carpet stains from spills that were not cleaned
- Broken appliances due to misuse or neglect
- Cracked tiles from dropped objects
- Mould caused by failure to ventilate properly
- Smoke damage or nicotine staining from indoor smoking
- Water damage from overflowing baths or blocked drains the tenant failed to report
- Ripped curtains or blinds
- Graffiti or crayon on walls

### The grey area

Some conditions fall between clear-cut wear and obvious damage:

- **Multiple picture hook holes** — one or two per wall is generally acceptable. Twenty holes across a wall may go beyond reasonable use.
- **Kitchen grease splatter** — some grease near a hob is normal. A thick layer of baked-on grease covering the extractor and surrounding surfaces suggests inadequate cleaning rather than normal use.
- **Carpet wear** — a slightly flattened pathway is fair wear. A threadbare patch where a heavy piece of furniture was dragged repeatedly may cross into damage.

In these grey areas, context matters. The age of the item, the quality at check-in, the length of the tenancy, and the number of occupants all factor into a reasonable assessment.

## Factors that affect the assessment

### Length of tenancy

A one-year tenancy will produce less wear than a five-year one. Adjudicators expect to see some deterioration in long tenancies and will be sceptical of claims that present normal long-term use as damage.

### Number of occupants

A property occupied by a family with children will show more wear than one occupied by a single adult. This is normal and expected.

### Age and condition at check-in

This is why the check-in inventory is so critical. If the carpet was already showing wear at check-in, additional wear during the tenancy is less significant. If the carpet was brand new at check-in and heavily worn after a one-year tenancy, that may raise questions.

### Quality of the item

Cheap laminate flooring has a shorter lifespan than high-quality hardwood. Budget paint fades and marks more quickly than premium alternatives. The expected lifespan of an item affects how much wear is reasonable over a given period.

### Type of property

A student house will show more wear than a professional couple's flat. The property's target market and the type of tenants it attracts are relevant context.

## How to document fair wear and tear in reports

### At check-in

Record the condition of everything in detail, including items that are already showing signs of age or use. This is your baseline. If the carpet is three years old and already showing some flattening in the hallway, note it. If the paint has minor scuffs, note them. This documentation prevents these conditions from being attributed to the tenant later.

### At check-out

When documenting conditions at check-out, describe what you see without making judgements about whether something is fair wear and tear or damage. Your job as a clerk is to record the condition. The decision about whether it constitutes fair wear and tear is for the landlord, tenant, and potentially the adjudicator.

That said, providing context helps. If a carpet was three years old at check-in and the tenancy lasted two years, noting that the carpet is now five years old (and typical carpet lifespan is seven to ten years) gives useful context for anyone assessing the claim.

### Use comparison language

In your check-out report, describe how conditions have changed since check-in:

- "Carpet in hallway — slightly more flattened than at check-in, consistent with continued use. No stains or damage."
- "Paint on living room walls — additional minor scuffs noted compared to check-in. No significant marks or damage."
- "Kitchen worktop — light scratching visible, not noted at check-in. Consistent with regular kitchen use."

This language is factual, comparative, and leaves the assessment of responsibility to others.

## Common mistakes in handling wear and tear

### Landlords claiming for everything

Some landlords treat any change from check-in as the tenant's responsibility. This is incorrect and will fail at adjudication. Adjudicators are experienced at recognising fair wear and tear, and landlords who claim for it lose credibility on their legitimate claims as well.

### Tenants denying responsibility for genuine damage

Conversely, some tenants try to argue that all deterioration is fair wear and tear. A cigarette burn on a carpet is not fair wear and tear. A broken window is not fair wear and tear. The tenant's responsibility extends to damage caused by their actions or negligence.

### Clerks making judgements

Inventory clerks should describe, not judge. Recording "this is fair wear and tear" or "this is tenant damage" in your report steps outside your role as an impartial observer. Describe the condition, note how it compares to check-in, and let the parties draw their conclusions.

### Not accounting for betterment

If a landlord needs to replace a carpet that was seven years old, they are getting a new carpet to replace an old one. The tenant should not pay the full cost of a new carpet — only the proportion that reflects the remaining useful life of the original. This is the betterment principle, and it applies to most items in a property.

## The depreciation framework

A practical approach to assessing fair wear and tear uses expected lifespans:

- **Interior decoration (paint)**: 3 to 5 years
- **Carpet**: 5 to 10 years depending on quality
- **Curtains and soft furnishings**: 5 to 7 years
- **Kitchen worktops**: 10 to 15 years
- **White goods (washing machine, fridge)**: 5 to 10 years
- **Built-in oven**: 10 to 15 years
- **Bathroom suite**: 15 to 20 years
- **Kitchen units**: 15 to 20 years

If an item has exceeded its expected lifespan, any wear is almost certainly fair wear and tear. If an item is within its first year and showing significant deterioration, that may indicate damage beyond normal use.
