Property inventory clerking is one of those careers that most people do not know exists until they stumble across it. Yet it is a genuine, growing profession that offers flexible hours, decent income, and low barriers to entry. If you are looking for a career change, a way to work for yourself, or a business you can start with minimal investment, inventory clerking is worth serious consideration.

This guide covers what the job involves, what skills you need, how to get started, and how to turn it into a sustainable career or business.

## What does an inventory clerk do?

An inventory clerk inspects rental properties and produces detailed reports documenting their condition. There are three main types of reports:

**Check-in reports** are prepared at the start of a tenancy. They record the condition of every room, fixture, fitting, and item of furniture, along with photographs, meter readings, and a key schedule.

**Check-out reports** are prepared at the end of a tenancy. They compare the property's current condition against the check-in report to identify any changes, damage, or missing items.

**Mid-tenancy inspections** are conducted during a tenancy to check on the property's condition and identify any maintenance issues.

The reports serve a critical function: they provide the evidence needed to resolve deposit disputes fairly. Without a good inventory, landlords struggle to make justified deductions, and tenants may face unfair claims.

## Who hires inventory clerks?

Your clients will typically fall into three categories:

**Letting agents** are the primary source of work for most inventory clerks. Agents manage properties on behalf of landlords and need inventories for every new tenancy. A single agency might manage hundreds of properties, providing a steady stream of bookings.

**Landlords** who manage their own properties sometimes hire inventory clerks directly. These tend to be smaller, less frequent jobs, but they can be valuable — especially if the landlord has a portfolio of properties.

**Property management companies** that look after blocks of flats or large portfolios also need inventory services, often on a larger scale.

## What skills do you need?

You do not need formal qualifications to become an inventory clerk. There is no mandatory certification, no regulated exam, and no licensing requirement. That said, the role demands a specific set of skills:

### Attention to detail

This is the most important skill. Your job is to notice and record everything — every mark on a wall, every scratch on a door, every stain on a carpet. If you miss something at check-in, it cannot be attributed to pre-existing damage at check-out.

### Good written communication

Your reports need to be clear, specific, and professional. You are writing documents that may be used as evidence in formal disputes. The language needs to be precise and unambiguous.

### Photography skills

You do not need to be a professional photographer, but you do need to consistently capture clear, well-lit, properly framed images. Most of the work is done with a smartphone, but understanding basic composition and lighting makes a significant difference.

### Organisation

If you are managing multiple bookings across different properties, you need to be organised. Reports must be accurate, delivered on time, and properly filed. Missing a booking or delivering a late report can damage your reputation quickly.

### Professionalism

You will be entering people's homes, often with tenants present. Courtesy, punctuality, and a professional demeanour are essential. First impressions matter, especially when you are building relationships with agents and landlords.

## Getting started

### Training

While there are no mandatory qualifications, several organisations offer training courses specifically for inventory clerks. These courses typically cover:

- How to conduct a thorough inspection
- Report writing standards
- Photography techniques
- Fair wear and tear assessment
- Deposit protection regulations
- Business setup and marketing

A training course is not strictly necessary, but it will give you confidence, credibility, and a head start in producing reports that meet industry standards.

### Equipment

The investment required to start is minimal:

- **Smartphone or tablet** — for photos and (if using software) report writing
- **Inventory software** — a professional platform for creating and managing reports
- **Measuring tape** — for recording room dimensions if required
- **Torch** — for inspecting dark corners, under sinks, and inside cupboards
- **Vehicle** — you will be travelling between properties, so reliable transport is essential

Digital inventory platforms like [Relentify](/inventory) allow you to build reports on-site, capture photos within the app, and generate professional documents automatically. This eliminates the need for paper forms, separate cameras, and hours of post-visit report writing.

### Legal and business setup

If you are working as a self-employed clerk, you will need to:

- Register as self-employed with the relevant tax authority
- Set up a business bank account
- Arrange professional indemnity insurance (recommended, not always required)
- Consider public liability insurance
- Create standard terms and conditions for your services

### Building your first client base

The most common routes to finding work include:

- **Contacting letting agents directly** — introduce yourself, explain your services, and offer to do a trial inventory so they can see the quality of your work
- **Online directories** — list your services on property industry directories and local business listings
- **Networking** — attend property events, join landlord forums, and connect with property professionals
- **Social media** — share examples of your work (anonymised) and property tips to build visibility
- **Referrals** — once you have a few clients, referrals become your most powerful growth channel

## What to charge

Pricing varies depending on location, property size, and the type of report. Here are typical ranges:

**Check-in inventory:**
- Studio or 1-bed: from around the equivalent of 60 to 90 in your local currency
- 2-bed: 80 to 120
- 3-bed: 100 to 150
- 4-bed+: 130 to 200+

**Check-out report:**
- Usually 60 to 80 percent of the check-in price

**Mid-tenancy inspection:**
- Typically 40 to 70 percent of the check-in price

These figures are approximate and will vary by market. Urban areas with higher property values and more rental activity tend to support higher prices. Rural areas may require lower pricing but also have less competition.

When pricing your services, factor in:

- Travel time and distance between properties
- Report writing time (if not done on-site)
- Software and equipment costs
- Insurance and business overheads
- Your target hourly rate

## A typical day

A full-time inventory clerk might handle 3 to 5 inspections per day, depending on property size and travel distances. A typical day might look like this:

- 09:00 — Check-in inventory for a 2-bed flat (60 to 90 minutes on-site)
- 10:30 — Travel to next property
- 11:00 — Check-out for a 3-bed house (90 to 120 minutes on-site)
- 13:00 — Lunch and admin
- 14:00 — Mid-tenancy inspection for a 1-bed flat (30 to 45 minutes)
- 15:00 — Check-in inventory for a 2-bed flat (60 to 90 minutes)
- 16:30 — Report finalisation and client communication

If you are using digital tools that allow you to build reports on-site, there is very little post-visit work. If you are using paper or basic tools, you will need additional time in the evening to type up and format reports.

## Growing the business

Once you have established a reputation and a reliable client base, there are several ways to grow:

**Expand geographically** — cover a wider area or target new towns and cities

**Take on staff** — hire and train additional clerks to handle more bookings. This shifts your role from doing inspections to managing a team and business development.

**Add services** — offer related services such as property inspections, compliance checks, or key management

**Specialise** — focus on a niche like HMOs, student accommodation, or high-value properties where the detail required (and the fees) are higher

**Build agent relationships** — aim for exclusive or preferred supplier arrangements with letting agents, which provide predictable, recurring revenue

## The outlook for inventory clerking

The rental market continues to grow in most countries, and with it the demand for professional inventory services. Regulatory pressure around deposit protection is increasing, making inventories more important, not less.

At the same time, the profession is becoming more technology-driven. Clerks who embrace digital tools and deliver faster, higher-quality reports will outperform those still working with pen and paper.

If you are considering a career in inventory clerking, the opportunity is real. The barriers to entry are low, the startup costs are minimal, and the demand is steady. What sets successful clerks apart is not qualifications or experience — it is attention to detail, professionalism, and consistency.

Start with one report. Make it excellent. And build from there.
