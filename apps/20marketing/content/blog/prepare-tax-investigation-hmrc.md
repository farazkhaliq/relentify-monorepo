Receiving a letter from HMRC saying they want to investigate your tax affairs is enough to make anyone's stomach drop. But tax investigations are a routine part of HMRC's compliance work, and being selected does not necessarily mean you have done anything wrong.

Understanding what HMRC looks for, what triggers an investigation, and how to prepare can make the entire process much less stressful. In most cases, if your records are in order and you have been honest, the investigation will conclude without major issues.

## Types of HMRC investigation

HMRC conducts several types of investigation, and the approach depends on what they suspect:

### Aspect enquiry

This is the most common type. HMRC has a question about a specific aspect of your tax return — a particular figure, a claim, or a disclosure. They want to understand it better and see the supporting evidence.

Aspect enquiries are targeted and usually resolve relatively quickly once you provide the information HMRC is requesting.

### Full enquiry

A full enquiry covers your entire tax return. HMRC will want to examine all your income, expenses, and claims. These are more intensive and take longer to resolve.

Full enquiries are less common than aspect enquiries and are typically triggered by more serious concerns about the accuracy of a return.

### Random enquiry

HMRC selects a small number of returns at random for investigation, regardless of whether anything looks wrong. This is part of their compliance checking process. Being selected randomly is not an accusation — it is simply bad luck.

### Criminal investigation

In cases of suspected deliberate fraud, HMRC may conduct a criminal investigation. This is entirely different from a civil enquiry and involves much more serious consequences. Criminal investigations are rare and are reserved for cases of significant, intentional tax evasion.

## What triggers an investigation?

While random selection does occur, most investigations are triggered by something specific:

### Inconsistencies in your return

If the figures on your tax return do not make sense — for example, very high turnover but very low profit, or expenses that seem disproportionate to income — HMRC may want to look more closely.

### Significant changes from prior years

A sudden drop in reported income, a large increase in expenses, or a dramatic change in profitability without an obvious reason can attract attention.

### Industry comparisons

HMRC has data on typical profit margins and expense ratios for different industries. If your figures are significantly different from the norm for your sector, it may prompt questions.

### Information from third parties

HMRC receives information from banks, clients, suppliers, and other government agencies. If the income they know about from these sources does not match what you have reported, that is a red flag.

### Late or incomplete returns

Repeatedly filing late, filing incomplete returns, or making frequent amendments can suggest that your affairs are not well-managed, prompting HMRC to take a closer look.

### Tips and reports

Sometimes HMRC receives information from disgruntled employees, business partners, or competitors. While anonymous tips are not taken at face value, they can trigger a check.

### Tax avoidance schemes

If you have participated in a tax avoidance scheme, especially one that HMRC has identified as aggressive, you are more likely to face an enquiry.

## How to prepare

### Keep comprehensive records

The single most important thing you can do is maintain thorough, organised records. If HMRC asks for evidence to support a figure on your return, you need to be able to produce it quickly and confidently.

Records to maintain:

- **Income records** — All invoices issued, bank statements showing receipts, contracts
- **Expense records** — Receipts, supplier invoices, bank statements showing payments
- **Bank reconciliations** — Monthly reconciliation reports
- **Asset records** — Purchase invoices for equipment, vehicles, etc.
- **Mileage logs** — If you claim vehicle expenses
- **Working-from-home calculations** — If you claim home office costs
- **Tax computations** — How your tax liability was calculated

Accounting software like [Relentify](/accounting) keeps all your transaction data, receipts, and reports in one place, which is invaluable if HMRC comes knocking. Digital records are far easier to search and present than boxes of paper.

### Be accurate and honest

This should go without saying, but accuracy and honesty are your best defences. HMRC's primary concern is whether you have paid the right amount of tax. If your return is accurate and your records support it, the investigation will likely conclude quickly.

If you have made a genuine mistake, it is better to disclose it voluntarily (either before or during the investigation) than to wait for HMRC to find it. Voluntary disclosure usually results in lower penalties.

### Understand your figures

You should be able to explain every significant figure on your tax return. Why was turnover this amount? What are the major expense categories? Why did profit change from last year? If you cannot explain your own figures, it does not inspire confidence.

### Engage your accountant early

If you receive an investigation notice, contact your accountant immediately. They can:

- Communicate with HMRC on your behalf
- Help you gather the necessary records
- Explain technical matters to HMRC
- Negotiate on your behalf if there are disputed amounts
- Advise you on your rights

Having professional representation makes the process less stressful and often leads to a better outcome.

## What happens during an investigation

### The opening letter

HMRC sends a letter notifying you of the investigation and requesting specific information. Read it carefully and note the deadline for responding.

### Information requests

HMRC will ask for records and explanations. Common requests include:

- Bank statements for all accounts (business and sometimes personal)
- Sales records and invoices
- Purchase records and receipts
- Contracts and agreements
- VAT records
- Payroll records
- Evidence supporting specific claims

### Meetings

HMRC may request a meeting, which can take place at your premises, at your accountant's office, or at an HMRC office. You are entitled to have your accountant present.

During the meeting, HMRC will ask questions about your business, your record-keeping, and specific aspects of your return. Be honest and straightforward, but do not volunteer information beyond what is asked.

### Review period

After gathering information, HMRC reviews everything and may ask follow-up questions. This can take weeks or months depending on complexity.

### Outcome

The investigation concludes with one of several outcomes:

- **No adjustments** — HMRC is satisfied that your return is correct. No additional tax is due.
- **Minor adjustments** — Small corrections are needed, resulting in a modest additional tax payment plus interest.
- **Significant adjustments** — Material errors or omissions are found, resulting in additional tax, interest, and potentially penalties.
- **Settlement** — For larger cases, HMRC may negotiate a settlement covering tax, interest, and penalties.

## Penalties

If HMRC finds that you have underpaid tax, the penalty depends on the nature of the error:

- **Genuine mistakes** — 0% to 30% of the additional tax, depending on whether you disclosed voluntarily
- **Failure to take reasonable care** — 15% to 30% (prompted) or 0% to 30% (unprompted)
- **Deliberate errors** — 20% to 70% (prompted) or 35% to 70% (unprompted)
- **Deliberate and concealed** — 30% to 100% (prompted) or 50% to 100% (unprompted)

"Prompted" means HMRC found the error. "Unprompted" means you disclosed it voluntarily. Voluntary disclosure always results in lower penalties.

## How to reduce your risk

### File accurately and on time

This is the most fundamental step. Accurate, timely returns are the lowest-risk returns.

### Keep records for the required period

Keep records for at least six years (five years after the 31 January deadline for the relevant tax year). HMRC can open an enquiry into returns going back further if they suspect deliberate errors.

### Do not overclaim expenses

Claim everything you are entitled to, but nothing more. If you are unsure whether something is allowable, ask your accountant rather than guessing.

### Report all income

This includes cash payments, foreign income, and income from side activities. HMRC has increasingly sophisticated data matching capabilities and is more likely than ever to spot unreported income.

### Get professional advice for complex matters

If your tax affairs are complex — multiple income sources, overseas income, complex business structures, significant asset disposals — professional advice is not a luxury. It is risk management.

A tax investigation is not a pleasant experience, but it need not be a catastrophic one either. With good records, accurate returns, and professional support, most investigations are resolved without drama. The best preparation is simply good practice: keep your books in order, be honest, and get advice when you need it.
