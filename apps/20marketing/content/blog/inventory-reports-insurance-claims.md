Most landlords associate property inventories with deposit protection. That is their primary purpose, and rightly so. But inventories serve another important function that is often overlooked: they provide evidence for insurance claims.

When a landlord's building or contents insurance is called upon — whether for fire damage, flood, theft, or tenant-caused destruction — the insurer will ask for proof of what was in the property and what condition it was in before the incident. A comprehensive inventory report provides exactly this evidence.

## How insurance claims work for landlords

### What landlord insurance covers

Landlord insurance policies vary, but they typically cover:

- **Building insurance** — structural damage from fire, flood, storm, subsidence, and other insured perils
- **Contents insurance** — damage to or theft of landlord-owned contents (furniture, appliances, fixtures)
- **Malicious damage** — intentional damage caused by tenants
- **Loss of rent** — income lost while the property is uninhabitable due to an insured event
- **Liability** — claims from tenants or visitors injured at the property

### What insurers require

When you make a claim, the insurer needs to verify:

1. **What was damaged or lost** — a specific list of items affected
2. **The condition and value before the incident** — proof that items existed and were in the condition you claim
3. **The extent of the damage** — what happened and how badly items were affected
4. **The cost of repair or replacement** — evidence of the financial loss

This is where the inventory comes in.

## How inventory reports support claims

### Proving what was in the property

For contents claims, the insurer needs to know what was actually in the property at the time of the incident. A check-in inventory that lists every item of furniture, every appliance, every piece of kitchen equipment — with descriptions and photographs — provides this proof.

Without an inventory, you are relying on memory and receipts. You might remember that there was a sofa, a dining table, and a television, but what about the smaller items? The kettle, the microwave, the set of pans, the curtains, the lamps? An itemised inventory with photographs documents everything, including the items you might otherwise forget to claim for.

### Proving the condition before the incident

Insurers do not pay for items that were already damaged. If you claim for a sofa destroyed in a flood, the insurer may ask what condition it was in before the flood. If the sofa was already heavily worn with stains and tears, the payout will reflect its diminished value.

The check-in inventory (or the most recent mid-tenancy inspection) provides the "before" evidence. If the inventory shows the sofa in good condition with clear photographs, the insurer has evidence to support a higher valuation. If the inventory shows existing damage, the payout is adjusted accordingly — but at least there is a clear, agreed-upon baseline.

### Documenting specific items

For high-value items, the inventory's details become particularly important:

- **Make and model** of appliances help the insurer determine replacement costs
- **Age and condition** help determine the item's current value (not just its replacement cost)
- **Photographs** provide visual proof that the item existed and was in the described condition
- **Serial numbers** (where recorded) help with theft claims and identify specific items

### Supporting building claims

While the inventory focuses primarily on contents and interior condition, it also documents elements that are relevant to building insurance claims:

- Condition of walls, floors, and ceilings
- State of kitchen and bathroom fixtures
- Condition of windows, doors, and locks
- External features like fences, sheds, and outbuildings

If a storm damages the roof and water ingress ruins the ceiling and carpet, the inventory's pre-incident documentation of the ceiling and carpet condition supports the claim.

## Types of claims where inventories are most valuable

### Fire damage

Fire can destroy the entire contents of a property. Without an inventory, reconstructing what was lost is difficult and the claim may be undervalued. With a detailed inventory, every item can be listed, valued, and claimed.

### Flood damage

Floods damage contents at ground level — carpets, furniture, appliances, kitchen equipment. The inventory documents all of these items, their condition, and their location in the property.

### Theft

If a property is burgled during a void period or while the tenant is away, the inventory provides a baseline of what should be in the property. Missing items can be identified by comparing the current contents against the inventory.

### Malicious tenant damage

Some landlord insurance policies cover malicious damage by tenants. The inventory documents the property's condition before the tenancy, and the check-out report documents the damage. Together, they provide the evidence the insurer needs to assess the claim.

### Storm damage

Wind, rain, and other storm damage to the building exterior and interior are covered by most landlord policies. The inventory's documentation of windows, doors, fences, and external areas provides the pre-incident baseline.

## Practical tips for maximising insurance value

### Keep inventories up to date

An inventory from five years ago may not reflect the current contents of the property. If you have added or replaced items since the last inventory, the old report will not cover them. Update your inventory when significant changes are made — new furniture, replacement appliances, refurbishment works.

### Record make, model, and age

For appliances and significant furniture items, recording the make, model, and approximate age helps the insurer determine replacement costs accurately. "Samsung washing machine, model WW70T4020EE, purchased 2024" is much more useful for a claim than "washing machine."

### Keep receipts alongside inventories

Where possible, keep purchase receipts for high-value items. These complement the inventory by providing proof of the purchase price and date. Digital storage makes this manageable — photograph receipts and store them alongside the inventory report.

### Photograph thoroughly

Insurance claims are easier to process with photographic evidence. The inventory's photographs do double duty — they serve deposit protection and insurance purposes simultaneously.

### Store reports securely

You need access to inventory reports potentially years after they were created. Cloud-based storage through platforms like [Relentify](/inventory) ensures that reports are accessible whenever needed, backed up automatically, and protected against loss.

### Review your insurance coverage

Make sure your insurance policy covers the contents you are providing. If you are letting a fully furnished property, your contents insurance needs to cover the full replacement value of all furnishings. The inventory can help you calculate this value when setting or reviewing your coverage level.

## The overlap with deposit protection

There is a natural overlap between the evidence needed for deposit claims and the evidence needed for insurance claims. Both require:

- A detailed record of what was in the property
- Documentation of the condition of each item
- Photographic evidence
- Clear dating of the documentation

A good inventory serves both purposes simultaneously. You do not need separate documentation for deposits and insurance — the same comprehensive report works for both.

However, there is one important distinction. Deposit deductions are limited to the tenant's liability — damage beyond fair wear and tear. Insurance claims are based on the actual loss to the landlord, regardless of who caused it. A boiler that fails due to age (not the tenant's fault, so no deposit deduction) may still be covered by insurance. The inventory documents its pre-failure condition and specifications, supporting the insurance claim even though no deposit deduction is warranted.

## When the inventory is not enough

Inventories support insurance claims, but they may not be sufficient on their own. Insurers may also require:

- Police reports (for theft or vandalism)
- Fire service reports (for fire damage)
- Surveyor reports (for structural damage)
- Contractor quotes or invoices (for repair costs)
- Proof of ownership (receipts, bank statements)

The inventory is one piece of the evidence puzzle — an important piece, but not the only one.

## The bottom line

A comprehensive property inventory is an asset that pays for itself in multiple ways. Beyond deposit protection, it provides the evidence base that supports insurance claims when things go wrong. Fire, flood, theft, storm damage, and malicious tenant damage can all be claimed against more effectively when you have a thorough, dated, photographic record of the property and its contents.

Every inventory you produce is an investment in protecting your property. Make it thorough, keep it current, and store it securely. When you need it — for a deposit dispute or an insurance claim — you will have the evidence that makes all the difference.
