The Employment Allowance is one of the most valuable tax reliefs available to UK employers, yet many small businesses either do not claim it or do not realise they are eligible. It reduces your employer National Insurance bill by up to a set annual amount — effectively free money for qualifying employers.

This guide explains how the Employment Allowance works and how to make sure you are claiming it.

## What is the Employment Allowance?

The Employment Allowance allows eligible employers to reduce their annual employer National Insurance liability by a fixed amount. The current allowance is set by the government and typically updated in the Budget or Autumn Statement.

This means that if your total employer NI bill for the year is less than or equal to the allowance, you pay no employer NI at all. If it is higher, you pay the difference.

For small employers, this can represent a significant saving — potentially thousands of pounds per year.

## Who qualifies?

Most employers qualify, but there are some important exclusions:

### You CAN claim if:
- You are a business employing at least one person (other than the sole director)
- Your employer NI liability in the previous tax year was below a specified threshold
- You are a charity
- You are a community amateur sports club

### You CANNOT claim if:
- You are a company with only one employee who is also a director (sole director companies)
- Your employer NI liability in the previous tax year exceeded the specified threshold
- You carry out functions of a public nature (public bodies, local authorities, NHS bodies)
- You are a service company working under IR35 where the NI relates to deemed employment payments
- You are a company connected to another employer and together you exceed the NI threshold

The sole director exclusion catches many people. If you are the only director and employee of your company, you cannot claim. However, if you employ even one other person (part-time or full-time), you become eligible — provided you meet the other conditions.

## How much can you save?

The saving depends on your employer NI liability and the current allowance level. Let us work through an example.

Suppose the Employment Allowance is £5,000 and you have five employees with combined annual salaries of £150,000. Your employer NI at 13.8% on earnings above the Secondary Threshold might total approximately £15,000 per year.

With the Employment Allowance, your bill drops to £10,000 — a saving of £5,000.

For smaller employers with lower NI liabilities, the saving can eliminate your employer NI bill entirely. An employer with a total NI liability of £3,000 would pay nothing and use £3,000 of the allowance, with the remaining £2,000 unused (it does not carry forward).

## How to claim

Claiming the Employment Allowance is straightforward:

### Step 1: Check eligibility
Review the conditions above. If you are unsure about the NI threshold, check your previous tax year's payroll records for total employer NI paid.

### Step 2: Claim through your payroll software
The Employment Allowance is claimed through your Employer Payment Summary (EPS) as part of your regular RTI submissions. Most payroll software has a checkbox or setting to indicate that you are claiming the allowance.

In Relentify and most other [payroll](/payroll) platforms, you simply enable the Employment Allowance option in your employer settings. The software then automatically offsets your employer NI against the allowance each month.

### Step 3: The allowance is applied monthly
Once claimed, the allowance is applied against your employer NI liability month by month until it is used up or the tax year ends. In the first few months of the tax year, your entire employer NI bill may be offset by the allowance. Once the allowance is fully used, you begin paying employer NI as normal.

### Step 4: Claim each year
The Employment Allowance must be claimed each tax year — it does not automatically renew. Make sure you claim at the start of each tax year (or as soon as you process the first payroll of the new year).

## What if you forget to claim?

If you did not claim the Employment Allowance at the start of the tax year, you can still claim it later in the year. The allowance will be applied retrospectively, and the overpaid employer NI will be offset against future payments.

If the tax year has already ended, you can make a retrospective claim for up to four previous tax years. Contact HMRC to request the adjustment.

## Connected companies

If you control multiple companies, the Employment Allowance can only be claimed by one of them (or shared between them, up to the total allowance). HMRC defines connected companies using control tests — if the same person or group of people control two or more companies, those companies are connected.

For connected companies, decide at the start of the tax year which company will claim the allowance. Choose the one with the highest employer NI liability to maximise the benefit.

## De minimis state aid

The Employment Allowance is classified as de minimis state aid under EU rules (still applicable under retained legislation). This means there is a ceiling on the total state aid a business can receive over a three-year period.

For most small businesses, the de minimis ceiling is not an issue. However, if you receive other forms of state aid (grants, subsidies, tax reliefs classified as state aid), you need to check that the combined total does not exceed the ceiling.

Your payroll software may ask you to confirm your state aid position when you enable the Employment Allowance.

## Common mistakes

### Not claiming at all
This is the most expensive mistake. Many small employers do not realise the Employment Allowance exists, or assume they are too small to benefit. If you employ anyone other than just yourself as director, check your eligibility.

### Claiming as a sole director company
Sole director companies with no other employees are not eligible. If you claim incorrectly, HMRC will recover the allowance plus potential penalties.

### Not re-claiming each year
The allowance must be claimed each tax year. If you claimed last year but forgot to claim this year, you are paying more employer NI than necessary.

### Not checking the NI threshold
If your previous year's employer NI exceeded the threshold, you are not eligible for the current year. Check the threshold amount before claiming.

### Connected companies claiming separately
If you have connected companies and each one claims the Employment Allowance separately, HMRC will recover the excess. Coordinate between your companies.

## Is it worth claiming?

If you are eligible, claiming the Employment Allowance is one of the easiest decisions in business. It costs nothing to claim, requires minimal administrative effort, and can save thousands of pounds per year.

Check your eligibility, enable the claim in your payroll software, and make sure you do it at the start of every tax year. It is free money — do not leave it on the table.
