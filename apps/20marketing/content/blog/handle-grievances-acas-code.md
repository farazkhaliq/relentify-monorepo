When an employee raises a formal complaint about something at work — whether it is about their treatment, working conditions, a colleague's behaviour, or a management decision — that is a grievance. How you handle it matters enormously, both for the employee's wellbeing and for your business's legal exposure.

In the UK, the ACAS Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures sets out the minimum standard that employers should follow. Failure to follow the Code does not automatically make you liable, but an employment tribunal will take it into account and can increase any compensation award by up to 25% if you have unreasonably failed to follow it.

This guide explains the grievance process in line with the ACAS Code.

## What counts as a grievance?

A grievance is a formal complaint by an employee about a workplace matter. Common grievances include:

- Unfair treatment by a manager
- Bullying or harassment by colleagues
- Discrimination based on a protected characteristic
- Concerns about working conditions
- Disagreements about contract terms, pay, or benefits
- Complaints about how a policy has been applied

Not every complaint is a formal grievance. Employees may raise concerns informally first, and many issues can be resolved through a conversation. The formal grievance process applies when the employee wants their complaint to be formally investigated and addressed.

## The ACAS Code: key principles

The ACAS Code is based on fundamental principles of fairness:

1. **Employers and employees should raise and deal with issues promptly** — do not let things drag on.
2. **Both parties should act consistently** — apply the same process each time.
3. **Investigations should be carried out to establish the facts** — do not prejudge.
4. **Employees should be informed of the basis of the problem** and given an opportunity to respond.
5. **Employees should have the right to be accompanied** at formal meetings.
6. **Employees should have the right to appeal** against any formal decision.

These principles apply regardless of the size of the business. Whether you have five employees or five hundred, the ACAS Code expects the same level of fairness.

## The grievance process: step by step

### Step 1: The employee raises the grievance

The employee should put their grievance in writing, setting out the nature of their complaint and what they would like to happen as a result. This gives you a clear record of what the issue is.

Some employees may raise their grievance verbally in the first instance. If the matter is serious, encourage them to put it in writing. If they are unable or unwilling to do so, record the details yourself and confirm them with the employee.

### Step 2: Arrange a grievance meeting

Once you receive the grievance, arrange a meeting with the employee without unreasonable delay. The purpose of the meeting is to:

- Hear the employee's complaint in full
- Ask questions to understand the detail
- Identify any witnesses or evidence
- Consider what the employee wants as a resolution

Before the meeting, write to the employee confirming:
- The date, time, and location
- That the meeting is to discuss their grievance
- Their right to be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative
- What the process will involve

### Step 3: Investigate

After the meeting, investigate the grievance. The investigation should be proportionate to the seriousness of the complaint:

- Gather relevant documents and records
- Interview witnesses
- Consider any previous complaints or relevant history
- Review relevant policies

The investigator should be impartial — ideally someone who is not directly involved in the complaint. In a small business where this is difficult, do your best to approach the investigation objectively.

### Step 4: Hold the grievance hearing

After the investigation, hold a formal hearing. At the hearing:

- Present the findings of the investigation to the employee
- Allow the employee to respond and add any further information
- The employee may be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative
- The companion can address the hearing and confer with the employee, but cannot answer questions on the employee's behalf
- Take notes of the hearing

If you need more time to consider the matter, adjourn and reconvene. Do not rush the decision.

### Step 5: Make a decision

Consider all the evidence and decide on the outcome. Possible outcomes include:

- **Upholding the grievance**: The complaint is substantiated. Decide what action to take to resolve the issue.
- **Partially upholding**: Some elements of the complaint are substantiated but not all.
- **Not upholding**: The complaint is not substantiated after investigation.

Whatever the outcome, explain your decision and the reasons in writing.

If the grievance is upheld, take appropriate action. This might include:
- Changing a working arrangement
- Providing training for a manager
- Disciplinary action against someone whose behaviour caused the grievance
- Reviewing a policy or procedure
- An apology

### Step 6: Right of appeal

The employee has the right to appeal the decision if they are not satisfied with the outcome. The appeal should be heard by a different person if possible — ideally someone more senior than the original decision-maker.

The appeal meeting follows a similar format to the original hearing. After the appeal, communicate the final decision in writing. The appeal decision is normally final.

## Timescales

The ACAS Code says issues should be dealt with promptly. While there are no specific statutory timescales for each step, a reasonable timeline might be:

- Acknowledge the grievance within 1-2 working days
- Hold the initial meeting within 5 working days
- Complete the investigation within 2-4 weeks (depending on complexity)
- Communicate the decision within 5 working days of the hearing
- Hear the appeal within 2 weeks of receiving it

These are guidelines, not absolutes. Complex grievances may take longer, but keep the employee informed of progress and avoid unnecessary delays.

## Practical considerations for small businesses

### When the complaint is about the owner

In a small business, the grievance may be about the owner or sole manager — the very person who would normally handle it. This is one of the hardest situations in small business HR.

Options include:
- Bringing in an external HR consultant or mediator
- Asking a trusted senior employee to handle the process
- Using ACAS's free conciliation service

Whatever you do, do not investigate your own complaint. Find someone who can approach the matter impartially.

### Informal resolution

Not every complaint needs a formal process. If an employee raises a concern, try to resolve it informally first. A conversation, an apology, or a small change to working arrangements can often resolve the issue without formal proceedings.

However, always offer the formal process if the informal approach does not resolve the matter, or if the employee specifically requests a formal grievance.

### Keeping records

Document every stage of the grievance process:
- The written grievance
- Meeting invitations and notes
- Investigation findings
- The decision letter
- The appeal (if applicable)

Store these records securely alongside the employee's other records. Platforms like Relentify keep all employee documentation in one place, making it easy to find when needed.

## Mediation

For grievances involving relationship breakdowns or interpersonal conflict, mediation can be more effective than a formal process. Mediation involves an independent third party helping both parties reach a resolution through facilitated discussion.

ACAS provides a free mediation service, and there are also private mediators available. Mediation is voluntary — both parties must agree to participate — but when it works, it can repair working relationships in a way that a formal process cannot.

## Common mistakes

### Ignoring the grievance
Hoping a complaint will go away is not a strategy. Unaddressed grievances escalate. Deal with them promptly.

### Not following the ACAS Code
The Code exists to ensure fairness. Deviating from it without good reason creates legal risk and can increase compensation awards by 25%.

### Treating the grievance as an inconvenience
An employee who raises a grievance is exercising a legitimate right. Treating them as a troublemaker — or subjecting them to any detriment for raising the complaint — is unlawful victimisation.

### Rushing to judgment
Investigate properly before making a decision. A premature conclusion based on incomplete evidence is unfair and legally vulnerable.

### No appeal
Always offer the right to appeal. It is a basic requirement of the ACAS Code and a fundamental principle of fairness.

## The bottom line

Handling grievances is part of being an employer. Do it well — promptly, fairly, and consistently — and you protect your business, support your employees, and build a culture where problems are addressed rather than ignored. Follow the ACAS Code, keep records, and seek external help when you need it.
