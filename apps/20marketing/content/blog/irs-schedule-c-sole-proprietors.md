If you operate a business as a sole proprietor in the United States — whether you are a freelancer, consultant, gig worker, or small business owner without a separate legal entity — Schedule C (Form 1040) is the tax form where you report your business income and deduct your business expenses. The net profit from Schedule C flows to your personal tax return and determines both your income tax and self-employment tax liability.

Understanding Schedule C helps you claim all legitimate deductions, avoid common mistakes, and minimize the risk of an IRS audit.

## What is Schedule C?

Schedule C, officially titled "Profit or Loss from Business (Sole Proprietorship)," is filed as part of your personal federal tax return (Form 1040). It reports:

- Your gross business income
- Your business expenses by category
- Your net profit or loss

The net profit is added to your other income on Form 1040, and the self-employment tax is calculated on Schedule SE based on this figure.

## Who must file Schedule C?

You must file Schedule C if you:

- Operate a business as a sole proprietor
- Are a single-member LLC (unless you have elected corporate tax treatment)
- Receive 1099-NEC income for services provided as an independent contractor
- Have a side business alongside employment income

Even if your business operated at a loss, you should file Schedule C to report the loss, which may offset other income on your tax return.

## Walking through Schedule C

### Part I: Income

**Line 1: Gross receipts or sales** — Report your total business income before any deductions. This includes all payments received for your services or products, whether or not you received a 1099.

**Line 2: Returns and allowances** — Subtract any refunds you gave to customers.

**Line 4: Cost of goods sold** — If you sell physical products, subtract the cost of goods sold (calculated in Part III). Service-based businesses typically leave this blank.

**Line 7: Gross income** — Your total income after subtracting returns and COGS.

### Part II: Expenses

This is where you claim your business deductions. Schedule C lists specific expense categories:

**Line 8: Advertising** — Marketing costs, online advertising, business cards, promotional materials.

**Line 9: Car and truck expenses** — Business use of your vehicle. You can use the standard mileage rate or actual expenses (fuel, maintenance, insurance proportional to business use). Keep a mileage log.

**Line 10: Commissions and fees** — Payments to agents, referral fees, platform fees.

**Line 11: Contract labor** — Payments to subcontractors for work on your projects. This is different from employee wages.

**Line 13: Depreciation** — The annual depreciation deduction for business assets (equipment, furniture, vehicles). Calculated on Form 4562.

**Line 15: Insurance** — Business insurance premiums (liability, professional indemnity, business property). Note: health insurance is deducted elsewhere on Form 1040, not on Schedule C.

**Line 16a: Mortgage interest** — Interest on business property mortgages.

**Line 16b: Other interest** — Interest on business loans or credit lines.

**Line 17: Legal and professional services** — Accounting fees, legal fees, consulting fees related to your business.

**Line 18: Office expense** — Office supplies, postage, small office items that are not capital equipment.

**Line 20a-b: Rent** — Rent for business property (vehicles, equipment, office space).

**Line 22: Supplies** — Materials and supplies consumed in your business operations.

**Line 24a: Travel** — Business travel expenses (flights, hotels, ground transport, meals while travelling at 50 percent).

**Line 25: Utilities** — Utilities for business premises.

**Line 26: Wages** — Employee wages (if you have employees as a sole proprietor — distinct from contract labor).

**Line 27a: Other expenses** — Any business expenses that do not fit the listed categories. Itemize these in Part V.

### Line 28-31: Net profit or loss

Total your expenses and subtract them from gross income. The result is your net profit (or loss), which transfers to Form 1040.

## The home office deduction

If you use a dedicated space in your home regularly and exclusively for business, you can claim a home office deduction. Two methods are available:

### Simplified method

Deduct $5 per square foot of your home office, up to a maximum of 300 square feet ($1,500 maximum deduction). No need to track actual home expenses.

### Regular method

Calculate the percentage of your home used for business (based on square footage) and apply that percentage to actual home expenses: rent or mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, repairs, and depreciation.

The regular method requires more record-keeping but may yield a larger deduction, especially if your home expenses are high.

## Cost of goods sold (Part III)

If you sell products, Part III calculates your cost of goods sold:

- Beginning inventory
- Plus purchases during the year
- Plus labor costs directly related to production
- Plus materials and supplies used in production
- Minus ending inventory
- Equals cost of goods sold

Service businesses that do not sell physical products can skip this section.

## Vehicle expenses (Part IV)

If you claim vehicle expenses on Line 9, Part IV asks for:

- When the vehicle was placed in business service
- Total miles driven during the year
- Business miles driven
- Whether you have written evidence supporting your mileage claims
- Whether another vehicle is available for personal use

Keep a mileage log throughout the year. The IRS frequently scrutinizes vehicle deductions, and a contemporaneous log is your best defense.

## Common Schedule C deductions people miss

### Professional development

Courses, conferences, books, and subscriptions related to your business are deductible.

### Software and subscriptions

Business software, cloud services, and professional subscriptions are deductible as expenses or may be depreciated depending on cost.

### Bank and payment processing fees

Business bank account fees and payment processor charges (Stripe, PayPal, Square fees) are deductible.

### Business portion of phone and internet

If you use your personal phone and internet for business, the business-use portion is deductible. Calculate a reasonable percentage based on actual business use.

### Retirement contributions

While not deducted on Schedule C itself, contributions to a SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, or solo 401(k) reduce your taxable income on Form 1040. These can significantly reduce your overall tax burden.

### Health insurance premiums

Self-employed individuals can deduct health insurance premiums for themselves and their family on Form 1040 (not Schedule C), up to the amount of net self-employment income.

## Common mistakes to avoid

### Not reporting all income

Report all business income, not just amounts shown on 1099 forms. Cash payments, payments under $600, and direct bank transfers are all taxable income even without a 1099.

### Mixing personal and business expenses

Only deduct expenses that are ordinary (common in your industry) and necessary (helpful and appropriate for your business). Personal expenses claimed as business deductions are a leading audit trigger.

### Inadequate record-keeping

Keep receipts and documentation for every deduction. Digital records stored in your [accounting software](/accounting) are acceptable. The IRS can disallow deductions you cannot substantiate.

### Overestimating home office or vehicle deductions

These are common audit targets. Be accurate and conservative. Claim only what you can legitimately substantiate.

### Filing Schedule C when another form applies

If your business is a partnership, S-corporation, or C-corporation, you file a different return. Schedule C is specifically for sole proprietors and single-member LLCs.

## Audit risk factors

Certain Schedule C items attract more IRS attention:

- **Large losses** — Especially if reported for multiple years
- **High deductions relative to income** — Expense ratios that seem disproportionate
- **Cash-intensive businesses** — Where income is harder to verify
- **Home office deduction** — Especially the regular method with large deductions
- **Vehicle expenses** — Particularly 100 percent business use claims
- **Meals and entertainment** — Frequently overstated

The best audit defense is accurate, well-documented records. Your accounting software records, receipts, and mileage logs should support every number on your Schedule C.
