Getting your pricing right is one of the most important decisions in running an inventory business — or offering inventory services as part of a letting agency. Charge too little and you cannot sustain the quality and service that clients expect. Charge too much and you lose work to competitors. Charge inconsistently and you confuse clients and undermine trust.

This guide covers how to think about inventory pricing, what factors should influence your rates, and how to structure your pricing for sustainability.

## What influences inventory pricing

### Property size

Larger properties take longer to inspect and generate more detailed reports. A studio flat might take 45 minutes on-site. A five-bedroom house could take two to three hours. Your pricing should reflect this time difference.

The most common pricing structure is based on the number of bedrooms:

- Studio or 1 bedroom
- 2 bedrooms
- 3 bedrooms
- 4 bedrooms
- 5+ bedrooms

Each step up represents additional time, additional rooms, and additional documentation.

### Furnished vs unfurnished

Furnished properties require significantly more documentation than unfurnished ones. Every item of furniture needs to be listed, described, and photographed. A fully furnished two-bedroom flat may take as long as an unfurnished four-bedroom house.

Many inventory providers charge a premium for furnished properties — typically 30 to 50 percent more than the unfurnished rate for the same property size.

### Report type

Different report types take different amounts of time:

**Check-in inventory**: The most time-intensive, as it creates the complete baseline record from scratch. This is typically your highest-priced service.

**Check-out report**: Usually quicker than a check-in because the inspector works from the existing check-in report, updating conditions rather than creating a new document. Typically priced at 60 to 80 percent of the check-in rate.

**Mid-tenancy inspection**: The quickest of the three, focusing on overall condition and specific concerns rather than documenting every detail. Typically 40 to 60 percent of the check-in rate.

### Location

Your geographic market affects both your costs and what clients will pay. Urban areas with higher living costs, higher property values, and more rental activity typically support higher prices. Rural areas may have lower costs but also less demand and more price sensitivity.

Travel distance between properties also matters. If you spend 45 minutes driving between inspections, that travel time needs to be factored into your pricing or managed through geographic focus.

### Competition

Research what other inventory providers charge in your area. This gives you a reference point, though you should not automatically match the cheapest competitor. Competing on price alone is not sustainable. Compete on quality, reliability, and professionalism — and price accordingly.

## Typical pricing ranges

Pricing varies significantly by market and region. The following ranges are approximate and intended as a general guide. Adjust for your local market:

### Check-in inventory

| Property size | Unfurnished | Furnished |
|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| Studio / 1-bed | Low to mid range | Mid range |
| 2-bed | Mid range | Mid to upper range |
| 3-bed | Mid to upper range | Upper range |
| 4-bed | Upper range | Premium |
| 5-bed+ | Premium | Premium plus |

### Check-out report

Typically 60 to 80 percent of the corresponding check-in rate.

### Mid-tenancy inspection

Typically 40 to 60 percent of the check-in rate.

### Combined packages

Some providers offer packages that bundle check-in, mid-tenancy, and check-out services at a discounted total rate. This can be attractive to clients and provides you with guaranteed repeat business for the full tenancy cycle.

## Calculating your minimum viable rate

Before setting prices, calculate what you need to earn per inspection to be profitable.

### Step 1: Calculate your annual costs

Add up all your business expenses:
- Software subscriptions
- Insurance (professional indemnity, public liability)
- Vehicle costs (fuel, insurance, maintenance, depreciation)
- Phone and equipment
- Marketing
- Accounting and admin
- Training and professional development
- Any other overheads

### Step 2: Determine your target income

What do you need (or want) to earn from the business? Include tax liabilities in this calculation.

### Step 3: Estimate your capacity

How many inspections can you realistically complete per week? Account for travel time, report production time (if any), administration, and non-billable activities.

A solo clerk might complete 15 to 25 inspections per week, depending on property sizes and geographic spread.

### Step 4: Calculate your minimum rate

(Annual costs + Target income) / (Weekly inspections x 48 working weeks) = Minimum rate per inspection

If this minimum rate is higher than what the market will bear, you need to either reduce costs, increase capacity, or accept a lower income. If the market rate is significantly higher than your minimum, you have pricing flexibility.

## Pricing strategies

### Per-property pricing

The most straightforward approach. Each inspection has a set price based on property size, type, and report type. Clients know exactly what they will pay before booking.

### Time-based pricing

Some providers charge by the hour rather than per property. This ensures you are compensated for the actual time spent, which is fairer for unusually large or complex properties. However, it introduces uncertainty for clients who prefer to know the cost upfront.

### Volume pricing for agents

Letting agents who provide regular work may expect volume discounts. This can be appropriate — the predictability and volume of agency work has value — but the discount should be modest enough that each inspection remains profitable.

A common approach is to offer a 10 to 15 percent discount for agencies that commit to a minimum monthly volume.

### Premium pricing for premium service

If your service is genuinely superior — faster delivery, more detailed reports, better photography, stronger dispute outcomes — you can charge a premium. Position this as a quality choice: "Our reports have a higher success rate in deposit disputes because of the level of detail we provide."

## Common pricing mistakes

### Undercharging to win work

The most common mistake. New clerks often set prices low to attract clients, then find themselves unable to raise them without losing business. Start at a sustainable rate from the beginning.

### Not accounting for all costs

Travel time, report production time, and administrative overhead are often forgotten. If you spend 90 minutes at a property but two hours driving and 30 minutes on administration, the total time investment is four hours.

### Inconsistent pricing

Different prices for similar work create confusion and can damage trust. Establish a clear rate card and apply it consistently. If you offer discounts, do so transparently and with clear criteria.

### Not reviewing annually

Costs increase over time — fuel, insurance, software, living expenses. If your prices do not increase with them, your profit margin shrinks. Review your pricing at least annually and adjust as needed.

### Discounting too aggressively

A 20 to 30 percent discount for volume work may seem reasonable, but if your margins are already thin, it can push individual inspections below profitability. Calculate the impact of any discount on your per-inspection profit before offering it.

## Communicating value to clients

Price sensitivity decreases when clients understand the value they receive. Help them see:

- **The quality of your reports**: Show samples that demonstrate the detail, photography, and professionalism
- **The protection inventories provide**: Explain how thorough inventories save money in deposit disputes
- **The cost of poor inventories**: A lost dispute can cost many times the price of a good inventory
- **The efficiency of your service**: Fast turnaround times, same-day report delivery, easy-to-use comparison reports

Using professional tools like [Relentify](/inventory) reinforces the quality message. When clients see reports produced by dedicated inventory software, they associate the service with professionalism and are more willing to pay a fair rate.

## Raising your prices

If you need to increase prices:

- Give existing clients advance notice (one to two months is reasonable)
- Explain the reason briefly (increased costs, investment in better tools, etc.)
- Apply the increase consistently across all clients
- Continue delivering the same or better quality to justify the new rate

Most clients accept reasonable price increases, especially when the service quality is strong. The clients who leave over a modest increase were probably not valuing your service appropriately in the first place.

## The bottom line

Pricing inventory services is about balancing what the market will pay with what you need to earn to be sustainable. Calculate your costs, understand your market, price for profitability, and communicate the value of what you deliver. Do not compete on price — compete on quality, reliability, and professionalism. The right clients will recognise and pay for that value.
