When most people think of a property inventory, they picture rooms, walls, floors, and furniture. But some of the most practically important elements of an inventory are the ones that get overlooked: appliances, meters, and utility connections.

Appliances break down, malfunction, and suffer damage. Meter readings determine who pays for what. Utility connections need to be transferred cleanly between tenancies. Documenting all of these properly at check-in prevents disputes, simplifies transitions, and provides crucial evidence if something goes wrong.

## Documenting appliances

### What to record for each appliance

Every appliance provided by the landlord should be documented with the following details:

**Identification:**
- Type of appliance (e.g., freestanding washing machine, integrated dishwasher)
- Make and brand
- Model number (usually on a label on the appliance)
- Serial number (if visible)
- Colour
- Approximate age (if known)

**Condition:**
- External condition (dents, scratches, discolouration, rust)
- Internal condition (for appliances you can open — oven interior, fridge shelves, washing machine drum)
- Cleanliness
- Working status — does it function correctly?

**Location:**
- Which room the appliance is in
- Whether it is freestanding, integrated, or built-in

### Testing appliances at check-in

It is not always practical to run a full cycle on every appliance during a check-in inspection, but you should do basic functional tests:

- **Oven**: Turn it on and confirm it heats. Check the grill. Note any error codes on the display.
- **Hob**: Turn on each ring or plate briefly. For gas hobs, check ignition works.
- **Fridge/freezer**: Confirm it is cold and running. Check the thermostat setting.
- **Washing machine**: Check the door opens and closes. Run a brief rinse cycle if time allows. Inspect the rubber seal and detergent drawer.
- **Dishwasher**: Check the door, racks, and spray arms. Run a quick rinse if possible.
- **Tumble dryer**: Check the door, drum, and lint filter. Run briefly to confirm it heats and tumbles.
- **Boiler**: Check the pressure gauge, note the reading, and confirm hot water is available.
- **Extractor fan**: Turn it on and confirm it operates.
- **Microwave**: Check the door, turntable, and run a brief test.

Record whether each appliance is "working" or "not tested" with a reason. If an appliance has a known issue (e.g., the oven temperature dial is stiff but the element works), note it.

### Photographing appliances

For each appliance, take:

- An exterior photograph showing the overall condition
- A close-up of any visible damage or wear
- A photograph of the make/model label
- An interior photograph (ovens, fridges, washing machines)
- A photograph of the appliance in its installed position

This seems like a lot of photos for a fridge, but when a tenant claims the dent was already there at check-in, or the landlord claims the shelf was intact, these photographs resolve the question instantly.

## Recording meter readings

Meter readings serve two purposes: they facilitate the transfer of utility accounts between tenancies, and they provide evidence of the property's utility status at the point of handover.

### Gas meter

- Location (external meter box, under stairs, kitchen cupboard)
- Meter type (standard, prepayment, smart)
- Serial number or MPRS number (if visible)
- Current reading
- Photograph showing the reading clearly

### Electricity meter

- Location
- Meter type (standard, prepayment, smart, Economy 7/dual rate)
- Serial number or MPAN (if visible)
- Current reading (for dual rate meters, record both readings)
- Photograph showing the reading clearly

### Water meter

- Location (if present — not all properties have one)
- Current reading
- Photograph showing the reading

### Practical tips for meter readings

- **Photograph the meter before writing down the reading.** If you misread it, the photo provides a backup.
- **Make sure the reading is legible in the photograph.** Meters are often in dark, awkward locations. Use your phone's torch. Take multiple shots if needed.
- **For digital meters**, capture the screen clearly. Some digital meters cycle through multiple displays — make sure you capture the correct one (the cumulative reading, not the current usage rate).
- **For smart meters**, note whether the in-home display shows the same reading as the main meter. Discrepancies can cause confusion later.
- **For prepayment meters**, note the remaining credit as well as the cumulative reading.

## Documenting utility connections and safety features

### Stopcock

Record the location of the main water stopcock. This is important for emergency situations — if a pipe bursts, the tenant needs to know where to shut off the water. Note whether it turns smoothly or is stiff.

### Gas shut-off valve

Record the location of the main gas shut-off valve (usually near the meter). Note its condition and operability.

### Consumer unit (fuse box)

Record the location of the consumer unit. Photograph it with the panel open, showing the circuit breakers and any labelling. Note whether it is a modern consumer unit with RCDs or an older-style fuse box.

### Smoke alarms and carbon monoxide detectors

- Location of each alarm or detector
- Type (mains-powered, battery-powered, or both)
- Working status — test each one by pressing the test button
- Battery condition (if applicable)

This is both an inventory item and a safety compliance check. In many jurisdictions, landlords are legally required to provide working smoke alarms and carbon monoxide detectors.

### Boiler details

In addition to testing whether the boiler produces hot water:

- Make and model
- Location
- Pressure reading (from the gauge)
- Date of last service (if a label is visible on the boiler)
- Any warning lights or error codes

## Why this level of detail matters

### Appliance disputes

Appliance damage and failure are common deposit dispute items. If a washing machine breaks during the tenancy, the question is whether it failed from normal use (the landlord's responsibility) or from tenant misuse (the tenant's responsibility). A check-in record showing the machine's age, condition, and working status provides essential context.

### Utility billing disputes

Incorrect meter readings at the start or end of a tenancy can lead to billing disputes between the tenant, the landlord, and the utility company. An accurate, photographed meter reading eliminates this issue.

### Safety compliance

Recording the presence and working status of smoke alarms, CO detectors, and gas safety features demonstrates that the property met safety requirements at the start of the tenancy. This protects the landlord in case of a future safety incident.

### Insurance claims

If an appliance fails and causes damage — a washing machine floods the kitchen, for example — the landlord's insurance company may ask for evidence of the appliance's condition and maintenance history. A check-in inventory showing the machine's make, model, age, and working status provides this.

## Integrating appliances and meters into your workflow

For inventory clerks and letting agents, appliances and meters should be a standard part of every inspection template. They should not be treated as an afterthought or left to the end when time is running short.

A structured template that includes sections for each appliance category, meter readings, and utility details ensures nothing is missed. Digital inventory platforms like [Relentify](/inventory) can include these sections as standard fields, with prompts for photographs, readings, and condition notes.

The time required to document appliances and meters properly is modest — perhaps 15 to 20 minutes added to a standard inspection. The protection it provides is disproportionately valuable, covering some of the most common and costly dispute categories.

Document the appliances. Photograph the meters. Record the readings. It is the part of the inventory that many people skip and almost everyone regrets skipping later.
