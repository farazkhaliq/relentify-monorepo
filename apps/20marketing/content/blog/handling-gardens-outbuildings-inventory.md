# Handling Gardens and Outbuildings in Property Inventories

Ask any experienced inventory clerk about the areas most likely to cause disputes, and gardens are near the top of the list. A beautiful garden at check-in can become an overgrown jungle by check-out if the tenant neglects it. A functioning shed can develop rot. A garage door that worked perfectly can seize up. Yet many inventories skim over outdoor areas with a few vague lines, creating inadequate evidence when disputes inevitably arise.

Thorough documentation of gardens, outbuildings, and all external areas is just as important as recording the condition of the kitchen or bathroom. This guide explains how to do it properly.

## Why Outdoor Areas Get Overlooked

There are practical reasons why outdoor inventories tend to be less thorough than interior ones. Weather can make outdoor documentation uncomfortable or difficult. Gardens look different depending on the season, making condition assessment subjective. Outbuildings may be locked, cluttered, or difficult to access.

But the biggest reason is simply habit. Many inventory clerks — and the templates they use — focus primarily on interior rooms. External areas get a paragraph where they deserve a page.

This is a mistake. Garden maintenance disputes account for a significant proportion of deposit deduction claims. And the sums involved can be substantial — professional garden clearance, lawn re-turfing, fence replacement, and shed repair quickly add up.

## Documenting the Garden

A systematic approach to garden documentation prevents gaps and ensures consistency.

### Boundaries

Start with the boundaries. Walk the perimeter and note the type and condition of each boundary — wooden fence, brick wall, hedge, chain-link fence. Record the height, material, and condition. Note any missing panels, leaning sections, broken posts, or gaps.

Photograph each boundary from multiple angles. Close-up shots of any damage or deterioration are essential. For hedges, note the current state of maintenance — neatly trimmed, overgrown, patchy.

### Lawns and Planted Areas

For lawns, record the overall condition: well-maintained, patchy, overgrown, or bare. Note any visible damage — bare patches, mole hills, waterlogging, weeds. Photograph the full lawn from several angles.

For flower beds, borders, and planted areas, document what is planted (or at least describe the planting scheme) and its current condition. This does not require botanical expertise — "established rose bushes, well-maintained" or "mixed herbaceous border, overgrown and weedy" provides adequate description.

Trees and significant shrubs deserve individual mention. Note their species (if known), approximate size, and condition. A mature tree that develops problems during a tenancy can become a costly liability.

### Hard Landscaping

Patios, paths, driveways, decking, and retaining walls should all be documented. Record the material (concrete, block paving, natural stone, timber decking) and condition. Note any cracking, sinking, staining, moss growth, or loose elements.

Decking deserves particular attention. It deteriorates relatively quickly if not maintained and can become slippery and dangerous. Record whether it has been recently treated and note any soft spots, splintering, or structural concerns.

Steps, handrails, and changes of level should be documented for both condition and safety.

### Water Features and Drainage

If the garden includes a pond, fountain, or water feature, record its current state — functional, drained, or neglected. Note the condition of any pumps, filters, or lighting.

Check and document visible drainage — gullies, drains, and downpipes. Note any blockages or standing water.

### Garden Furniture and Equipment

If the property is let furnished with garden furniture, barbecues, or other equipment, itemise everything. Record the make, material, and condition of each item. Photograph each piece.

Items left in the garden deteriorate quickly if not maintained. A wooden table that looks fine at check-in may be split and weathered by check-out. Clear documentation establishes the starting condition.

## Documenting Outbuildings

### Sheds

Garden sheds are workhorses of the rental property. They store tools, bikes, garden equipment, and everything else that does not fit inside. They also suffer from neglect.

Record the following for every shed:

- Construction material (wood, metal, plastic) and overall condition
- Roof condition — any leaks, missing felt, sagging
- Door and lock functionality — can it be opened, closed, and secured?
- Window condition (if applicable)
- Floor condition — rotting, damp, solid
- Shelving and internal fittings
- Contents (if any items are included in the inventory)

Photograph the shed externally from all sides and internally. Close-up shots of any damage are essential.

### Garages

Garages need thorough documentation because they often suffer from issues that are expensive to repair.

Record:

- Door type and operation (manual, automatic, remote-controlled)
- Door condition — dents, rust, alignment, seal condition
- Floor condition — cracks, staining, oil marks
- Walls and ceiling condition — damp, cracks, peeling paint
- Lighting and electrical fittings
- Any shelving, workbench, or storage units included
- Utility meters or consumer units located in the garage

Test the garage door during the inventory. If it is automated, check the mechanism and remote control. A non-functioning automatic door is a common dispute point.

### Greenhouses and Conservatories

For greenhouses, record the frame condition, the number and condition of glass or polycarbonate panels, door and vent operation, and any staging or shelving.

Conservatories that form part of the main property should be treated as internal rooms, but those that are separate structures need the same external documentation as other outbuildings.

### Other Structures

Log cabins, summer houses, studios, carports, bike stores, bin stores, and any other garden structures all need documenting. Apply the same systematic approach: external condition, internal condition, doors and windows, fixtures and fittings.

## Seasonal Considerations

Gardens change dramatically with the seasons. An inventory conducted in summer will show the garden at its most visible — plants in full growth, lawns green, everything accessible. A winter inventory may find dormant plants, wet lawns, and areas obscured by fallen leaves.

**Spring and summer inventories** benefit from full visibility but may not reveal drainage problems or the condition of surfaces beneath vegetation.

**Autumn and winter inventories** can reveal drainage issues and structural problems hidden by foliage, but dormant plants cannot be properly assessed.

Whichever season the inventory is conducted in, note the date prominently and acknowledge any seasonal limitations. "Garden photographed in January — planted areas dormant; full planting assessment will be more accurate in spring/summer."

For [digital inventory tools](https://relentify.com/features/inventory), timestamped photographs provide evidence of the exact conditions on the day of the inventory, which is invaluable if disputes arise months or years later.

## Setting Expectations in the Tenancy Agreement

A good inventory works hand-in-hand with clear tenancy terms about garden maintenance responsibilities.

Common approaches include:

- **Tenant maintains to the standard recorded in the inventory:** This is the most common arrangement and is straightforward if the inventory is thorough.
- **Landlord provides gardening service:** The landlord arranges regular garden maintenance and includes the cost in the rent.
- **Specific maintenance schedule:** The tenancy agreement specifies required actions (mowing weekly, hedge trimming twice yearly, etc.).

Whichever approach is used, the inventory establishes the baseline standard. Without it, "maintaining the garden" is entirely subjective.

## Check-Out Comparisons

At check-out, the garden inventory becomes your reference document. Compare the current state to the check-in record systematically:

- Walk the same route and photograph from the same angles
- Check each boundary section against the original record
- Compare lawn and planting condition
- Inspect hard landscaping for new damage
- Check every outbuilding inside and out
- Test all doors, locks, and mechanisms

Normal wear and seasonal variation are expected. A lawn that was lush in July will naturally look different in November. But overgrown borders, broken fence panels, a shed door hanging off its hinges, or a patio stained beyond reasonable wear are legitimate deduction points — provided your check-in inventory proves the original condition was better.

## Best Practices

**Be thorough:** Spend as much time on the garden as you would on a major interior room. For properties with large gardens or multiple outbuildings, this might mean 30-60 minutes of outdoor documentation.

**Photograph extensively:** Take more photos than you think you need. Storage is cheap; re-visiting the property because you missed something is not.

**Use consistent descriptions:** Develop standard terminology for garden condition. "Well-maintained," "moderately maintained," "overgrown," and "neglected" provide a clear scale that is easy to understand and compare.

**Note maintenance evidence:** If the garden has clearly been recently maintained (freshly mowed lawn, trimmed hedges), note this. It establishes that the standard was set at a high level.

**Check access:** Ensure you can access all outbuildings and external areas. If a shed is locked and the key is not available, record this and arrange to return.

Gardens and outbuildings deserve the same professional attention as the rest of the property. A thorough outdoor inventory protects landlords, informs tenants of their responsibilities, and provides clear evidence when disputes arise. Do not let the great outdoors be the weak point in an otherwise excellent inventory.
