The cost of a professional property inventory is typically modest — somewhere between the equivalent of 60 to 200 in your local currency, depending on property size and type. The cost of not having one can be many times that amount.

When a deposit dispute arises and the landlord has no inventory, the outcome is almost always the same: the deposit is returned to the tenant in full or with minimal deductions, regardless of the actual condition of the property. Without a documented baseline, there is simply no evidence to support the landlord's claims.

This article walks through realistic dispute scenarios — the kind that deposit protection schemes see regularly — to illustrate what happens when the evidence is not there.

## Scenario 1: The stained carpet

A landlord lets a two-bedroom flat with new cream carpets throughout. The tenancy lasts two years. When the tenants move out, there are noticeable stains in the living room and both bedrooms — coffee, wine, and general discolouration. The landlord estimates the carpet replacement will cost the equivalent of around 1,200.

**With an inventory:** The check-in report shows photographs of new, unstained carpets in every room, with descriptions noting "new cream carpet, no marks or stains." The check-out report shows the stains clearly, with comparison photographs. The landlord claims the replacement cost minus a depreciation allowance for two years of fair wear and tear on new carpets. The adjudicator awards a proportional amount — perhaps 800 after accounting for the two years of normal life used.

**Without an inventory:** The landlord states the carpets were new at the start of the tenancy. The tenant claims the stains were already there when they moved in. The adjudicator has no photographic evidence from check-in to verify either claim. With no baseline, the adjudicator cannot determine that the stains occurred during the tenancy. The full deposit is returned to the tenant. The landlord absorbs the entire 1,200 replacement cost.

## Scenario 2: The damaged kitchen

A landlord lets a furnished house with a well-equipped kitchen. Over a three-year tenancy, several items are damaged: the oven door handle is broken, two cupboard doors have been pulled off their hinges, the worktop has deep knife cuts, and half the crockery set is missing. The total cost of repair and replacement is estimated at around 900.

**With an inventory:** The check-in inventory lists every kitchen item — the exact quantity of plates, bowls, cups, and utensils — and documents the condition of the fitted kitchen with photographs. The oven door handle is shown intact. The cupboard doors are photographed in good condition. The worktop is shown without marks. At check-out, the damage and missing items are clearly identified by comparison. The adjudicator awards a deduction that accounts for the age and condition of items at check-in, perhaps 600.

**Without an inventory:** The landlord lists the damage and missing items. The tenant claims the oven handle was already broken when they moved in and disputes the crockery count. Without a check-in photograph of the intact handle or a documented list of crockery, the landlord cannot prove the condition at the start. Most of the claim fails. The deduction awarded, if any, is minimal.

## Scenario 3: The neglected garden

A property with a well-maintained rear garden is let on a tenancy agreement that requires the tenant to maintain the garden. After 18 months, the tenant vacates. The lawn is dead, the hedges are overgrown, the fence has been damaged, and there are weeds growing through the patio. The landlord gets quotes for garden restoration totalling around 500.

**With an inventory:** Check-in photographs show a neatly mowed lawn, trimmed hedges, an intact fence, and a clean patio. Mid-tenancy inspection photos (conducted at six months) show the garden beginning to decline. Check-out photographs show the full extent of the neglect. The progression of deterioration is clear, and the evidence supports the landlord's claim. The adjudicator awards a reasonable deduction.

**Without an inventory:** The landlord describes the garden as "well-maintained" at the start but has no photographs to prove it. The tenant says the garden was already in poor condition when they moved in. With no visual evidence from check-in, the adjudicator cannot determine the baseline. The claim fails.

## Scenario 4: The smoking damage

A non-smoking property is let to a tenant who, it later transpires, has been smoking inside throughout the tenancy. At check-out, the ceilings and walls are yellowed with nicotine staining, the curtains smell of smoke, and the property requires professional deep cleaning and full redecoration. The estimated cost is around 2,000.

**With an inventory:** Check-in photographs show white ceilings, clean walls, and fresh curtains. The contrast with the check-out photographs is stark — yellowed surfaces, discoloured curtains, and visible residue. Combined with the non-smoking clause in the tenancy agreement, the evidence strongly supports the landlord's claim. The adjudicator awards a substantial deduction, reduced only by the normal depreciation of decoration over the tenancy period.

**Without an inventory:** The landlord claims the yellowing is from smoking during the tenancy. The tenant denies smoking inside and suggests the discolouration was pre-existing. Without check-in photographs showing white ceilings and walls, the landlord cannot prove the property's condition at the start. The adjudicator has to weigh one person's word against another's, with no documentary evidence. The outcome is uncertain at best, and the landlord may recover nothing.

## Scenario 5: The appliance failure

A landlord provides a nearly new washing machine (purchased six months before the tenancy). After a one-year tenancy, the tenant reports the machine is broken. Inspection reveals that the drum is damaged, consistent with overloading. The replacement cost is around 400.

**With an inventory:** The check-in report notes the washing machine make, model, age (six months), and condition (excellent, fully working). At check-out, the machine is documented as non-functional, with the drum visibly damaged. The age and condition at check-in demonstrate that mechanical failure from normal use is unlikely after only 18 months of total life. The evidence supports a claim for damage from misuse.

**Without an inventory:** The landlord states the machine was nearly new. The tenant claims it was already malfunctioning when they moved in. Without documentation of the machine's condition and working status at check-in, the landlord cannot prove it was in good working order at the start. The claim is weak.

## The pattern is clear

In every scenario, the same dynamic plays out:

- **With an inventory**, the landlord has documented evidence of the property's condition at the start. Changes during the tenancy are provable. Deductions are supported by facts.
- **Without an inventory**, every claim becomes a contest of assertions. The tenant says one thing, the landlord says another, and the adjudicator has no independent evidence to settle the matter. In the absence of evidence, the default position favours the tenant — the deposit is returned.

## The cumulative cost

Consider a landlord with five properties, each let every two years on average. Over a decade, that is roughly 25 tenancies. If even a quarter of those tenancies involve a deposit dispute, that is six disputes.

With inventories, the landlord has a strong evidence base for each one and recovers reasonable deductions where justified. Without inventories, the landlord loses most or all of those disputes. The cumulative cost — thousands in unrecouvred damage, cleaning, and replacements — dwarfs the cost of producing inventories for every tenancy.

## The false economy

Some landlords skip inventories to save money or time. This is a false economy of the highest order. The cost of a professional inventory is a small fraction of even a single lost deposit dispute.

## The moral of the story

An inventory is not paperwork for its own sake. It is the evidence that protects your financial interest in your property. Without it, you are relying on the hope that nothing will go wrong — and when it does, you have nothing to fall back on.

Produce an inventory for every tenancy. Make it thorough. Make it photographic. And keep it safe. One day, you will be glad you did.
