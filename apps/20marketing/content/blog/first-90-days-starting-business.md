The first 90 days of a new business set the tone for everything that follows. Get the foundations right, and you create a platform for growth. Rush through them or skip critical steps, and you spend months cleaning up problems that should have been solved at the start.

This guide gives you a practical timeline — week by week — for your first three months. It covers the essential tasks in the order they should be completed, so you can focus on building your business rather than wondering what you have forgotten.

## Week 1-2: Legal and structural foundations

### Register your business

Whether you are setting up as a sole trader or forming a limited company, registration is the first step. For a limited company, this means filing with the companies authority in your jurisdiction. For a sole trader, you register with the tax authority.

Do not overthink your company name at this stage. It should be available, professional, and reasonably descriptive of what you do. You can always trade under a different name later.

### Open a business bank account

This needs to happen before you send your first invoice or pay your first expense. Mixing personal and business finances is a mistake that creates accounting headaches, makes tax returns more complicated, and looks unprofessional.

Many digital banks can open a business account within a day or two. Traditional banks may take longer, so start the process early.

### Get essential insurance

At minimum, you need public liability insurance if anyone visits your premises or you work at client sites. Professional indemnity insurance is essential for service-based businesses. Employers' liability insurance is a legal requirement if you have employees.

Do not delay this. Operating without appropriate insurance exposes you to risk that could end your business before it starts.

## Week 2-3: Financial systems

### Set up your accounting

This is the single most important system you will establish. Every pound, dollar, or euro that flows through your business needs to be tracked from day one.

Choose accounting software that fits your current needs but can grow with you. Many platforms offer basic plans that include invoicing and expense tracking, then charge significantly more for features like bank reconciliation, multi-currency support, or payroll. An all-in-one platform like Relentify includes all of these on every plan, which means you will not need to upgrade or switch as your business grows.

### Create your invoice template

Before you can bill a client, you need a professional invoice template. It should include your business name, address, registration number, payment terms, bank details, and VAT number if applicable.

Most accounting software generates invoices for you, but take the time to check that all the required information is present and that the layout looks professional.

### Understand your tax obligations

Know what taxes you need to pay and when. This typically includes income tax or corporation tax, VAT (if registered), and payroll taxes if you have employees.

Set aside a percentage of every payment you receive for tax. A common rule of thumb is 25 to 30 percent, though the exact figure depends on your tax situation. Keeping tax money in a separate account prevents the unpleasant surprise of a large tax bill with nothing set aside to pay it.

## Week 3-4: Operations and processes

### Set up your workspace

Whether you work from home, rent an office, or use a co-working space, make sure your workspace is functional and professional. If you take video calls with clients, your background matters. If you need to store files or equipment, make sure you have a system for keeping things organised.

### Create standard operating procedures

Even if you are a one-person business, documenting how you do things saves time in the long run. Write down your process for onboarding new clients, delivering your service, invoicing, and following up on payments.

These do not need to be elaborate. A simple checklist for each process is enough to start with. You can refine them as you learn what works.

### Set up a CRM or client tracking system

From the moment you start talking to potential clients, you need a way to track those conversations. A spreadsheet works in the very short term, but a proper CRM system is worth setting up early.

Track who you have spoken to, what you discussed, when to follow up, and the status of each opportunity. This information is easy to remember when you have three prospects. When you have thirty, it becomes impossible without a system.

## Week 4-6: Marketing and visibility

### Build your online presence

You need, at minimum, a professional website and a Google Business profile. Your website does not need to be elaborate — a clear description of what you do, who you help, and how to contact you is sufficient.

Make sure your website loads quickly, works on mobile devices, and includes the basic trust signals that potential clients look for: your business registration details, insurance information, testimonials if you have them, and clear contact information.

### Set up your email and communications

Use a professional email address that matches your domain name. A business email at your own domain costs very little and makes a significant difference to how you are perceived.

Set up email templates for common communications — introduction emails, quotes, follow-ups, and thank-you messages. These save time and ensure consistency.

### Start building your network

Tell everyone you know that you have started a business. Ask for introductions. Attend industry events. Join relevant online communities. The first clients for most new businesses come through personal networks, so work yours actively.

## Week 6-8: Refining your offering

### Review your pricing

After a few client conversations (or completed projects), you will have a much better sense of whether your pricing is right. If every prospect says yes immediately, you may be too cheap. If nobody is willing to pay your rates, you may need to adjust.

Pricing is not set in stone. Many successful businesses review and adjust their pricing quarterly during the first year.

### Gather feedback

Ask early clients for honest feedback. What worked well? What could be improved? Would they recommend you to others?

This feedback is invaluable for refining your service and building a testimonial bank. A few genuine testimonials can make a significant difference to your conversion rate.

### Start thinking about recurring revenue

One-off projects are fine, but recurring revenue is what builds a sustainable business. Think about how you can turn one-time work into ongoing relationships — retainer agreements, maintenance contracts, subscription services, or regular review cycles.

## Week 8-10: Growth foundations

### Automate what you can

By now, you will have identified tasks that are repetitive and time-consuming. Look for opportunities to automate:

- **Invoicing**: Set up recurring invoices for regular clients
- **Bank reconciliation**: Connect your bank feed to your accounting software
- **Reminders**: Automate payment reminders for overdue invoices
- **Scheduling**: Use online booking tools to let clients schedule meetings

Every hour you save on admin is an hour you can spend on billable work or business development.

### Review your financial position

Two months in, you should have enough data to understand your cash flow patterns. Are clients paying on time? Are your expenses in line with expectations? Is your revenue growing?

Look at your profit and loss statement (your accounting software should generate this automatically). Compare actual figures against your projections. If there are significant gaps, understand why and adjust your plan.

### Consider professional support

If you have not already engaged an accountant, now is a good time. An accountant can review your setup, confirm that your tax registrations are correct, and advise on the most tax-efficient way to structure your remuneration.

The cost of a good accountant is almost always recovered through tax savings and avoided penalties.

## Week 10-12: Scaling preparation

### Document what is working

By the end of your first 90 days, you will know which marketing channels are generating enquiries, which services are most popular, and which clients are the most profitable. Document these findings. They form the basis of your strategy for the next quarter.

### Plan for growth

If your first three months have gone well, start thinking about what growth looks like. Do you need to hire? Should you invest in marketing? Are there new services you could offer?

Growth does not have to mean expansion. It can also mean improving margins, raising prices, or focusing on higher-value clients. The best growth strategy depends on your goals and your capacity.

### Review your tech stack

The tools you chose in week one may not be the tools you need going forward. Review whether your accounting software, CRM, communication tools, and project management systems are still serving you well.

The most common regret at this stage is choosing software that seemed affordable at the start but becomes expensive as you grow — platforms that charge per user, per feature, or per transaction. Switching to a platform that includes everything you need at a predictable price can save both money and the disruption of migrating data later.

## The 90-day checklist

**Days 1-14:** Register business, open bank account, get insurance, set up accounting

**Days 14-28:** Create invoice template, understand tax obligations, set up workspace, document processes, implement CRM

**Days 28-42:** Build website, set up professional email, start networking, begin marketing

**Days 42-56:** Review pricing, gather feedback, explore recurring revenue models

**Days 56-70:** Automate repetitive tasks, review financials, engage professional support

**Days 70-90:** Document what works, plan for growth, review tech stack

## The most important thing

Do not try to make everything perfect before you start. The businesses that succeed are the ones that start, learn, and adapt. Your systems, processes, and pricing will all evolve over the first year. What matters is that you have them in place — even in rough form — so you are building on a solid foundation rather than scrambling to catch up.

The first 90 days are about getting the basics right. Everything else builds from there.
