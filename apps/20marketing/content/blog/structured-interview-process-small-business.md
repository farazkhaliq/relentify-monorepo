Most small businesses hire based on gut feeling. The interview is a casual conversation, the questions vary from candidate to candidate, and the final decision comes down to whether the interviewer "liked" the person. This approach feels natural, but it is unreliable and can lead to biased, inconsistent hiring decisions.

Structured interviews are different. Every candidate answers the same questions, responses are scored against predefined criteria, and the decision is based on evidence rather than instinct. Research consistently shows that structured interviews are better at predicting job performance than unstructured ones.

This guide explains how to build a structured interview process that works for a small business.

## What makes an interview "structured"?

A structured interview has three key elements:

1. **Predetermined questions**: Every candidate is asked the same questions in the same order.
2. **Scoring criteria**: Each answer is evaluated against a predefined scale or rubric.
3. **Consistent process**: The same format, interviewers, and evaluation method are used for every candidate.

This does not mean the interview is robotic or inflexible. You can ask follow-up questions to probe deeper. But the core questions and evaluation framework are consistent, which makes it possible to compare candidates fairly.

## Why small businesses need structure

When you are hiring one or two people a year, it is tempting to wing it. But small businesses actually have more at stake with each hire:

- **Fewer people to absorb a bad hire**: In a team of five, one wrong hire affects 20% of the workforce.
- **Less budget for mistakes**: The cost of a bad hire (recruitment, training, severance, rehiring) is proportionally larger for a small business.
- **Higher individual impact**: Each person in a small team has a bigger effect on culture, productivity, and outcomes.

Structure protects you from making expensive mistakes and helps you defend your decisions if they are ever questioned.

## Step 1: Define what you are looking for

Before writing a single interview question, clarify the specific skills, experience, and attributes you need. Pull these from the job description and prioritise them:

**Essential criteria** (must-have):
- Technical skills required for the role
- Relevant experience level
- Qualifications or certifications that are genuinely necessary

**Desirable criteria** (nice-to-have):
- Additional skills that would add value
- Industry-specific experience
- Cultural attributes that fit your team

For each criterion, define what "good" looks like. This is your scoring rubric. For example:

**Criterion: Project management experience**
- Score 1: No project management experience
- Score 2: Has managed small tasks or personal projects
- Score 3: Has managed team projects with deadlines and budgets
- Score 4: Has managed complex, multi-stakeholder projects with demonstrated results

## Step 2: Write your questions

Design questions that assess each criterion. There are several effective question types:

### Behavioural questions
These ask candidates to describe past experiences. They are the most reliable predictors of future performance.

- "Tell me about a time when you had to manage a project with a tight deadline. What was the situation, what did you do, and what was the result?"
- "Describe a situation where you disagreed with a colleague about an approach. How did you handle it?"

### Situational questions
These present hypothetical scenarios relevant to the role:

- "If a client called with a complaint about a delivery error, how would you handle it?"
- "Imagine you had three urgent tasks due on the same day and could only complete two. How would you decide which to prioritise?"

### Technical questions
These assess specific knowledge or skills:

- "Walk me through how you would set up a marketing campaign for a new product launch."
- "What accounting software have you used, and how did you use it?"

### Motivation questions
These explore why the candidate wants the role:

- "What attracted you to this position?"
- "Where do you see yourself in two years, and how does this role fit into that?"

Prepare five to eight core questions that cover all your essential and desirable criteria. This is usually enough for a 45-minute to one-hour interview.

## Step 3: Create your scorecard

For each question, create a scoring rubric with clear descriptors for each level. A simple four-point scale works well:

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 1 | Does not meet the criterion |
| 2 | Partially meets the criterion |
| 3 | Fully meets the criterion |
| 4 | Exceeds the criterion |

Write specific descriptors for each score on each question. "Fully meets the criterion" on a project management question means something different from "fully meets" on a communication question.

The scorecard ensures that different interviewers evaluate candidates against the same standard. Without it, one interviewer's "good" might be another's "average."

## Step 4: Plan the interview format

Decide on the logistics:

- **Duration**: 45-60 minutes is standard for most roles. Senior or specialist roles may need longer.
- **Format**: In-person, video call, or phone. Be consistent across all candidates.
- **Interviewers**: Ideally two people interview each candidate. This provides a second perspective and reduces individual bias.
- **Structure**: Introduction (5 min) → Questions (35-45 min) → Candidate's questions (10 min) → Close (5 min).

Give candidates a brief overview of the format at the start. Knowing the structure reduces anxiety and allows candidates to perform at their best.

## Step 5: Conduct the interviews

During the interview:

- **Follow the question order**: Ask each candidate the same questions in the same sequence.
- **Take notes**: Write down key points from each answer. You will not remember the details later.
- **Use follow-up questions**: If a candidate's answer is vague, probe further. "Can you give me a specific example?" or "What was the outcome?" are useful follow-ups.
- **Do not telegraph the right answer**: Avoid leading questions or visible reactions that signal what you want to hear.
- **Score immediately after**: Rate each answer on your scorecard as soon as the interview ends, while the conversation is fresh.

### What not to ask

Avoid questions that could be discriminatory or are unrelated to the role:

- Marital status or family plans
- Age, religion, or nationality
- Health conditions or disabilities (unless directly relevant to a genuine occupational requirement)
- Questions about protected characteristics

Stick to your predetermined questions and you will naturally avoid these issues.

## Step 6: Evaluate and decide

After all interviews are complete:

1. **Compile scores**: Add up each candidate's scores across all questions and interviewers.
2. **Review notes**: Look beyond the numbers at the qualitative notes from each interviewer.
3. **Compare fairly**: Use the scores and notes to compare candidates side by side.
4. **Discuss**: If multiple interviewers are involved, discuss any significant disagreements in scoring.
5. **Decide**: The candidate with the highest total score and strongest qualitative evidence is usually the right choice.

If two candidates score similarly, consider which one better meets your essential (rather than desirable) criteria, and which would be the stronger fit for the team and the specific challenges of the role.

## Making it work for a small business

You do not need an HR department to run structured interviews. Here are some practical tips:

- **Start simple**: Even using the same five questions for every candidate is a significant improvement over unstructured conversations.
- **Templates**: Create reusable question sets and scorecards for each role. Adapt them over time.
- **Two heads are better**: Even if you are the sole interviewer, consider having a colleague join for a second perspective.
- **Keep records**: Store your scorecards and notes. They protect you if a hiring decision is ever questioned.
- **Improve iteratively**: After each hire, review what went well and what could be better in the interview process.

## The payoff

Structured interviews take more preparation, but they save time in the long run. You spend less time deliberating because the data guides the decision. You make fewer bad hires because you are assessing what actually matters. And you build a fairer process that treats every candidate equally.

For small businesses where every hire counts, that is a worthwhile investment.
