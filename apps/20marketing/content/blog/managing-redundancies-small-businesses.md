Making people redundant is one of the hardest things a business owner can do. It affects people's livelihoods, disrupts teams, and carries significant legal obligations. But sometimes it is necessary — when a business loses a contract, restructures, adopts new technology, or faces a downturn.

This guide explains how to manage redundancies properly, legally, and with as much sensitivity as the situation allows.

## What is redundancy?

Redundancy occurs when an employee's role is no longer needed. It is not about the employee's performance — it is about the role. Genuine redundancy situations include:

- **Business closure**: The entire business or a location is closing down.
- **Reduced need for work**: The business needs fewer people to do a particular type of work.
- **Restructuring**: The business is reorganising, and certain roles are being eliminated or merged.

If you are really dismissing someone because of their performance, attitude, or conduct, that is not redundancy — it is a different process with different rules. Dressing up a dismissal as a redundancy is a common mistake that creates legal risk.

## The redundancy process

### Step 1: Establish a genuine business reason

Before starting any redundancy process, be clear about the business reason. Can you articulate why the role is no longer needed? If the answer involves the individual rather than the role, you may not have a genuine redundancy situation.

Document the business case: what has changed, why the current staffing level is no longer sustainable, and what alternatives have been considered.

### Step 2: Consider alternatives

Before making anyone redundant, explore whether alternatives exist:

- **Voluntary redundancy**: Would anyone in the affected area volunteer to leave?
- **Redeployment**: Are there other roles in the business that affected employees could move into?
- **Reduced hours**: Would employees accept a reduction in working hours instead of redundancy?
- **Natural attrition**: Can you achieve the reduction by not replacing people who leave voluntarily?
- **Temporary measures**: Could a pay freeze, reduced overtime, or temporary layoff avoid the need for redundancies?

You are not required to avoid redundancy at all costs, but you should demonstrate that you considered alternatives before proceeding.

### Step 3: Identify the pool

The "pool" is the group of employees from which those to be made redundant will be selected. Defining the pool correctly is critical.

If only one person does a particular role and that role is being eliminated, the pool may be that one person. But if several people do similar work and only some positions are being cut, the pool includes all of them, and you need a fair selection process to decide who goes.

Getting the pool wrong — for example, restricting it to exclude someone you want to keep — undermines the fairness of the entire process.

### Step 4: Establish selection criteria

When the pool contains more people than the number being made redundant, you need objective selection criteria. Common criteria include:

- **Skills and qualifications**: Relevant to the business's future needs.
- **Performance**: Based on documented performance records, not subjective opinions.
- **Attendance**: Sickness absence records, but be careful not to discriminate against those with disabilities or pregnancy-related absence.
- **Disciplinary record**: Current warnings or disciplinary outcomes.
- **Length of service**: Can be a factor but should not be the sole criterion (this can indirectly discriminate on age).

Score each employee in the pool against the criteria. The process should be transparent, consistent, and documented.

### Step 5: Consultation

Consultation is a legal requirement in most jurisdictions. Even for individual redundancies, you must consult with the affected employee before making the final decision.

Consultation should be genuine — not a box-ticking exercise. It involves:

- Explaining the business situation and why redundancy is being considered
- Discussing the selection criteria and how they have been applied
- Exploring alternatives to redundancy
- Considering the employee's suggestions and feedback
- Allowing the employee to be accompanied by a colleague or representative

For collective redundancies (a specified number of employees within a defined period), additional rules apply, including formal notification to government authorities and extended consultation periods.

### Step 6: Make the decision

After consultation, make your decision. If the employee is selected for redundancy:

- Confirm the decision in writing
- Explain the business reason, the selection process, and why they were selected
- State the redundancy date and the notice period
- Detail the redundancy payment they are entitled to
- Offer the right to appeal

### Step 7: Calculate redundancy pay

Employees who meet the qualifying criteria are entitled to statutory redundancy pay. The calculation is based on:

- **Age**: Different multipliers for different age brackets
- **Length of service**: Number of complete years worked
- **Weekly pay**: Subject to a statutory cap

The specific formula varies by jurisdiction. Many countries use a formula along the lines of:

- Half a week's pay for each complete year of service where the employee was under a certain age
- One week's pay for each year where they were in a middle age bracket
- One and a half weeks' pay for each year where they were in an older age bracket

Weekly pay is capped at a statutory maximum, regardless of the employee's actual earnings.

Some employers offer enhanced redundancy pay above the statutory minimum. If you do, make sure the terms are clear and consistently applied.

### Step 8: Notice period

Redundant employees are entitled to their contractual or statutory notice period, whichever is longer. During the notice period, they should be given reasonable time off to look for new work or arrange training.

You can require the employee to work their notice, place them on garden leave, or make a payment in lieu of notice. The approach depends on the circumstances and the terms of the contract.

### Step 9: Process the final pay

The final pay for a redundant employee includes:

- Pay up to the termination date (including notice pay)
- Payment for any accrued but untaken holiday
- Statutory or enhanced redundancy pay
- Any other contractual entitlements

Statutory redundancy pay is usually tax-free up to a threshold. Enhanced payments above the statutory amount may also be tax-free up to the same threshold. Amounts above that are subject to tax and social contributions.

## Supporting affected employees

Redundancy is stressful for everyone involved. Support the affected employees:

- **Communication**: Be honest, clear, and compassionate. Avoid corporate euphemisms.
- **Practical support**: Offer outplacement support, reference letters, or help with job searching.
- **Remaining team**: Address the concerns of employees who are staying. Explain the situation, acknowledge the impact, and discuss the plan going forward.

## Common mistakes

### Making it personal
Redundancy is about the role, not the person. If your selection is influenced by personal preferences, you are not managing a genuine redundancy.

### Inadequate consultation
Consultation must be genuine and meaningful. Presenting the decision as already made and asking the employee to sign the paperwork is not consultation.

### Poor documentation
Without clear records of the business case, selection criteria, scoring, and consultation, you cannot demonstrate that the process was fair.

### Not exploring alternatives
A tribunal will examine whether you genuinely considered alternatives. If you went straight to redundancy without exploring other options, the process may be found unfair.

### Ignoring collective consultation rules
If you are making a specified number of redundancies within a defined period, collective consultation obligations are triggered. Ignoring them can result in protective awards and penalties.

## The bottom line

Redundancy is never easy, but a fair, well-documented process protects both the business and the employees affected. Follow the steps, consult genuinely, select objectively, and treat people with dignity throughout. When you look back on the process, you want to know you handled it as well as it could have been handled.
