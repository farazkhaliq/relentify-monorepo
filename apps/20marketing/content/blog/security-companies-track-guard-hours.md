Security companies face a unique time tracking challenge. Unlike most businesses where the output of work is visible — a clean building, a built wall, a served meal — the output of security work is the absence of incidents. A guard's value is proven by what did not happen on their watch. This makes it especially important to verify that guards were present, on time, and actively monitoring the premises they were assigned to protect.

Clients expect proof. Regulators expect records. And the business needs accurate data for payroll, billing, and operational management. This guide explains how security companies can track guard hours and patrol times effectively.

## The security industry's specific needs

### Proof of presence

Clients pay for security coverage during specific hours. If a guard is scheduled for a 10pm to 6am shift, the client expects evidence that the guard was on site for the full eight hours. Without verification, the client has no way to distinguish between a guard who was diligent and one who was absent.

### Patrol verification

Many security contracts require regular patrols — walking a defined route at defined intervals. The client needs evidence that patrols happened on schedule, not just that the guard was somewhere on the premises.

### Lone working

Security guards frequently work alone, often at night. There is no colleague to verify attendance and no manager observing. The timesheet system must provide the verification that human oversight cannot.

### Multiple sites

A security company may operate across dozens of client sites, each with different coverage requirements, shift patterns, and billing arrangements. Guards may work at different sites on different days or even rotate during a single shift.

### Incident correlation

If an incident occurs — a break-in, vandalism, an alarm activation — the client may want to know exactly where the guard was at that moment. Timestamped location data provides this information.

## Building a guard hour tracking system

### GPS-verified clock-in and clock-out

The foundation is GPS-verified attendance. When a guard arrives at a site, they clock in on their mobile device. The system records the exact time and GPS coordinates, confirming the guard was at the correct location. The same applies at clock-out.

For security companies, the geofence radius can be relatively tight — the guard should be at or very near the building they are protecting. A radius of 50 to 100 metres is typically appropriate for most premises.

Platforms like [Relentify](/timesheets) support GPS-verified clock-in per site, providing the attendance evidence that security clients demand.

### Checkpoint-based patrol tracking

Beyond clock-in and clock-out, many clients require patrol records. The most effective approach is checkpoint-based tracking:

1. Define checkpoints along the patrol route (specific doors, corridors, building corners, or perimeter points)
2. Place NFC tags, QR codes, or define GPS waypoints at each checkpoint
3. Guards scan or check in at each checkpoint during their patrol
4. The system records the time and sequence of checkpoint visits

This creates a detailed, timestamped record of the patrol route. The client can see that Checkpoint A was visited at 11:15pm, Checkpoint B at 11:22pm, and so on — proving that the guard physically walked the route.

### Scheduled patrol alerts

Configure the system to generate alerts if a scheduled patrol is missed. If the contract requires an hourly patrol and the guard has not checked into any checkpoint in the last seventy-five minutes, the control room receives a notification. This serves both an operational purpose (ensuring the guard is working) and a safety purpose (a missed patrol may indicate the guard is in difficulty).

### Incident reporting

Integrate incident reporting into the same system. When a guard encounters something noteworthy — an open door, a suspicious person, a maintenance issue — they log it through the app with a timestamp, GPS location, and optional photo. This record is available to the client alongside the attendance and patrol data.

## Client reporting

Security clients expect regular reports demonstrating that their coverage was delivered as contracted. The timesheet and patrol data should generate these reports automatically:

### Attendance summary

For each billing period, a report showing every shift at the client's site: guard name, clock-in time, clock-out time, total hours, GPS verification status.

### Patrol log

A detailed record of every patrol, showing the checkpoints visited, the times, and any gaps. This demonstrates not just that the guard was present, but that they were actively monitoring the premises.

### Exception report

Any anomalies flagged during the period: late arrivals, early departures, missed patrols, clock-ins from unexpected locations. The report should show how each exception was resolved.

### Incident log

A summary of all incidents reported during the period, with timestamps, locations, and descriptions.

These reports can be generated on demand or scheduled for automatic delivery — weekly, monthly, or per billing cycle. They serve as both billing support and performance evidence.

## Operational management

### Real-time monitoring

The control room or operations manager should have a real-time dashboard showing:

- Which guards are currently on duty and at which sites
- Whether guards clocked in on time
- Patrol status (current, overdue, completed)
- Any alerts or incidents

This live view enables rapid response to problems — a guard who has not clocked in can be contacted immediately, a missed patrol can be investigated, and cover can be arranged for unexpected absences.

### Shift handover

Many security sites operate 24/7 with guards rotating in shifts. The timesheet system should capture handover points cleanly: Guard A clocks out and Guard B clocks in, with overlapping timestamps confirming continuity of coverage. Any gap between clock-out and clock-in represents an uncovered period that should be flagged.

### Multi-site scheduling

For companies managing guards across many sites, the scheduling and timesheet system should support bulk scheduling and site-level views. A regional manager should be able to see the status of all their sites at a glance, drill into individual sites, and reassign guards as needed.

## Billing and payroll

### Billing from verified hours

Every invoice should be traceable to verified timesheet records. When a client is billed for 168 hours of coverage in a month, the supporting data should show exactly which shifts, by which guards, with GPS verification for every clock-in and clock-out.

This level of transparency builds client confidence and pre-empts billing disputes. It also protects the security company — if a client claims that coverage was not provided on a specific date, the GPS-verified records provide objective evidence.

### Guard payroll

Guard pay should be calculated from the same verified data. Because security work often involves night shifts, weekend work, and bank holidays — all of which may attract premium rates — the system must differentiate these categories and apply the correct rates automatically.

### Margin management

For each client site, calculate the margin: client billing minus guard cost (including all employment costs). Sites with thin or negative margins may need contract renegotiation, staffing adjustments, or other operational changes.

## Compliance and licensing

Security companies operate under regulatory frameworks that often require:

- Licences for guards and the company
- Minimum training standards
- Record-keeping of hours worked and assignments
- Adherence to working time regulations (guards are especially vulnerable to excessive hours due to the nature of shift work)

A comprehensive timesheet system supports all of these requirements by maintaining complete, auditable records of who worked, where, when, and for how long. This data is invaluable during regulatory inspections and licence renewals.

## Summary

For security companies, timesheet management is not a back-office function — it is a core operational capability that directly affects client confidence, regulatory compliance, and commercial performance. GPS-verified attendance, checkpoint-based patrol tracking, real-time monitoring, and transparent client reporting form the foundation of a well-managed security operation.

The investment in the right system pays returns in every dimension: fewer client disputes, stronger compliance, more accurate payroll, and a workforce that knows its presence and performance are being measured objectively.
