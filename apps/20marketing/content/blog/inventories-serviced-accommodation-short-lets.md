Serviced accommodation and short-term lets are a different world from traditional assured shorthold tenancies. Guests stay for days or weeks rather than months or years. Turnover is constant. Properties are furnished to a higher standard, stocked with consumables, and expected to be guest-ready at all times. The inventory process needs to reflect this.

A standard inventory report designed for a twelve-month tenancy does not translate well to a property that might see fifty different guests in a year. The pace is faster, the wear patterns are different, and the risks are concentrated around contents, cleanliness, and rapid turnaround rather than long-term structural damage. This guide covers how to adapt your inventory approach for the specific demands of serviced accommodation and short lets.

## Why short lets need inventories at all

Some operators skip inventories entirely, reasoning that the risk per guest is low and the turnaround is too fast to justify the effort. This is understandable but misguided for several reasons.

### Cumulative damage is hard to attribute

When a property sees dozens of guests per year, damage accumulates gradually. A scratch on a table, a chip on a mug, a stain on the sofa. Without a baseline record, there is no way to identify when the damage occurred or who was responsible. Over time, the property degrades and the operator absorbs the cost because there is no evidence to support a claim against any individual guest.

### Platform dispute resolution requires evidence

Most short-let platforms — Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo — have their own dispute resolution processes. If a guest damages something and you want to claim against their deposit or the platform's damage protection, you need evidence. That means a documented, dated record of the property's condition before their stay. An inventory is that record.

### Insurance claims need documentation

If a guest causes significant damage — a broken window, a flooded bathroom, a stained mattress — your insurance provider will ask for evidence of the pre-damage condition. Without it, claims are harder to process and more likely to be reduced or rejected.

### It protects your reviews

Guest reviews are the lifeblood of short-let operations. If a guest arrives and finds damage left by a previous guest — a broken appliance, a stained carpet, a missing item — they will mention it in their review. Regular inventory checks catch these issues before the next guest arrives, protecting your rating and your revenue.

## How short-let inventories differ from standard ones

A traditional inventory report is created at the start of a tenancy and compared against a check-out report at the end. For a twelve-month tenancy, that is two reports per year. For a short let, the same property might need some form of inventory check after every guest — or at least at regular intervals.

This changes the process in several important ways.

### Speed is essential

You do not have an hour to walk through the property with a clipboard. Short-let inventory checks need to be fast — ideally fifteen to twenty minutes — because they happen between guests, often on the same day as a changeover. The check needs to be thorough enough to catch problems but quick enough to fit into a tight turnaround schedule.

### Focus shifts to contents and consumables

In a standard tenancy, the inventory focuses heavily on the condition of walls, floors, ceilings, and fixtures. These are still relevant for short lets, but the higher-risk areas are contents: crockery, cutlery, glassware, linen, towels, appliances, remote controls, keys, and welcome packs. These are the items most likely to go missing or get damaged between guests.

### Cleanliness is part of the inventory

For a standard tenancy, cleanliness at check-in sets the expectation for check-out. For a short let, cleanliness is a guest experience issue that needs checking at every changeover. Many operators combine their inventory check with a cleaning inspection, using the same walkthrough to confirm both the condition and the cleanliness of the property.

### Photographic evidence carries more weight

Because individual guest stays are short, written descriptions of minor damage can be hard to attribute to a specific guest. Time-stamped photographs taken immediately before and after each stay provide much stronger evidence for platform disputes or insurance claims.

## Building a short-let inventory template

The most practical approach for high-turnover properties is a standardised template that covers every room and every key item, designed to be completed quickly and consistently.

### What to include

**Kitchen.** List every item: number of plates, bowls, mugs, glasses, pans, utensils, and small appliances. Note the condition of each. Check that the oven, hob, fridge, dishwasher, and microwave are working. Confirm consumables (washing-up liquid, bin bags, coffee, tea) are stocked.

**Living area.** Check the sofa, cushions, throws, and any decorative items. Test the television and remote control. Confirm Wi-Fi equipment is present and working. Note the condition of any artwork, rugs, or ornaments.

**Bedrooms.** Check the mattress, bed frame, headboard, and bedside tables. Count pillows and confirm linen is fresh. Check wardrobes and drawers for items left by previous guests. Note the condition of curtains or blinds.

**Bathrooms.** Check for limescale, mould, or damage to tiles, grouting, and sealant. Confirm towels are present and fresh. Check that the shower, taps, and toilet flush are working. Stock toiletries if applicable.

**General.** Test all light switches and bulbs. Check smoke alarms and carbon monoxide detectors. Confirm keys and access devices are accounted for. Note any external areas (balcony, patio, parking) and their condition.

### Keep it checklist-based

For speed, the template should be checklist-based rather than free-form. Each item gets a condition tick (matching your standard rating scale), a cleanliness tick, and a space for notes only when something is wrong. This lets a trained operative complete the check in fifteen minutes while still producing a defensible record.

## Scheduling inventory checks

Not every guest changeover needs a full inventory. The right frequency depends on your property type, guest profile, and risk tolerance.

### Full inventory: quarterly or after flagged stays

A comprehensive inventory — the kind you would do at the start of a standard tenancy — should be done quarterly at minimum. This catches gradual degradation that individual changeover checks might miss: worn carpets, fading paint, ageing appliances, or furniture that has shifted from "good" to "fair" condition.

You should also trigger a full inventory after any stay that raises concerns: a damage report from the cleaning team, a guest complaint, or a significantly longer booking (a month or more).

### Changeover check: after every guest

A lighter changeover check should happen after every guest. This is the fast, checklist-based walkthrough described above. Its purpose is to confirm the property is guest-ready and to catch any damage or missing items before the next arrival.

The changeover check does not replace the full inventory — it complements it. Think of it as a quick health check versus an annual physical.

### Seasonal deep check

Properties used for holiday lets often have peak and off-peak seasons. Schedule a thorough inventory at the start and end of peak season. This is when wear is heaviest and when the financial stakes of maintaining your listing quality are highest.

## Handling damage between guests

When a changeover check reveals damage, you need to act quickly. The window for claiming against a guest through a platform's resolution centre is typically 14 days after checkout, and it shrinks to 72 hours on some platforms.

### Document immediately

Photograph the damage with a time-stamped image. Note what was damaged, the estimated cost of repair or replacement, and the condition of the same item before the guest's stay (referencing your inventory or previous changeover check).

### Compare against the pre-stay record

Pull up the inventory or changeover check from before the guest's stay. If the item was in good condition before and is now damaged, you have a clear before-and-after comparison. This is the core evidence you need for a claim.

### File the claim promptly

Submit your claim through the platform's resolution centre or directly with the guest. Include the before-and-after photographs, the inventory records, and the cost of repair or replacement. The stronger your documentation, the faster and more favourably the claim is likely to be resolved.

### Update the inventory

After the damage is repaired or the item is replaced, update your inventory to reflect the new condition. This ensures the next changeover check starts from an accurate baseline.

## Managing contents and consumables

Contents management is one of the biggest operational headaches in serviced accommodation. Items go missing, break, or wear out faster than in a standard let, and the cost of replacing them adds up.

### Maintain a master contents list

Keep a master list of every item in the property, with quantities and replacement costs. This serves as both your inventory baseline and your restocking guide. When an item is damaged or missing, the replacement cost is already documented — you do not have to research it during a dispute.

### Standardise across properties

If you operate multiple short-let units, standardise your contents as much as possible. Using the same crockery, linen, and furnishings across all properties means you can buy replacements in bulk, swap items between units, and train cleaning teams on a single standard.

### Track consumable costs

Consumables — toiletries, coffee, tea, cleaning products — are a recurring cost that is easy to underestimate. Track what you spend per property per month. This data helps with pricing, budgeting, and identifying properties where consumption is unusually high (which might indicate a problem with guest behaviour or cleaning efficiency).

## Technology and workflow

The biggest challenge with short-let inventories is not the content of the check — it is fitting it into a fast-paced operational workflow without it becoming a bottleneck.

Mobile-friendly inventory tools are essential. A clerk or cleaning operative should be able to open a template on their phone, walk through the property, tap condition ratings, snap photographs, and submit the report in under twenty minutes. If the process requires a laptop, printed forms, or manual photo uploads, it will not survive contact with a busy changeover schedule.

Look for software that supports reusable templates, automatic date and time stamping on photographs, and easy comparison between consecutive reports. The ability to flag issues in real time — so that a property manager is alerted to damage before the next guest arrives — is particularly valuable for high-turnover operations.

## Making it sustainable

The operators who succeed with short-let inventories are the ones who make the process lightweight and habitual. If it feels like a burden, it will be skipped. If it is built into the changeover workflow — as automatic as changing the linen and restocking the coffee — it becomes a natural part of operations.

Start with a simple template. Train your team to complete it consistently. Use the data to protect your property, support your claims, and maintain the guest experience that keeps your reviews high and your occupancy strong. The upfront effort is modest. The cost of skipping it — in unrecoverable damage, lost disputes, and declining reviews — is not.
