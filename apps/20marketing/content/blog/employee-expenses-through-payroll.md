Employee expenses are a fact of life for most businesses. Whether it is travel costs, client meals, equipment purchases, or conference attendance, your employees will spend money on behalf of the business and expect to be reimbursed. The question is how to handle that reimbursement efficiently, compliantly, and without creating a payroll headache.

This guide covers how to process employee expenses through your payroll system and the tax implications you need to be aware of.

## Expenses through payroll versus separate reimbursement

There are two main approaches to reimbursing employee expenses:

**Through payroll**: The reimbursement is included as a line item in the employee's regular pay run. It appears on their payslip alongside their salary and deductions.

**Separate payment**: The reimbursement is paid outside the payroll cycle, often through a direct bank transfer or expense management system.

Both approaches are legitimate, but each has different administrative and tax implications.

Processing through payroll is simpler in some ways — it uses your existing payment infrastructure and creates a clear record in one system. However, you need to be careful about the tax treatment. Legitimate business expenses that are reimbursed at cost should not be subject to income tax or social contributions, but this depends on proper categorisation and record keeping.

Separate payment gives more flexibility and faster reimbursement but requires a separate payment process and reconciliation with your accounts.

## Setting up an expense policy

Before you can process expenses efficiently, you need a clear policy. Without one, you will spend more time adjudicating individual claims than processing them.

A good expense policy covers:

- **What is claimable**: List the categories of expense the business will reimburse. Common categories include travel, accommodation, meals, equipment, subscriptions, and professional development.
- **Spending limits**: Set maximum amounts for each category. For example, a daily meal allowance, a maximum hotel rate, or a per-transaction limit for equipment.
- **Approval process**: Who approves expenses? For most small businesses, the line manager or business owner reviews and approves claims before payment.
- **Submission deadlines**: How quickly must expenses be submitted? A common rule is within 30 days of the expenditure.
- **Receipt requirements**: What evidence is required? For most tax purposes, you need a receipt or invoice showing the date, vendor, amount, and nature of the purchase.
- **Non-claimable items**: Explicitly state what the business will not reimburse. This prevents awkward conversations later.

Distribute the policy to all employees and make it easily accessible. Review it annually to ensure the limits and categories remain appropriate.

## Tax treatment of expenses

The tax treatment of expense reimbursements depends on whether the expense is a legitimate business cost. The general principle is:

- **Wholly and exclusively for business purposes**: Reimbursements for genuine business expenses are not taxable. The employee should not pay income tax or social contributions on them.
- **Personal benefit or mixed use**: If an expense has a personal element (for example, a meal during a non-business trip), the reimbursement may be taxable as additional income.

To keep expense reimbursements tax-free, you need:

1. A clear business purpose for each expense
2. Proper documentation (receipts, invoices)
3. Approval from an appropriate person
4. Accurate categorisation in your payroll and accounts

If you include expense reimbursements in payroll without proper categorisation, your payroll software may treat them as taxable income by default. Make sure non-taxable reimbursements are coded correctly so they are not subject to deductions.

## Mileage and travel expenses

Business mileage is one of the most common expense categories and has specific rules in most jurisdictions.

Many countries publish approved mileage rates that employers can use to reimburse employees for business travel in their own vehicles. If you reimburse at or below the approved rate, the payment is not taxable. If you pay above the approved rate, the excess is taxable as income.

To claim mileage:
- The employee should record the date, destination, purpose of the journey, and miles driven
- Commuting (home to normal workplace) is not a business journey and is not reimbursable
- Journeys to a temporary workplace, client site, or meeting location are business journeys

Some employers provide a flat monthly car allowance instead of reimbursing actual mileage. This is simpler to administer but is generally taxable as income, which means the employee pays tax on it and you pay employer contributions.

## Flat-rate allowances

In some jurisdictions, employers can pay flat-rate allowances for certain expenses without requiring receipts. These approved amounts cover common costs like meals during business travel, overnight stays, or laundry while working away from home.

Using approved flat-rate allowances simplifies administration because you do not need individual receipts. However, you must ensure the allowance genuinely relates to a business purpose — paying a daily meal allowance to an employee who works from their normal office does not qualify.

Check the approved rates published by your tax authority. Paying within these limits keeps the payment non-taxable.

## Processing expenses in your payroll run

When processing expenses through payroll, follow these steps:

### 1. Collect and approve claims
Gather all expense claims that need to be reimbursed in the current pay period. Each claim should include receipts, a description of the business purpose, and manager approval.

### 2. Categorise correctly
Separate taxable and non-taxable expenses:
- Non-taxable: Legitimate business expenses reimbursed at cost or within approved rates
- Taxable: Amounts above approved rates, personal elements, or benefits in kind

### 3. Enter into payroll
Add the reimbursement as a separate line item, coded appropriately:
- Non-taxable reimbursements should be excluded from tax and social contribution calculations
- Taxable elements should be included in gross pay

### 4. Include on payslip
The reimbursement should appear on the employee's payslip so there is a clear record. Even non-taxable reimbursements should be visible for transparency.

### 5. Process payment
The reimbursement is included in the employee's net pay for the period.

### 6. Update your accounts
Expense reimbursements are a business cost, not a salary expense. Make sure they are recorded in the correct expense category in your accounting system. When your [payroll](/payroll) and accounting are integrated, this can happen automatically.

## Record keeping

Keep all expense records for the required retention period (typically three to seven years, depending on your jurisdiction). Records should include:

- The original receipt or invoice
- A description of the business purpose
- The approval record
- The payroll record showing the reimbursement

Good record keeping protects you during tax audits. If you cannot demonstrate that an expense reimbursement was for a genuine business purpose, the tax authority may reclassify it as taxable income — with penalties and interest.

Digital expense management reduces the burden of paper receipts. Many businesses now have employees photograph receipts and submit them through an app or online system, with the images stored centrally.

## Common mistakes

### Not separating taxable and non-taxable amounts
Lumping all expenses into a single payroll line item makes it difficult to demonstrate the tax treatment. Keep taxable and non-taxable amounts separate.

### Missing receipts
A claim without a receipt is hard to justify to a tax authority. Enforce your receipt policy consistently.

### Paying above approved rates without taxing the excess
If you reimburse mileage above the approved rate, the excess must be treated as taxable income. This is a common audit finding.

### Slow reimbursement
Employees who wait months for reimbursement become frustrated and may stop incurring expenses on behalf of the business — or stop submitting claims altogether, which creates accounting problems.

### No policy
Without a clear policy, every expense claim becomes a negotiation. Invest the time to write a policy upfront and stick to it.
