The property industry uses several types of condition reports, and the terminology can be confusing. Inventories, schedules of condition, and dilapidations reports are sometimes used interchangeably in conversation, but they are distinct documents that serve different purposes, in different contexts, for different audiences.

Understanding the differences matters. Using the wrong type of report — or conflating two different ones — can leave you with a document that does not serve its intended purpose. This guide explains what each one is, when it is used, and how they relate to each other.

## The property inventory

### What it is

A property inventory is a detailed record of a residential rental property's condition, contents, and fixtures at a specific point in time. It is the standard document used in the private rental sector to establish the baseline condition of a property at the start of a tenancy.

### When it is used

- At the beginning of a residential tenancy (check-in inventory)
- At the end of a residential tenancy (check-out inventory)
- During a tenancy (mid-tenancy inspection)

### What it covers

- Condition of every room — walls, floors, ceilings, doors, windows
- All fixtures and fittings — light switches, handles, radiators, curtain rails
- All contents and furniture (for furnished properties) — itemised with condition
- Appliances — make, model, working condition
- Cleanliness throughout
- Meter readings
- Key schedule
- Photographs of everything

### Who uses it

- Private landlords
- Letting agents
- Tenants
- Deposit protection schemes (as evidence in disputes)

### Key characteristics

The inventory focuses on both **condition and contents**. For furnished properties, it serves as an asset register as much as a condition report. It is designed to be practical and accessible — the audience includes landlords, tenants, and adjudicators, none of whom are necessarily property professionals.

The language is descriptive and specific but does not require technical building knowledge. "Light scuff mark on skirting board near door" is the level of detail expected.

## The schedule of condition

### What it is

A schedule of condition is a formal record of a property's physical condition at a specific point in time. It is typically more focused on the building fabric — walls, floors, structural elements, finishes — and less concerned with contents or furnishings.

### When it is used

- At the start of a commercial lease, to protect the tenant from being held responsible for pre-existing defects at lease end
- Before building works commence, to record the condition of adjacent properties (known as a "party wall" schedule of condition)
- At the start of some residential tenancies, particularly for unfurnished properties where a full inventory is not required

### What it covers

- Building fabric — walls, floors, ceilings, structural elements
- Finishes — paintwork, plasterwork, tiling
- Windows and doors — frames, glass, hardware
- External elements — brickwork, rendering, roofing (for commercial leases)
- Mechanical and electrical installations (in commercial contexts)
- Photographs supporting each observation

### Who uses it

- Commercial tenants and landlords
- Surveyors
- Solicitors (attached to leases)
- Party wall surveyors

### Key characteristics

A schedule of condition is typically **appended to a lease** as a formal record. It serves a legal function: it limits the tenant's repairing obligation at the end of the lease. If the schedule shows that the walls were cracked at the start, the tenant cannot be required to return them in perfect condition.

The language tends to be more technical than a residential inventory. "Hairline cracking to plaster finish on north elevation at first floor level, approximately 1.5m from junction with return wall" reflects the surveyor's professional terminology.

A schedule of condition does **not** typically include contents, furnishings, or cleanliness assessments. It is concerned with the building, not what is in it.

## The dilapidations report

### What it is

A dilapidations report (or dilapidations assessment) is a document that identifies breaches of a tenant's repairing and maintenance obligations under a lease. It lists the works that the tenant is required to carry out (or pay for) to return the property to the condition required by the lease.

### When it is used

- Towards the end of a commercial lease (interim or terminal dilapidations)
- After a commercial tenant has vacated (final dilapidations)
- Sometimes during a lease if the landlord believes the tenant is not maintaining the property adequately

### What it covers

- Specific lease clauses that have been breached
- The condition defects that constitute the breach
- The remedial works required to address each defect
- The estimated cost of those works
- Photographs of the defects

### Who uses it

- Commercial landlords (typically prepared by their surveyor)
- Commercial tenants (who may commission a response or counter-schedule)
- Solicitors and barristers (in dilapidations negotiations or litigation)
- Building surveyors (who prepare and negotiate them)

### Key characteristics

A dilapidations report is fundamentally **legal and financial** in nature. It is not simply a condition report — it is a claim. Each item in a dilapidations schedule links a physical defect to a specific lease obligation and a proposed remedy with a cost.

The language is technical and legalistic. "The tenant has failed to comply with clause 4.2 of the lease in that the internal decorations have not been maintained in good and tenantable repair. Remedial works required: hack off defective plaster to reveal, prepare and re-plaster to match, and redecorate. Estimated cost: £2,400."

Dilapidations reports can run to hundreds of items and tens of thousands in claimed costs. They are often the starting point for negotiations between landlord and tenant, and they sometimes lead to litigation.

## How they compare

| Feature | Inventory | Schedule of Condition | Dilapidations Report |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Sector** | Residential rental | Commercial (and some residential) | Commercial |
| **Purpose** | Record condition and contents at a point in time | Record building condition to limit tenant liability | Identify breaches of lease obligations and claim costs |
| **Contents included?** | Yes (furnished properties) | No | No |
| **Cleanliness assessed?** | Yes | No | Sometimes (if lease requires) |
| **Legal attachment** | Standalone document | Usually appended to lease | Standalone claim document |
| **Prepared by** | Inventory clerk, agent, or landlord | Building surveyor | Building surveyor |
| **Audience** | Landlord, tenant, adjudicator | Solicitors, surveyors, landlord, tenant | Solicitors, surveyors, landlord, tenant |
| **Language** | Descriptive, accessible | Technical, professional | Technical, legal, financial |
| **Cost implications** | Informs deposit deductions | Limits repairing obligation | Claims specific remedial costs |

## Where they overlap

There is genuine overlap between these documents, which is why they are sometimes confused.

An **inventory** and a **schedule of condition** both record a property's condition at a specific point in time. The difference is scope (inventories include contents and cleanliness; schedules focus on building fabric) and context (inventories are for residential tenancies; schedules are more common in commercial).

A **schedule of condition** and a **dilapidations report** are connected but serve opposite purposes. The schedule records what the property looked like at the start of the lease. The dilapidations report identifies how it has deteriorated beyond what the lease allows. The schedule limits the dilapidations claim.

An **inventory** and a **dilapidations report** serve analogous functions in their respective sectors. Both compare the property's condition at the end of a period against a baseline and identify what the occupier should pay for. The inventory informs deposit deductions in residential lettings. The dilapidations report informs a formal claim in commercial lettings.

## Which do you need?

**If you are a residential landlord or letting agent**, you need a property inventory. This is the standard document for the private rental sector, and it is what deposit protection schemes expect.

**If you are a commercial tenant taking a new lease**, you should commission a schedule of condition from a building surveyor and have it appended to the lease. This limits your exposure to dilapidations claims at lease end.

**If you are a commercial landlord approaching lease end**, you may need a dilapidations report prepared by your surveyor to identify the works the tenant is responsible for.

**If you are an inventory clerk**, your work is in the residential sector, producing inventories, check-in and check-out reports, and mid-tenancy inspections. Schedules of condition and dilapidations reports are the domain of building surveyors.

## Using the right tool for the job

Whatever type of report you need, the principle is the same: thorough, accurate, photographic documentation of a property's condition at a specific point in time. The technology that supports this work — digital reporting tools, timestamped photography, cloud storage — applies across all three document types.
