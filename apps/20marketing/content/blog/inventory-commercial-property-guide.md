# Property Inventories for Commercial Premises: What You Need to Know

When people think of property inventories, residential lettings usually come to mind — cataloguing the condition of a flat before a tenant moves in. But commercial property inventories are equally important and significantly more complex. A commercial lease might cover an office, retail unit, warehouse, restaurant, or industrial space, each with unique fixtures, fittings, and condition considerations that residential inventories never encounter.

Whether you are a landlord, tenant, or inventory professional, understanding the specific requirements of commercial inventories protects your interests and prevents costly disputes.

## Why Commercial Inventories Matter

Commercial leases typically involve longer terms, higher rents, and more significant financial stakes than residential agreements. A dilapidations claim at the end of a commercial lease can easily run into tens of thousands — or hundreds of thousands for larger premises. The inventory created at lease commencement is the primary evidence for resolving these disputes.

Without a thorough inventory, the landlord may struggle to prove that damage occurred during the tenancy. Equally, the tenant may be unable to demonstrate that pre-existing issues were present before they took occupation. The result is expensive legal disputes where both parties lose time and money.

A comprehensive commercial inventory eliminates this uncertainty. It creates a factual, dated, and evidenced record of the property's condition that both parties can rely on.

## How Commercial Inventories Differ From Residential

While the principle is the same — documenting the condition of a property at a point in time — commercial inventories differ in several important ways.

**Scale and complexity:** A commercial inventory might cover 50,000 square feet of office space across multiple floors, or a restaurant with specialist kitchen equipment, extraction systems, and refrigeration units. The volume of items and the technical knowledge required are significantly greater.

**Building systems:** Commercial premises have mechanical and electrical installations that residential properties typically do not — HVAC systems, fire suppression systems, emergency lighting, commercial lifts, building management systems, and security installations. These all need documenting.

**Fit-out and alterations:** Commercial tenants often carry out significant fit-out works — partition walls, raised floors, suspended ceilings, specialist lighting, data cabling. The inventory must distinguish between the landlord's original building and the tenant's alterations.

**Compliance and certification:** Commercial properties have compliance requirements around fire safety, asbestos, electrical testing, and energy performance. The inventory should note the status of required certifications.

**External areas:** Car parks, loading bays, signage, landscaping, boundary fences, and shared areas all fall within the scope of a commercial inventory.

## What to Include in a Commercial Inventory

A comprehensive commercial inventory should cover the following areas.

### Building Exterior

Document the condition of the building fabric — walls, roof, windows, doors, and drainage. Note any existing damage, weathering, or repair needs. Photograph the frontage, any signage, car parking areas, and external landscaping.

For retail premises, pay particular attention to the shopfront, signage, shutters, and any external display areas. These are high-visibility elements that are often the subject of dilapidations claims.

### Building Interior — Shell and Core

Record the condition of floors, walls, ceilings, doors, and windows throughout the premises. Note the type and condition of floor coverings, wall finishes, and ceiling systems. Document any existing damage — cracks, staining, wear marks, or missing tiles.

For office spaces, record the condition of raised access floors, suspended ceilings, and any integral blinds or curtains. For retail, note the condition of display fixtures, counter areas, and changing rooms. For industrial units, document floor loading, column positions, and clear heights.

### Mechanical and Electrical Systems

This is where commercial inventories become truly specialist. Document:

- **HVAC systems:** Type, age, condition, and service history of heating, ventilation, and air conditioning equipment
- **Electrical installations:** Distribution boards, lighting systems (including emergency lighting), power outlets, and any specialist electrical provisions
- **Plumbing:** Incoming water supply, drainage, sanitary fittings, and any specialist plumbing (commercial kitchens, laboratories)
- **Fire safety:** Fire alarm systems, sprinklers, fire extinguishers, fire doors, and escape route signage
- **Lifts and escalators:** Service records, current certification, and visible condition
- **Security systems:** CCTV, intruder alarms, access control systems

Where possible, note the last service date and any certification status. Photographs of equipment nameplates, serial numbers, and service stickers provide valuable reference information.

### Tenant Alterations

If the tenant has carried out fit-out works, the inventory should clearly distinguish between landlord and tenant items. This distinction is critical at lease end, when the tenant may be required to remove their alterations and reinstate the property to its original condition.

Document all tenant installations — partition walls, specialist flooring, IT infrastructure, kitchen equipment, shop fittings — and note their condition.

### Fixtures, Fittings, and Equipment

List all fixtures and fittings included in the lease. For furnished or equipped premises (such as restaurants or serviced offices), this list can be extensive. Record make, model, serial number, and condition for significant items.

## Photography and Evidence

Commercial inventories demand extensive photographic evidence. For a large premises, you might take hundreds or even thousands of photographs. Each image should be date-stamped and clearly labelled with the location and subject.

Photograph:

- Every room or area from multiple angles
- Any existing damage or defects in close-up
- Equipment nameplates and serial numbers
- Service stickers and certification labels
- Meter readings (gas, electricity, water)
- External areas including boundaries and car parks

Video walkthroughs can supplement still photography, providing context that individual photos cannot capture.

## Schedule of Condition vs Full Inventory

In commercial property, the term "schedule of condition" is commonly used alongside or instead of "inventory." They serve slightly different purposes.

A **schedule of condition** is typically prepared when a tenant takes a lease of premises that are not in perfect condition. It records the existing state of the property and is appended to the lease. The tenant's repairing obligation is then limited to maintaining the property in the condition shown in the schedule — they are not required to improve it.

A **full inventory** is a more comprehensive document that records all fixtures, fittings, and equipment in addition to the condition of the property.

In practice, many commercial inventories combine both elements — documenting condition and listing items in a single report.

## Timing and Process

**Pre-lease inventory:** Conduct the inventory before the tenant takes occupation. Both parties should have the opportunity to review and agree the inventory within a specified period (typically 7-14 days). Any disagreements should be noted and resolved.

**Mid-term inspections:** Depending on the lease terms, periodic inspections may be required or advisable. These are particularly important for long leases (10+ years) where conditions can change significantly.

**End-of-lease check-out:** A full check-out report comparing the current condition to the original inventory identifies any dilapidations. This forms the basis for any end-of-lease claims.

## Professional Standards

Commercial inventory work requires more specialist knowledge than residential. Consider using an inventory professional or surveyor who has:

- Experience with commercial premises of similar type and size
- Understanding of building systems and services
- Knowledge of commercial lease terms and dilapidations principles
- Professional indemnity insurance appropriate to the values involved

For large or complex premises, a building surveyor rather than a residential inventory clerk may be more appropriate. The fees are higher, but the protection they provide is proportionate to the financial stakes.

## Protecting Your Position

For landlords, a comprehensive inventory is your first line of defence against leaving money on the table at lease end. It establishes the benchmark against which dilapidations are measured.

For tenants, reviewing and agreeing the inventory at lease commencement protects you from claims for pre-existing damage. If you disagree with any aspect of the inventory, raise it in writing within the agreed review period.

For both parties, keeping the inventory updated throughout the lease term — particularly after any alterations or significant events — maintains its value as a living document.

Commercial property inventories require more time, expertise, and investment than residential ones. But given the financial values involved, they represent one of the best-value risk management tools available to landlords and tenants alike.
