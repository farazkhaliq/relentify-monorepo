When a deposit dispute reaches adjudication, the outcome almost always comes down to evidence. And the strongest form of evidence, the one that adjudicators rely on most, is photographs.

A well-worded inventory report is valuable. But a well-worded report supported by clear, dated, comprehensive photographs is significantly more powerful. Photos do not lie, do not forget, and do not rely on interpretation. They show exactly what the property looked like at a specific moment in time.

This guide covers why photo evidence matters, how to capture it properly, and how to organise it so that it serves its purpose when you need it most.

## Why photographs matter more than words

Written descriptions are subjective. One person's "good condition" is another person's "needs attention." When an adjudicator reads "kitchen — clean" in a check-in report and "kitchen — not clean" in a check-out report, they have very little to work with. Clean compared to what? Not clean in what way?

Photographs remove that ambiguity. A photo of a clean oven at check-in and a photo of a grease-covered oven at check-out tells the story without any need for interpretation.

Deposit protection services consistently report that cases with clear photographic evidence are resolved faster and with clearer outcomes than those without. When the evidence speaks for itself, there is less room for disagreement.

## What to photograph at check-in

The check-in is where you build your evidence base. Every photograph taken at this stage becomes the "before" image that check-out photos will be compared against. Be thorough.

### Room overview shots

Start each room with a wide-angle shot from the doorway. This establishes the general layout and condition. Then move around the room, capturing each wall, the floor, the ceiling, and any notable features.

### Close-ups of existing damage

Any pre-existing damage must be photographed in detail. This includes:

- Marks, scuffs, or holes in walls
- Stains on carpets or flooring
- Scratches on doors, frames, or woodwork
- Chips or cracks in tiles
- Damage to furniture (for furnished properties)
- Wear on worktops, sinks, and bathroom fixtures

If you do not photograph existing damage at check-in, you have no way to prove it was there before the tenant moved in.

### Appliance condition

Photograph each appliance — front, interior (if applicable), and any visible damage or wear. Open the oven door and photograph inside. Show the condition of the fridge shelves. Document the washing machine drum and detergent drawer.

### Meters

Photograph every meter showing the current reading clearly. Gas, electricity, and water meters should all be captured. Make sure the reading is legible in the image.

### Keys

Photograph the keys laid out with labels identifying what each one is for. This provides a visual record that complements the written key schedule.

### External areas

Gardens, paths, driveways, fences, and outbuildings all need photographic coverage. Capture the overall condition as well as any specific features or issues.

## What to photograph at check-out

At check-out, the goal is to mirror the check-in photos as closely as possible. This makes comparison straightforward.

### Match the check-in angles

Try to photograph each room from the same position and angle as the check-in shots. This allows anyone viewing the reports to immediately see what has changed.

### Document changes

If damage has occurred during the tenancy, photograph it from multiple angles. A single photo of a stain on the carpet may not convey its size or severity. Take a wide shot to show its location in the room and a close-up to show the detail.

### Photograph cleanliness issues

If the property has not been cleaned to the check-in standard, capture the specific areas that fall short. Inside the oven, behind the toilet, kitchen cupboard interiors, window tracks — these are the areas where cleaning issues are most commonly found and most commonly disputed.

### Compare side by side

The most effective evidence is a direct comparison: the check-in photo next to the check-out photo of the same area. Modern inventory software can generate this comparison automatically, but even manually paired images make a strong case.

## Technical best practices for inventory photos

### Lighting

Good lighting is essential. Natural light is best, but if rooms are dark, turn on all available lights. Avoid using flash if possible — it can wash out details and create misleading shadows. If you must use flash, take a second photo without it for comparison.

### Resolution

Use a camera or device that captures images at a high enough resolution to zoom in on details. Most modern smartphones are more than adequate. Avoid heavily compressed images or screenshots of photos.

### Timestamps

This is critical. Every photo should have a verifiable timestamp that proves when it was taken. There are several ways to achieve this:

- **Device metadata**: Most smartphones embed date, time, and GPS data in photo metadata (EXIF data). This is the simplest approach but can be challenged if the metadata is editable.
- **Inventory software timestamps**: Professional inventory platforms like [Relentify](/inventory) automatically embed timestamps and location data in photos taken within the app, creating a verifiable record.
- **Physical timestamps**: Some clerks include a physical date card or newspaper in the first photo of each session.

### Consistency

Use the same device, the same settings, and the same approach for check-in and check-out. Consistency makes comparison easier and makes the evidence appear more professional and reliable.

### Organisation

Label and organise photos by room. A folder of 200 unnamed photos is difficult for anyone to work through. A structured report with photos grouped by room, each labelled with a description, is far more effective.

## How adjudicators use photographs

When a deposit dispute is submitted to a protection scheme, the adjudicator reviews the evidence from both parties. Here is how photographs typically influence their decision:

### Establishing baseline condition

The adjudicator uses check-in photos to understand what the property looked like at the start. If there are no check-in photos, they have to rely on the written description, which is weaker.

### Identifying changes

Check-out photos are compared against check-in photos to identify changes. The adjudicator is looking for specific, visible differences that go beyond fair wear and tear.

### Assessing severity

Photos help adjudicators assess whether damage is minor (fair wear and tear) or significant (tenant liability). A small scuff on a skirting board looks very different in a photo from a large gouge in a door.

### Weighing credibility

A landlord who submits 200 well-organised, timestamped photos covering every room at check-in and check-out presents a significantly more credible case than one who submits a handful of blurry images taken on an unknown date.

## Common photography mistakes

### Not taking enough photos

The single most common mistake. Clerks and landlords often take a few shots per room and move on. At check-in, you should aim for a minimum of 5 to 10 photos per room — more for kitchens, bathrooms, and furnished rooms. The cost of taking too many photos is zero. The cost of not having the right photo when you need it can be significant.

### Blurry or poorly lit images

A photo that is too dark to see detail or too blurry to read is no better than no photo. Take a moment to check each image after capturing it. If it is not clear, retake it.

### Missing close-ups

Wide-angle room shots are useful for context but do not show detail. A wide shot of a living room will not reveal a cigarette burn on the carpet. Close-ups are essential for documenting specific conditions.

### No photos of items in good condition

It is tempting to only photograph damage and issues. But photos of items in good condition at check-in are equally important. If a worktop is pristine at check-in, that photo proves it. Without it, a tenant could claim the scratch at check-out was already there.

### Inconsistent coverage

Photographing the living room thoroughly but only taking one shot of the bathroom creates gaps in your evidence. Every room needs the same level of coverage.

## Building a photo evidence workflow

For landlords managing a small number of properties, a smartphone and a well-organised folder structure may be sufficient. Label photos clearly, back them up, and keep them accessible for the duration of the tenancy.

For letting agents and inventory clerks handling multiple properties, a dedicated inventory platform is essential. Tools like [Relentify](/inventory) allow you to capture photos within the app, automatically link them to specific rooms and items, embed timestamps, and generate reports that pair check-in and check-out images for easy comparison.

Whatever your approach, the principle is the same: capture everything, label it clearly, and store it securely. When a dispute arises — and eventually, one will — your photographs will be the foundation of your case.

## The return on investment

Professional photography in inventories costs nothing extra if you are already conducting the inspection. It simply requires discipline and thoroughness. The return is significant: fewer disputes, faster resolutions, and more successful claims when deductions are warranted.

A single lost deposit dispute can cost hundreds or even thousands. A comprehensive photographic record, consistently maintained across every tenancy, is the most cost-effective protection available to any landlord or agent.

Take the photos. Take more than you think you need. And keep them safe.
