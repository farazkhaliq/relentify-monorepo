Every letting agent needs inventories. The question is whether to produce them in-house or outsource them to independent inventory clerks. Both approaches have merits, and the right choice depends on your agency's size, capacity, quality standards, and strategic goals.

This guide breaks down the advantages and disadvantages of each approach and helps you decide which is right for your business.

## The case for outsourcing

### Impartiality

Independent inventory clerks are third parties with no stake in the outcome of deposit disputes. Their reports carry more weight with adjudicators because they are perceived as impartial. An in-house inventory prepared by the same agency that manages the property can be challenged as biased, even if it is entirely accurate.

This perceived impartiality is one of the strongest arguments for outsourcing. In a deposit dispute, an independently prepared inventory removes one potential line of attack from the tenant.

### Specialist expertise

Professional inventory clerks do nothing but inventories. They inspect properties every day, know what adjudicators look for, and have refined their processes through hundreds or thousands of inspections. This specialisation typically produces higher-quality reports than a generalist property manager who handles inventories alongside tenant queries, maintenance coordination, and viewings.

### No training or management overhead

When you outsource, you do not need to train staff on inventory processes, manage their quality, or cover for them when they are ill or on leave. The inventory company handles all of that. You simply book the inspection and receive the report.

### Scalability

If your portfolio grows, you can increase your inventory bookings without hiring additional staff. If it contracts, you reduce bookings without the burden of underutilised employees. Outsourcing provides flexibility that in-house staffing does not.

### Cost predictability

Outsourced inventories are a variable cost — you pay per inspection. There are no fixed salary costs, no employer contributions, no equipment purchases, and no software subscriptions to manage. This can be advantageous for agencies with fluctuating workloads.

## The case for keeping inventories in-house

### Control over quality and timing

When you produce inventories in-house, you control every aspect of the process — the template, the standards, the timing, and the delivery. If you need an urgent check-in tomorrow morning, you do not need to check a third party's availability. You assign it to your team member.

This control is valuable when your standards are high and you want to ensure consistency across every property.

### Deeper property knowledge

Your in-house team already knows the properties. They have managed viewings, handled maintenance, and communicated with landlords and tenants. This familiarity can lead to more informed inventories — they know the history of each property and can document it more contextually.

### Revenue retention

If you charge landlords separately for inventories, keeping the work in-house means the revenue stays within your business rather than going to an external provider. For agencies with large portfolios, this can be a meaningful income stream.

### Brand consistency

Your inventory reports carry your agency's branding and reflect your standards. When clients see a report with your logo and format, it reinforces your brand. Outsourced reports carry the inventory company's branding, which may not align with how you want to present your service.

### Integration with other services

In-house inventories can be combined with other tasks. A property manager conducting a check-in can also handle the key handover, meet the tenant, explain the property's features, and deal with any immediate questions. This integration saves time and creates a smoother experience for the tenant.

## Factors to consider when deciding

### Portfolio size

Small agencies (under 50 managed properties) often find it more practical to handle inventories in-house, as the volume does not justify a dedicated relationship with an external provider. Large agencies (200+ properties) benefit more from outsourcing because the volume makes in-house delivery logistically challenging without dedicated inventory staff.

Mid-sized agencies (50 to 200 properties) are in the decision zone. Either approach can work, depending on the other factors below.

### Team capacity

Do you have staff with the time, skill, and inclination to conduct inventories? If your team is already stretched managing properties, adding inventory work creates pressure that can compromise quality. If you have team members who are well-suited to detailed inspection work and have capacity, in-house may be viable.

### Quality requirements

Be honest about the quality your team produces. Look at your most recent in-house inventory reports. Are they detailed enough? Are the photographs comprehensive? Would they stand up in a deposit dispute?

If the quality is inconsistent or below what you would want submitted as evidence, outsourcing to a specialist may deliver better outcomes.

### Cost comparison

Compare the true cost of in-house delivery against outsourcing:

**In-house costs:**
- Staff time (salary, employer costs, proportional to time spent on inventories)
- Training and supervision
- Software subscription
- Equipment (phones, tablets)
- Travel expenses

**Outsourced costs:**
- Per-inspection fee
- Booking and coordination time

For many agencies, the all-in cost of in-house delivery is higher than they expect once staff time is properly accounted for.

### Dispute outcomes

Review your recent deposit dispute outcomes. If you are consistently winning claims with your current approach, the evidence quality is working. If you are losing disputes that should have been won, the inventory quality may be a factor.

## The hybrid approach

Many agencies find that a hybrid approach works best:

- **Outsource check-in and check-out inventories** to specialist clerks for the impartiality and quality benefits
- **Conduct mid-tenancy inspections in-house** since these are less about formal evidence and more about property management

This gives you the best of both worlds: impartial, high-quality check-in and check-out reports for deposit protection, and in-house property visits for ongoing management and relationship building.

## Making outsourcing work

If you decide to outsource, the relationship needs to be managed well:

### Choose the right provider

Look for inventory clerks or companies that:
- Use professional digital inventory software
- Produce photographic, timestamped reports
- Have experience with your type of properties
- Can handle your volume requirements
- Have good reviews and references

### Establish clear standards

Communicate your expectations clearly. Provide sample properties to calibrate the level of detail you expect. Review the first few reports carefully and provide feedback.

### Integrate into your workflow

Set up a smooth booking and delivery process. The inventory provider should fit into your existing tenancy management workflow without creating administrative bottlenecks.

### Review regularly

Periodically review the quality of outsourced reports. Standards can slip over time. Regular quality checks ensure the service continues to meet your requirements.

## Making in-house work

If you keep inventories in-house, invest in doing it properly:

### Professional software

Use a dedicated inventory platform like [Relentify](/inventory) that provides structured templates, integrated photography, automatic timestamps, and professional report generation. Do not rely on generic tools.

### Dedicated training

Train the team members who will conduct inventories. Ensure they understand what adjudicators look for, how to photograph effectively, and how to write precise condition descriptions.

### Quality assurance

Review reports regularly. Spot-check for completeness, accuracy, and photographic quality. Address any shortcomings promptly.

### Consistency

Ensure all team members follow the same process, use the same template, and apply the same standards. Inconsistency between reports undermines their credibility.

## The decision framework

Ask these questions to guide your decision:

1. Do we have team members with capacity and aptitude for detailed inspection work?
2. Is our current in-house quality sufficient for deposit disputes?
3. Can we maintain consistent quality as our portfolio grows?
4. Is impartiality important for our client relationships and dispute outcomes?
5. What is the true cost comparison when staff time is properly accounted for?

If the answers point more toward gaps in capacity, quality, or impartiality, outsourcing is likely the better choice. If your team is capable, has capacity, and delivers consistent quality, in-house may work well.

Either way, the inventory itself must be thorough. The decision about who produces it is secondary to the decision to produce it properly.
