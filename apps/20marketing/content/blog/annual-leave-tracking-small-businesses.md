Annual leave management is one of those tasks that seems simple until it is not. With two or three employees, you can probably track holidays in your head or on a shared calendar. By the time you reach ten or twenty, the complexity multiplies: overlapping requests, part-time entitlement calculations, carried-over days, public holidays, and the inevitable dispute about who booked the Christmas week first.

This guide covers the fundamentals of annual leave tracking for small businesses — from calculating entitlements to choosing the right tools and handling the edge cases that catch most businesses off guard.

## Legal entitlements

Annual leave entitlements vary by jurisdiction, but most countries guarantee a minimum number of paid holiday days per year. Common examples include:

- 28 days (including public holidays) in the UK for full-time workers
- Varies by state and employer in the United States (no federal mandate for private sector)
- 20 days minimum across much of the European Union
- 20 days in Australia for full-time workers
- 4 weeks in New Zealand

These are statutory minimums. Many employers offer more. Your starting point is to understand the legal minimum in your jurisdiction and ensure your policy meets or exceeds it.

### Part-time workers

Part-time workers are typically entitled to a pro-rata share of the full-time entitlement. A worker who works three days per week is entitled to three-fifths of the full-time allowance. The calculation is usually:

**Part-time entitlement = Full-time entitlement x (Part-time hours / Full-time hours)**

For example, if full-time entitlement is 28 days and a worker works 24 hours per week out of a standard 40:

28 x (24/40) = 16.8 days

Whether you round this up to 17 is a policy decision, but you should never round down below the statutory minimum.

### Accrual during the year

Leave typically accrues over the leave year. A worker who starts mid-year is not entitled to the full annual allowance immediately — they accrue a proportional amount based on the time remaining in the leave year.

**Accrued entitlement = Annual entitlement x (Months worked / 12)**

Some businesses front-load the full entitlement at the start of the year, which is simpler to administer but creates a risk if a worker leaves early and has taken more leave than they have earned.

## Building your leave policy

A clear leave policy prevents disputes and ensures everyone understands the rules. Your policy should cover:

### The leave year

Define when your leave year starts and ends. Common options are January to December, April to March (aligned with the tax year in some countries), or tied to each worker's start date. A fixed leave year is easier to administer.

### Requesting leave

Specify how workers should request leave — through a system, by email, or verbally to their manager. Set expectations for how far in advance requests should be made (one week for a single day, one month for a week, etc.) and how quickly the manager will respond.

### Approval criteria

What determines whether a request is approved or denied? Common factors include:

- Whether the team will have adequate coverage during the absence
- Whether the requested dates overlap with busy periods or blackout dates
- Whether another team member has already booked the same dates
- The order in which requests were received (first come, first served)

Be transparent about these criteria. Workers who understand the decision-making process are less likely to feel their requests are handled unfairly.

### Carry-over

Can unused leave be carried into the next year? If so, how much? Many businesses allow a limited carry-over (e.g., five days) with a deadline for using the carried-over days (e.g., by the end of March). Unlimited carry-over can create a liability on the balance sheet and operational disruption when workers take large blocks of leave.

### Public holidays

Clarify whether public holidays are included in the annual leave allowance or are additional. In some jurisdictions, the statutory minimum includes public holidays; in others, they are on top.

## Tracking leave effectively

### Spreadsheets

Many small businesses start with a spreadsheet — a shared Google Sheet or Excel file with a row for each worker and columns for each month. This works for very small teams but breaks down quickly:

- No approval workflow (requests happen verbally or by email)
- No automatic calculation of remaining entitlement
- No visibility into team coverage
- Risk of conflicting edits
- Difficult to audit

### Calendar-based tracking

Shared calendars (Google Calendar, Outlook) provide a visual overview of who is off when. This is useful for coverage planning but does not track entitlements, manage approvals, or calculate remaining balances.

### Dedicated leave management tools

Purpose-built tools handle the entire process: requests, approvals, entitlement calculations, balance tracking, and reporting. Many timesheet systems include leave management as an integrated module, which makes sense — leave and working time are closely related.

When your timesheet system also tracks leave, you get a unified view of each worker's time: days worked, days off, remaining entitlement, and overall attendance patterns. This integration is more valuable than managing the two separately.

## The connection between timesheets and leave

Annual leave and timesheets are two sides of the same coin. Together, they answer the complete question: for any given period, how many days did this worker work, and how many days were they absent?

This connection matters for:

### Payroll accuracy

Leave days may be paid differently from working days (particularly if the worker has variable hours or shift premiums). Your payroll process needs to distinguish between the two.

### Working time calculations

When calculating whether a worker has exceeded their weekly or annual working hours limit, leave days must be excluded. A worker who takes two weeks of leave has fewer available working days, which affects overtime calculations and utilisation metrics.

### Absence patterns

Combining timesheet data with leave records reveals absence patterns — workers who take frequent short-notice leave, teams with consistently high absence rates, or seasonal trends in leave usage. These patterns inform staffing and scheduling decisions.

### Forecasting

Historical leave data helps you forecast when absences will peak (school holidays, festive season, summer) and plan staffing levels accordingly.

## Common pitfalls

### Not tracking at all

Some small businesses operate on an honour system — workers take leave as needed, and nobody tracks the balance. This works until it does not. A worker who believes they have days remaining disagrees with the manager who thinks they have used their allowance. Without records, there is no resolution.

### Inconsistent approval

If leave decisions appear arbitrary — one worker's request is approved while another's is denied without clear reasoning — resentment follows. Apply your approval criteria consistently and communicate the reasons for any refusal.

### Ignoring the carry-over deadline

If your policy allows carry-over with a deadline, enforce it. Workers who accumulate large leave balances are a financial liability and an operational risk — when they finally take the leave, the business loses them for an extended period.

### Forgetting leavers

When a worker leaves the business, they are owed payment for any accrued but unused leave. Conversely, if they have taken more leave than they have earned, the business may be entitled to deduct the excess from their final pay (depending on the contract and local law). Accurate leave records make this calculation straightforward.

### Not accounting for public holidays

For shift workers and those who work non-standard schedules, public holiday entitlements can be complex. A worker who is scheduled to work on a public holiday may be entitled to a substitute day off, enhanced pay, or both. Track public holiday working alongside regular leave to ensure compliance.

## Making it work

Annual leave tracking is not glamorous, but it is one of those foundational processes that prevents a disproportionate amount of friction. The investment is modest — a clear policy, a reliable tracking method, and a consistent approval process — but the return is significant: fewer disputes, better coverage planning, accurate payroll, and a team that feels treated fairly.

Start with a policy. Choose a tool that fits your size and budget. Communicate the rules clearly. And track everything — because the leave dispute you resolve with accurate records is one you never need to escalate to a grievance.
