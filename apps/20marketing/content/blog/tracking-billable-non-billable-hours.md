# Tracking Billable vs Non-Billable Hours: A Guide for Service Businesses

In service businesses — consulting firms, agencies, law practices, accountancy firms, IT services — time is the product. Every hour your team spends on client work generates revenue. Every hour spent on internal administration, training, or business development does not. The ratio between these two categories — your utilisation rate — is one of the most important metrics for service business profitability.

Yet many service businesses track time poorly or not at all, leaving them blind to where their most expensive resource — people — is actually being spent. This guide explains how to track billable and non-billable hours effectively, what the numbers tell you, and how to optimise the balance.

## Defining Billable and Non-Billable

The distinction seems straightforward, but in practice it requires clear definitions.

**Billable hours** are time spent directly on client work that can be charged to the client. This includes project execution, client meetings, research directly related to a client matter, and travel to client sites (if your engagement terms allow billing for travel).

**Non-billable hours** fall into several categories:

- **Business development:** Pitching new clients, writing proposals, networking events
- **Internal administration:** Timesheets, expense reports, internal meetings, email management
- **Professional development:** Training, conferences, certifications, reading
- **Management:** Team supervision, performance reviews, strategic planning
- **Bench time:** Time when staff are available but not assigned to client work

Some firms add a third category — **internally billable** — for work that benefits the firm but is not charged to a client. Building internal tools, developing methodologies, or creating training materials might fall here.

Whatever categories you use, define them clearly and ensure everyone in the organisation understands and applies them consistently.

## Why the Balance Matters

The utilisation rate — billable hours as a percentage of total available hours — directly drives profitability.

Consider a simple example. A consultant costs the firm 60,000 per year in salary and overhead. They work 1,800 available hours per year (after holidays and sick leave). If they bill at 100 per hour:

- At 80% utilisation (1,440 billable hours): revenue of 144,000, gross margin of 84,000
- At 60% utilisation (1,080 billable hours): revenue of 108,000, gross margin of 48,000
- At 40% utilisation (720 billable hours): revenue of 72,000, gross margin of 12,000

The difference between 60% and 80% utilisation — just one extra billable day per week — nearly doubles the gross margin from this individual. Across a team of 10, that is hundreds of thousands in additional profit.

This is why tracking matters. You cannot improve what you do not measure.

## Setting Up Time Tracking

### Choose the Right Tool

Spreadsheets work for solo practitioners but break down quickly in teams. Dedicated [timesheet software](https://relentify.com/features/timesheets) provides the structure, automation, and reporting needed for meaningful analysis.

Look for software that supports:

- Easy time entry (mobile, desktop, timer-based, or manual)
- Project and client coding
- Billable/non-billable categorisation
- Approval workflows
- Reporting and analytics
- Integration with invoicing and accounting

### Establish Time Codes

Create a coding structure that captures where time goes with useful granularity without being so detailed that people dread filling in their timesheets.

A practical structure might include:

**Billable codes:**
- Client project work (by project or client)
- Client meetings
- Client-related travel
- Client-related research

**Non-billable codes:**
- Business development / proposals
- Internal meetings
- Administration
- Training and development
- Management
- Leave (holiday, sick, personal)

Review and refine your codes after the first quarter. If a code is never used, remove it. If people are consistently using "other" or "miscellaneous," you are missing a category.

### Set Expectations

Mandate time tracking for everyone — including partners and senior management. If leadership does not track their time, the message to the team is that it does not matter. People who do not track their own time also cannot credibly ask others to do so.

Set a minimum tracking expectation: all available hours must be accounted for. This does not mean every minute must be a billable client hour — it means every hour must be categorised so you can see the full picture.

Daily tracking is far more accurate than weekly. Remembering what you did on Monday by the time you fill in your timesheet on Friday results in significant inaccuracy. Encourage (or require) daily time entry.

## Analysing the Data

### Utilisation Rate by Person

Calculate each team member's utilisation rate monthly and quarterly. Compare it to targets appropriate for their role:

- **Delivery staff** (consultants, developers, designers): 70-85% target
- **Managers:** 50-65% target (lower due to management responsibilities)
- **Partners/directors:** 30-50% target (lower due to business development and leadership)
- **Support staff:** Typically not measured on utilisation

These benchmarks vary by industry and firm culture. A consulting firm might target 80% for consultants, while a creative agency might target 65% due to the less predictable nature of the work.

### Non-Billable Analysis

Equally important is understanding where non-billable time goes. If administration consistently absorbs 20% of available hours, that is a process problem worth solving. If business development is near zero, your pipeline will eventually dry up.

Healthy non-billable allocation might look like:

- Administration: 5-10%
- Business development: 5-10%
- Training and development: 3-5%
- Management: 5-10% (for those with management responsibilities)
- Bench time: As low as possible

### Project-Level Analysis

Track billable hours by project to understand which clients and projects are profitable and which are consuming more time than budgeted.

A project that was quoted at 100 hours but is consuming 150 has a utilisation problem — too many hours are being spent for the revenue generated. This might indicate scope creep, poor estimation, or inefficient delivery.

Conversely, a project delivered in 80 hours when 100 were quoted suggests either excellent efficiency or over-estimation — both worth understanding.

## Optimising the Balance

### Reduce Administrative Burden

The easiest win is reducing time spent on low-value administration. Automate timesheet reminders, expense processing, and report generation. Streamline internal meetings. Eliminate unnecessary approval steps.

Every hour freed from administration is an hour available for billable work or meaningful non-billable activity like business development.

### Address Bench Time

Bench time — staff available but not assigned to client work — is the most expensive form of non-billable time. It means you are paying people to not generate revenue.

Some bench time is unavoidable and even desirable (it allows for training and business development). But sustained high bench time signals a problem: either insufficient client demand, poor resource planning, or a mismatch between staff capabilities and market needs.

Track bench time by person and by team. If certain individuals are consistently underutilised, explore whether they need additional skills, whether you need more sales effort in their area, or whether the team is overstaffed.

### Invest in Business Development

Paradoxically, some firms under-invest in non-billable business development in pursuit of short-term utilisation targets. This creates a dangerous cycle: high utilisation now but a dry pipeline later.

Business development time is an investment in future revenue. Track it as a separate category and ensure it receives appropriate attention, particularly from partners and senior staff.

### Review Pricing

If your utilisation is high but profitability is low, the problem might not be time management — it might be pricing. Review your bill rates against market benchmarks and cost structures. An increase of 5-10% in bill rates has the same profit impact as several percentage points of utilisation improvement.

## Common Pitfalls

**Micromanagement:** Time tracking should inform business decisions, not become a tool for surveillance. Focus on patterns and trends, not whether someone took 15 minutes longer for lunch on Tuesday.

**Unrealistic targets:** Setting utilisation targets above 85% for delivery staff is counterproductive. People need time for training, team coordination, and the occasional mental break. Burnout reduces quality and increases turnover.

**Gaming the system:** If people feel pressured to hit targets, they may categorise non-billable work as billable or inflate hours on client projects. This corrupts your data and, if clients are billed accordingly, damages trust. Create a culture where honest time reporting is valued.

**Ignoring the data:** Collecting time data and not analysing it is worse than not collecting it at all. It imposes a burden on the team with no return. Commit to regular review and action.

## Making Time Tracking Sustainable

The most sophisticated time tracking system fails if people do not use it consistently. Make it easy, make it meaningful, and make it consequential.

**Easy:** Minimise the number of clicks to log time. Mobile access, timer functions, and favourites lists all reduce friction.

**Meaningful:** Share utilisation reports with the team. When people understand how their time tracking connects to business performance, compliance improves.

**Consequential:** Include utilisation metrics in performance reviews and compensation discussions. Not as the only measure — quality and client satisfaction matter too — but as one factor in overall performance.

Billable versus non-billable tracking is not just an accounting exercise. It is a lens through which you understand how your most valuable resource — your people's time — is being deployed. The insight it provides drives better pricing, better resource allocation, and ultimately better profitability.
