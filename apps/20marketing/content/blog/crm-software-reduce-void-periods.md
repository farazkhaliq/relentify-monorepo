Void periods are the silent revenue killer in any letting agency. Every day a property sits empty, the landlord loses rental income, and your agency loses management fees. Over a portfolio of hundreds of properties, even small improvements in void period duration add up to significant sums.

Most agencies accept void periods as an inevitable part of the business. Some voids are unavoidable — a tenant gives notice and the timeline is what it is. But many voids are longer than they need to be, not because the market is slow, but because the internal processes that lead to re-letting are disorganised, reactive, and slow.

A CRM can change that by giving you visibility, automation, and a structured approach to managing the transition between tenancies.

## Understanding where time is lost

Before looking at solutions, it helps to understand where time actually disappears during the void cycle. In most agencies, the biggest delays fall into three categories.

First, there is the awareness gap. The agent does not begin marketing the property until after the current tenant has left. If a tenant gives two months' notice, that is two months of marketing time that could have been used to line up the next tenant — but often, the re-letting process does not start until the keys are handed back.

Second, there is the preparation gap. The property needs cleaning, minor repairs, or a fresh coat of paint between tenancies. If this work is not scheduled in advance, it happens after the tenant leaves, adding days or weeks to the void period.

Third, there is the referencing gap. An applicant has been found, but the referencing process drags on because documents are missing, employers are slow to respond, or the agency does not chase things proactively.

Each of these gaps is addressable — and a CRM provides the tools to address them.

## Early warning: anticipating vacancies

The most effective way to reduce void periods is to start the re-letting process before the current tenant has left. This requires advance visibility of upcoming vacancies.

A CRM that tracks tenancy end dates — including any notice periods and break clauses — can automatically flag properties that will become available in the coming weeks and months. Instead of reacting to vacancies after they happen, you can see them coming and begin preparing.

This might mean starting to market the property while the current tenant is still in residence. It might mean scheduling viewings in the final weeks of the tenancy. It might mean pre-instructing the cleaning and maintenance work so that it can begin the day after the tenant moves out.

The point is not to rush the current tenant out. It is to ensure that the agency is fully prepared the moment the property becomes available.

## Automating the re-letting workflow

When a property becomes available, there is a predictable sequence of tasks that need to happen: marketing goes live, enquiries are handled, viewings are scheduled, offers are considered, referencing begins, and the new tenancy is set up.

In many agencies, this sequence is managed informally. Someone remembers to update the listings. Someone else arranges viewings. The referencing might sit with a third person. Without a structured workflow, tasks fall through the cracks and delays accumulate.

A CRM allows you to define this sequence as an automated workflow. When a property's status changes to "available," the system can automatically generate the full task list, assign each task to the appropriate team member, set deadlines, and send reminders when things are overdue.

This is not about replacing human judgment. It is about ensuring that the predictable, repeatable parts of the process happen consistently, every time, without relying on anyone's memory.

## Managing the applicant pipeline

Reducing void periods is not just about speed — it is about maintaining a pipeline of qualified applicants who are ready to move when a property becomes available.

A CRM helps you build and manage this pipeline. Every enquiry, every viewing, every application can be logged against both the property and the applicant. You can see at a glance how many people have expressed interest in a property, where each applicant is in the process, and what the next action is for each.

If a property has generated twenty enquiries but only two viewings, that tells you something about the marketing or the price. If a property has had ten viewings but no offers, that tells you something about the property itself or how it is being presented. The CRM gives you the data to diagnose problems and act on them quickly.

You can also maintain a waiting list of applicants who are looking for a specific type of property. When something matching their criteria becomes available, you can contact them immediately — often before the property even hits the portals. This direct matching can dramatically shorten void periods.

## Speeding up referencing

Referencing is one of the most common bottlenecks in the re-letting process. Applicants are slow to provide documents. Employers are slow to respond. Previous landlords do not return calls.

A CRM cannot force people to respond faster, but it can ensure that your agency does not contribute to the delay. Automated reminders can chase applicants who have not submitted their documents. Task assignments can ensure that someone is actively monitoring each reference rather than letting them sit idle. And status tracking can give you a real-time view of where every reference stands.

Some agencies also use the CRM to track which referencing companies are fastest and most reliable, allowing them to make better choices about who to work with.

## Coordinating with contractors

The gap between one tenant leaving and the next moving in often involves maintenance or preparation work. Cleaning, painting, minor repairs, garden maintenance — these tasks need to be coordinated efficiently to minimise the time the property is empty.

A CRM that lets you manage contractor relationships and schedule work against specific properties can help you pre-book these services. If you know a tenant is leaving on the 15th, you can have the cleaners booked for the 16th, the painter for the 17th, and the new check-in inspection for the 18th.

Without this coordination, the work happens in a haphazard order, each task triggering the next only when someone remembers to arrange it.

## Reporting and continuous improvement

One of the most valuable aspects of using a CRM to manage void periods is the data it generates. Over time, you can see patterns — which properties have the longest voids, which parts of the process create the most delay, which team members manage the fastest turnarounds.

This data allows you to identify systemic issues and address them. If referencing is consistently the bottleneck, you can review your referencing process. If certain property types take longer to let, you can adjust your marketing strategy for those properties. If one branch consistently has shorter voids than another, you can study what they are doing differently.

Without a CRM, this analysis is almost impossible. You might have a general sense that voids are too long, but you cannot pinpoint where the problem lies or measure whether your improvements are working.

## The financial impact

Even a modest reduction in void periods has a meaningful financial impact. If a property rents for 1,000 per month and you reduce the average void period by just one week across your portfolio, the landlord saves roughly 230 per property per void. Across a portfolio of 200 properties that each experience one void per year, that is over 46,000 in recovered rental income.

For the agency, shorter voids mean more management fees collected, happier landlords, and a stronger reputation. Landlords talk to each other, and an agency known for fast re-lets attracts more instructions.

## Getting started

If you are not currently tracking void periods systematically, start with the basics. Record the date each property becomes vacant and the date the next tenancy begins. This alone will give you visibility of your average void period and highlight the outliers.

From there, build out your re-letting workflow in your CRM. Define the tasks, assign responsibilities, and set deadlines. Use the data to identify your biggest bottlenecks and address them one at a time.

Void periods will never be eliminated entirely. But they can be managed, measured, and minimised — and the difference between an agency that does this well and one that does not is often the difference between growing and standing still.
