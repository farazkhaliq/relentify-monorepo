# Timesheets for Hospitality and Pop-Up Events: Managing Temporary Staff Hours

Hospitality and events are industries where workforce management is uniquely challenging. A music festival might employ 200 temporary staff for a single weekend. A pop-up restaurant operates for three months with a rotating crew. A hotel ramps up staffing for the summer season and scales back in autumn. In each case, tracking hours accurately for a fluid workforce is essential — for payroll accuracy, legal compliance, and cost control.

Yet many hospitality and event businesses still rely on paper sign-in sheets, WhatsApp messages, or the honour system. These approaches fail at scale, create payroll disputes, and leave businesses vulnerable to compliance issues.

## The Unique Challenges of Event and Hospitality Timesheets

### Variable Workforce

Unlike an office where the same 20 people show up Monday to Friday, hospitality and events deal with constantly changing rosters. A catering company might have a core team of 10 and draw from a pool of 50 casual workers depending on the event. A festival might engage entirely different staff for setup, the event itself, and teardown.

This means your timesheet system must handle people who work infrequently, work different shifts every time, and may work for you only once. Onboarding to the system needs to be instant and frictionless.

### Non-Standard Hours

Hospitality events do not follow standard business hours. A wedding reception runs from 6pm to 2am. A festival operates around the clock for four days. A pop-up market is open 10am to 10pm on weekends only.

Your timesheet system must handle overnight shifts that cross midnight, split shifts with unpaid breaks, and schedules that change daily. Systems designed for nine-to-five office workers simply do not work.

### Multiple Locations and Roles

Staff may work different roles at different locations during a single pay period. A catering company employee might serve at a corporate lunch on Tuesday, bartend at a private party on Friday, and work as a kitchen porter at a wedding on Saturday. Each assignment may have a different pay rate.

Timesheets need to capture not just hours but location, role, and applicable rate for each shift.

### Last-Minute Changes

Events are dynamic. Staff call in sick, events run over, weather changes plans, guest numbers fluctuate. Schedules change constantly, and timesheet records need to reflect what actually happened, not what was originally planned.

## Setting Up an Effective System

### Digital Clock-In/Clock-Out

Replace paper sign-in sheets with digital clock-in systems. [Timesheet software](https://relentify.com/features/timesheets) that runs on smartphones allows staff to clock in and out from the event location.

GPS verification confirms that staff are at the correct venue when they clock in — important when you have multiple events running simultaneously and want to prevent clock-in from the wrong location or from home.

Photo verification adds another layer, requiring a selfie at clock-in that confirms the person clocking in is actually the person assigned to the shift.

### Shift-Based Scheduling

Rather than expecting staff to log their own hours from memory, build the schedule first and have staff confirm attendance against it.

Create shifts for each event: location, date, start time, end time, role, and number of staff needed. Assign specific people to each shift. When they clock in, the system matches their attendance to the scheduled shift and flags any discrepancies (late arrival, early departure, no-show).

This approach is far more reliable than open-ended time logging and gives you real-time visibility into who is where.

### Break Management

Working time regulations in most jurisdictions require rest breaks for shifts above a certain duration. Hospitality shifts are often long — 8, 10, or 12 hours — making break compliance essential.

Configure your system to require break clock-outs for shifts above the legal threshold. This creates an automatic compliance record and ensures staff are actually taking their mandated breaks.

For shorter events where breaks are impractical, document the arrangement in advance and ensure it complies with local regulations.

### Rate Management

Hospitality and events often involve multiple pay rates: standard rate, weekend rate, bank holiday rate, night shift premium, different rates for different roles. Your timesheet system should automatically apply the correct rate based on the shift details.

Configure rate rules once and let the system calculate. Manual rate application across hundreds of shifts is error-prone and time-consuming.

## Managing Temporary and Casual Staff

### Fast Onboarding

When you engage a new casual worker, they need to be in your system immediately — not after a week of paperwork. The onboarding process should take minutes: name, contact details, bank details for payment, any required certifications, and emergency contact.

Send new staff a link to download the clock-in app and register. They should be ready to work by their first shift.

### Right-to-Work and Compliance

Before anyone works their first shift, you must verify their right to work. Your system should flag any worker who has not completed right-to-work checks, preventing them from clocking in until compliance is confirmed.

Similarly, if the role requires specific certifications (food hygiene, personal licence, SIA badge for security), track these in the system and flag any that have expired.

### Self-Service Access

Give casual workers access to view their upcoming shifts, clock-in/out records, and pay history. This reduces admin queries ("What hours did you have me down for last Saturday?") and builds trust.

## Event-Day Operations

### Real-Time Monitoring

On event day, you need real-time visibility into who has arrived, who is missing, and who is running late. A dashboard showing clock-in status by shift and location lets you react quickly to no-shows — reassigning staff, calling reserves, or adjusting plans.

### Handling Overruns

Events rarely end exactly when planned. When a function runs an hour late, the staff working it need to be paid for the additional time. Your system should allow flexible clock-out times that differ from the scheduled end time, with the actual hours worked taking precedence.

Set up approval workflows so that overtime beyond a threshold requires manager approval. This prevents uncontrolled cost overruns while ensuring staff are fairly compensated.

### Multi-Event Coordination

If you are running multiple events on the same day, coordination becomes critical. Staff moving between events need to clock out of one and into the next. Your system should make this seamless — a single swipe that ends one shift and starts another.

## Post-Event Processing

### Timesheet Review and Approval

After each event, review the timesheets before processing payroll. Check for:

- Clock-in/out times that match the scheduled shift (within reasonable tolerance)
- Break compliance records
- Any flagged discrepancies (late arrivals, early departures, missing clock-outs)
- Overtime that needs approval

Manager approval should be required before timesheets are finalised. This creates an audit trail and prevents errors flowing into payroll.

### Payroll Integration

Export approved timesheets directly to your payroll system or [accounting software](https://relentify.com/features/accounting). Manual re-entry of timesheet data into a separate payroll system doubles the work and doubles the error risk.

Look for timesheet software that integrates with your payroll provider or exports in a compatible format.

### Cost Analysis

After the event, analyse the actual labour cost against budget. How many total hours were worked? What was the blended hourly cost? How does this compare to what you quoted the client?

This analysis improves your quoting accuracy for future events and identifies inefficiencies — overstaffing during quiet periods, understaffing during peak times, or excessive overtime.

## Building Your Casual Worker Pool

Over time, your timesheet system becomes a valuable database of your casual workforce. You can see who has worked for you before, their availability patterns, their reliability (attendance and punctuality records), and which roles they have performed.

When planning future events, this data helps you staff efficiently — booking reliable workers first, matching skills to roles, and avoiding workers with poor attendance records.

## Legal Compliance

Hospitality and event businesses face specific compliance obligations around working hours.

**Maximum working hours:** Most jurisdictions limit weekly working hours. Track cumulative hours for workers who do multiple events per week to ensure compliance.

**Rest periods:** Minimum daily and weekly rest periods must be observed. A worker who finishes at 2am cannot legally start at 7am in many jurisdictions.

**Young workers:** If you employ anyone under 18, additional restrictions on hours, night work, and break requirements apply.

**Record keeping:** You are legally required to keep timesheet records for a specified period. Digital records are far easier to store and retrieve than paper.

Getting timesheet management right for hospitality and events takes investment in the right tools and processes. But the payoff is significant — accurate payroll, legal compliance, better cost control, and happier staff who trust that their hours are recorded fairly.
