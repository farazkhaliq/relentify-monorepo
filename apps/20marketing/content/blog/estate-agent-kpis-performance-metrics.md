Every estate agency has a general sense of how things are going. Business feels busy or quiet. Landlords seem happy or restless. Revenue is up or down. But general impressions are not a reliable basis for management decisions. They are shaped by recency, by the loudest voices, and by whatever happens to be top of mind on any given day.

KPIs — key performance indicators — replace impressions with data. They provide an objective, measurable view of how the business is performing, where it is improving, and where it needs attention. For estate agency managers, the right KPIs are the difference between guessing and knowing.

## Choosing the right KPIs

The most common mistake with KPIs is measuring too many things. If you track fifty metrics, none of them will receive the attention they deserve. The purpose of KPIs is focus — identifying the handful of metrics that best represent the health and direction of your business.

A good KPI has three qualities. It is measurable — you can calculate it reliably and consistently. It is actionable — if the number moves in the wrong direction, you know what to do about it. And it is meaningful — it connects to a business outcome that you care about.

For a letting agency, the most valuable KPIs fall into four categories: portfolio health, operational efficiency, financial performance, and client satisfaction.

## Portfolio health KPIs

### Properties under management

The most fundamental metric for a letting agency. How many properties are you currently managing? Track this number monthly and observe the trend. Growth indicates that instructions are outpacing losses. A flat or declining number suggests that landlord churn is a problem.

### Net portfolio growth

Properties under management minus properties lost in the same period. This gives a clearer picture than the gross number, because a portfolio that adds twenty properties but loses fifteen has only grown by five — and the loss rate may indicate a service quality issue.

### Occupancy rate

The percentage of your managed properties that are currently occupied. An occupancy rate below 95 percent warrants investigation. Which properties are vacant? How long have they been empty? What is being done to fill them?

### Average void period

The average number of days between one tenancy ending and the next beginning, across your portfolio. This is one of the most important KPIs for any letting agency because void periods directly affect landlord revenue and your management fee income.

Track this overall and by property type, area, and branch to identify where voids are longest and why.

## Operational efficiency KPIs

### Time to let

The number of days from a property being listed to a tenancy agreement being signed. This measures the efficiency of your marketing, viewings, and referencing process end to end.

### Enquiry response time

The time between receiving an enquiry and making personal contact with the applicant. Research consistently shows that faster response times correlate with higher conversion rates. If your average response time is measured in hours rather than minutes, there is room for improvement.

### Viewing to offer conversion rate

The percentage of viewings that result in an offer. A low conversion rate may indicate problems with property presentation, pricing, or the way viewings are conducted. A high conversion rate suggests strong matching between applicants and properties.

### Renewal rate

The percentage of tenancies that are renewed at expiry. A high renewal rate means less turnover, fewer void periods, and more stable income. A declining renewal rate is an early warning of tenant dissatisfaction or market changes.

### Compliance rate

The percentage of compliance certificates that are current across your portfolio. This should be 100 percent at all times. Anything less is a risk that needs immediate attention.

## Financial performance KPIs

### Revenue per property

Total management fee income divided by the number of properties under management. This metric shows how effectively you are monetising your portfolio and whether ancillary fees are contributing to revenue.

### Rent collection rate

The percentage of rent due that is actually collected within the expected period. A rate below 98 percent suggests arrears issues that need to be addressed.

### Average rent achieved vs asking price

The rent at which properties are actually let compared to the initial asking price. Consistently letting below asking price may indicate overpricing in your valuations.

### Cost per instruction

The total cost of marketing and sales activities divided by the number of new instructions won. This helps you understand the efficiency of your instruction-winning process and allocate your marketing budget effectively.

### Revenue per team member

Total revenue divided by the number of fee-earning staff. This is a measure of team productivity and helps with capacity planning.

## Client satisfaction KPIs

### Landlord retention rate

The percentage of landlords who remain with your agency from one year to the next. High retention indicates satisfaction. Declining retention demands investigation.

### Complaint rate

The number of complaints received per hundred properties under management, measured monthly or quarterly. Track the trend rather than the absolute number — a rising complaint rate is a signal even if the absolute number is low.

### Net promoter score

If you survey your landlords and tenants — and you should — a net promoter score (NPS) provides a single number that captures overall satisfaction. Ask one question: "How likely are you to recommend our agency to a colleague or friend?" on a scale of 0 to 10.

Promoters (9-10) minus detractors (0-6) gives you the NPS. A positive score is good. A score above 50 is excellent. Track it over time.

### Referral rate

The number of new leads generated by referrals as a percentage of total leads. A high referral rate is both a satisfaction indicator and a growth driver.

## Setting targets

KPIs without targets are just numbers. For each KPI, establish a target that is ambitious but achievable, based on your historical performance and industry benchmarks.

Review targets quarterly. If you consistently exceed a target, raise it. If you consistently miss one, investigate whether the target is unrealistic or whether there is a genuine performance issue.

## Reporting cadence

Different KPIs need different reporting frequencies.

**Weekly**: enquiry response time, viewing activity, offers pending, compliance alerts. These are operational metrics that require timely attention.

**Monthly**: properties under management, occupancy rate, void period average, rent collection rate, revenue per property. These are performance metrics that show trends over time.

**Quarterly**: landlord retention, complaint rate, NPS, cost per instruction. These are strategic metrics that change slowly and benefit from a longer view.

## Using KPIs in practice

KPIs are most effective when they are visible, discussed, and acted upon. Display your key metrics on a dashboard that the team can see. Review them in weekly and monthly meetings. When a metric moves in the wrong direction, investigate the cause and agree on corrective actions.

Avoid the temptation to use KPIs as punishment. If an agent's conversion rate drops, the first response should be support, not criticism. Perhaps they need training. Perhaps their patch has changed. Perhaps the properties they are working with are harder to let.

KPIs are diagnostic tools, not judgment tools. They tell you where to look, not necessarily what to conclude.
