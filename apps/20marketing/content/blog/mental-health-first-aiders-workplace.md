Mental health challenges affect a significant proportion of the working population at any given time. Anxiety, depression, stress, and burnout do not check their symptoms at the office door. They affect concentration, productivity, relationships with colleagues, and attendance. For employers, the cost of unaddressed mental health issues is substantial — in absenteeism, presenteeism, staff turnover, and lost talent.

Mental health first aiders (MHFAs) are employees who have been trained to recognise the signs of mental health difficulties, provide initial support, and guide colleagues toward appropriate professional help. They are not therapists or counsellors. They are a first point of contact — the mental health equivalent of a physical first aider.

## The Case for Mental Health First Aiders

The argument for having trained MHFAs in your workplace goes beyond compassion, though compassion alone is reason enough.

From a business perspective, early intervention reduces the severity and duration of mental health episodes. An employee who receives support at the first signs of difficulty is far more likely to stay at work, remain productive, and recover quickly than one who struggles in silence until they reach a crisis point.

There is also a cultural benefit. The presence of MHFAs sends a signal to the entire workforce that mental health is taken seriously and that seeking help is encouraged rather than stigmatised. This openness benefits everyone, not just those who directly use the service.

## What Mental Health First Aiders Do

MHFAs are trained to perform several specific functions. They are not expected to diagnose conditions, provide ongoing therapy, or solve complex personal problems. Their role is focused and practical.

They spot early warning signs. Changes in behaviour, mood, attendance patterns, or work quality can indicate that a colleague is struggling. MHFAs are trained to notice these signals and respond appropriately.

They provide initial support. This might be as simple as asking someone if they are okay and listening without judgement. Sometimes people need to be heard more than they need advice. A supportive conversation can make a meaningful difference.

They signpost professional help. MHFAs know what resources are available, both within the organisation and externally. They can direct colleagues to employee assistance programmes, counselling services, GP appointments, or crisis helplines as appropriate.

They maintain confidentiality. Trust is fundamental to the MHFA role. People will only approach an MHFA if they are confident that their conversation will remain private.

## Selecting the Right People

Not everyone is suited to the MHFA role, and it should never be assigned without the individual's full consent and enthusiasm. Look for people who are naturally empathetic, good listeners, respected by their colleagues, and genuinely interested in supporting others.

Aim for diversity in your MHFA team. Having MHFAs across different departments, seniority levels, genders, and age groups makes it more likely that every employee will feel comfortable approaching someone. A junior team member might hesitate to discuss their struggles with a senior manager, but they might open up to a peer in a different department.

Consider the workload implications. Being an MHFA requires time — time for training, time for conversations with colleagues, and time for their own reflective practice. If someone is already overwhelmed with their regular duties, adding the MHFA role will only create more stress.

## Training and Certification

Several accredited training providers offer MHFA courses that typically run over one or two days. The training covers common mental health conditions, listening skills, crisis response, and signposting techniques.

Choose a training provider that is reputable and recognised. Look for courses that include practical scenarios and role-playing exercises rather than purely theoretical content. MHFAs need to practise their skills in a safe environment before they encounter real situations.

Training should be refreshed regularly. Mental health knowledge evolves, and skills can deteriorate without practice. Annual refresher sessions keep your MHFAs confident and competent.

## How Many MHFAs Do You Need?

There is no legal minimum, but a common guideline is one MHFA for every ten employees. This ratio ensures reasonable coverage without overburdening any individual.

For larger organisations, consider your physical layout. If you have multiple offices, floors, or sites, ensure each location has at least one MHFA. For remote or hybrid workforces, ensure MHFAs are accessible virtually as well as in person.

## Supporting Your MHFAs

Supporting others with their mental health can be emotionally demanding. Your MHFAs need their own support structures to prevent burnout and secondary trauma.

Provide regular supervision or debriefing opportunities where MHFAs can discuss their experiences in a confidential setting. This might be facilitated by an external counsellor or occupational health professional.

Create a peer support network among your MHFAs so they can share experiences and learn from each other. Regular meetups, even informal ones, help maintain cohesion and morale.

Make it clear that MHFAs can step back from the role temporarily or permanently if it becomes too much. The role should be supportive, not sacrificial.

## Legal and Ethical Considerations

While there is no legal requirement to have MHFAs in most jurisdictions, employers do have a duty of care to their employees that includes mental health. Having trained MHFAs demonstrates that you are taking reasonable steps to support employee wellbeing.

Confidentiality is the most important ethical consideration. MHFAs must understand the boundaries of confidentiality, including the limited circumstances where they may need to break confidence, such as when someone is at immediate risk of harm to themselves or others.

Document your MHFA programme clearly, including the roles and responsibilities of MHFAs, the boundaries of their role, confidentiality protocols, and escalation procedures. This protects both the MHFAs and the organisation.

## Promoting the Service

Having MHFAs is only useful if employees know they exist and how to access them. Promote your MHFA programme through multiple channels: induction packs for new starters, posters in communal areas, mentions in team meetings, and information on your company intranet.

Make sure employees know who their MHFAs are. Some organisations use badges, lanyards, or desk signs to identify MHFAs. Others prefer a more discreet approach where the list of MHFAs is available on the intranet or through HR.

Normalise the conversation about mental health across the organisation. The more openly mental health is discussed, the more comfortable people will feel reaching out for support.

## Measuring Impact

Measuring the impact of your MHFA programme can be challenging because the most valuable outcomes — conversations that prevent crises, early interventions that avoid sick leave — are often invisible.

Useful metrics include the number of interactions MHFAs have (anonymised, without identifying individuals), employee engagement survey scores related to wellbeing, sickness absence rates, and employee retention. Track these over time to identify trends.

Qualitative feedback from both MHFAs and the wider workforce provides context that numbers alone cannot capture. Regular pulse surveys can gauge how supported employees feel and whether awareness of the programme is high enough.

## Integrating with Broader Wellbeing Initiatives

MHFAs work best as part of a wider wellbeing strategy, not as a standalone measure. They complement employee assistance programmes, flexible working arrangements, manageable workloads, and a healthy organisational culture.

Use your HR systems to track wellbeing initiatives and their outcomes. Tools that help you [manage employee records and track patterns](/payroll) make it easier to spot trends that might indicate systemic issues, such as high stress levels in a particular team or increasing absence rates during certain periods.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Do not treat MHFAs as a substitute for professional support. They are a first point of contact, not a replacement for counselling or therapy. Ensure professional services are available and accessible for employees who need them.

Do not overload your MHFAs. If a small number of people are handling a disproportionate volume of conversations, your ratio is wrong or you need additional support structures.

Do not assume that appointing MHFAs alone creates a mentally healthy workplace. If the underlying culture is toxic, the workload is unreasonable, or management practices are poor, no amount of first aiders will fix the root causes.

## Getting Started

If your organisation does not yet have MHFAs, the process of setting up a programme is straightforward. Identify interested volunteers, arrange accredited training, establish your protocols and support structures, and promote the service to your workforce.

The investment is modest relative to the potential benefits. A mentally healthier workforce is more engaged, more productive, more loyal, and more resilient. And for the individuals who receive timely support, the impact can be life-changing.
