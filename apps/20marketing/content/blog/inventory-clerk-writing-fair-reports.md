The quality of an inventory clerk's work comes down to one thing: the report. You can inspect a property with the sharpest eye in the industry, but if your report does not capture what you observed clearly, precisely, and fairly, it fails in its purpose.

Fair and accurate reports protect landlords, tenants, and your own professional reputation. They are the documents that deposit adjudicators rely on to make decisions, and they need to withstand scrutiny from both parties. This guide covers how to write inventory reports that are objective, thorough, and defensible.

## The principles of good report writing

### Objectivity

You are an impartial observer. Your report should describe what you see, not what you think caused it. "Scratch on door, approximately 15cm, starting from handle height" is objective. "Scratch on door caused by tenant's dog" is not — you were not there when it happened, and making assumptions about cause undermines your neutrality.

Stick to facts. Describe conditions, dimensions, locations, and severity. Leave the interpretation of cause and responsibility to the landlord, tenant, and (if needed) the adjudicator.

### Precision

Vague language is the enemy of useful reports. "Some marks on wall" is almost meaningless. How many marks? Where on the wall? How large? What kind of marks — scuffs, stains, pen, crayon, nail holes?

Precise descriptions serve everyone. They help adjudicators understand the condition without visiting the property. They help landlords understand what they are working with. And they protect tenants from exaggerated claims.

Compare these two descriptions of the same condition:

- **Vague**: "Carpet worn in hallway"
- **Precise**: "Carpet shows flattened pile in main walkway from front door to living room, consistent with regular foot traffic. No stains or discolouration."

The precise version tells the reader exactly what is there and, implicitly, what is not. It also signals that this is likely fair wear and tear, without making that judgement explicitly.

### Consistency

Use the same terminology, the same structure, and the same level of detail throughout every report. If you describe wall condition as "good — no marks or damage" in the living room, use the same phrasing for walls in the same condition in other rooms.

Inconsistency creates confusion. If one room is described in great detail and another is glossed over, it may appear that you were thorough in some areas and careless in others.

## Structuring your report

### Follow a room-by-room approach

Work through the property systematically, room by room. Within each room, follow the same order: ceiling, walls, floor, doors, windows, fixtures, fittings, furniture (if applicable). This ensures nothing is missed and makes reports easy to navigate.

### Use clear headings and sections

Every room should have its own section heading. Within each room, use sub-headings for major elements (ceiling, walls, floor, etc.). This structure allows anyone reading the report to quickly find the information they need.

### Separate condition from cleanliness

Condition and cleanliness are different things. A carpet can be in good condition but poorly cleaned, or it can be clean but worn. Report on both, but keep the descriptions separate so there is no ambiguity.

### Include photo references

Every significant observation should reference a corresponding photograph. This can be as simple as numbering your photos and including the reference in the text: "Wall behind sofa — two small pin holes, each approximately 2mm (see photo 47)."

## Describing conditions effectively

### Walls

Note the finish (painted, wallpapered, exposed brick), colour, and condition. For damage, describe the type, size, and location:

- "Emulsion paint, white. Clean condition, no marks or damage."
- "Emulsion paint, magnolia. Small area of scuffing on south wall, behind door, approximately 10cm x 5cm, at waist height."
- "Woodchip wallpaper, cream. Three nail holes on east wall above fireplace, each approximately 3mm."

### Floors

Note the type (carpet, laminate, tile, hardwood, vinyl), colour, and condition:

- "Beige carpet, medium pile. Good condition throughout, no stains or wear."
- "Laminate flooring, oak effect. Light surface scratching near doorway, consistent with furniture movement. No deep marks or damage."
- "Ceramic tiles, white. One cracked tile in front of bath, approximately 10cm crack running diagonally. Grout yellowing in corners."

### Fixtures and fittings

For each fixture, note what it is, its material, finish, and condition:

- "Brushed chrome door handle. Good working order, no marks."
- "White plastic light switch, single. Slight yellowing consistent with age. Working."
- "Brass window latch. Stiff to operate, slight verdigris on surface."

### Furniture (furnished properties)

For each item, note the type, material, colour, and condition:

- "Two-seater fabric sofa, grey. Good condition. Light pilling on seat cushions. No stains or tears."
- "Pine dining table, seats four. Surface has multiple light ring marks, consistent with regular use. No deep scratches or structural damage."
- "Double divan bed with mattress. Mattress firm, no visible stains. Base has small scuff on right side, approximately 5cm."

## The language of condition

Develop a consistent vocabulary for describing condition. Here is a framework that works well:

**Excellent** — like new, no signs of use
**Good** — minor signs of normal use, no damage
**Fair** — visible wear consistent with age and use, no significant damage
**Poor** — noticeable wear or minor damage
**Damaged** — specific damage requiring attention (describe in detail)

When using these terms, always add descriptive detail. "Good condition" alone is not enough. "Good condition — light surface wear on edges, no chips or deep marks" tells the full story.

## Handling ambiguous situations

Some conditions are genuinely difficult to categorise. Is that mark on the wall a scuff or a stain? Is that carpet wear normal for a two-year tenancy, or is it excessive? Here is how to handle ambiguity:

### Describe, do not judge

Report what you see in factual terms. Let the description speak for itself. If a mark could be either a scuff or a stain, describe it as "discoloured area" and note its size, shape, colour, and location. Photograph it clearly. Let the parties decide what it is.

### Note uncertainty

If something is unclear, say so. "Small area of discolouration on ceiling tile — unclear whether stain or shadow from lighting angle" is more honest and more useful than guessing.

### Photograph thoroughly

When conditions are ambiguous, take extra photos from multiple angles and distances. The more visual evidence available, the easier it is for others to make a fair assessment.

## Common report writing mistakes

### Being too brief

A report that covers a three-bedroom house in two pages is almost certainly too brief. Every room needs proper coverage. Every significant feature needs documentation. Brevity in inventory reports is not a virtue — it is a risk.

### Being too subjective

Avoid language that implies blame or makes judgements about cause. "Stain on carpet — appears to be red wine" implies the tenant spilled wine. "Dark red stain on carpet, approximately 20cm diameter, between sofa and coffee table" describes what you see without assigning cause.

### Inconsistent grading

If you rate the living room walls as "good" with minor scuffs, and the bedroom walls as "fair" with the same type of minor scuffs, your grading is inconsistent. This undermines the report's credibility and can be challenged by either party.

### Missing the small things

Door handles, light switches, plug sockets, window catches — these small items are frequently ignored but are also frequently damaged. A thorough report covers everything, not just the obvious.

### Not recording items in good condition

If you only note things that are damaged or worn, the report has gaps. At check-out, if a door handle that was not mentioned is found damaged, the landlord has no proof it was in good condition at check-in. Record everything, including items with no issues.

## The standard you should aim for

Write every report as if it will be used in a deposit dispute. Because one day, it will. When that happens, your report needs to be clear enough for someone who has never seen the property to understand its condition, detailed enough to answer specific questions about individual rooms and items, and fair enough that neither party can reasonably claim it is biased.

That is the standard. Meet it consistently, and your reports will serve their purpose every time.
