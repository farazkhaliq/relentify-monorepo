When your business buys a laptop, a vehicle, or a piece of machinery, you cannot simply deduct the full cost as an expense in the year you buy it. These are fixed assets — items that will be used over multiple years — and accounting rules require you to spread the cost over their useful life. This spreading process is called depreciation.

Depreciation is one of those accounting concepts that seems unnecessarily complicated at first but is actually quite logical once you understand the reasoning. It ensures your financial reports accurately reflect the cost of running your business and the declining value of your assets.

## What is a fixed asset?

A fixed asset (also called a non-current asset or capital asset) is something your business owns and uses over a period longer than one year. Common examples include:

- Computers and laptops
- Office furniture (desks, chairs, shelving)
- Vehicles (cars, vans, trucks)
- Machinery and equipment
- Buildings and property
- Leasehold improvements (renovations to rented premises)
- Intangible assets (software licences, patents)

The key distinction is between capital expenditure (buying an asset to use over time) and revenue expenditure (buying something that is consumed immediately or within the year). A new printer is a fixed asset; printer paper is a revenue expense.

Most businesses set a capitalisation threshold — a minimum value below which purchases are expensed immediately rather than capitalised. A common threshold is 250-500. Anything below this is treated as an expense even if it could theoretically last more than a year.

## What is depreciation?

Depreciation is the process of allocating the cost of a fixed asset over its useful life. It recognises that assets lose value over time through use, wear and tear, or obsolescence.

When you buy a computer for 2,000, it does not become worthless the day you buy it. Nor is it worth 2,000 five years later. Depreciation distributes the cost across the years you use the asset, matching the expense to the revenue it helps generate.

### Why not just expense it immediately?

If you bought 50,000 worth of equipment in January and expensed it all immediately, your profit for that month would be massively understated. The next month (with no equipment purchase) would look much more profitable. This distorts your financial results and makes it impossible to compare periods meaningfully.

By depreciating the 50,000 over five years, each year bears 10,000 of the cost — a much more accurate picture of your ongoing business costs.

## Depreciation methods

### Straight-line depreciation

The simplest and most common method. The cost is spread evenly over the asset's useful life.

**Formula:** (Cost - Residual value) / Useful life = Annual depreciation

**Example:** A vehicle costs 20,000, has an expected useful life of 5 years, and a residual value of 5,000.

Annual depreciation = (20,000 - 5,000) / 5 = 3,000 per year

After 5 years, the vehicle is recorded at its residual value of 5,000 on the balance sheet.

### Reducing balance depreciation

Also called diminishing balance or declining balance. A fixed percentage is applied to the remaining book value each year. This results in higher depreciation in the early years and lower depreciation later.

**Formula:** Book value x Depreciation rate = Annual depreciation

**Example:** Equipment costs 10,000, depreciated at 25% reducing balance.

- Year 1: 10,000 x 25% = 2,500 (book value now 7,500)
- Year 2: 7,500 x 25% = 1,875 (book value now 5,625)
- Year 3: 5,625 x 25% = 1,406 (book value now 4,219)
- Year 4: 4,219 x 25% = 1,055 (book value now 3,164)
- Year 5: 3,164 x 25% = 791 (book value now 2,373)

The reducing balance method reflects the reality that many assets lose more value in their early years. A new car loses a significant percentage of its value in the first year, then less each subsequent year.

### Which method to choose?

Most small businesses use straight-line depreciation for its simplicity. Your accountant can advise on which method best reflects the pattern of value reduction for each type of asset.

Common conventions:
- Computer equipment: 3-4 years straight-line or 33% reducing balance
- Office furniture: 5-10 years straight-line
- Vehicles: 4-5 years straight-line or 25% reducing balance
- Buildings: 25-50 years straight-line
- Leasehold improvements: over the remaining lease term

## The asset register

An asset register is a record of all your fixed assets. For each asset, it tracks:

- Description and identification details
- Date of purchase
- Purchase cost
- Depreciation method and rate
- Accumulated depreciation to date
- Current book value (cost minus accumulated depreciation)
- Location
- Disposal date and proceeds (when sold or scrapped)

Maintaining an accurate asset register is important for:
- Calculating depreciation correctly each period
- Preparing your balance sheet
- Insurance purposes
- Capital allowance claims for tax
- Asset verification and audit

## Depreciation vs capital allowances

This distinction confuses many business owners. They are related but different:

**Depreciation** is the accounting treatment — how you spread the cost in your financial reports. It is determined by accounting standards and your chosen method.

**Capital allowances** are the tax treatment — how your tax authority allows you to deduct the cost for tax purposes. The rates and rules are set by the tax authority and may differ from your accounting depreciation.

When calculating your tax liability, you:
1. Start with your accounting profit (which includes the depreciation charge)
2. Add back the depreciation (because the tax authority does not use your depreciation)
3. Deduct capital allowances (the tax authority's version of depreciation)

The net effect might be the same, more, or less than your accounting depreciation, depending on the capital allowance rates and any special reliefs available.

## Disposing of fixed assets

When you sell, scrap, or dispose of a fixed asset, you need to account for it:

### Calculate the gain or loss

**Proceeds - Book value = Gain or loss on disposal**

- If you sell an asset for more than its book value, you have a gain
- If you sell it for less than its book value, you have a loss

**Example:** You sell a vehicle with a book value of 5,000 for 6,500.

Gain on disposal = 6,500 - 5,000 = 1,500 gain

### Record the disposal

The accounting entries remove the asset and accumulated depreciation from the balance sheet and record the gain or loss in the profit and loss statement.

### Tax implications

Disposal of fixed assets may trigger capital allowance adjustments (balancing charges or balancing allowances) for tax purposes. Your accountant can advise on the specific treatment.

## Impairment

Sometimes an asset loses value more quickly than depreciation reflects. A piece of machinery might become obsolete due to technological change, or a vehicle might be damaged beyond economic repair.

In these cases, you may need to record an impairment — a one-off write-down of the asset to its recoverable value. Impairment is separate from depreciation and is recorded as an additional expense.

## Practical tips

### Be consistent

Choose a depreciation method and rate for each type of asset and apply it consistently from year to year. Changing methods mid-stream distorts your financial reports and may require disclosure.

### Review useful life estimates periodically

If an asset is lasting significantly longer or shorter than your original estimate, adjust the useful life going forward. A computer expected to last 3 years that is still going strong after 5 years should not keep depreciating below zero.

### Do not forget residual values

If you expect an asset to have value at the end of its useful life (e.g., you can sell a vehicle), factor in the residual value. Depreciating to zero when the asset will be sold for 5,000 overstates your expenses.

### Reconcile with physical assets

At least once a year, compare your asset register with the actual assets in your business. Are all registered assets still in use? Have any been disposed of without being recorded? This physical verification keeps your register accurate.

### Keep purchase documentation

Maintain invoices and receipts for all fixed asset purchases. You will need these for capital allowance claims, insurance, and potential audits.

Fixed assets and depreciation are a fundamental part of business accounting. Getting them right ensures your financial reports are accurate, your tax claims are correct, and you have a clear picture of the assets your business owns and their current value.
