Parental leave is no longer just about maternity. Statutory Paternity Pay and Shared Parental Leave give both parents options for taking time off when a child arrives. As an employer, you need to understand both schemes — who qualifies, how payments are calculated, and what your obligations are.

This guide covers both Statutory Paternity Pay (SPP) and Shared Parental Leave and Pay (ShPL/ShPP) from the employer's perspective.

## Statutory Paternity Pay: the basics

Statutory Paternity Pay is available to eligible employees when their partner has a baby, or when they adopt a child. It provides paid time off for the partner who is not taking maternity or adoption leave.

### Eligibility for SPP

An employee qualifies for SPP if they meet all of the following:

- They are the biological father, the mother's spouse or partner, or the intended parent in a surrogacy arrangement
- They have been continuously employed by you for at least 26 weeks by the end of the 15th week before the expected week of childbirth (or by the week they are matched with a child for adoption)
- They earn at least the Lower Earnings Limit on average
- They have given you correct notice
- They will be taking time off to care for the child or support the mother

The employee does not need to be the biological parent — being the partner of the person giving birth is sufficient.

### How much paternity leave?

Eligible employees can take either one week or two consecutive weeks of paternity leave. The leave must be taken within 56 days of the birth (or placement for adoption). It cannot be taken in odd days or split into two separate weeks.

The employee must give you notice of their intention to take paternity leave by the 15th week before the expected week of childbirth. They should confirm the expected date of birth, how much leave they plan to take, and when they want it to start.

### Calculating SPP

SPP is paid at the flat statutory rate set by the government, or 90% of the employee's average weekly earnings — whichever is lower. Unlike SMP, there is no higher earnings-related rate for the first few weeks. The flat rate applies throughout.

Average weekly earnings are calculated in the same way as for SMP — over the eight-week relevant period ending with the qualifying week.

SPP is processed through your normal payroll, subject to income tax and National Insurance deductions. You can recover SPP from HMRC in the same way as SMP — 92% for most employers, or 100% plus compensation for small employers.

## Shared Parental Leave: the basics

Shared Parental Leave allows eligible parents to share up to 50 weeks of leave and up to 37 weeks of pay between them. It is designed to give families more flexibility in how they divide time off work after a child arrives.

The key principle is that the mother (or primary adopter) curtails their maternity (or adoption) leave early, and the unused balance of leave and pay is converted into a shared pot that either parent can take.

### Eligibility for ShPL

Both parents must meet separate eligibility tests:

**The mother (or primary adopter) must:**
- Be entitled to maternity leave or Statutory Maternity Pay (or Maternity Allowance)
- Have given notice to curtail their maternity leave or pay

**The partner must:**
- Have been continuously employed for at least 26 weeks by the 15th week before the expected week of childbirth
- Still be employed at the start of each period of ShPL
- Share responsibility for the child with the mother

Additionally, the other parent must meet an "employment and earnings test" — they must have been employed or self-employed for at least 26 of the 66 weeks before the expected week of childbirth, and have earned at least a minimum amount in any 13 of those weeks.

### How ShPL works in practice

The process works as follows:

1. **The mother curtails maternity leave**: She gives notice that she will end her maternity leave early. The minimum maternity leave is two weeks after the birth.
2. **The unused leave becomes available**: The remaining weeks of leave and pay (up to the 52-week and 39-week maximums) go into a shared pool.
3. **Both parents submit notices**: Each parent submits a notice to their employer stating how much ShPL they intend to take and when.
4. **Leave can be taken in blocks**: ShPL can be taken in continuous blocks (which the employer must accept) or discontinuous blocks (which the employer can refuse).
5. **Both parents can be on leave at the same time**: Unlike paternity leave, ShPL allows both parents to take leave simultaneously.

### Shared Parental Pay (ShPP)

ShPP is paid at the flat statutory rate, or 90% of average weekly earnings — whichever is lower. It follows the same calculation as SPP.

The total number of weeks of ShPP available depends on how many weeks of SMP the mother has taken. For example, if the mother takes 20 weeks of SMP before curtailing, there are 19 weeks of ShPP available (39 minus 20).

ShPP is processed through payroll with normal tax and NI deductions and can be recovered from HMRC in the same way as SMP and SPP.

### Notice requirements for ShPL

The notice requirements for ShPL are more complex than for other types of statutory leave:

- **Notice of entitlement and intention**: At least eight weeks before the first period of ShPL. This must include the expected week of childbirth, the maternity leave curtailment date, the total ShPL and ShPP each parent intends to take, and a non-binding indication of when they plan to take it.
- **Period of leave notice**: At least eight weeks before each period of ShPL. This is a binding request for specific dates.
- **Variation notice**: If the employee wants to change their planned leave dates, they must give at least eight weeks' notice. Each parent gets up to three variation notices.

Employers must respond to a continuous leave request within 14 days. You must accept continuous leave requests (consecutive weeks). Discontinuous leave requests (non-consecutive blocks) can be refused, in which case the employee can take the leave as one continuous block instead, withdraw the request, or agree a different pattern with you.

### Shared Parental Leave in Touch (SPLIT) days

Each parent can work up to 20 SPLIT days during ShPL without bringing the leave to an end. These are separate from and in addition to the 10 KIT days available during maternity leave.

SPLIT days are voluntary for both parties. Payment is by agreement, and the days do not extend the total ShPL entitlement.

## Managing parental leave as an employer

Parental leave — whether paternity leave or ShPL — involves significant administrative requirements. Here is how to manage them effectively:

### Have a clear policy

Write a parental leave policy that covers:

- Eligibility for each type of leave
- Notice requirements and deadlines
- How to apply (forms, contacts, timelines)
- Pay arrangements (statutory and any enhanced pay you offer)
- Keeping in touch days
- Return to work procedures

Make the policy accessible to all employees. Do not wait until someone becomes pregnant to think about it.

### Track dates carefully

The various qualifying weeks, notice periods, and leave windows involve multiple overlapping dates. Missing a deadline can delay payments or create compliance issues. Use your [payroll](/payroll) and HR system to track these dates and set reminders.

### Process payroll correctly

SPP and ShPP should be processed through your standard payroll, reported via RTI, and recovered from HMRC via your EPS. The calculations are straightforward once you have determined eligibility and the relevant period for average earnings.

### Budget for absence

When an employee takes parental leave, you need to cover their work. Budget for this — whether through temporary staff, overtime for existing employees, or workload redistribution. The cost of parental leave extends beyond the statutory pay itself.

### Stay compliant

Dismissing an employee or subjecting them to any detriment because they have taken (or plan to take) paternity leave or shared parental leave is automatically unfair and discriminatory. Employees on parental leave retain all their employment rights, including the right to return to their job.

## Recovering costs from HMRC

Both SPP and ShPP can be recovered from HMRC:

- **Small employers** (qualifying for Small Employers' Relief): Recover 100% plus 3% compensation
- **All other employers**: Recover 92%

Claim recovery through your Employer Payment Summary as part of your regular RTI submissions.

## Key takeaways

Paternity leave and shared parental leave give employees flexibility in how they manage the arrival of a new child. As an employer, your obligations are clear:

- Check eligibility carefully against the qualifying conditions
- Respond to notices within the required timescales
- Calculate and pay SPP/ShPP through your normal payroll
- Recover costs from HMRC
- Keep records for at least three years
