Disputes over inventory findings are an inevitable part of property management. No matter how thorough your documentation, some tenants will disagree with what is recorded — at check-in, at check-out, or both. How you handle these disagreements matters. A professional, fair, and evidence-based approach protects your position while maintaining a constructive relationship.

This guide covers the common types of inventory challenges tenants raise, how to respond to them, and how to prevent disputes from escalating.

## Common challenges at check-in

### "This damage was here before — you missed it"

A tenant reviews the check-in inventory and identifies damage that they believe is pre-existing but was not recorded. This is a legitimate concern. No inventory is perfect, and it is possible to miss a small mark, scuff, or defect during the inspection.

**How to handle it:**
- Thank the tenant for raising it. This is the system working as designed — the review period exists for exactly this reason.
- Inspect the item in question. If the tenant is correct, update the inventory to include the damage with a new photograph and a note that it was reported during the review period.
- Provide the tenant with an updated copy of the inventory.
- If you disagree that the damage was pre-existing (for example, it appears fresh), note the tenant's claim in the file and add the photograph. The record now includes both perspectives.

### "I disagree with the condition rating"

A tenant may feel that an item rated as "good" should be rated as "fair" or that a description is too generous, failing to note wear or issues they consider significant.

**How to handle it:**
- Review the item with the tenant. If their assessment is reasonable, adjust the description to be more precise.
- If you believe the original assessment is accurate, explain your reasoning. Reference the photographs for support.
- Remember that a more conservative (lower) condition rating at check-in actually protects the tenant. If they want the baseline to reflect a lower condition, that works in their favour at check-out.

### "The property was not clean enough when I moved in"

Tenants sometimes feel that the property's cleaning standard at check-in was not acceptable, even if the inventory describes it as clean.

**How to handle it:**
- If the property genuinely was not cleaned to a professional standard, address it. Arrange for cleaning or adjust the inventory to reflect the actual standard.
- If the tenant's expectation is unreasonably high (for example, expecting surgical cleanliness in a well-maintained but lived-in property), explain the standard that was documented and why it is considered acceptable.
- Either way, ensure the check-in inventory accurately reflects the cleaning standard as found. This is the baseline that check-out will be compared against.

## Common challenges at check-out

### "That damage was already there"

The most frequent check-out dispute. The tenant claims that damage identified in the check-out report was present at check-in and was either recorded (in which case they are right, and no deduction is warranted) or missed (in which case it is a matter of evidence).

**How to handle it:**
- Review the check-in inventory and photographs for the area in question.
- If the damage appears in the check-in record, acknowledge it and withdraw the claim.
- If the damage does not appear in the check-in record, and the check-in photographs clearly show the area without damage, present the evidence. The side-by-side comparison is usually conclusive.
- If the check-in documentation does not clearly cover the area in question (a gap in the evidence), you may need to concede or negotiate. This is why thorough check-in documentation is so important.

### "That is fair wear and tear"

Tenants commonly argue that deterioration is normal use rather than damage. Sometimes they are right.

**How to handle it:**
- Assess the claim objectively. Consider the length of the tenancy, the condition at check-in, the age of the item, and the number of occupants.
- If the deterioration is genuinely consistent with fair wear and tear, withdraw the claim. Claiming for normal use undermines your credibility.
- If the deterioration goes beyond what is reasonable, explain your reasoning with reference to the evidence. Note the condition at check-in, the change observed, and why it exceeds normal use.

### "The cleaning was adequate"

Tenants may feel they cleaned the property sufficiently, while the inventory or landlord disagrees.

**How to handle it:**
- Compare the check-out cleanliness against the check-in standard, using photographs. If the check-in showed a professionally cleaned property and the check-out shows substandard cleaning, the evidence supports the claim.
- Be specific about which areas fall short. "The property is not clean" is too vague. "The oven interior has significant grease build-up not present at check-in (see photos 24 and 89)" is evidence.
- If the tenant offers to re-clean, consider allowing it. A tenant who addresses the issue willingly is a better outcome than a formal dispute.

### "The deduction is too high"

Even when the tenant accepts that there is an issue, they may dispute the cost of remediation.

**How to handle it:**
- Provide evidence for every deduction — invoices, quotes, or receipts.
- Apply betterment where appropriate. If the item was not new at check-in, the deduction should reflect its remaining useful life, not the full replacement cost.
- Be prepared to negotiate. A reasonable compromise that both parties can accept is almost always better than a formal dispute.

## The dispute resolution process

### Step 1: Direct communication

Most disputes can be resolved through direct communication. Present the evidence clearly and calmly. Listen to the tenant's perspective. Look for common ground.

### Step 2: Formal proposal

If direct discussion does not resolve the matter, put your proposal in writing. Include:
- A summary of each proposed deduction
- The evidence supporting each claim (reference to check-in and check-out reports, photographs, invoices)
- The total amount proposed for deduction
- A clear deadline for the tenant to respond

### Step 3: Negotiation

Be willing to negotiate. If a tenant accepts most of the deductions but disputes one or two items, consider whether the disputed items are worth escalating over. The cost and stress of formal adjudication may outweigh the amount in question.

### Step 4: Formal adjudication

If negotiation fails, either party can escalate to the deposit protection scheme's dispute resolution service. At this point, both parties submit evidence, and an independent adjudicator makes a binding decision.

## Preventing disputes before they start

### Thorough check-in documentation

The single most effective way to prevent disputes is a comprehensive, photographic, timestamped check-in inventory. When the evidence is clear, there is very little room for disagreement.

### Tenant involvement at check-in

Conducting the check-in with the tenant present and getting their signed acknowledgment of the inventory significantly reduces later challenges. The tenant has seen the property, reviewed the documentation, and had the opportunity to raise concerns.

### Clear expectations

At the start of the tenancy, communicate clearly about:
- What the inventory is and how it will be used
- The tenant's responsibilities for the property
- The standard expected at check-out
- How deposit deductions work

### Mid-tenancy check-ups

Regular inspections during the tenancy allow you to identify and address issues before they become check-out disputes. A tenant who is told about a cleaning concern at month six has time to address it before the tenancy ends.

### Professional check-out

A thorough, impartial check-out report that follows the same format as the check-in provides the clear comparison needed to assess any claims fairly.

## The right mindset

Handling inventory disputes well requires a combination of firmness and fairness. Be firm about legitimate claims supported by strong evidence. Be fair in acknowledging when the tenant has a valid point. And be professional throughout — even when the tenant is not.

Most tenants are reasonable people who want a fair outcome. When you present clear evidence and reasonable proposals, most disputes resolve without formal adjudication. The inventory is what makes that possible.
