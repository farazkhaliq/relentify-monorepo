Choosing timesheet software should be straightforward. You need a system that records when people work, makes that data accessible, and feeds it into payroll or invoicing. In practice, the market is crowded with options that range from simple clock-in apps to enterprise workforce management platforms, and the differences between them are not always obvious from a features page.

This guide is designed to help you cut through the noise. Rather than comparing specific products, it focuses on the features and considerations that actually matter when selecting a timesheet system — so you can evaluate any tool against your real requirements.

## Start with your actual problem

Before looking at software, define the problem you are solving. This sounds obvious, but many businesses start by browsing tools and end up buying features they do not need while missing capabilities they do.

Ask yourself these questions:

- **Who needs to track time?** Office staff, field workers, remote employees, contractors, or a mix?
- **What are they tracking?** Shift attendance, project hours, billable time, or all three?
- **Where are they working?** A single site, multiple locations, client premises, or from home?
- **What happens with the data?** Does it feed into payroll, client invoicing, project costing, or compliance reporting?
- **What is broken right now?** Late submissions, inaccurate records, payroll disputes, compliance gaps, or manual admin overhead?

The answers will narrow your search significantly. A construction company with workers across multiple sites has very different needs from an accounting firm tracking billable hours. A tool that is perfect for one will be wrong for the other.

## Core features every timesheet system needs

Regardless of your industry or team size, certain capabilities are non-negotiable. If a tool does not do these well, everything else is irrelevant.

### Clock-in and clock-out

The most basic function, but the implementation matters. Workers should be able to start and end their shifts quickly, from whatever device they have. For most teams, this means a mobile app. For site-based workers, it might mean a shared tablet or kiosk. For office staff, a desktop browser.

Look for a system where clocking in takes less than ten seconds. If it requires navigating menus, loading screens, or entering codes, adoption will suffer. The best systems make it a single tap.

### Manager approval workflow

Raw timesheet data needs review before it becomes payroll input. Look for a system where managers can see submitted timesheets, approve or reject them, and add notes. The approval workflow should be simple — a dashboard showing pending submissions with one-click approve — not a multi-step process that adds more admin than it saves.

### Reporting and export

Timesheet data is only useful if you can get it out of the system in the format you need. At minimum, the tool should export to CSV or Excel. Better systems offer direct integrations with payroll providers, accounting software, or invoicing tools. Check what reports are available: total hours per employee, overtime summaries, project breakdowns, and site-level reports are the most commonly needed.

### Break tracking

Employment law in most jurisdictions requires employers to record rest breaks. Your timesheet system should handle this — either by letting workers log breaks manually or by applying break rules automatically based on shift length. A system that ignores breaks is a compliance gap waiting to become a problem.

### Edit and audit trail

Mistakes happen. Workers clock in late, forget to clock out, or log time to the wrong project. The system needs to allow edits — but it also needs to record those edits. An audit trail showing who changed what and when is essential for compliance and for resolving disputes.

## Features that matter for specific use cases

Beyond the basics, the right features depend on your situation. Here are the most common requirements and what to look for.

### GPS verification (field and mobile teams)

If your workers are based at client sites, construction locations, or multiple premises, GPS-verified clock-ins confirm they were on site when they logged their time. This is not about surveillance — it is about accuracy. It prevents honest mistakes (clocking in from the car park before arriving on site) and protects against time fraud.

GPS verification is particularly valuable for businesses that bill clients for on-site hours, since it provides evidence that the hours invoiced were genuinely worked at the client's location.

### Project and job tracking

Service businesses, agencies, and consultancies need to know not just how many hours someone worked, but what they worked on. Look for a system that lets workers allocate time to specific projects, clients, or job codes. This data feeds into project profitability reports and client invoicing.

The best project-tracking timesheets let you set budgets per project and alert you when hours approach or exceed the budget. This prevents scope creep from silently eroding margins.

### Multi-site management

If you manage workers across multiple locations — branches, client sites, or temporary project sites — you need a system that organises timesheets by site. This lets site managers approve timesheets for their location without seeing data from other sites, and it gives central management a consolidated view across all locations.

### Shift scheduling integration

Some timesheet tools include shift scheduling, letting you plan rotas and then compare scheduled hours against actual hours worked. This is useful for retail, hospitality, healthcare, and any business with variable shift patterns. If your scheduling and timesheets are in separate systems, someone has to reconcile them manually — which defeats the purpose of automating either.

### Overtime rules and alerts

Different jurisdictions and employment contracts have different overtime rules. Some trigger overtime pay after a daily threshold, others after a weekly threshold, and some use both. Your timesheet system should let you configure these rules and flag when a worker is approaching or exceeding overtime limits. Without this, overtime costs become visible only at payroll — by which point it is too late to manage them.

### Contractor and freelancer support

If you work with contractors or freelancers alongside permanent staff, your system needs to handle both. Contractors may track time differently — by project rather than by shift — and their data may feed into invoices rather than payroll. Look for a system that accommodates different worker types without forcing everyone into the same workflow.

## Integration and data flow

A timesheet system does not exist in isolation. The data it captures needs to flow into other systems: payroll, accounting, project management, or HR software. The quality of these integrations should be a significant factor in your decision.

### Payroll integration

The most common integration point. Check whether the tool connects directly to your payroll provider or whether you need to export data and import it manually. Direct integrations save time and reduce errors. If your payroll provider is not supported, CSV export with configurable columns is the next best option.

### Accounting software

If you track billable hours, the timesheet data should flow into your invoicing or accounting system. Some timesheet tools integrate with accounting platforms to create draft invoices based on tracked hours. This eliminates the gap between "time recorded" and "time invoiced" — a gap where billable hours are routinely lost.

### API access

If you have custom reporting needs or want to pull timesheet data into a business intelligence tool, check whether the system offers an API. A well-documented API gives you flexibility to build the integrations and reports you need without being limited to what the vendor provides out of the box.

## Usability and adoption

The best timesheet system in the world is useless if your team does not use it. Adoption is the single biggest risk in any timesheet implementation, and it is almost entirely a function of usability.

### Mobile experience

For most teams, the mobile app is the primary interface. Test it yourself before committing. Is it fast? Is the clock-in button prominent? Can a worker complete their entire timesheet without zooming, scrolling, or hunting for options? If the mobile experience is clunky, workers will resist using it.

### Offline capability

Field workers do not always have reliable connectivity. If your team works in basements, rural areas, or underground, the app needs to work offline and sync when connectivity returns. A system that fails without signal will generate incomplete records and frustrated workers.

### Onboarding speed

How quickly can a new worker start using the system? If it requires a training session, onboarding is a bottleneck. The best tools are intuitive enough that a new worker can clock in on their first day with a two-minute explanation.

## Pricing models

Timesheet software pricing varies widely and the cheapest option is rarely the best value. Understand the pricing model before committing.

### Per-user per-month

The most common model. You pay a fixed amount per active user per month. This scales linearly with your team size. Watch for minimum user counts or bundled tiers that force you to pay for more seats than you need.

### Flat rate

Some tools charge a single monthly fee regardless of user count. This is attractive for growing businesses but may come with feature limitations at lower tiers.

### Free tiers

Several timesheet tools offer free plans for small teams. These are useful for testing but often lack features you will need as you grow — approval workflows, GPS verification, integrations, or reporting. Factor in the cost of switching later if you outgrow the free tier.

### Hidden costs

Check for charges that are not obvious on the pricing page: setup fees, data export fees, integration fees, or charges for premium support. Also check what happens to your data if you cancel — can you export everything, or are you locked in?

## Evaluating vendors

Once you have shortlisted tools that meet your feature and integration requirements, evaluate the vendors themselves.

### Trial period

Always trial before buying. Most tools offer a 14 or 30 day free trial. Use it with real data and real users, not just a test account. The trial is where usability issues surface.

### Support quality

Submit a support ticket during your trial. How fast is the response? Is it a real person or an automated reply? Support quality is invisible when everything works and critical when it does not.

### Roadmap and updates

Is the product actively developed? Check release notes or a public changelog. A tool that has not shipped updates in six months may be in maintenance mode. You want a vendor that is investing in the product.

### Data ownership

Confirm that your data belongs to you and that you can export it at any time in a standard format. Avoid tools that make data export difficult or charge for it — this creates lock-in that makes switching costly later.

## Making the decision

With your requirements defined, features evaluated, and trials completed, the decision usually comes down to three factors:

1. **Does it solve your actual problem?** Not the problem the vendor's marketing describes, but the specific issue you identified at the start of this process.

2. **Will your team use it?** The most feature-rich tool is worthless if adoption is low. Prioritise usability over feature count.

3. **Does the data go where it needs to?** Timesheets that do not connect to payroll, invoicing, or reporting are just a digital version of the paper forms they replaced.
