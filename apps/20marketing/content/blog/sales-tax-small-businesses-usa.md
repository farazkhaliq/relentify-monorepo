Sales tax in the United States is unlike the single national consumption tax systems found in many other countries. Instead, it is a patchwork of state, county, and city taxes, each with its own rates, rules, and exemptions. For small business owners, navigating this system is one of the more challenging aspects of financial compliance.

Understanding your sales tax obligations is essential. Collecting and remitting sales tax correctly keeps you on the right side of the law. Getting it wrong — whether by failing to collect, collecting the wrong amount, or remitting late — can result in penalties, interest, and back-tax assessments.

## How US sales tax works

### State-level tax

Forty-five states and the District of Columbia impose a sales tax. The five states with no state sales tax are Alaska, Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, and Oregon (though Alaska allows local jurisdictions to impose their own sales tax).

State sales tax rates range from roughly 2.9 percent to over 7 percent, depending on the state.

### Local taxes

Many counties, cities, and special taxing districts impose additional sales tax on top of the state rate. This means the total sales tax rate can vary significantly even within a single state.

For example, a business might charge 6.25 percent state tax plus 2 percent city tax plus 0.5 percent county tax, for a total combined rate of 8.75 percent.

### Destination vs origin-based sourcing

States use one of two approaches to determine which tax rate applies:

**Destination-based** (most states): Tax is based on where the buyer is located. If you ship goods to a customer in another city, you charge that city's rate.

**Origin-based** (some states): Tax is based on where you, the seller, are located. You charge your own location's rate regardless of where the buyer is.

Knowing which approach your state uses is critical for calculating the correct tax.

## When you need to collect sales tax

### Nexus: the trigger

You are required to collect sales tax in a state only if you have "nexus" — a sufficient connection to that state. Nexus can be established through:

**Physical nexus:**
- Having an office, warehouse, or retail location in the state
- Having employees working in the state
- Storing inventory in the state (including in third-party fulfillment centers like Amazon warehouses)
- Having an affiliate or representative in the state

**Economic nexus:**
Since the 2018 Supreme Court decision in South Dakota v. Wayfair, states can require remote sellers to collect sales tax based on economic activity alone — typically exceeding a threshold of $100,000 in sales or 200 transactions in the state during a year.

Each state sets its own economic nexus thresholds. You need to monitor your sales by state and register for sales tax collection when you cross a threshold.

### What is taxable?

Sales tax generally applies to tangible personal property (physical goods). However:

- **Services** are taxable in some states but not others
- **Digital products** (software, downloads, streaming) have varying treatment
- **SaaS products** are taxable in an increasing number of states
- **Food and groceries** are exempt or taxed at a reduced rate in many states
- **Clothing** is exempt in some states
- **Medical supplies** are often exempt

The rules vary enormously by state. What is taxable in Texas may be exempt in Pennsylvania. You must check the rules for each state where you have nexus.

## Registering for sales tax

Once you determine you have nexus in a state, you must register for a sales tax permit before collecting tax. The process typically involves:

1. Applying through the state's department of revenue website
2. Providing your business details, EIN, and ownership information
3. Receiving a sales tax permit or certificate of authority
4. Beginning to collect tax from the effective date

Do not collect sales tax without a valid permit. In some states, collecting tax without registration is illegal.

## Collecting sales tax

### Calculating the correct rate

For each transaction, you need to determine the correct combined rate (state + county + city + special district) based on either the destination or origin, depending on the state.

This is where sales tax becomes operationally challenging. With over 11,000 tax jurisdictions in the US, manual rate lookup is impractical for businesses with any significant volume.

Your [accounting software](/accounting) or a dedicated sales tax calculation service can automate this process, applying the correct rate based on the buyer's address.

### Exempt customers

Some customers are exempt from sales tax:

- Government entities
- Nonprofit organizations (in some states, for qualifying purchases)
- Resellers (who will collect tax when they sell to the end consumer)

Exempt customers should provide you with a valid exemption certificate. Retain these certificates in your records — if you are audited, you will need to prove the exemption was valid.

### Documenting tax collected

For every taxable sale, your records should show:

- The total sale amount
- The tax rate applied
- The tax amount collected
- The jurisdiction(s) the tax applies to
- The date of the transaction

## Filing and remitting sales tax

### Filing frequency

Each state assigns a filing frequency based on your sales volume — typically monthly, quarterly, or annually. Higher-volume sellers file more frequently.

You must file a return for every period, even if you had zero sales. Failure to file a zero return can result in estimated assessments and penalties.

### Filing deadlines

Deadlines vary by state. Common patterns include the 20th of the month following the reporting period, but this is not universal. Know your deadlines and set reminders.

### Remitting payment

File the return and remit the tax collected to the state, minus any deductions or discounts allowed. Some states offer a small discount (typically 1-3 percent) for timely filing, which compensates sellers for the cost of collecting and remitting.

### Penalties for late filing

Penalties for late filing and payment vary by state but commonly include:

- Late filing penalties (flat fee or percentage of tax due)
- Late payment penalties (percentage of unpaid tax)
- Interest on unpaid amounts
- Potential license revocation for chronic non-compliance

## Record-keeping requirements

Maintain detailed records of:

- All sales (taxable and exempt)
- Tax collected by jurisdiction
- Exemption certificates received
- Sales tax returns filed
- Payments made to each state

Retain these records for the period specified by each state — typically three to four years, though some states require longer.

## Managing multi-state sales tax

For businesses selling into multiple states, sales tax compliance becomes a significant operational challenge. Key strategies include:

### Use automation

Sales tax calculation and filing software can determine the correct rate, apply exemptions, file returns, and remit payments across all states where you have obligations.

### Monitor nexus thresholds

Regularly review your sales by state to identify when you are approaching economic nexus thresholds. Register proactively rather than discovering obligations after the fact.

### Voluntary disclosure

If you discover you should have been collecting sales tax in a state but were not, most states offer voluntary disclosure agreements (VDAs). These typically reduce penalties and limit the look-back period, making them preferable to waiting until the state discovers the issue.

## Common mistakes

### Not registering where you have nexus

Many small businesses are unaware of economic nexus rules and fail to register in states where they have obligations. This creates a growing liability that becomes harder to resolve over time.

### Applying the wrong rate

Using your home state's rate instead of the destination state's rate (or vice versa) results in incorrect tax collection. Automation eliminates this risk.

### Failing to file zero returns

Even if you have no sales in a state during a filing period, you must still file the return. Failure to file can trigger estimated assessments.

### Not collecting exemption certificates

If you fail to collect valid exemption certificates from exempt customers, you may be liable for the uncollected tax if audited.
