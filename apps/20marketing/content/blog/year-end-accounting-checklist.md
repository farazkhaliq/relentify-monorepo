The end of your financial year is one of the most important dates in your business calendar. It is the point at which you close your books, finalise your financial statements, and prepare for tax filing.

Done well, year-end accounting is a smooth process that takes a few hours. Done poorly — or left until the last minute — it can become a stressful scramble through disorganised records, missing receipts, and unexpected tax bills.

This checklist walks you through everything you need to do to close your year cleanly and start the new year with accurate books.

## Before year end: preparation

### 1. Confirm your year-end date

This sounds obvious, but make sure you know exactly when your financial year ends. For sole traders, it is typically 5 April (aligned with the tax year). For limited companies, it is the anniversary of incorporation unless you have changed it.

### 2. Back up your data

Before making any year-end adjustments, back up your accounting data. If you are using cloud-based software, confirm that automatic backups are running. If you are using desktop software, create a manual backup.

### 3. Chase outstanding invoices

Collect as much outstanding money as possible before year end. Send reminders on all overdue invoices and follow up by phone if necessary. This improves your cash position and, under accrual accounting, reduces your aged debtors balance.

### 4. Process outstanding supplier invoices

Make sure all supplier invoices received before year end are entered into your system, even if you have not yet paid them. Under accrual accounting, these need to be recognised in the current period.

## Reconciliation and verification

### 5. Reconcile all bank accounts

This is the most critical step. Reconcile every bank account, credit card, and payment account to the year-end date. Your accounting records should match your bank statements exactly, with any differences explained by timing items (uncleared cheques, deposits in transit).

If you have been reconciling monthly throughout the year, this should be quick. If you have not, this is where the pain starts.

### 6. Reconcile accounts receivable

Review your accounts receivable ledger against your actual outstanding invoices. Are there any invoices that have been paid but not recorded? Any duplicates? Any invoices you know will never be collected?

### 7. Reconcile accounts payable

Similarly, review your accounts payable. Make sure all bills are recorded and that any payments made near year end are correctly reflected.

### 8. Reconcile petty cash

If you maintain a petty cash float, count it and reconcile it against your petty cash records. The physical amount should match the accounting balance.

### 9. Review inter-company balances

If you operate multiple entities, reconcile any inter-company balances. Money owed between your own entities should net to zero across the group.

## Year-end adjustments

### 10. Record depreciation

Calculate and record depreciation on all fixed assets. Your accounting software or accountant will determine the appropriate method (straight-line, reducing balance) and rates for each asset type.

If you have bought or sold assets during the year, make sure the depreciation calculation reflects the actual period of ownership.

### 11. Review and write off bad debts

Are there any invoices you know will not be paid? Write them off as bad debt. This removes them from your accounts receivable and recognises the loss in your profit and loss statement.

Be realistic but cautious. An invoice that is six months overdue and the client is not responding is likely a bad debt. An invoice that is 45 days old from a reliable client is probably just late.

### 12. Accrue for expenses incurred but not yet billed

Under accrual accounting, if you have received goods or services before year end but have not yet received the invoice, you need to accrue for the cost. Common examples:

- Utility bills covering the year-end period
- Professional fees for work done but not yet invoiced
- Employee bonuses declared but not yet paid

### 13. Adjust prepaid expenses

If you have paid for something in advance that covers next year (like annual insurance or software subscriptions), the portion relating to next year should be recorded as a prepaid expense rather than a current-year cost.

### 14. Review stock/inventory values

If you carry inventory, count it at year end and value it correctly. The standard rule is to value stock at the lower of cost or net realisable value. Write down any obsolete, damaged, or slow-moving stock.

### 15. Record any provisions

If there are known future liabilities (pending legal claims, warranty obligations, restructuring costs), you may need to create a provision in your accounts.

## Tax preparation

### 16. Calculate tax liabilities

Based on your year-end profit, calculate your expected tax liability. For limited companies, this is corporation tax. For sole traders, it feeds into your self-assessment.

If you make payments on account, check whether your current-year payments are sufficient or whether you will have a balancing payment due.

### 17. Review capital allowances

Identify any assets purchased during the year that qualify for capital allowances or similar tax relief. These reduce your taxable profit and therefore your tax bill.

### 18. Check for tax-deductible expenses you may have missed

Review your records for any business expenses that were paid personally but not yet claimed through the business. Common ones include mileage, home office costs, phone bills, and small purchases.

### 19. Gather documentation

Collect all the documents your accountant will need:

- Bank statements for all accounts (full year)
- Reconciliation reports
- Asset register showing purchases and disposals
- Stock count and valuation
- Loan statements showing year-end balances
- Details of any unusual transactions

## Financial statements

### 20. Generate your profit and loss statement

Run your P&L for the full financial year. Review it for anything that looks unusual — unexpected spikes in expenses, revenue that seems too high or too low, or categories that do not make sense.

### 21. Generate your balance sheet

Run your balance sheet as at the year-end date. Check that the balances are sensible:

- Bank balances match your reconciliation
- Accounts receivable matches your outstanding invoices
- Fixed assets reflect purchases, disposals, and depreciation
- Liabilities include all known obligations

### 22. Generate your trial balance

The trial balance should balance (debits equal credits). If it does not, there is an error somewhere in your records that needs to be found and corrected.

### 23. Compare with prior year

Compare your year-end figures with the previous year. Significant changes need explanation. Your accountant will ask about them, so it is better to identify and understand them proactively.

## Closing and carrying forward

### 24. Review and close the period

Once all adjustments are made and you are satisfied with the figures, close the period in your accounting software. This prevents accidental changes to the prior year's figures. Most accounting platforms like [Relentify](/accounting) allow you to lock completed periods so that historical data cannot be modified without deliberate unlocking.

### 25. Set opening balances for the new year

Your year-end closing balances become the opening balances for the new year. Verify that these carry forward correctly in your accounting system.

### 26. Update your chart of accounts

Is your chart of accounts still fit for purpose? Year end is a good time to add new accounts you need, deactivate accounts you no longer use, and consolidate accounts that have become redundant.

### 27. Archive year-end records

Store your year-end financial statements, reconciliation reports, and supporting documents securely. You will need them for tax filing and may need them for audits or enquiries for several years.

## After year end

### 28. Review your accounting processes

What went well this year? What was painful? Use year end as an opportunity to improve your processes for the coming year. If reconciliation was difficult because you left it too long, commit to monthly reconciliation. If you were missing receipts, set up a better system for capturing them.

### 29. Set up the new year

Update any recurring invoices, standing orders, or scheduled payments for the new year. Check that your software's date ranges and reporting periods are set correctly.

### 30. Meet with your accountant

Schedule a meeting with your accountant to review the year-end figures, discuss tax planning for the coming year, and address any issues that arose during the close.

Year-end accounting does not have to be stressful. If you maintain good records throughout the year and follow a structured process at year end, it becomes a routine exercise rather than a crisis. Start this checklist two to four weeks before your year end, and you will close your books with confidence.
